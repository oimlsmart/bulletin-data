Exploiter la puissance des énergies renouvelables pour un avenir durable

Le monde se trouve aujourd'hui à un moment crucial où le besoin de solutions énergétiques durables est devenu primordial. L'ère des combustibles fossiles s'achève lentement, avec leurs effets néfastes sur l'environnement et la diminution de leur disponibilité. Dans ce contexte, les sources d'énergie renouvelables apparaissent comme la pierre angulaire d'un avenir durable. Cette édition du Bulletin de l'OIML explore l'importance des énergies renouvelables et souligne l'immense potentiel qu'elles recèlent pour répondre à nos besoins énergétiques mondiaux, grâce à de nombreux exemples concrets présentés par nos auteurs.

Les énergies renouvelables, dérivées de sources telles que le soleil, le vent, l'eau et la chaleur géothermique, offrent une alternative propre et abondante aux combustibles fossiles. Contrairement à leurs équivalents non renouvelables, ces sources ne s'épuisent pas avec le temps et produisent peu ou pas d'émissions nocives. En passant des combustibles fossiles aux énergies renouvelables, nous pouvons atténuer de manière significative le changement climatique, réduire la pollution atmosphérique et assurer un avenir plus durable aux générations futures :

- L'énergie solaire, exploité au moyen de cellules photovoltaïques ou d'énergie solaire concentrée, est extrêmement prometteuse. Le soleil est une source d'énergie abondante : en une heure, il fournit à la Terre plus d'énergie que la population entière n'en consomme en un an ! L'exploitation de l'énergie solaire permet non seulement de réduire les émissions de carbone, mais aussi d'assurer l'indépendance énergétique des communautés et des nations. En outre, les progrès de la technologie solaire font baisser les coûts, ce qui la rend de plus en plus accessible et économiquement viable.

- L'énergie éolienne est une autre source d'énergie renouvelable qui a connu une croissance remarquable ces dernières années. En exploitant la puissance des éoliennes, les pays du monde entier produisent de l'électricité propre à une échelle sans précédent.

- L'hydroélectricité, produite en captant l'énergie de l'eau qui coule, est depuis longtemps utilisée comme source d'énergie renouvelable. Elle fournit un approvisionnement énergétique constant et fiable, tandis que les réservoirs formés par les barrages peuvent servir à

d'autres fins, telles que la lutte contre les inondations et le stockage de l'eau.

- L'énergie géothermique exploite la chaleur emprisonnée dans la croûte terrestre et la convertit en électricité ou en applications de chauffage direct. Cette forme d'énergie renouvelable est non seulement abondante, mais aussi disponible 24 heures sur 24 et 7 jours sur 7, ce qui en fait une source d'énergie fiable.

Cependant, malgré l'enorme potentiel des sources d'énergie renouvelables citées ci-dessus, plusieurs défis empêchent leur adoption à grande échelle. Les coûts initiaux, l'intermittence et l'intégration au réseau restent des obstacles majeurs qui doivent être surmontés par des avancées technologiques, des politiques de soutien et des investissements adéquats. Les gouvernements, les entités du secteur privé et les institutions de recherche doivent collaborer pour accélérer le développement et le déploiement des technologies d'énergie renouvelable. En outre, la sensibilisation et l'éducation du public jouent un rôle essentiel pour favoriser un état d'esprit durable et encourager les individus à adopter des solutions en matière d'énergies renouvelables.

En tant que métrologues légales, il est clair que nous avons un rôle clé à jouer pour assurer une transition en douceur vers les énergies renouvelables, qui ne sont pas seulement un impératif environnemental, mais aussi une opportunité économique. Elles permettent de lutter contre le changement climatique, de réduire la dépendance à l'égard de ressources limitées, et de créer des emplois verts. En exploitant la puissance de l'énergie solaire, éolienne, hydroélectrique et géothermique, nous pouvons construire un avenir durable où l'énergie est abondante, propre et accessible à tous.

Les rédacteurs du Bulletin de l'OIML souhaitent remercier tout particulièrement la République populaire de Chine d'avoir parrainé cette édition et d'avoir fourni une série d'articles très instructifs, le Royaume d'Arabie Saoudite pour ses rapports et articles détaillés sur les énergies renouvelables, ainsi que la Fédération de Russie pour ses contributions. Une fois de plus, nous offrons à nos lecteurs et aux parties prenantes une édition du Bulletin riche en informations et nous espérons que vous le trouverez instructif et utile.

# Harnessing the power of renewable energy for a sustainable future

</div>

The world now stands at a crucial juncture where the need for sustainable energy solutions has become paramount. The era of fossil fuels is slowly waning, with their detrimental effects on the environment and diminishing availability. In this context, renewable energy sources emerge as the cornerstone of a sustainable future. This edition of the OIML Bulletin explores the significance of renewable energy and highlights the immense potential it holds for addressing our global energy needs, thanks to multiple real-life examples presented by our contributing authors.

Renewable energy, derived from sources such as sunlight, wind, water, and geothermal heat, offers a clean and abundant alternative to fossil fuels. Unlike their nonrenewable counterparts, these sources do not deplete over time and produce little or no harmful emissions. By transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy, we can significantly mitigate climate change, reduce air pollution, and secure a more sustainable future for generations to come:

- Solar energy, harnessed through photovoltaic cells or concentrated solar power, holds immense promise. The sun is an abundant source of power, providing more energy to the Earth in one hour than the entire population consumes in a year! Harnessing solar power not only reduces carbon emissions but also offers energy independence to communities and nations. Furthermore, advancements in solar technology are driving down costs, making it increasingly accessible and economically viable.

- Wind power is another renewable energy source that has witnessed remarkable growth in recent years. By harnessing the power of wind turbines, countries across the globe are generating clean electricity on an unprecedented scale.

- Hydropower, generated by capturing the energy of flowing water, has long been utilised as a renewable energy source. It provides a consistent and reliable energy

supply, while reservoirs formed by dams can serve additional purposes such as flood control and water storage.

Geothermal energy harnesses the heat trapped within the Earth's crust, converting it into electricity or direct heating applications. This form of renewable energy is not only abundant but is also available 24/7, making it a reliable power source.

However, despite the tremendous potential of the renewable energy sources cited above, several challenges impede their widespread adoption. Initial costs, intermittency, and grid integration remain key hurdles that need to be addressed through technological advancements, supportive policies, and adequate investment. Governments, private sector entities, and research institutions must collaborate to accelerate the development and deployment of renewable energy technologies. Furthermore, public awareness and education play a vital role in fostering a sustainable mindset and encouraging individuals to embrace renewable energy solutions.

As legal metrologists, it is clear that we have a key role to play to ensure the smooth transition to renewable energy which is not just an environmental imperative, but also an economic opportunity. It offers a pathway to combat climate change, reduce dependence on finite resources, and create green jobs. By harnessing the power of solar, wind hydroelectric, and geothermal energy, we can build a sustainable future where energy is abundant, clean, and accessible to all.

Again, we offer our readers and stakeholders an information-rich edition of the Bulletin and we hope you find it informative and useful.

# Digital sampled value stream calibrator for performing metrological work at digital substations

</div>

KISELEV V.V., KUTSOBIN A.A., DUBROVSKAYA T.A. VNIIMS, Russian Federation

## Abstract

The present article describes a digital sampled value (SV) stream calibrator. The instrument is designed to generate signals transmitted according to the IEC 61850-9-2 [2] standard communication protocol in order to perform verification of measuring instruments and commissioning of digital substations (DS). Its principle of operation, scope of application, and the metrological and technical characteristics are described, and a study of the calibrator was carried out and its characteristics determined. The results are relevant for the assessment of the metrological characteristics of digital intelligent measuring devices, metering devices, and for the quality control of the electrical energy used in the power industry.

## 1 Introduction

Recently, the overall pace of innovation at the global level has been increasing, while the introduction of innovations in the energy sector has become a complex long-term task. This is important from the point of view of legal metrology. The concept of digitalization of the electric power industry in the Russian Federation became widespread after the "Digital Economy of the Russian Federation" program was approved in 2017, revealing the policy of transition to a digital economy. Digital technologies are a new tool for increasing efficiency, reducing capital requirements and operating costs. Ultimately, digitalization will help industrial companies to reduce production costs and reduce the negative impact on the environment.

The absolute core of digitalization of the electrical power industry is a digital substation. To date, measuring complexes using a technological bus based on [1], as well as intelligent electronic devices equipped with a "digital" interface, are one of the priority directions for the development of the electric power industry to a new generation of power objects - the DS. According to the standards developed by the "Federal Grid Company of Unified Energy System", a Public Joint Stock Company (hereinafter referred to as FGC UES), this term means a substation of an electric power system with a high level of automation, in which the processes of information exchange between the components of a substation, as well as the management of a substation are carried out digitally based on [1] series of standards. These technologies have great technical and economic potential.

To compare the previously implemented and new technologies, it is possible to give approximate calculations for the equipment of a "traditional" substation and a similar one for the tasks of the DS. In the first case, a copper cable will be required that is 10 times the length of the fiber-optic cable required for DS, about 30 times more automatic control cabinets vs protection and control cabinets (taking into account double server redundancy), six times more area, and, finally, the total equipment and installation costs for a "traditional" substation will exceed the cost of the DS option by 2.5 times.

Besides, taking into account the fact that one operator with a tablet can remotely manage and monitor everything at the DS, it is not difficult to imagine the overall benefit of the capital and operational investments. Thus, the use of digital technologies is advantageous in the new construction of a central data center not only in terms of reducing maintenance and electricity costs, but also by saving resources.

In addition, there will be the ability for systems to adapt quickly to changing conditions, and there will be an increase in reliability and security by ensuring the transparency of the algorithms implemented. In addition, the effect of introducing innovations in the form of digitalization will be cost reduction, improving the quality of energy supply to consumers, creating new intelligent ecosystems as close as possible to subscribers, and building a solid foundation for the further development of the domestic fuel and energy (and related) sectors of the economy.

Currently, various companies continue to develop and implement various solutions, prototypes of devices, and experimental models. The application of information technology in the electrical power industry is a rapidly developing field, and there are many opportunities for further research and development. The commissioning of DSs as a consequence raises the question of metrological support for SV-stream measurements of electric power quantities. VNIIMS was assigned a

number of tasks in this field of measurement, among which was the development of a digital SV-stream calibrator.

The present article describes a digital SV-stream calibrator, designed to generate signals transmitted according to the [2] protocol in order to perform the verification of measuring instruments and commissioning of DS, developed and manufactured in the Russian Federation. This SV-stream calibrator is designed to generate signals, i.e. electric power quantities transmitted according to the [2] protocol, and is necessary for metrological and commissioning work during the manufacture of measuring instruments for DS and in the process of their operation. This is important for legal metrology.

## 2 Main technical characteristics

For the direct verification, calibration and testing of measuring instruments with a digital input, the digital SV-stream calibrator has the following functions and characteristics:

- generation of calibrated values of three main and three backup SV-streams according to the [2] profile and the corporate profile of FGC UES IEC 61850 with different nominal sampling rates of the digital output;

calibrator accuracy class for active and reactive energy: 0.03;

- generation of SV-streams of calibrated values of the electrical parameters of a three-phase four-wire network;

- generation of calibrated values of active and reactive electric energy of forward and reverse directions and four-quadrant reactive energy;

- assignment of timestamps with an accuracy of no more than 1 $ \mu s $;

- control of phase alternation, availability of load current and power direction;

emergency alarm and self-diagnosis;

built-in real-time clock;

- time synchronization via the Precision Time Protocol (PTP);

- maintaining and displaying event logs;

- ability to print data from the device;

- generation of calibrated values of electrical energy quality parameters and generation of reports;

calibrated values of electric power quality parameters (hereinafter referred to as PQP) according to the Russian National Standard GOST 30804.4.30-2013, class A;

calibrated values of harmonics and inter-harmonics according to GOST 30804.4.7-2013.

An important requirement for the calibrator was the availability of an automated calibration (verification) mode of measuring instruments with the ability to create a library of streams with various parameters that can be selected later when creating a test scenario.

## 3 Technical implementation of the SV-stream calibrator

When creating the calibrator, special software was developed called the "Module for automatic test start and comparison module" based on "MMS", which consists of three main modules, according to the software block diagram shown in Figure 1:

- Interface module;

- Manufacturing Message Specification (MMS)-Client;

Comparison module.

The software sets instantaneous current and voltage values for an external generation module, which generates a data stream according to [2].

There are three possible generation modes: Counter, PQP and Telemechanics.

From the tested intelligent device, the software "Module for automatic test start and comparison module" based on "MMS" receives the calculated RMS values of currents, voltages, capacities, and frequencies, as well as the electric energy values according to the IEC 61850-8-1 (MMS) protocol. Based on the data received, the software compares the generated data and the data from the device. When the device is turned on, the software checksum and the license key of the external generation module are checked. Upon successful launch, the interface module is triggered.

The interface module is a user interaction module which is responsible for the control of the generation of:

- selection of the generation operation mode;

- setting parameters for stream and interfaces;

- setting parameters for stream generation;

- stream generation control;

management of communication channels;

indication of flow generation.

The interface module is capable of representing reference information about the software version and error information for incorrectly set control parameters.

The external generation module calculates the parameters, generates a frame according to the selected mode, and at a certain time corresponding to the last sample in the frame, the generated frame is sent to the specified network interface (or several interfaces).

All the parameters that are set in the interface module can be divided into two groups:

- parameters of the stream and interfaces for which the stream is issued;

signal parameters, depending on the generation mode.

The calibrator itself is an industrial computer with an LCD screen and touch function buttons. The picture of the device is represented in Figure 2. Local control of the digital calibrator is carried out using a virtual keyboard.

The digital calibrator implements an automated calibration (verification) mode of the measuring instruments. To start it, it is possible to create a library of generated signals, with the assignment of current, voltage and power factor parameters for each signal. Also, the calibrator is capable of saving streams with various parameters that can be selected later when generating a signal.

When creating a typical scenario for calibration (verification), it is necessary to select from the library the signals that will be included into the scenario, specifying the duration of the data stream in seconds. When all the calibrations are included in the test program, the device must be selected and automatic playback of the script started.

The calibrator allows tracking of which signal is currently being played in the scenario by counting the remaining playback time. The script itself can be edited and calibrations can be removed from the list. The number of scenarios created is unlimited, allowing the creation of an extensive database of scenarios for the calibration (verification) of measuring instruments.

# Photovoltaics metrology and related international standards: Towards improved energy efficiency

</div>

ASS. PROF. KHALED M. AHMED (PHD) SASO, Saudi Arabia

## 1 Introduction

The goal of authorities around the world is to increase the security, accessibility, and sustainability of the energy supply for citizens as this can indirectly improve the global strategy for securing a sustainable and reliable energy supply for all. A review clause allowing an upward revision of the EU level objective by 2023 and a commitment to reach a 32% proportion of renewables by 2030 are both already in place as part of this policy framework for energy and climate for 2030 [EU, 2018].

Being the renewable energy technology with the greatest potential for cost reduction (five-fold over the last ten years) and efficiency advances, photovoltaics (PV) is predicted to significantly contribute to reaching these goals. The price per watt approach, or euros per watt-peak of the PV modules' rated electrical power, now governs the PV industry. The methods and standards for calibrating the power of PV modules and systems are crucial for determining if PV is economically viable, given that the annual world PV output reached 120 GW in 2019 [Jag, 2019] and that the market value of just the PV module components would approach $ \in30 $ billion. The PV market depends on high precision of the power measurement due to the rising relevance of the PV contribution to the energy supply and to financial investments.

Because a PV module effectively converts incident sunlight directly into direct-current (DC) electricity, the electrical power produced by a PV module is affected by the intensity and spectral composition of the sunlight

that illuminates it. As a result, measuring solar irradiance is necessary to determine the electrical performance of PV cells and modules, and this measurement may be explained from two interrelated perspectives. While the second examines the spectrum irradiance, or the distribution of the total irradiance over the wavelengths that make up the first, the first takes into account the irradiance as a whole and measures the overall (total) irradiance. The primary requirement of international standards is the PV calibration at standard test conditions (STC), which is established by [IEC TS 61836] and calls for a total irradiance of 1000 W/m $ ^{2} $ with a spectral irradiance distribution of the reference spectrum [IEC 60904-3] for terrestrial applications.

The rating of PV goods based on the energy that can be produced at the PV system's installation location as opposed to their power output at STC is an important movement that is currently taking place in the assessment of PV devices. A rated energy output for these conditions can be calculated [IEC 61853-3] based on a series of performance measurements at a range of irradiance and device temperatures, which together make up the PV module's power matrix [IEC 61853-1]. The energy rating is based on internationally agreed climate specific data sets. The energy rating is a rating of energy output under usual conditions rather than a prediction of the actual output of PV products at a specific site or a direct prediction of future energy production. The latter are conceptually similar to STC in some ways, but they are described in considerably greater detail and with more characteristics, which can better represent the real conditions that PV modules deployed in the field would meet throughout their lifetime, in addition to a number of climate zones [IEC 61853-4].

The traceability of all measured quantities to international standards is a crucial criterion for every measurement. Measuring electrical parameters under well-defined solar irradiation circumstances is necessary for evaluating the electrical performance of PV devices. The latter exact and traceable measurement is the most challenging and important, whereas those of the electrical quantities are very simple.

## 3 PV Traceability standards

The traceability chain of the solar irradiance measurements for photovoltaics is described in the international standard [IEC 60904-4]. Under the direction of the JRC, this standard was initially created and most recently amended. The second edition was released in November 2019. The prerequisites for traceability are laid out in IEC 60904-4, along with several paths to achieving it. Additionally, its annex provides a thorough description of typical implementations of methods that are currently on the market. In essence, the measurement of irradiance can be traced to either the World Radiometric Reference (WRR), a traditional detector-based measurement standard for direct natural sunlight, or to the International System (SI) irradiance scale, through standard detectors, spectroradiometers, standard lamps, and black-body sources (Figure 2). The WRR needs measurements to be taken outside in proper conditions and has total and spectral irradiance that is similar to the prescribed PV reference spectrum [IEC 60904-3]. The other approaches require more work during the metrological transfer to PV devices in terms of linearity and spectral mismatch because they are laboratory-based, often have significantly lower irradiance intensities, and have highly different spectral irradiance compared to the reference spectrum.

When the primary calibration (i.e. transfer to a PV reference device, often a solar cell) has been accomplished traceably using these procedures, the secondary calibration (i.e. transfer to other PV devices) can be carried out and is governed by distinct IEC standards [IEC 60904-2] [IEC 60904-1]. The first PV device in the PV traceability chain must be calibrated against a measurement standard (i.e. a calibrated reference), which measures irradiance traceable to international measurement standards, even though the traceability transfer between two PV devices is fairly simple due to their shared operating principle. In the case of natural sunlight, the WRR, which is determined via cavity radiometers (commonly referred to as cavities), serves as the international standard for irradiance. However, compared to PV devices, cavity radiometers have fundamentally different properties, most notably a very broad spectral responsivity that extends well beyond the visible portion of the electromagnetic spectrum and a substantially slower reaction to incident electromagnetic radiation. As a result, specialized testing techniques and knowledge are needed for the calibration of PV devices against cavities. As it calibrates a PV device against something that is not a PV device, the metrological transfer from the WRR to the first PV reference cell in the traceability chain is referred to as a primary calibration. After that, the transfer is made between more similar PV devices (secondary calibrations), making it easier and, to some extent, more economical for a larger range of measurement facilities.

## 4 World Radiometric Reference (WRR)

The World Reference Radiation (WRR) is an established primary (measurement) standard that is based on a collection of cavity radiometers known as the World Standard Group (WSG) and is transferred every five years to secondary (measurement) standards during the International Pyrheliometer Comparison (IPC). According to IEC 60904-4, ESTI, for instance, uses three of these secondary standards to transfer the calibration chain to PV devices. The Global Sunlight Method (GSM) and the Direct Sunlight Method (DSM) are the two main techniques used at ESTI. The DSM was initially produced by NREL and applied at ESTI for comparison and validation purposes, whereas the former was created at ESTI and is unique.

## 5 World Photovoltaic Scale (WPVS)

The dilemma of whether they all concur and, if so, which primary calibration approach is best arises since many primary calibration techniques have historically been used in the PV community. A consensus was reached during international round-robins, and it was decided that all legitimate measurements should be taken into account for calculating the average reference

value, creating the World Photovoltaic Scale (WPVS). The WPVS was chosen by many labs to be used going forward in its own PV calibration chain since it is the best reference and offers the highest level of accuracy for solar irradiance measurements for PV. Instead, other labs chose to use it solely for their proficiency testing (as required by [ISO/IEC 17025]), that is, just to make sure their own results correspond with the WPVS (within declared accuracy).

It has been validated and demonstrated that the techniques for calibrating the irradiance of PV devices generate outcomes that are compatible with one another within specified measurement accuracy. However, a key comparison reference value (KCRV) can be determined based on all legitimate measurements taken on the same device, as is customary in international metrology at the highest level. In most cases, the weighted average is employed, with the contributing findings' measurement accuracy serving as the basis for the weighting. As it incorporates data from all verified techniques, this will produce a value that is more trustworthy than any individual value, enhancing the accuracy of the final average in the process. The Photovoltaic Solar Energy Project (PEP) of the Technology, Growth and Employment Working Group of the G7 summit, which was executed in the 1990s as a result, served as the inspiration for the WPVS [Ost, 1998].

## 6 Interlaboratory comparisons in the field

Sun irradiance measurements, from broadband measurements with cavity radiometers to those with spectroradiometers to the calibration of PV reference cells and modules, are compared across laboratories. These measurements serve the objective of periodically comparing the laboratory's measuring techniques and results to those of other laboratories in the field. This is crucial to ensuring that the outcomes from all reputable laboratories are equivalent on a global scale. Additionally, as a fact-based quality-control evaluation, participation in such inter-laboratory comparisons is mandatory for ISO/IEC 17025 accredited laboratories. Peer laboratories include the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) in the United States, the National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology (AIST) in Japan, and the Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB) and Fraunhofer Institute for Solar Energy Systems (FhG-ISE) in Germany, respectively, for reference cell calibration and PV module calibration. A reduction over time in the gap between labelled and confirmed PV module power is indicative of the impact of global standardization and periodic inter-laboratory comparisons of PV device performance measurements

[Lop, 2018]. The determination of the power calibration and energy yield [Gra, 2019] for PV devices is influenced by two crucial irradiance measurement factors. In order to maintain a high level of comparability with other facilities, measurements made using cavities and spectroradiometers must be made.

The first is the use of broadband radiation detectors (such cavity radiometers) to measure the amount of direct normal (beam) sun irradiance (DNI). Such a measurement is crucial for the development and deployment of solar energy conversion systems, for improving our comprehension of the Earth's energy budget for studies on climate change, and for science and technology applications involving the solar flux, in addition to being essential for determining the incident irradiance in PV device calibrations.

The second consideration is how to measure the spectral content of incoming real or artificial sunlight utilized to evaluate the electrical performance of PV systems. This information is essential for the accurate characterisation, calibration, and energy yield calculation of PV devices because of the wide range of PV technologies now available and their varied spectral responsivity (SR) to the incident light.

Instead, one or more PV reference cells are typically used in assessments of the performance of PV devices to determine the total irradiance. Irradiance measurement calibration is essentially transferred to the test object (DUT) (such as a PV module). A spectral mismatch error [IEC 60904-7] is introduced in the DUT performance measurement because measurements are conducted under real or artificial light that will always deviate more or less from the reference spectrum because the reference device and the device under test generally have different SRs. Mathematical a posteriori correction of this spectral error is possible, but it necessitates knowledge of the SR of the reference device and the DUT as well as the spectral makeup of the actual or simulated sunlight utilized for the measurement. Spectroradiometers can measure the latter. Over time, it became clear that precise measurements of the spectrum irradiance are far from easy and call for cutting-edge tools and expertise. In order to gather and disseminate information and best practices in this area, the JRC annually organizes the International Spectroradiometer Comparison (ISRC).

## 7 Validation and verification of PV device performance

PV technology is developing both continuously and quickly. Several times a year, both for new emerging technologies such as perovskite solar cells (PSC) and for

more established technologies such as crystalline silicon, claims of a new world record or other outstanding PV device performance are made. Typically, the makers of brand new devices make the claims. However, it is possible that they lack the tools or the knowledge necessary to precisely and credibly gauge the performance of a PV device. Therefore, it is crucial that independent calibration laboratories are not only impartial to commercial or national interests but also possess the necessary measuring technological expertise verify such claims.

## Poly-Crystalline Silicon cells

For PV modules based on conventional crystalline silicon technology, advancements are continuously being made. A polycrystalline silicon PV module's efficiency at the time of measurement was higher than the declared world record efficiency, according to ESTI [JRC, 2019].

## Perovskite solar cells

Perovskite solar cells (PSC) are a newer, developing PV technology that was only presented in 2009, however since then, it has significantly improved in terms of efficiency. Scientists have learned a lot more fascinating facts about perovskite since it was first discovered in this new form. By altering its composition, it can, for instance, absorb a certain range of light wavelengths.

However, a significant difficulty is how to precisely determine these devices' electrical properties. A PSC manufacturer asked ESTI to calibrate and certify the performance of perovskite mini-modules that could break world records. At ESTI, a novel procedure for measuring these devices was created and tested. Three different devices have been confirmed to exhibit efficiencies that are greater than the current reported record value [JRC, 2019].

## Organic solar cells

One of the newest and most promising PV technologies is organic photovoltaics (OPV). An OPV mini-module from WAYS/Nanobit/NCU was certified at ESTI to have an efficiency of (13.57 $ \pm $ 0.32) %, which represents a new world-record efficiency for this type of device and was duly included in the relevant table [Gre 2019b]. It has 16 series-connected cells and a total designated illumination area of 66.62 $ cm^{2} $ [JRC, 2021].

## 8 Summary

Solar energy has the potential to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, slow global warming, and improve energy efficiency. For instance, solar energy captured via a photovoltaic (PV) solar panel installed on the home's roof can be used to heat the water, and solar energy can also be used to power faraway homes. Additionally, large-scale desalination plants now use solar energy instead of electricity to provide necessary power, among many other uses in a variety of areas. Because it is a renewable resource, solar energy can therefore help to alleviate the global energy problems.

In-depth material and device testing is required for solar cell development and manufacture in order to increase efficiency and match individual cells for panel construction. Fast testing is necessary, but it also calls for a full grasp of measurement implications and cell physics. Conversion efficiency of PV devices must be precisely tested because it directly affects both their performance and price in the solar energy sector. The foundation for measuring standards for rating PV devices is the calibration of reference solar cells, as detailed in the IEC 60904 series.

In addition to having an effect on optical detector metrology generally, the ability to primary calibrate reference solar cells can be useful for exploring technological difficulties in the creation of new photovoltaic materials. National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) must pay particular attention to the calibration and testing of the reference solar cells under standard test conditions (STC) as well as preserving the traceability to the World Photo-Voltaic Scale (WPVS) because it has been demonstrated that PV measurement uncertainty has a substantial economic impact.

The seventh Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) set forth by the United Nations is "to substantially increase the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix." It is simple to understand why alternative energy sources demand their own SDG because everyone benefits from the switch to renewable energy whether it be in terms of reducing global warming, fostering economic growth, or enhancing public health.

Solar power has quickly risen to the top of the list of renewable energy sources due to its adaptability and cost. Furthermore, the coronavirus pandemic has helped us to better appreciate the worldwide linkages that unite us all. According to experts, organizations and consumers will now give renewable energy sources even higher priority.

Solar thermal and solar photovoltaic (PV) technology are often thought of as the two main forms of solar energy. In simple words, solar PV converts sunlight into energy, whereas solar thermal energy captures heat. Individual systems for homes and small towns and

larger concentrated solar power plants that feed into power grids are the two major installation types that are used. Given the growing importance of renewable energy, it makes sense that the standardization of the solar power business is crucial. The fabrication of arrays, energy conversion, reflectance or material qualities, integrating into the smart grid, and ensuring workplace safety all depend on standards.

Moreover, standards can assist future solar users in understanding how to incorporate solar energy into their own energy mix as part of an ongoing energy and environmental management strategy. This benefits the solar sector in turn.

In order to reduce energy expenditures and carbon emissions, organizations wishing to include energy efficiency in their daily operations can use the ISO 50001 framework, for instance. ISO 14001 is a logical step up from ISO 50001 and offers recommendations for environmental management. It aids companies in analyzing their processes to make them more sustainable.

The International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) offers useful leadership and direction for both solar power unit manufacturers and installation when it comes to more sector-specific solar standards. International standards are developed by the IEC through a consensus-building process comprising 20000 professionals from business, government, and top testing labs such as BSI. For instance, the IEC 61215 series of standards for solar PV modules includes key components for evaluating both thin-film amorphous silicon and Cadmium Telluride (CdTe)-based photovoltaic (PV) modules. Companies should also take into account IEC 61730, which addresses testing and construction regulations, and IEC 62108, which outlines the minimal requirements for determining whether a product is capable of operating for an extended period of time in typical open-air conditions.

IEC 62947 also discusses quality control procedures for producing PV modules. This freshly released standard describes best practices for product design, manufacturing procedures, and the choice and control of materials used in the production of PV modules that have satisfied all of the aforementioned standards.

Finally, when it comes to the weather-proofing and durability of the device, compliance is necessary on both counts. The ability to function and live in a range of conditions, including extreme cold and heat, is evaluated by the BS EN 60068 family of standards for environmental testing of electrical equipment.

# Determination of carbon dioxide emissions from coal and fuel oil in a stationary combustion source using the fuel analysis approach

</div>

HAIFENG WANG, XIAOPING SONG, JIA LI National Institute of Metrology of China (wanghf@nim.ac.cn)

## 1 Abstract

Accurate determination of the emission of greenhouse gases (GHGs) is fundamental in achieving the goal of reducing GHG emissions. Combustion of fossil fuels is the main source of GHGs. The emission of carbon dioxide $ \mathrm{(CO_{2})} $ associated with the use of coal and fuel oil in a stationary combustion source (SCS) can be determined using the fuel analysis approach. In this approach, method 1 determines $ \mathrm{CO_{2}} $ emissions using the calorific value and emission factors of the fuel; method 2 uses the carbon content of the fuel and an oxidation factor to calculate $ \mathrm{CO_{2}} $ emissions. The procedure, analytical method, instrumentation, calibration, and uncertainty budget of methods 1 and 2 were examined in detail in relation to an SCS. The reference calorimeter, certified reference materials for calorific value and elemental contents, and calibration or verification regulations ensure the accuracy and consistency of estimations of $ \mathrm{CO_{2}} $ emissions.

Keywords: legal metrology, carbon dioxide, fossil fuel coal, calorific value, carbon content uncertainty

## 2 Introduction

Combustion of fossil fuels, industrial and agricultural production, and other human activities release tremendous amounts of greenhouse gas (GHG). Such gases include carbon dioxide $ \mathrm{(CO_{2})} $ , methane $ \mathrm{(CH_{4})} $ , and

nitrous oxide $ \left( \mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O} \right) $ of which $ \mathrm{CO}_{2} $ is the primary GHG. Every nation has an obligation to reduce its emission of GHGs and thus help limit global warming. China proposed to strive to reach its peak carbon emissions by 2030 and to achieve carbon neutrality by 2060. Achieving the goal of reducing GHG emissions depends largely on the accurate determination of the amount of GHGs emitted. In 2006, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) [1] published guidelines for national GHG inventories to guide governments in estimating the GHG emissions of their country. Furthermore, corporations are willing to determine the carbon footprint based on the emission of GHGs in order to participate in carbon trading in the future. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) published its standard ISO 14064 [2] to guide companies or organizations in estimating GHG emissions at organizational or project levels.

In 2022, the global emission of GHGs associated with fossil fuels, including coal, natural gas, petroleum, and petroleum products, was 36.6 billion tons, accounting for approximately 90% of total GHG emissions. Therefore, determination of GHG emissions from fossil fuels is both fundamental and important.

Two approaches are available for determining GHG emissions from the combustion of fossil fuels: the fuel analysis approach and the continuous emission monitoring system (CEMS). The CEMS measures the flowrate, GHG concentration, and moisture content of flue gas, and then determines the GHG emission rate continuously. The CEMS is only applicable to a stationary combustion source (SCS). Comparatively, the fuel analysis approach is used in the determination of $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions not only by SCSs, but also by industries that use fossil fuels as raw material. Because the analysis of coal employs the same principles, instrumentation, and a similar procedure to those used in the analysis of fuel oil, the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions from both coal and fuel oil used in SCSs were examined in the present study.

## 3 Fuel analysis approach for determining the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions from coal

In the fuel analysis approach, two methods can be used to determine $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions. In method 1, $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission $ (E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}}) $ is considered the product of coal consumption (m, ton), the calorific value (q, TJ/ton), and an emission factor (EF, mass of emitted $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ per unit energy, kg $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ /TJ) (Eq.1) [1].

In method 2, $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission is considered to be the product of m, the carbon content of coal $ (c_{\mathrm{C}} $ , ton/ton), an oxidation factor (OF), and the ratio of the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ molecular weight to carbon atomic weight (44/12) (Eq. 2) [1].

$$
E _ {\mathrm {C O 2}} = m \cdot q \cdot E F
$$

$$
E _ {\mathrm {C O} 2} = m \cdot c _ {\mathrm {C}} \cdot O F \cdot \frac {4 4}{1 2}
$$

## 3.1 Weighing of coal

In this study, the coal was weighed using an electronic truck scale, railway scale, or belt scale. The Chinese national standard GB 17167-2006 [3] specifies that the relative maximum permissible error (MPE) of the static scale and the dynamic scale should be no more than 0.1 % and 0.5 %, respectively. For example, the coal in a railway carriage was weighed using an electronic truck scale, and the relative MPE was 0.1 %. It is hypothesized that the error is distributed homogeneously; therefore, the relative uncertainty of the mass result $ \left(u_{\mathrm{r}}(m)\right) $ of coal is 0.06 %.

## 3.2 Sampling and preparation of a specimen

Commercial coal is composed of particles of various sizes and its composition is inhomogeneous. ISO 13909 [4] specifies standard methods for sampling, specimen preparation, and the estimation of precision of coal. To acquire a representative specimen of coal for analysis, primary increments were collected from positions distributed over an entire lot. Once an adequate number of fuel portions had been collected manually or mechanically, they were combined into a gross sample.

The gross sample was crushed into coal particles with a diameter less than 10 mm, and then divided into a reduced mass of 10 kg. The water content of the residual sample was determined using the loss on drying (LOD) method and accounted for as the total moisture $ ( M_{t} ) $ . On the other hand, the residual sample was crushed again and then divided twice, so a sample with a 0.21 mm diameter and a mass of 60-300 g was acquired for general analysis. The water content of the general analysis test sample was also determined using the LOD method and accounted for as the air-dried moisture $ ( M_{ad} ) $ .

The estimated precision of sampling and specimen preparation was related to the variance of the coal, the number of samples taken from the lot, the number of increments comprising each sample, and the mass of the sample relative to the nominal top size. Based on ISO 13909 [4], the Chinese national standard GB/T 18666-2002 [5] specifies the MPE of ash and the gross calorific value $ ( q_{\mathrm{g r}}) $ of sampling and specimen preparation. The MPE of $ q_{\mathrm{g r}} $ for coal with ash content $ ( A_{\mathrm{d}} $ %) of <10, 10-20, and >20 was 0.56, 0.056 $ A_{\mathrm{d}} $ , and 1.12 MJ/kg, respectively [5]. For example, for coal with a value of $ A_{\mathrm{d}} $ of 15 %, the MPE of $ q_{\mathrm{g r}} $ is 0.84 MJ/kg. Accordingly, the expanded uncertainty $ ( U ) $ of $ q_{\mathrm{g r}} $ was estimated as 0.56 MJ/kg. Typical bituminous coal has a $ q_{\mathrm{g r}} $ value in the range of 4500-5500 cal/g (18.8-23.0 MJ/kg); therefore, the relative uncertainty $ ( u_{\mathrm{r}}) $ of the calorific value attributable to sampling and specimen preparation was estimated as 1.12 %. The MPE and $ u_{\mathrm{r}} $ of carbon content attributable to sampling and preparation remain to be explored. In the present study, the corresponding value of $ u_{\mathrm{r}} $ was estimated as being equal to that of $ q_{\mathrm{g r}} $ (1.12 %). Increasing the number of samples and increments can decrease the $ u_{\mathrm{r}} $ of the calorific value and the carbon content, which obviously increases the cost of sampling.

It should be noted that the MPE and the uncertainty were estimated based on the good practice that all processes fully conformed with the standard methods. The actual uncertainty might be greater than that estimated when the coal is wet by rain, the coal particle is oversize, or the coal is mixed artificially.

## 3.3 Measurement of the calorific value of the coal

The calorific value of the coal was measured using a bomb calorimeter in accordance with an international standard method (ISO 1928:2020) [6]. The coal was ignited and combusted in the bomb filled with oxygen at high pressure, and the heat was absorbed by the calorimeter. The heat released was calculated using the heat capacity and the temperature increase of the calorimeter. The heat capacity was determined using the

certified reference material (CRM) of benzoic acid for calorific value. The additional heat of the combustion of the fuse, the ignition, the formation of nitric acid, and the formation of sulfuric acid were subtracted from the measured heat, and then $ q_{\mathrm{gr}} $ was acquired.

The latent heat of water should be subtracted from $ q_{\mathrm{gr}} $ to acquire the net calorific value $ (q_{\mathrm{net}}) $ . The calorific value measured on the basis of an air-dried (ad) sample should be modified to an as-received (ar) value. Thus, $ q_{\mathrm{net, ar}} $ was calculated according to Eq.3 [6]. The uncertainty of $ q_{\mathrm{net, ar}} $ (u) was estimated as 60 J/g.

$$
q _ {\mathrm {n e t}, \mathrm {a r}} = \left(q _ {\mathrm {g r}, \mathrm {a d}} - 2 0 6 H _ {\mathrm {a d}}\right) \times \frac {1 0 0 - M _ {\mathrm {t}}}{1 0 0 - M _ {\mathrm {a d}}} - 2 3 M _ {\mathrm {t}}
$$

where $ q_{\mathrm{net,ar}} $ is the net calorific value of as-received coal, (J/g), $ q_{\mathrm{gr;ad}} $ is the calorific value of air-dried coal (J/g), 206 is the latent heat per 1 % hydrogen content, $ H_{\mathrm{ad}} $ is the hydrogen content of air-dried coal ( % ), $ M_{\mathrm{t}} $ is the total moisture, and $ M_{\mathrm{ad}} $ is the moisture of air-dried coal.

The CRM of benzoic acid was certified using a reference calorimeter calibrated by electric energy; thus, the certified value was traceable to SI units of temperature, voltage, resistance, and time. The National Institute of Metrology (NIM) of China developed the CRM of benzoic acid using the reference calorimeter (Table 1). This CRM was used not only to calibrate the instrument, but also to verify the instrument according to the Chinese national verification regulation (JJG 672- 2018) [7]. NIM of China participated in the international comparison of benzoic acid and coal organized by the Euro-Asian Metrology Cooperation (COOMET) to

ensure consistency in the calorific value. The China Coal Research Institute (CCRI) developed a series of CRMs of coal for the calorific value, C, hydrogen (H), nitrogen (N), and sulfur (S) for validation of the analytical method (Table 1). The exact determination of $ q_{\mathrm{net}} $ used the contents of S and H, which were determined using an elemental analyzer (EA), as described below.

## 3.4 Emission factor

There are several different types of coal including anthracite, bituminous coal, and lignitous coal with diverse EFs, and the EF can vary even for coal of the same type. The IPCC summarized the EFs of various fossil fuels from published data and listed the mean value for every type of fuel as its default value together with lower and upper limits (Table 2). For method 1, the default EF specified by the IPCC is recommended (Table 2). It should be noted that there might be an obvious bias between the default EF and the actual EF, which could result in either an overestimation or an underestimation of the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission.

## 3.6 Determination of the oxidation factor

In method 1, the default OF is 1.0. In method 2, the OF can be determined using the carbon content and the mass of coal, slag, and fly ash according to the mass balance theory. However, such measurements involve a time-consuming and expensive process. In a modern power plant, the OF value of carbon is high and stable. Therefore, new guidance regarding GHG emissions by the power industry in China specifies that the default OF value of carbon for method 2 is 0.99 [10]. It should be noted that when coal is combusted in a small-scale furnace, the OF value might deviate from the default value.

For example, the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission $ (E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}}) $ of 1 gram coal of three coal samples were determined using methods 1 and 2, respectively. $ E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}} $ of three samples determined using method 1 $ (E_{\mathrm{C O_{2},1}}) $ were 2.167 g, 2.534 g, and 2.593 g, respectively (Table 4). On the other hand, $ E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}} $ of three samples determined using method 2 $ (E_{\mathrm{C O_{2},2}}) $ were 2.254 g, 2.516 g, and 2.525 g, respectively (Table 4). The relative bias of $ E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}} $ between two methods of three samples were 3.88 %, 0.72 %, and 2.68 %, respectively (Table 4). This indicates that when the actual value of the emission factor of the sample is close to the default value specified by the IPCC, the error of $ E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}} $ using method 1 is relatively small.

## 3.8 Uncertainty of the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission

The uncertainty of $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ in method 1 was evaluated using the mathematical model based on Eq. 1. The $ u_{\mathrm{r}} $ of m, q, EF, and the sampling and sample preparation were 0.06 %, 0.29 %, 3.86 %, and 1.12 %, respectively (Table 5). All the uncertainty components were combined and

thus, the relative expanded uncertainty of $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ $ \left(U_{\mathrm{r}}\left(E_{\mathrm{CO2}}\right), k=2\right) $ was determined as 8.1 % (Table 5). The main source of uncertainty is attributed to the EF.

The uncertainty of $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ in method 2 was evaluated using the mathematical model based on Eq. 2. The $ u_{\mathrm{r}} $ of $ m,c_{\mathrm{C}} $ and the sampling were 0.06 %, 0.60 %, and 1.12 %, respectively (Table 5). The relative expanded uncertainty of $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ $ (U_{\mathrm{r}}(E_{\mathrm{CO2}}), k=2) $ was determined as 2.6 % (Table 5). The main source of uncertainty is attributed to the sampling and specimen preparation, which can be reduced by increasing the number of samples and increments.

The accuracy of $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ in method 2 is obviously better than that in method 1. Therefore, method 2 is recommended for use by organizations and companies for the determination of $ \mathrm{CO}_{2} $ emissions in SCSs.

Use of the PE ampoule represents a convenient procedure for analysis of volatile fuel oil that can provide an accurate result. Furthermore, NIM of China developed the CRM for the calorific value of isooctane for the validation of the analytical method (Table 1).

The $ E_{\mathrm{CO_{2}}} $ of fuel oil can be determined using method 1 and with reference to Eq.1. The IPCC specifies the default EF of $ \mathrm{CO_{2}} $ for several types of fuel oil (Table 2) [1].

The $ c_{\mathrm{C}} $ of fuel oil can also be measured using an EA. To avoid volatilization, the fuel oil was sealed in a tin (Sn) capsule (Fig. 3). The open end of this capsule was sealed by cold-welding (Fig. 3). The fuel oil combusts quickly in an EA and therefore a higher flowrate of oxygen was needed. A series of CRMs of normal alkanes from decane to hexadecane with the certified value of the contents of carbon and hydrogen are being developed at NIM of China for validation of the analytical method.

The guidance regarding GHG emissions by the power industry in China specifies that the default OF value of carbon for fuel oil is 0.98 [10]. Therefore, the $ E_{\mathrm{C O_{2}}} $ of fuel oil can be determined using method 2 and with reference to Eq. 2.

It should be noted that in a mobile combustion source such as a motor car, the OF value of carbon might deviate markedly from the default value above (0.98). Determination of the $ E_{\mathrm{CO2}} $ associated with a mobile combustion source should be performed by a different method with reference to the IPCC guidance [1].

## 5 Discussion

In the fuel analysis approach, method 2 based on the carbon content of fuel has better accuracy than method 1. Therefore, the latest guidance regarding the GHG emissions of the power industry in China recommends using method 2 instead of method 1 [10]. Moreover, method 2 conforms both with the high-level method of the IPCC and with ISO 11064 [1, 2].

The emissions of $ \mathrm{C H_{4}} $ and $ \mathrm{N_{2}O} $ cannot be determined using method 2. In contrast, method 1 is suitable for determining the emissions of $ \mathrm{C H_{4}} $ and $ \mathrm{N_{2}O} $ from SCSs [1]. The EF of both $ \mathrm{C H_{4}} $ and $ \mathrm{N_{2}O} $ is specified by the IPCC 2006 guidelines [1].

The GHG emission described above is contributed from the combustion of fossil fuels. In the furnace of a power plant, limestone is usually added to reduce the $ \mathrm{S O}_{2} $ emissions. The extra $ \mathrm{C O}_{2} $ emissions due to the decomposition of the limestone should be considered in the accounting of GHG emissions of power plant companies.

As described above, in the fuel analysis approach, the instruments require calibration and verification using CRMs, while the analytical methods require validation using the CRMs of coal or fuel oil. The uncertainty arising from the sampling and preparation of specimens needs to be investigated. A new OIML Recommendation providing guidance on the verification of an EA for coal and fuel oil is urgently needed.

## 6 Conclusions

The fuel analysis approach was employed in this study to determine the $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions of coal and fuel oil in an SCS. In method 1, $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions were calculated based on the calorific value (J/g) and the EF (kg $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ /TJ) of the fuel. The calorific value of the coal and that of the fuel oil was measured using a bomb calorimeter. The uncertainty in the estimation of $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions using method 1 (typically, 8.1 %) is mainly derived from that associated with the default EF.

In method 2, $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions were calculated based on the carbon content (ton/ton) and the oxidation factor of the fuel. The carbon content of the fuel was measured using an EA. The uncertainty in the estimation of $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emission using method 2 (typically, 2.6 %) is mainly derived from that associated with the sampling and preparation of specimens. Metrology techniques including the reference calorimeter, CRMs for calorific value and elemental contents, calibration specification, and verification regulations ensure the accuracy and consistency of the determination of $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ emissions.

Climate change has become the focus of global attention, while as a clean energy, the share of photovoltaic energy in global power generation is everincreasing annually. Energy meters are the most common and widely distributed operating equipment in the power system. ## 2 Basic principles

## 2.1 Basic model

For low-voltage substation areas, a substation area is an area powered by a transformer, usually corresponding to a substation area master meter and multiple user meters. The metering point at $ \mathrm{M}_{0} $ in the substation area master meter provides energy input for the metering points at $ \mathrm{M}_{1},\mathrm{M}_{2},\dots\mathrm{M}_{n} $ for the next level of user meters. Currently, most research on measurement point anomalies is based on this topology [7-9], as shown in Figure 1.

Based on the subordinate relationship of household transformers in the substation area and the law of energy conservation [10-11], the total and partial electricity consumption relationship can be obtained which is "the total meter power supply in the substation area = the sum of actual electricity consumption in each sub meter + line loss + fixed loss in the substation area". The mathematical relationship is expressed as follows:

$$
y (i) = \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {P} \Phi_ {j} (i) \left(1 - e _ {j}\right) + L L (i) + e _ {0}
$$

Where y(i) represents the total meter power supply measurement value for the measurement period i; $ \Phi_{j} ( i ) $ represents the electricity consumption measurement value of sub meter j for the measurement period i; $ e_{j} $ represents the estimated measurement error of measurement point j; p represents the total number of sub meters in the substation area; $ LL ( i ) $ represents the line loss within the metering period i of the substation area; $ e_{0} $ represents the fixed loss in the substation area.

The measurement value of the total meter power supply y(i) and sub meter electricity consumption $ \Phi_{j} (i) $ are known quantities, and the measurement error $ e_{j} $ and fixed loss $ e_{0} $ are undetermined constants. The expression of line loss $ LL(i) $ is the most crucial part of this equation and is also the focus of various studies [7-8,12]. In reference [7], the line loss $ LL(i) $ is expressed as the product of the power supply y(i) and the line loss rate $ e_{y} $ where the line loss rate $ e_{y} $ is an undetermined constant, expressed in mathematical equation as:

$$
y (i) = \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {P} \Phi_ {j} (i) \left(1 - e _ {j}\right) + e _ {y} y (i) + e _ {0}
$$

Reference [8] regards line loss as system noise and uses filtering methods to filter it out reasonably. In reference [12], the line loss in the model is treated as an implicit variable, and various variables in the model are estimated through iterative calculations based on the maximum likelihood estimation rule.

## 2.2 Optimization of line loss estimation

Due to the frequent and drastic changes in low voltage substation area line losses under actual operating conditions, especially when there are significant changes in substation area power supply, significant changes in electricity consumption characteristics of electricity users, or large electricity users at the edge of the substation area, the assumed condition in formula (2) that "the line loss rate in the substation area is a stable constant" deviates significantly from reality. Since formula (1) is based on the law of energy conservation in the substation area, if the line loss fitting is not accurate, it will inevitably affect the error solution of the user meters in the model [8-9,12-17].

At present, the high-frequency data collection capability of HPLC can achieve the collection of electricity meter operation data every 15 minutes, which means there are 96 points of data per day, which is conducive to further optimizing the estimation of line losses. This article is based on high-frequency data acquisition and Kirchhoff's law to optimize the modeling of substation area line losses, which is represented as a physical quantity related to the quadratic term of user electricity consumption. Specifically, this paper deduces the line loss term $ L L(i) $ from the tree structure topology diagram of the substation area shown in Figure 2, and summarizes the line loss expression form of the general substation area, where $ U_{0} $ represents the voltage at the total meter of the substation area, $ U_{1}, $ $ U_{2} $ and $ U_{3} $ represent the voltage at the three sub meters, and R represents the line impedance. The current relationship of each branch has been marked according

to Kirchhoff's law in the figure, and it is assumed that all users in the substation area are resistive loads.

$$
I _ {j} = \frac {\Phi_ {j} \left(1 - e _ {j}\right)}{U _ {j} \cos \varphi_ {j} t}, j = 1, 2, 3
$$

## 3 Model validation

This article verifies the effectiveness of the model through simulation verification and on-site measurement.

## 3.1 Simulation environment verification

This article describes corresponding experiments through laboratory equipment, scientifically sets up various influencing factors involved in the model, configures different parameter files, and efficiently simulates the electricity consumption behavior of different users under different working conditions in the low-voltage substation area. By constructing a simulation platform, the accuracy of model calculations under various operating conditions on site, such as different electricity load characteristics, different substation area line losses, different current fluctuations, different accuracy of total meters, different scale and type of substation areas, were fully simulated. The applicability of the model was verified in the simulation environment. This simulation verification includes a total of 303 substation areas, covering various typical electricity consumption environments. Based on the real highfrequency data of these substation areas, the simulation data of the substation areas are simulated and generated. By adjusting the metering value of the electricity meter by simulation and conducting blind testing verification of the adjusted electricity consumption at each metering point (blind testing: increasing or decreasing the electricity consumption at a certain metering point to simulate the out of tolerance), the error of the electricity meter is calculated.

## 3.1.1 Simulation data design

Based on the topology and real high-frequency data of the 303 substation areas mentioned above, simulate the simulation data of the pilot substation areas in the simulation laboratory. The specific simulation process is as follows:

(1) Set the substation area topology and line parameters (which can be substituted for on-site verification of topology structure, phase, and line parameters, or specified from a typical topology library);

(2) Recalculate the operating data of the substation area by assuming conditions (such as zero measurement error, completely consistent collection clock, and constant user load in the collection area). Based on the simulated physical topology of the substation area and user electricity consumption, recalculate and generate the highfrequency electricity data of the substation area total meter:

(2.1) Copy the high-frequency active and reactive power data of all sub meter users in the substation area, and copy the high-frequency voltage data of the total meter in the substation area;

(2.2) Replace the simulated current of the user with the average value obtained from the calculation of active and reactive power under the aforementioned assumed conditions;

(2.3) In the set substation area topology, calculate the comprehensive current of each phase line to obtain the line loss value of each branch and the voltage value of each sub meter;

(2.4) Obtain the high-frequency electricity consumption simulation data of the total meter from the line losses of each branch and the electricity consumption of each sub meter.

## 4.1 Application situation

Since 2019, the State Grid Corporation of China Limited has established an intelligent energy meter status evaluation system. Based on the technology of intelligent energy meter status evaluation and replacement, a closed-loop management process and vertical maintenance system for abnormal alarm, analysis, dispatch, handling, and evaluation have been constructed, ensuring the timeliness of abnormal disposal.

"expiration verification" mode of smart energy meters. It can quickly calculate the operation error of smart energy meters without power outage, achieve on-line monitoring and accurate judgment of smart energy meters, and change "periodic verification" to "state replacement".

By the end of 2022,25 provincial-level market supervision and management departments in China have issued local regulations related to the evaluation of the status of smart energy meters.A total of 91.4938 million smart energy meters have been delayed in use.

## 1 Abstract

A power metrology laboratory is an important part of the basic guarantee and supporting role of measurement. The advent of the digital age and the development of information technology, together with the digital transformation of power metrology laboratory has become an inevitable trend.

In order to improve the efficiency of the inspection process and improve the quality of laboratory management, the construction framework of a power metrology intelligent laboratory is proposed, the control and data sharing methods of power metering equipment are studied, the application mode of digital calibration certificates in the power industry is explored, the value of measurement data to support measurement supervision is explored, and the construction of power metrology intelligent laboratories and other industries in various countries provides theoretical and technical references.

Keywords: power metrology intelligent laboratory; digital transformation; informatization; construction framework

## 2 Introduction

The International Bureau of Metrology (BIPM) and the International Organization of Legal Metrology (OIML) declared the theme of World Metrology Day 2022 as 'Metrology in the Digital Era' [1].

It is proposed to establish an international unit system digital framework, promote the digital transformation of measurement, and support the healthy development of the digital economy. It is foreseeable that the digital transformation of measurements will be a major and far-reaching change, which will play a greater role in the healthy development of the global digital economy[2].

The power metrology laboratory conducts operations such as verification, calibration, and testing of power measurement equipment to ensure the accuracy and reliability of electrical quantities, which is the technical support for scientific research innovation and industrial development in the power industry. Effective management and resource utilization of power metrology laboratories not only reflect the strength of a laboratory's software and hardware, but also serve as an important means for the laboratory to practice legal metrology. With the increase in laboratory tasks, power metrology laboratories in various countries have encountered problems such as a low degree of detection automation, insufficient equipment and data sharing, and a low level of management informatization; the data is time-consuming and is prone to error, and also there are management risks and reputation risks.

In view of the above problems, it is proposed to use the Internet of Things (IoT) and other technologies to build an intelligent platform for power metrology laboratories. The platform includes a three-tier architecture consisting of a perception layer, a network layer, and an application layer, which together promote the coordination of laboratory personnel, equipment and other elements. Relying on the intelligent platform, the visualization, automation and paperless laboratory detect business are realized, and the informatization and intelligence of laboratory management are realized.

The remote metering system of non-vehicle chargers uses existing charging process data to estimate the measurement error model of the charging machine, and obtains the error estimate and estimation uncertainty. Assuming that in an error calculation, the relative error estimate is $ \delta $ , the standard uncertainty of this estimate is $ \sigma $ , and the error limit of the charging pile is $ \delta_{\mathrm{max}} $ , then the conclusion can be drawn. If $ \delta+\sigma\leq\delta_{\mathrm{max}} $ , the verification is qualified. If $ \delta-\sigma\geq\delta_{\mathrm{max}} $ , the verification is unqualified, and if $ \delta-\sigma\leq\delta_{\mathrm{max}} \leq\delta+\sigma $ , the result is uncertain, so solving the uncertainty evaluation in the evaluation process is the key to the system.

In this article, the error of one measurement system relative to another measurement system can be estimated by using the measured difference of the same physical quantity between the two in the charging process data. At present, the majority of charging piles use smart meters, which can wirelessly transmit charging process data to the database in the cloud. After obtaining the charging process data, if the current and voltage measurement values of the electric vehicle BMS are compared with the measured current and voltage values of the charging piles at the same time, the current and voltage measurements error of the electric vehicle BMS system relative to the charging pile at each time can be obtained. This method presents an improvement over offline calibration, as it eliminates the need for power outages. However, it introduces potential safety hazards and still relies on periodic access to the standard, which restricts its practical application in engineering scenarios.

Online calibration: Given the limitations of in-situ calibration, online calibration has emerged as a promising approach for measuring operational errors of ITs. However, several challenges need to be addressed:

1) The absence of a physical standard makes it challenging to directly compare and obtain the operational error of the IT.

2) The output signal of the IT is susceptible to load fluctuations and other factors, which can lead to misjudgment or omission, thereby compromising the reliability of the evaluation.

3) The metering performance of individual ITs in a substation is independent, making it difficult to efficiently and accurately evaluate them online.

To enhance the online calibration of IT, researchers have explored the use of IT measurement data. These techniques can be categorized into two groups, i.e., rulebased and data-driven online evaluation methods.

Data-driven methods: Methods of this category achieve online calibration of ITs by leveraging machine learning or other artificial intelligence techniques. Generally, the output data from a calibrated IT serves as a training set for a machine learning model. This trained model can subsequently predict the operating state of the IT based on real-time output data. By adopting this approach, it becomes feasible to estimate the operating error of the IT in real-time, thereby facilitating timely corrective actions if necessary.

Rule-based methods: A multi-parameter equivalent model of IT is constructed based on fundamental physical laws, such as circuit theory and electromagnetic theory [6]. The periodic calibration data of the IT is then utilized in conjunction with this equivalent model to address the IT's error. This online evaluation method enables qualitative analysis of the IT's operating condition in real-time. The aforementioned online evaluation methods facilitate qualitative analysis of IT operating conditions. However, their effectiveness heavily relies on accurate IT calibration data, which can pose challenges in terms of obtaining precise model parameters within a reasonable timeframe in practice. Moreover, the dynamic nature of model parameters over time limits the generalizability of these online evaluation methods in practical engineering applications. Therefore, there is a pressing requirement to improve the generalization capabilities of existing online evaluation methods.

Summary: Table 1 illustrates the technical attributes of the current IT evaluation techniques. It is worth noting that despite advancements in online calibration technology for ITs, there remains a reliance on IT calibration data. This dependency hinders the fulfillment of real-time and precise online evaluation in contemporary power grids, where the need for calibrated results or standards is eliminated.

Online evaluation technology of ITs based on deep fusion of knowledge and data: Constructing robust and accurate dynamic evaluation models for IT errors, solely relying on IT measurement data, poses a significant challenge in the realm of online IT monitoring technology. Future trends in technology: As the power grid undergoes advancements in scale and intelligence, the field of online monitoring is anticipated to witness significant developments in three key areas:

1) The transition from individual components to integrated systems: This involves grouping substation ITs, secondary circuits, energy meters, and other multi-tiered measurement equipment to perform mutual calibration based on the correlations among different equipment. This approach aims to achieve higher precision online calibration.

2) Shift from substation to wide-area monitoring: By leveraging wide-area measurement technology in the power system, the grouping of ITs in a single substation can be expanded to encompass a larger geographical area, facilitating larger-scale and more efficient online monitoring.

3) Progress from real-time error monitoring to error prediction and alerting: This direction involves delving into the temporal characteristics of measuring device output signals and exploring the patterns of progressive changes in IT states. The goal is to advance from real-time monitoring to intelligent prediction for ITs, thereby enhancing the controllability and observability of the power system.

In the context of the increasingly urgent global energy transformation, the topic of online monitoring of ITs has been steadily gaining interest over the past years. With the development of power grid scale and intelligence, the development of online monitoring technology for ITs will focus on the following areas:

(1) The transition from individual components to integrated systems;

(2) Shift from substation to wide-area monitoring;

These three directions hold great promise for the future of online monitoring for ITs and offer opportunities for enhancing the performance and capabilities of power systems.

## 1 Introduction

One country that has year-round access to solar energy is the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia. This offers the perfect opportunity to create a national solar power hub to meet the Kingdom's rapidly growing electric power needs, particularly in large-scale projects and future sustainable smart cities of the fourth generation (such as NEOM).

The demand for electricity in Saudi Arabia is rising as a result of the country's construction boom and expanding population. The continuous high loads necessitate the generation of appropriate and sufficient power. However, it is well acknowledged that traditional energy production using fossil fuels is a major contributor to environmental pollution and has an adverse effect on human health due to the emissions of dangerous gases such as nitrogen oxides (NO, $ NO_{2} $ & $ N_{2} O $ ), sulfur oxides $ (SO_{2} $ & $ SO_{3} $ ), and carbon oxides (CO & $ CO_{2} $ ). Therefore, it is crucial to develop a different approach that both protects the environment and public health, and that also supports Saudi Arabia's present conventional power generation. Saudi Arabia is strategically positioned due to its location in the so-called "sun belt", extensive desert terrain, and year-round clear skies, which have helped it rise to the top of the list of solar PV energy producers. It is worth attempting to generate clean energy in Saudi Arabia using direct sunlight through PV cells because the average amount of solar energy that strikes the country is 2200 thermal kWh/m $ ^{2} $ [Alawaji, 2001].

The use of solar energy in Saudi Arabia has been increasing since 1960. The King Abdulaziz City for Science and Technology (KACST) began a systematic large research and development effort for the development of solar energy technology in 1977. The KACST Energy Research Institute and the US National Renewable Energy Laboratory started working together on the Saudi Solar Radiation Atlas project in 1994 [Said et al., 2008].

One of the highest direct normal irradiation (DNI) supplies in the world is found in Saudi Arabia. To take advantage of this high DNI and generate electricity from a renewable, clean, and sustainable source, Saudi Arabia is preparing for a substantial deployment of both photovoltaic (PV) and concentrated solar power (CSP). This would aid in the preservation of hydrocarbon resources, which are currently used in substantial amounts to produce electricity and desalinate seawater. However, given that the efficiency of solar systems declines when running at high temperatures, high DNI in combination with the sweltering midsummer ambient temperatures pose a significant technical barrier for the PV systems.

This calls for the creation of cooling systems that are adapted to the local climate. On the other hand, despite the fact that this high DNI is better for CSP systems, heavy dust loadings might have a bigger effect on reflectors. In Saudi Arabia, CSP is being taken into consideration for both direct thermal applications in industrial processes such as water desalination and energy production. The difficulties and possibilities of solar energy in Saudi Arabia will be highlighted in this study, including overheating and prospective cooling systems as well as solar thermal process heat and desalination options.

To satisfy the demands of a rapidly expanding population and economy, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia predicts that the nation will require enormous energy resources in the coming decades for electricity generation, desalination, and process heat [EIA, 2014]. The Kingdom is planning a sustainable energy mix that incorporates renewable energy based on local resources in order to use petroleum for higher value uses and export. Solar energy has long been seen as promising in Saudi Arabia due to an anticipated large solar resource [Baras et al., 2012; Hepbasli and Alsuhaibani, 2011].

As the leading Saudi Arabian government agency for renewable energy, King Abdullah City for Atomic and Renewable Energy (KACARE) is creating the Renewable Resource Monitoring and Mapping (RRMM) Solar Measurement Network in order to support significant increases in the Kingdom's solar generating capacity and move the nation towards a sustainable energy mix. According to [Bryden et al., 2013], Saudi Arabia's installed solar photovoltaic capacity as of 2013 was 7 MW. ## 1 Introduction

Renewable energy resources, such as solar and wind power, have experienced repaid growth and transformation in the global energy industry. Smart meters have become essential in maximizing the potential of renewable energy as we shift away from fossil fuels. These meters provide advanced features and collect realtime data, benefiting both consumers and energy providers. In this article, we will examine how smart meters can facilitate the integration of renewable energy into our daily lives.

This article aims to identify the distinct advantages that smart meters offer to users of renewable energy.

## 1.1 Smart meter

An electricity smart meter, also known as a smart electricity meter or simply a smart meter, is an advanced digital device that measures and records electricity consumption in real-time. It replaces traditional analog meters and incorporates advanced communication technology to facilitate two-way communication between the meter and the utility company.

One of the primary advantages of electricity smart meters is their ability to provide more accurate billing. With real-time monitoring, these meters offer precise measurements of electricity usage, eliminating the need for estimated bills or manual meter readings. This ensures that consumers are billed accurately, leading to greater transparency and fairness in the billing process. Another key benefit of smart meters is the ability to monitor energy consumption in real-time. These meters display current energy usage information, allowing consumers to gain a better understanding of their electricity usage patterns. Armed with this knowledge, individuals can make informed decisions about their

energy consumption, adjust their habits, and potentially reduce their energy costs.

The digital display feature of smart meters enhances user experience by presenting energy usage information in a user-friendly format. The display may provide information such as current usage and historical data. This accessibility and visibility enable consumers to make informed choices regarding their energy usage and identify areas where energy conservation measures can be implemented.

Beyond individual benefits, smart meters also contribute to improved grid management. With the ability to transmit real-time data, utility companies can better monitor and manage electricity distribution. This enables them to detect and respond promptly to issues such as power outages or voltage fluctuations, leading to enhanced reliability and efficiency of the power grid. Smart meters also offer data logging and reporting capabilities, which allow consumers and utility companies to access detailed historical energy usage data. This data can be analyzed to identify trends, evaluate the effectiveness of energy-saving initiatives, and provide insights for future energy planning. It also supports the development of innovative pricing structures, such as time-of-use tariffs, which incentivize consumers to shift their energy usage to off-peak hours.

Electricity smart meters bring numerous benefits to both consumers and utility companies. From accurate billing and real-time energy monitoring to improved grid management and promotion of energy conservation and renewable energy integration, these advanced devices play a vital role in creating a more efficient, transparent, and sustainable energy ecosystem.

## 1.2 Renewable resources

Electricity networks are transitioning to become more efficient and environmental friendly by incorporating renewable energy resources (RERs), such as solar, wind, or micro grids installed on house rooftops. However, integrating RERs into the distribution network poses challenges, including ensuring sufficient demand, generation stability, and interoperability between RERs and the distribution network. These challenges address a key issue, which is to produce electricity according to demand.

Smart meters play a vital role in this transition and offer numerous benefits to users of renewable energy. One of the key advantages is the provision of real-time information on energy consumption. By providing immediate and accurate data on how much energy is being used, smart meters enable users to make informed decisions about their energy consumption habits. This

empowers them to optimize their energy usage, identify areas where energy can be saved, and ultimately reduce costs. Another advantage of smart meters is their ability to facilitate remote power connection and disconnection. This feature allows users to conveniently manage their energy supply without the need for physical intervention. For example, if a household produces excess solar energy, they can easily connect to the grid and sell the surplus energy back to the utility company. This feature promotes a two-way flow of electricity, allowing households to become active participants in the energy market. By selling surplus energy, users can not only offset their own energy costs but also contribute to a greener future by increasing the share of renewable energy in the overall electricity mix. Smart meters offer a wide range of benefits for users of renewable energy.

Smart meters monitor the quality of the electricity supply, ensuring that users receive a stable and reliable power flow. By constantly monitoring voltage levels, frequency, and other parameters, smart meters can detect and alert users about any irregularities or power quality issues. This enables prompt actions to be taken improving the overall reliability of the electricity supply. Data-driven decision-making is another benefit of smart meters. By collecting and analyzing energy consumption data, users can gain valuable insights into their energy usage patterns. This information can be used to make informed decisions about time-of-use pricing, and other strategies to further optimize energy consumption. Smart meters assist users in selecting the appropriate solar systems for residential use. By analyzing energy consumption patterns and solar potential, smart meters can provide recommendations on the size and type of solar system that would best meet the household's energy needs. This helps users make informed decisions about investing in solar energy and maximizes the benefits of renewable energy generation.

## 2 Smart meters enhance renewable resources feasibility

The deployment of smart meters is crucial for both the supply and demand sides of RERs. These smart meters are equipped with communication technologies that enable an instant information exchange between supply and demand meters. This interchangeability enhances demand forecasting. Moreover, having a smart meter provides access to more sophisticated electricity tariff structures, including net metering arrangements that prioritize the consumption of energy produced by your solar system before selling any excess back to the grid.

## 2.1 Enhanced electricity supply and monitoring

Smart meters enable energy distributors to monitor the quality of the electricity supply more efficiently and gain valuable insights into the behavior of the grid through the data provided by these smart meters. This data can be utilized to optimize grid management and promptly detect outages or fluctuations in supply, allowing distributors to respond quickly to any issues. This ensures a reliable electricity supply, minimizing downtime and providing renewable energy users with a more stable and consistent power source. Moreover, smart meters facilitate the seamless integration of renewable energy into daily lives. Additionally, providers can strategically invest in renewable energy projects by identifying areas with high renewable energy potential.

## 2.2 Facilitating net metering and feed-in tariffs

Incentive mechanisms, such as net metering and feed-in tariffs, promote the production of renewable energy by consumers, and smart meters play a crucial role in facilitating the implementation of these programs. For instance, net metering allows consumers to inject surplus electricity by rooftop solar panels or small wind turbines into the grid, as described above. Furthermore, smart meters accurately measure the bidirectional flow of electricity, ensuring seamless implementation of net metering and guaranteeing fair compensation for energy producers.

# Unnatural gas: Fuel for thought for legal metrology

WIM VOLMER

Senior Advisor

Specialist International Metrology

Dutch Authority for Digital Infrastructure -

Ministry of Economic Affairs and Climate Policy, The Netherlands

## Introduction

One does not need to follow the news on a daily basis to know that energy and the environment are "hot" topics in today's and tomorrow's world. Humanity's efforts to keep our planet a pleasant place to live on for our children and grandchildren are closely linked, and one of those links is legal metrology.

Traditional energy carriers are to be gradually replaced by renewable alternatives in order to reduce our $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ footprint. Natural gas may be less damaging than some other forms of energy, but (partly) replacing it by hydrogen, bio-gas and/or green gas will reduce the amount of greenhouse gas emitted nonetheless. As an ironic side-note, despite its common name "natural gas", use of the substance itself is far from eco-friendly. Adding more fuel to the game of words, it implicitly categorises bio-gas, green gas, hydrogen and synthetic gases (power to gas) as unnatural gases. This in turn implies that those are not eco-friendly. Happily leaving that terminology debate to the linguists, one should also note that on the emissions side of the energy chain, new measurement challenges arise as well. Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) initiatives are definitely needed on the road to zero emissions. Additionally, $ \mathrm{N O_{x}} $ emissions are to be reduced, meaning those measurements are also of increasing importance.

Whether or not all the measurements listed above are under legal metrology control varies per country/ region and may even change over time. However, for the ongoing revision project of OIML R140 Measuring systems for gaseous fuel and the future revision of OIML R137 Gas meters, it is beneficial to keep those developments in mind and prepare for them. Ideally the updated Recommendations should be as future-proof as possible. Once ready, countries, economies and/or regions can then decide to apply the Recommendations to specific applications, as needed.

## Scenarios and their implications

In a mindset of "even the smallest step helps", there is a wide variety of scenarios that might become common practice somewhere in the world. However, most likely these will be one of the following approximate categories:

natural gas mixed with up to 5% renewables;

natural gas mixed with up to 25 % renewables;

98 % or purer hydrogen;

99. 997 % or purer hydrogen, for fuel cell applications;

trading emissions rights based on $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ and/or $ \mathrm{N O_{x}} $ content (not yet covered by R 140).

Regardless of how matters work out, one thing is clear and that is that gas composition and therefore its energy content will change. The current published versions of R 140 and R 137, on the one hand do not exclude any of the above categories, but on the other are not fine-tuned to deal with them in a well harmonised manner either. Moreover, besides $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ being a more challenging gas to measure, $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ and $ \mathrm{N O_{x}} $ are noncombustible gases. Combined, these technical matters require the respective Recommendations to be updated.

## R 140 Gas measuring systems

While consumers may be used to buying cubic metres of gas, what they are actually buying is energy. The energy content of a cubic metre may vary, if the gases are not standardised. The gas industry is well aware of this and has been trading gas based on energy content for many decades already intra industry, but not to consumers. Therefore they typically measure the quantity of the gas usually expressed as cubic metres at base conditions as well as its composition. That composition, in turn, is used to calculate the heating value, and when multiplied by the quantity, results in the overall energy content of for instance 24 hours' worth of gas supply.

Besides a gas meter (or several in parallel), this determination requires pressure and temperature measuring instruments, as well as an analytical device, nowadays typically a gas chromatograph. In short, it requires a fairly extensive measuring system to determine the actual basis for invoicing. R140 deals with such systems, referring to R137 for most of the gas meter related requirements. In view of the current and future developments, as far as R140 goes, the analytical device determining the composition of the gas is the most challenging topic to be addressed.

Since natural gas compositions do differ across the globe, it now takes some seven different reference gas mixtures to test a gas chromatograph for its capabilities

to cover all possible components over all currently known natural gases. With the above applications in mind, defining a limited number of reference gases covering all possible scenarios is simply impossible. Therefore, the major technical update in the new R 140 deals with testing analytical devices.

## R 137 Gas meters

In many people's minds, hydrogen gas is often associated with images of the Hindenburg disaster. As legal metrologists it is good to be aware of safety issues as well, but hydrogen also presents other challenges. One often overlooked property of hydrogen is that it causes some materials to become more brittle. While this may or may not be a metrological problem, three other hydrogen characteristics definitely are. Firstly, hydrogen produces far less heat when burned than methane. Secondly, it has a much lower density than other gases, and thirdly hydrogen molecules are so small that leakage and permeation through materials is far more likely to occur.

The lower energy content of hydrogen basically affects most gas meters currently on the market. The reason for this is that the meters will need to operate at higher flowrates, to deliver the same amount of energy. The lower density affects the various measurement principles to different extents. Imagine for example a turbine rotor (effectively a chic propeller) needing to be accelerated by a gas flow, more gas mass, means better interaction. Note that many measurement principles, including newer technologies, can be affected by this change in fluid density. The operation of diaphragm gas meters is based on flexible bellows that are alternatingly filled and emptied. If hydrogen molecules slip through the bellow material instead of being contained by it, mismeasurements occur. Here, one can also raise questions on the long-term effects of hydrogen on the diaphragm material. In summary, additional testing is needed to assess the impact of hydrogen on the various metering principles.

Bio-gas and green gas (definitions vary) originate from organic materials decomposing, thereby emitting among others, methane and $ \mathrm{C O}_{2} $ . The presence of $ \mathrm{C O}_{2} $ is undesirable for at least two reasons. One: it is noncombustible and two, when combined with moisture, it quickly reacts into acidic fluids which accelerate material corrosion. As a side note, specifically for ultrasonic meters, $ \mathrm{C O}_{2} $ molecules are great at "absorbing" ultrasonic energy, thereby potentially

reducing the reliable operation of such meters. All in all, in these applications one wishes to have as little $ \mathrm{C O}_{2} $ as possible making its way into the main grid. This in turn requires accurate composition determination, which then enables accurate billing as well.

Conversely, on CCS applications, the intention is to measure (almost) pure $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ with the aim of storing it and, in doing so, preventing it from adding to the greenhouse effect. This is done to counterbalance the use of non-renewable fuels, which cannot be prevented given the energy demand and limited availability of renewable sources. This relatively new field of technology currently struggles with a wide variation of technical requirements being imposed by many different regional, national and even local organisations. Having suitable OIML Recommendations for these applications will assist in accelerating the applications described above.

## (Other) emission gases

Typically, gases emitted from heating processes (e.g. gas powered electricity plants and refinery distillation installations) are non-combustible. Strictly speaking they are therefore outside the scope of the current published R 140, which is restricted to fuel gases. However, with the expanding ETS (Emissions Trading Scheme), the likelihood of measurements of - in this case primarily $ \mathrm{C O_{2}} $ - forming the basis for trade transaction / taxation, is growing rapidly.

Currently mostly for environmental reasons, the measurement of $ \mathrm{N O_{x}} $ is foreseen to grow rapidly as well. Therefore, here too, having suitable Recommendations in place would benefit all stakeholders.

- motivate a forward-thinking approach regarding the revisions of R 140 and R 137,

- extend the scope to non-traditional gases, including non-combustible ones, and

- formulate technology-independent requirements where possible,

so that legal metrology can be an enabler for the global energy transition.

## Abstract

This paper considers the compliance of the existing regulatory framework of legal metrology with prospective digital technologies, and a conclusion is drawn concerning the need for the preemptive development of the regulatory framework in order to comply with modern technologies and support the digital transformation processes in metrology.

## Keywords

Legal metrology, measuring instrument, digital transformation, digital economy, electronic document, self-verification, virtual instrument, digital twin, big data, blockchain, artificial intelligence.

## Introduction

Today's world has entered the era of the fourth industrial revolution, also known as "Industry 4.0". The concept of "Industry 4.0" involves the mass introduction of so-called cyber-physical systems into production in order to serve the ever-growing needs of mankind. The realization of the "Industry 4.0" concept is impossible without digitalization of all spheres of human life and social relations. Digitalization in the modern world, in turn, is characterized by the transition from the old stages of processes automation and reengineering to the new stage of digital transformation. At this new stage,

existing models of activity, products and services, which are implemented on the basis of specialized digital platforms, are fundamentally changing, and completely new models are emerging. A digital platform can be defined as a system of algorithmic relations of a significant number of market and/or society participants, united by a single information environment. The main idea of such an environment is to reduce transaction costs and increase the efficiency of interaction between the participants.

Ultimately, digital transformation is designed to help build the digital economy. The digital economy is an entirely new stage in the development of the world economy, in which technological changes are fundamentally changing entire industries and spheres of social life. These technological changes are based on digitally stored data and the information extracted from that data. The digital economy can be described as an economic activity in which the key factor of production is data in digital form. The processing of these data, compared with traditional forms of economic activity, can significantly improve the efficiency of various types of production, technology, equipment, storage, sale and delivery of goods and services.

Russia has set a goal of creating the basis of the digital economy by 2024. This goal is currently being implemented within the framework of the national program "Digital Economy of the Russian Federation" [1]. The main tasks of the program, among others, include creating a system of legal regulation of the digital economy, creating a system for financing digital technologies and platforms projects, and implementing digital technologies and platform solutions in the areas of public administration and public services. Thus, on the one hand, the scale and problems of digital transformation and the construction of the digital economy require direct and active participation of the state. On the other hand, the state itself must be transformed to meet the new requirements of the digital economy and the social sphere. At the turn of 2016-2018, the expert community developed the concept of the "State as a Platform" [2,3], many provisions of which were considered in the development of the national program.

Obviously, when introducing elements of the digital economy, the state must perform its regulatory function in a proactive manner. This means that new platforms, tools and services that appear in the process of digital transformation must fit seamlessly into the regulatory framework prepared in advance for them. Otherwise, the emerging digital economy will be outside the legal framework and therefore unable to realize its full potential. In this article the authors choose to focus on some aspects related to the implementation of legal regulation of digital transformation in one of the areas of state regulation, namely in the field of assurance of measurement uniformity. The terms "assurance of

measurement uniformity" and "metrology" are almost synonymous, but if metrology is more of a science, then assurance of measurement uniformity reflects the main content and goal of metrology. In particular, in the article we would like to analyze how the emergence and implementation of new digital tools and services in metrology corresponds to the current legislation and regulations, and to focus the attention of the expert community and executive authorities on the need for preemptive modernization of the regulatory framework of legal metrology.

In this regard, the authors have highlighted several technologies that are already being implemented or that are about to be implemented in everyday practice, and which, according to the authors, are not supported or are in direct contradiction with the existing regulatory framework.

## 1 Application of electronic (machine-readable) documents

The concepts of electronic and machine-readable documents have evolved with the development of information technology. In the 1980s, a machine-readable document was simply a document suitable for automatic reading of the information it contained. If "information" means not just the text or the image of a document itself, but separate logical fields suitable for further computer processing (symbols, strings, integers, floating point numbers, arrays of numbers, images and other machine-readable formats), this definition is still relevant today. So, for the purposes of this paper, we will consider the electronic document as a document in the form of a database divided into fields suitable for reading (including API) and further machine processing. Accordingly, a simple scan of a paper document (i.e. picture) should not be considered today as a kind of electronic document.

In the Russian Federation the main legal act regulating the rules and relations around measurements and metrology is the Federal Law "On Assurance of Measurement Uniformity" [4] (hereinafter referred to as "the Law") and the main metrological database is the Federal State Information System ARSHIN [5] (further FGIS ARSHIN). Today the Law implies the use of only two electronic machine-readable documents in the FGIS ARSHIN: 1) decision on type approval of measuring instruments (MI) or certified reference material (CRM) and 2) results of MI verification. At the same time there is a whole range of other documents issued during the activities on assuring measurement uniformity, which it is reasonable to convert into electronic format (MI type description, verification report, verification procedures,

methods and results of attestation of reference standards and test equipment, measurement procedures, etc.). Conversion of these documents into electronic form will greatly simplify and accelerate their analysis, verification, actualization, modification, development, prototyping, agreement, approval and other standard procedures typically performed with the documents. Besides, the machine-oriented format will make it possible to automate document searches by various criteria and organize the collection of statistics analysis and optimization of the document database.

## 2 Self-monitoring and self-verification of measuring instruments

The modern stage of MI technologies is characterized not only by the transition from analogue MIs to digital ones, but also by the global appearance of so called "smart" MIs. One of the features of smart MIs is the advent of deep self-diagnostics and self-monitoring which, in a number of cases with a high degree of probability, can guarantee not only the operability of the MI but also the compliance of their metrological parameters with the prescribed specifications. In our conditions such a kind of deep self-diagnostics or self-monitoring can be interpreted as self-verification. The development of verification procedures for such MIs, compared with traditional procedures, is essentially reduced to establishing a list of checking parameters and tolerance limits for them. Being within limits guarantees the compliance of the metrological parameters with the prescribed specifications. Furthermore, such MIs with self-diagnostics, being directly connected to the control system on a network via an industrial interface, or as participants of the "Internet of things" (IoT), can transmit the results of their metrological checks on demand or cyclically at the required interval. In this case, any unawareness of the metrological serviceability of MIs, which usually takes place between verifications, is eliminated. The concept and value of "between-verification interval" also largely loses its traditional meaning.

Thus, self-verification has undoubted advantages compared to the usual verification practice, but from the standpoint of the current regulatory framework, is outside the legal field. From a formal point of view, MIs cannot verify themselves without the participation of an engineer-verifier and outside a laboratory accredited in the national accreditation system, although in this case neither the verifier nor the laboratory is explicitly needed. The official registration of the result of such self-verification (e.g. entry in the FGIS ARSHIN, etc.) is not defined either.

## 3 Remote measurements and remote verification

The existing regulations and practice of verification usually assume that the verifier directly performs a certain sequence of operations on MIs. For this purpose, the MI is transported to the place of verification, or the verifier travels to the location of the MI. At the same time, very often modern MIs are able to transmit their measurements over unlimited distances thanks, first of all, to the technologies of IoT. Developers of MIs create built-in software that can generate and send detailed protocols of remote measurement or verification. In the case of remote verification, unlike self-verification where MIs work autonomously, the operator is typically required to perform a sequence of actions (connection to a measurement standard, signal supply, use of CRM, etc.). But the presence of the verifier directly at the location of the MI is not required, he/she can analyze the protocol and make a "Pass/Fail" decision remotely. It is worth keeping in mind that the developers have to do as much as possible to protect the information (transmitted measurement protocols) from accidental or intentional distortion in order to nullify any risk of obtaining unreliable information by the verifier.

Existing legal metrology does not contain a formal ban on remote verification, i.e. if the remote verification procedure has been developed, tested and approved at the stage of MI type approval, it can be legally applied in practice. The problem is that today there are practically no such procedures and they are practically not being developed for a number of reasons. The first reason is some kind of conservatism of thinking and approaches, considering that various telecommunication capabilities, including IoT, appeared and became available relatively recently. In addition, there remains a degree of distrust of such measurements today. For example, the recent COVID-19 pandemic has forced metrologists to make such attempts at remote verification, despite the formal absence of approved procedures. But practically none of these attempts has been successful, first of all, because of the lack of experience and, as a consequence, the low level of confidence in the results.

The second reason is that the implementation of remote verification in a number of cases requires constructive modifications of MIs or changes in approaches to certain procedures. For example, when the verification procedure implies the sealing of the MI by the verifier at the end of measurements. In such cases, some modern methods of authorized access (electronic keys, electronic locks, passwords, etc.) should be used.

With a broad legitimization and implementation of remote verification procedures, there will be significant

cost savings due to the lower transportation and travelling expenses, as well as reduced turnaround time.

## 4 Digital twins of measuring instruments and measuring systems

A "digital twin" is a digital copy of a physical object, in our case a measuring instrument or measuring system. Such a twin, in the ideal case, fully reproduces the functionality and operating logic of the physical object. Twins are useful in cases when any manipulations with a real object are physically impossible, they have an unacceptable duration, for safety reasons, or they are too expensive, etc. For example, it is problematic to perform variable in-situ testing of a control system or an emergency shutdown system of a nuclear power plant and any other hazardous production facilities. A digital twin usually makes it possible to collect the necessary array of measurements in a short time, at low cost and without violating any safety rules. In other words, it is possible to greatly simplify or reduce the time and cost of the measuring task, and in some cases to obtain data that are physically inaccessible under normal conditions.

However, the legitimacy of accepting the array of measurements obtained by using the twin instead of the test results of the real physical object is a big concern. Obviously, it is necessary to have a corresponding procedure of digital twin evaluation by means of some numerical or probabilistic indexes, in other words, a testing procedure for the twin itself. Moreover, today there is no place for digital twins in the regulatory framework of legal metrology. So, that is one more possible focus point for legislators.

## 5 Autonomous metrology software virtual instruments

In the classical case, the MI is a functionally complete device, at the output of which we have a measurement result in one form or another. Built-in software (SW), also known as firmware (FW), has always been perceived as an integral functional part of the MI, and is involved in the transformation of a physical input signal into a measurement result. But recently the autonomous SW running on a variety of external resources is increasingly used for obtaining or further transforming a measurement result. Sometimes the term "virtual instrument" is used for such kind of metrological SW. In terms of the final measurement result, such external

stand-alone SW plays just as large a role as the FW. But if FW is usually validated as a part of MI during type approval, calibrations, verifications and so on, then there are no validation procedures for autonomous SW and virtual devices in the current regulatory framework.

Also metrologically significant external SW appears, for instance, during automation of calibration or verification procedures. The degree of automation depends on the type of measurements and, for some types, it can be rather high. For example, in the case of electrical instruments the verifier manually secures only the physical connections, then the switching of ranges, the measurements themselves, information processing calculation of errors or uncertainties, creation of protocols and other operations are performed automatically by the SW. The default assumption is that all the manipulations are performed according to the approved "paper" verification procedure, but in practice nobody validates automation SW and confirms the compliance of automated manipulations with the approved procedure.

Current trends show that measurements are increasingly going into the virtual realm, and physical instruments are becoming more and more virtual. Maybe now is the time to officially recognize the term "virtual instrument" as a part of the metrological environment, and develop corresponding validation procedures, similar to physical MI, e.g. type approval, periodic testing or validation, etc.

## 6 Technical systems and devices with measuring functions

The concept of technical systems and devices with measuring functions (TSDMF) was first introduced in the Law [4] to identify devices that perform measuring functions along with their basic functions. Thus mandatory requirements for TSDMF and the forms of their conformity assessment are established by the legislation on technical regulation, but performing of the measuring functions of TSDMF is regulated by the legislation on the assurance of measurement uniformity (metrology). According to the definition, the difference between TSDMF and MI lies in the word "basic": if the measuring functions are basic, then most likely we deal with MI, because TSDMF performs measuring functions "along with the basic". The problem is what to consider as the main or "basic" functions.

Let us take, for example, a modern complex for monitoring the traffic situation and automatic recording of violations. It measures the speed of the vehicle and creates a time and spatial (geographical) coordinates reference point for these measurements. Thus the speed,

time and coordinates are certainly measurements which must be regulated by the metrology legislation. But in addition, the complex performs a number of nonmeasuring functions, for example, it fixes the lane crossing line, detects unfastened seatbelts , detects drivers talking on their cell phone, improper stopping and parking, etc. Such functions are performed, as a rule, using artificial intelligence technologies, namely computer vision, and generally have nothing to do with metrology.

The current legislation does not give a clear answer to the question of whether such a video-measuring complex belongs to MI or TSDMF, in other words, which function should be considered as a basic one. If the complex passes the procedures of state regulation as an MI (i.e. type approval and verification), it is not clear how to assess its compliance with non-metrological parameters. And vice-versa, if the conformity assessment is carried out according to the legislation on technical regulation (certification or declaration of conformity), it is not clear how to assure measurement uniformity.

## 7 The application of pure information technologies in metrology

The modern world is characterized not only by the emergence of new digital or information technologies (IT), but also by their interpenetration in various fields of human activity. Technologies which were considered as "pure IT" technologies at the time of their emergence are now widely used outside IT: in finances, public administration, manufacturing, medicine, education, science, personal data management, etc. Metrology is not an exception. For example, concepts such as "big data", "blockchain" and "artificial intelligence" are already being implemented or are on the verge of implementation in activities related to measurement uniformity. We will come back to artificial intelligence (AI) separately later on; in this section we will look at examples of big data and blockchain implementation.

Big data is commonly referred to not only as data themselves, but also as the tools, approaches and methods that make it possible to extract useful information for certain purposes from large volumes of structured or unstructured data. Formally, big data refers to data that arrives at a rate of 100-150 Gb per day, although big data technologies can certainly be applied to smaller data volumes. For example, if we look at FGIS ARSHIN contents, nowadays it contains records of over 96000 approved MIs and more than 9000 approved reference materials, more than 115000 measurement standards, more than 44000 certified

measurement procedures, and also more than 13 million MI verification results.

Although in terms of daily growth in gigabytes FGIS ARSHIN does not formally fall under the classification of big data, there are evident attributes of a big data source. Accordingly, the typical big data techniques and methods can be applied to the processing of such data, particularly blending and integration, crowdsourcing, statistical and regression analysis, machine learning, predictive analytics, time series analysis, visualization, pattern recognition, etc. As more and more documents become electronic (machine-readable), as discussed in section 1 of this article, the amount of useful information potentially extractable by big data technologies will increase significantly.

Blockchains are another example of IT entering various fields of activity, including metrology. Wilson S. Melo Jr [6] discusses the interdependence of legal metrology and blockchain technology: just as metrology needs blockchains, blockchains need regulation by legal metrology. The article provides examples of where and how blockchains can be used for the needs of metrology. Again, the author explains why blockchains in turn also need regulation from the metrology side.

## 8 Artificial intelligence

Artificial Intelligence is certainly the main component and driver of "Industry 4.0". It is difficult nowadays to name any field of human activity in which AI technologies have not been introduced or have not already been successfully applied. Metrology is also on this list. Artificial intelligence is primarily a technology for working with data, including big data. Metrology also deals with data, which are often represented by large arrays. Artificial intelligence comes to the rescue when interconnections in the data are not obvious and hardly can be formalized. In other words, when the measurement function, i.e. the dependence of the measurement result on the input observed data, cannot be formalized in the form of a functional or algorithmic relationship. Despite the numerous research projects and experimental developments, the practical use of AI in MIs is still negligible. There are only a few examples of complete MIs with built-in neural network algorithms that have successfully passed the type approval procedure and put into real industrial use. One example is described in [7]. Very often AI cannot be implemented directly in the MI as a part of FW, because neural network technologies require significant computing resources. In this case AI can be utilized in post-processing mode within external stand-alone SW (see section 5 of this paper).

On one hand, the use of AI to obtain a measurement result does not create any technical collisions. Indeed, for a user of MI or a user of external SW the algorithms themselves are usually not of interest, and in most cases, they are closed for the user. In terms of the final measurement result, it makes no difference whether neural networks or fixed computational algorithms are running under the hood. However, from the experts' point of view, the use of AI in metrology has a number of features and carries a number of risks associated with incorrect operation. For example, when the combination of input data is very different from the training sample, the neural network may produce significant outliers. Functional uncertainty may also arise, if the neural network is retrained during execution.

From the standpoint of the current regulatory framework of legal metrology, the AI cannot be a subject of activity on assurance of measurement uniformity. If during MI verification by a deterministic procedure the wrong "Pass/Fail" decision is made, the verifier is responsible for that. If the erroneous decision is made by the AI, in this case there is formally no responsible individual. So, this is some kind of legal question or a gap in the legislation.

## 9 Flexibility of technologies versus conservatism of procedures

In many cases, modern MIs are electronic devices, i.e. complex units consisting of some hardware (HW) and embedded metrological SW called firmware (FW). Over time, the second component is becoming more and more important in many applications. Firmware is a kind of software, and the word "software" itself means that this component of the device is "soft" and easily changeable. The necessity of FW replacement (update) is caused by finding errors, optimizing computing procedures, developing more effective algorithms, etc. Regular FW updating is normal practice nowadays for almost all modern electronic devices, but for MI it is not so easy as, for example, for smartphones or TVs.

In modern legal metrology these two components of MI are still inseparable, and the instrument itself is treated as a whole. For example, during the type approval procedure the particular FW version is tested and the version ID is recorded in the type description. From this moment, the FW becomes an integral part of the MI. If the developer has created a new FW, this entails changes in the type description, which is practically equivalent to new type approval tests, both in terms of time and cost. This approach is certainly an anachronism and holds back the development of instrumentation, as well as creating enormous difficulties for

both manufacturers and users of MI. Let us imagine, for example, a smartphone manufacturer that has certified a new model, spent several months and a lot of money on certifying it, but who then has to repeat this certification procedure all over again with each new FW version. In reality, the users simply download a new version of the FW via the Internet, and often the smartphone does this itself in automatic mode. Such a procedure does not seem that out of the ordinary and could work in a similar way for MIs, given that we live in the IoT era.

There is, of course, a difference between smartphone FW and measuring instrument FW (MI FW). The smartphone FW is published by the manufacturer itself; also, the manufacturer makes some commitments or guarantees on its own behalf. The MI FW almost always has a metrologically significant part, which has to be legitimized by the certifying body. In other words, some kind of check (approval, verification, validation, etc.) of any new FW version must be carried out. Typically, an ordinary FW update should not require any changes in the type description, especially if the new FW does not cause any changes in the metrological parameters of the MI. The difference with smartphones is that the new MI FW must be updated not via the manufacturer's own resource, but via that of the certifying body. In other words, new MI FW must become available for downloading and be updated only after approval by the certifying body. As an option, blockchain technology can be used to protect the database of certified FWs, as discussed in section 7 of this article.

Such a flexible approach can and should be applied not only to MI FW. The concept of electronic documents (see section 1 of this article) allows an integral text document to be turned into a mini-database. For example, the MI type description is currently just a text document, scanned and stored in the FGIS ARSHIN. Let us imagine this document as an electronic one, i.e. as a set of database fields. If, for example, the power consumption of the MI was changed, a short test (only power measurement) would be carried out, and only one field of the type description would be corrected in the FGIS ARSHIN. Or, for example, if the manufacturer rebranded the product and began to produce green instead of blue MIs, as a result the MI would no longer pass visual identification according to the type description. In this case, it would probably be possible to change only one field of the type description (i.e. the photo) simply by the manufacturer's notification. So, the transition to wholly electronic documents would allow many similar problems and contradictions caused by conservative legislation and procedures to be resolved. And the overall flexibility of approaches to design, software and documentation will ultimately make life easier for all parties in the measurement market, including developers, producers, users, and state officials.

## 10 Flexible integration of measuring systems versus fixed architectures

Modern technologies, especially digital ones, are constantly increasing the level of unification and compatibility, and as a consequence, the ability to integrate components, measurement modules and instruments with each other. On the one hand, unified: output signals, control interfaces, communication protocols, dimensions for modular and rack-mounted devices, connections and connectors, power supply, input/output data formats for SW, etc. - all of which leads to two major implications:

many measuring systems become vendor-independent, which means that users can easily replace one manufacturer's instrument with a similar one from another manufacturer and it will perform in the system exactly the same functions with the same efficiency;

more flexibility in configuring measuring systems and the ability to reconfigure systems in the field depending on current measurement needs.

On the other hand, current legal metrology, along with common practice, implies a strict description and definition of any object of state regulation of assurance of measurement uniformity, whether this is an individual indivisible MI or a large measuring system consisting of dozens or hundreds of autonomous MIs. In practice, this leads to the application of standard procedures of state regulation, in particular type approval and verification, to large and often distributed measuring systems. The "cubes" from which such systems are assembled, i.e. separate autonomous MIs, are also the objects of state regulation. As a result, the double or in some cases triple nesting of MIs, subjected to type approval and verification, is created. Herewith in the vast majority of cases the upper-level MI, that is the measuring system itself, is not a subject of direct inputto-output verification, its verification is secured by unitby-unit verification of separate "cubes". The best-known examples of such an approach are various billing systems, for example, automated systems of commercial accounting of energy resources, systems of measurement of quantity and quality of oil, etc.

Besides the obvious redundancy of multi-level state regulation procedures for measuring systems, the biggest problem with this approach is the inability to perform any kind of reconfiguration, which means absence of any flexibility. Often, simply replacing one of the "cubes" with a functionally similar one entails a change in the type description for the entire system. Thus, all achievements of modern technologies in terms of free integration of MIs one with another and with

other digital solutions (modules, metrological SW, virtual instruments, etc.) are almost nullified by the practice of MI definition in legal metrology. Possibly in some cases it is justified, especially when integral error (uncertainty) of the system from input to output is very important. But even in these cases, the metrological parameters of the measuring system can be defined and assigned by other ways than type approval, for example by the development and validation of a measurement procedure. So, there is a vital need to develop additional standards and regulations that allow free configuration of measuring systems and their further legitimization without resorting to a type approval procedure.

## 11 Conclusion

In conclusion, it should be noted that in this article the authors did not set themselves the task of developing any proposals for the improvement and refinement of the specific provisions of legal metrology. This article is just an attempt to focus attention on the fact that some modern digital solutions and technologies, which are already implemented or about to be implemented in metrological practice, are not supported with the current norms of legal metrology. What is more, in some cases these technologies are in obvious contradiction with the current legislation. Without resolving these issues and ensuring the preemptive development of the regulatory framework, the progressive movement towards digital transformation in metrology and building a real digital economy will be practically impossible.

## Introduction

The OIML TC 5/SC 1/p 2 Project Group (PG) held its first meeting on 23-25 May 2023. The meeting took place online, and was held each day for four hours. The purpose of the meeting was to discuss comments received on the 2019 edition of D 11:2013 General requirements for measuring instruments - Environmental conditions.

## Background

In late 2022 and early 2023 PG members were asked to provide feedback, comments, and suggested changes to the 2019 edition of D 11:2013. The resulting feedback was compiled and discussed during the PG meeting which was attended by 22 participants representing Australia, Austria, Brazil, Canada, Germany, India, Iran Japan, Netherlands, Poland, Russia, Slovenia, United Kingdom, United States, CECIP, and the BIML. A total of 106 comments were received.

## Outcome

There were a large number of comments that identified a need to update the version number of some of the standards referenced in the 2019 edition of OIML D 11. Some of these have been addressed, while others have been actioned for further review.

Several comments were related to editorial concerns and have also been addressed. A number of technical concerns resulted in extensive discussions and ultimately it was decided that further discussion and resolution should be developed by subgroups. These include the following:

It was suggested that the requirements for durability and durability checking facilities need further clarification. A subgroup has been created to review and propose updates to these requirements.

There is concern that the OIML D 11 requirements for surges on AC and DC mains are stricter than those required by EN 61000-6-2. It was suggested that the OIML D 11 requirements may be better suited to align with IEC 61000-4-5. A subgroup will study this further and develop recommendations for updates to OIML D 11, Table 27.

Several concerns were raised relating to influence quantities and operating conditions concerning entries in OIML D 11, Table 5. A subgroup comprising Brazil, Canada, Netherlands, United States, and CECIP will therefore work on revisions to Table 5.

The PG members discussed the notion of including detailed information on the revision history in an annex to allow users to better appreciate the development of requirements contained in OIMLD11. This option will be studied further in a subgroup.

Over the course of the meeting, PG members were able to review 75% of the comments submitted. Additional online meetings to discuss outstanding comments are expected to take place later in the summer of 2023.

A First Committee Draft (1CD) is projected for distribution in 2024, and the PG is expecting to complete the revision of OIML D 11 by October 2025.

6-9 June 2023 NMi Netherlands and online

DR PHILLIP MITCHELL AND SOWMYA RAMAKRISHNAN Project Group conveners

## Introduction

The primary aim of this meeting was to discuss comments received from the Project Group members on the first committee draft (1CD) of OIML R 46. There were extensive discussions on several key topics and this report presents a summary of each of these.

## Meeting scheduling and format

This meeting was scheduled to align with two other related meetings under the topic of measuring instruments for electrical quantities. OIML TC 12/p 3 met on Monday 5 June 2023 to discuss Electric Vehicle Service Equipment (EVSE). The following week, the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) TC 13 WG 11 met to discuss the revision and development of IEC standards for electricity metering equipment. This alignment of meetings provided opportunities for members to participate in multiple meetings and enhances liaison and alignment between international standards.

The format of all three meetings was hybrid. The challenges of hybrid meetings were discussed, and there was general consensus that a hybrid meeting is the least preferred format. Several issues were raised: there are conflicting motivations for the duration of meeting days; face-to-face participants expect to make the most of each day while online participation is widely regarded as difficult beyond 3 or 4 hours. This difficulty is compounded depending on the location of online participants with some joining in the early hours of the

morning and other staying up very late. Members also noted the potential difficulties in being able to attend a meeting face-to-face when online attendance is also possible. For future meetings, we aim to proceed with either fully online or fully face-to-face meetings.

The convener would like to thank all members for their participation, with special thanks to NMi Netherlands for hosting the meeting and to the BIML for coordinating the online participation.

## Scope for OIML R 46

The 2012 edition of OIML R 46 is limited to alternating current (a.c.) meters. The project proposal for this current project included looking at expanding the scope to include direct current (d.c.) meters. After considering other international standards, including American National Standards Institute (ANSI) and IEC standards, the committee agreed that OIML R 46 should only include a.c. meters. Further, it was agreed to support a project proposal to develop a separate recommendation for d.c. meters.

## Subgroup 1 - Quantities and harmonics

A key technical topic is the definitions and nomenclature for various electrical quantities. This is a significant topic that related to many of the comments received on the 1CD.

One of the main challenges is the lack of a consistent definition for reactive energy. It is known that in the presence of different signals containing harmonics (i.e. where the current and voltage signals are not purely sinusoidal) different reactive energy meters may give different results.

![](page=7,bbox=[1240, 1968, 1641, 2520])

The committee welcomed a presentation from the U.S. on how ANSI are addressing quantities through ANSI C12.31. It was then agreed to align the next draft of OIML R 46 with the definitions and nomenclature from ANSI C12.31. This will include active energy, reactive energy and apparent energy. It was also agreed to describe signals using phase angles, rather than power factor values.

## Other subgroups

It was agreed that subgroup 3 on Smart Street Lighting will be widened to Specialised Devices and Applications. This is to recognise that not all electricity meters are used to meter typical residential or commercial loads with associated characteristics and sufficiently wide operating range. Draft requirements are to be developed.

Subgroup 4 on d.c. metering will be closed. As discussed above, it is expected that a separate project will be created to develop a new OIML Recommendation for d.c. meters.

Subgroup 5 on Modular Components and Remote Displays was discussed. It was agreed that remote displays are the priority and this is to include all types of remote displays including web enabled devices. Draft requirements will be developed.

## Other technical issues

A significant number of other issued were discussed and resolved. These included requirements for:

demand and demand meters;

interval and multi-tariff meters;

measurement uncertainty for type approval and verification; and

synchronous clocks.

## Next steps

Members agreed to continue revising OIML R46 through the work of the subgroups and the main Project Group. Subgroups will prepare drafts and expect to convene online meetings by late 2023. It is anticipated that the second committee draft (2CD) will be prepared by March 2024. The convener will also arrange for online project group meetings to discuss and resolve issues before finalising the 2CD.

# Conformity assessment digitalization and legal metrology for a fair and more accurate world

CRISTINA DRAGHICI

CASCO Secretary and Director of Conformity Assessment and Consumer Matters

ISO's Committee for Conformity Assessment (CASCO) held its annual plenary in April 2023. One of the events associated with the plenary was a workshop titled "Digitalization in conformity assessment: Current practices, challenges, and implications for the future". The workshop consisted of two panels that focused on the digitalization of conformity assessment methodologies, including the evolution of these methods, current practices, and the application of conformity assessment standards to ensure trust.

The BIML Director, Mr. Anthony Donnellan, participated in the first panel discussion, which explored innovative digital approaches used in inspection, calibration, metrology, and accreditation. Digital tools have revolutionized the assessment process by replacing or offering an alternative to traditional methods, such as on-site inspections or physical testing, with digitally operated laboratories and inspections conducted using drones or digital twins. The panel also addressed the benefits and risks associated with these

digital approaches as part of the overall digital transformation, and offered ideas on how to mitigate risks.

Mr. Donnellan specifically discussed the role of digitalization in metrology and its impact for conformity assessment and the broader quality infrastructure, since metrology plays a vital role in conformity assessment by providing accurate measurement data and reference standards. Digitalization has enabled the development of sophisticated measurement systems that can collect, analyse, and interpret vast amounts of data in real time, and this enhanced capability has greatly improved the accuracy and traceability of measurements, leading to more reliable conformity assessment results.

Highlighting the significance of post-market surveillance and the facilitation of traceability through software and impartial data gathering, Mr. Donnellan provided examples of digital solutions in metrology, such as digital certificates of conformity assessment in measurement and digital certificates of calibration.

Mr. Donnellan also outlined the current benefits of digitalization, including reducing time-to-market by eliminating issues with certification, resulting in increased efficiency, productivity gains, and a better consumer experience. He highlighted risk reduction through the use of digital solutions, by minimizing the need for human interaction in potentially hazardous contexts. He also discussed potential improvements made possible by digitalization, such as preventative and predictive interventions that could result in better and more sustainable product lifecycles.

Addressing the challenges related to digitalization, Mr. Donnellan emphasized the importance of ensuring data and system interoperability and accessibility recommending the use of open-source software to the greatest extent possible. He advised consolidating various digital certificates of conformity assessment in measurement and calibration to derive the maximum value from them, and concluded by stressing the need to preserve traceability, in systems that consumers can

# OIML Certification System (OIML-CS)

![](page=2,bbox=[828, 454, 1060, 683])

## Introduction

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

The OIML-CS comprises two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B. Competence of the OIML Issuing Authorities and their Test Laboratories is demonstrated through self-declaration under Scheme B and accreditation or peer assessment under Scheme A.

The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate. Participating in the OIML-CS commits, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS that are established in OIML B 18:2022 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS). Signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation and test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an OIML Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

The OIML-CS was launched on 1 January 2018 and has replaced the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

## OIML certificates

OIML certificates issued under Scheme A and Scheme B can be downloaded from the database on the OIML website at https://www.oiml.org/en/oiml-cs/certificat_view.

The database also includes certificates issued under the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the MAA. Although these two systems are no longer in operation, the certificates remain valid.

## Background

Many countries and economies with emerging metrology systems suffer from a lack of resources for the operation of a sound legal metrology system. Although these resources cannot be provided by the OIML, the Organisation supports initiatives for the development of legal metrology. To highlight the importance of metrology activities in CEEMS, and to provide an incentive for their improvement, in 2009 the OIML established an Award for "Excellent achievements in legal metrology in Developing Countries".

Following the establishment of the Advisory Group on matters of Countries and Economies with Emerging Metrology Systems (CEEMS), and an increased focus on OIML activities in this area, it was decided to rename the Award the "OIML CEEMS Award" from 2018.

The OIML CEEMS Award is intended to raise the awareness of, and create a more favorable environment for legal metrology and to promote the work of the OIML. The Award intends: "to acknowledge and honour new and outstanding activities achieved by individuals national services or regional legal metrology organisations contributing significantly to legal metrology objectives on national or regional levels."

The BIML will prepare a list of candidates highlighting the importance of the achievements. The Award winner will be selected by the CIML President and announced at the 58th CIML Meeting in October 2023.

## The Award

The Award will consist of:

- a Certificate of Appreciation signed by the CIML President;

- a token of appreciation, such as an invitation to make a presentation of the Award-winning achievement at the next CIML Meeting or OIML Conference at the OIML's expense;

an engraved Award trophy.

## How can candidates be proposed?

Nominations may be made by CIML Members or OIML Corresponding Member Representatives via the OIML website https://www.oiml.org/en/myaccess/general-admin/award-nominations and must contain facts, documents and arguments explaining why the candidate deserves the Award. The closing date is 31 August 2023.

## Selection criteria and procedure

The criteria which will be used to assess the candidates' contribution or achievement will include:

- its significance and importance;

- its novelty;

![](page=6,bbox=[313, 2074, 555, 2309])

- its attractiveness and adaptability for other legal metrology services.

# Promotion of the OIML Bulletin: Become a Mentor

</div>

![](page=7,bbox=[104, 515, 396, 925])

![](page=7,bbox=[111, 2080, 395, 2464])

The OIML Bulletin is one, if not the only, international publication dedicated to legal metrology topics.

In accordance with CIML Resolutions 2019/30 and 2020/21, there is a clear desire for the Bulletin to be an attractive publication for legal metrology worldwide, and for it to be an excellent advertisement for our Organisation.

This can be achieved through long-term planning of the future editions and identification of key topics of high interest, for instance, legal control of measuring instruments in the fields of energy, health and the environment, where important aspects such as new technology, legal requirements, or test/verification procedures will be addressed.

In addition, support is sought from CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who are ready to take on the responsibility of acting as "Mentors" for certain key topics / editions and technical articles. These are not necessarily expected to be written by the "Mentors" themselves, but by experts that a "Mentor" has identified and contacted.

In order to identify key topics of significant interest and "Mentors" to lead them, it was proposed by the CIML President that the BIML prepares, and makes publicly available on the OIML website, a plan for the upcoming eight to ten editions of the Bulletin.

The table on the following page is intended to be "dynamic", i.e. proposed key topics may be moved to other editions depending on available "Mentors" and authors for technical articles. The table can also be found at www.oiml.org/en/publications/bulletin/ future-editions.

All CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives are encouraged to support the OIML Bulletin, to share their legal metrology experiences with the legal metrology community worldwide, and to take responsibility either as a "Mentor" for one of the next editions of the Bulletin, or by promoting it at TC/SC/Project Group meetings, RLMO meetings, CEEMS AG meetings, and other opportunities.

CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who would like to be a "Mentor" for a specific edition / key topic, or who would like to suggest that a new key topic be added to the list, are asked to contact the BIML (chris.pulham@oiml.org).

The Editors of the Bulletin encourage the submission of articles covering topics such as national, regional and international activities in legal metrology and related fields, evaluation procedures, accreditation and certification, and measuring techniques and instrumentation. - a titled, typed manuscript in Word or WordPerfect either on disk or (preferably) by e-mail;
