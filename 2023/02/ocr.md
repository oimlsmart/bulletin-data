Édition spéciale : Organisations Régionales de Métrologie Légale (RLMO)

Depuis 2007, l'OIML offre un forum aux représentants des six Organisations Régionales de Métrologie Légale (RLMO) afin qu'ils se réunissent pour présenter et discuter des développements récents et des activités les plus importantes au sein de leurs RLMO, qu'ils apprennent à se connaître et qu'ils explorent les possibilités de collaboration.

À l'origine, ce forum, appelé Table Ronde des Organisations Régionales de Métrologie, se déroulait uniquement en personne, pendant quelques heures chaque année, au cours de la même semaine que la réunion du CIML. Cependant, l'arrivée de la pandémie COVID-19 a entraîné le passage à un format virtuel, ce qui a eu l'effet très positif de conduire à une Table Ronde considérablement élargie, comprenant deux réunions intérimaires par an, en plus de la réunion annuelle. Je pense que malgré la pandémie qui a empêché les réunions en personne ces dernières années, le fait que notre présence en ligne ait augmenté a renforcé l'aspect collaboratif et a conduit à des échanges plus forts et encore plus fructueux, qui sont grandement appréciés par toutes les parties concernées.

Les RLMO sont indépendantes les unes des autres et de l'OIML, mais elles ont toutes pour objectif commun d'organiser et de promouvoir les activités et les pratiques de métrologie légale au sein des économies de leur région. Des informations sur les six RLMO sont disponibles sur le site web de l'OIML, y compris des liens vers les sites web individuels de chaque RLMO.

Bien que les RLMO aient décidé de conserver leur statut indépendant, elles ont toutes accepté que le BIML accueille les réunions des Tables Rondes sur Zoom, ainsi qu'une page spéciale sur le site web de l'OIML et un espace de travail dédié aux membres de la Table Ronde. Les ordres du jour élargis des réunions annuelles de la Table Ronde comprennent non seulement des rapports sur les activités des RLMO, mais aussi des mises à jour sur l'OIML-CS, les CEEMS, l'e-Learning et, plus récemment, le Groupe de Travail sur la Numérisation (OIML DTG) - le tout du point de vue des RLMO. En outre, des discussions en forum ouvert sont régulièrement organisées sur des thèmes centraux tels que les compteurs intelligents et la numérisation. Les EVSE viennent d'être ajoutés, et la sécurité du trafic routier suivra.

Cette édition d'avril 2023 du Bulletin de l'OIML contient des articles provenant de trois des RLMO, les articles des autres RLMO étant prévus dans des éditions ultérieures de cette année. J'apprécie le travail de tous les auteurs, ainsi que celui de Chris Pulham et de son équipe éditoriale au BIML, qui nous présentent le Bulletin de l'OIML chaque trimestre. C'est un véritable effort !

J'espère que vous prendrez plaisir à lire ces articles et à en apprendre davantage sur les RLMO et les activités dans leurs régions. J'ai eu le privilège de présider la Table Ronde RLMO depuis 2019, et je me réjouis de le voir continuer à se développer !

# Special edition: Regional Legal Metrology Organizations (RLMOs)

</div>

Since 2007, the OIML has provided a forum for representatives from the six Regional Legal Metrology Organizations (RLMOs) to come together to report on and discuss the most important recent developments and activities in their RLMOs, and to get to know each other and explore opportunities for collaboration.

The forum, called the RLMO Round Table (RT), was originally only in-person, for a couple of hours each year during the same week as the CIML meeting. However, the advent of the COVID-19 pandemic resulted in moving to a virtual format, which has had the very positive effect of leading to a greatly expanded RT, including two Interim RT meetings per year, in addition to the annual meeting. I feel that despite the pandemic preventing in-person meetings in recent years, the fact that our online presence has increased has fortified the collaborative aspect and has led to stronger and even more fruitful exchanges, which are greatly appreciated by all concerned.

The RLMOs are independent from each other and from the OIML, but all share a common purpose of organizing and promoting legal metrology activities and practices among the economies of their regions. Information about the six RLMOs can be found on the OIML website, including links to the individual websites of each RLMO.

While the RLMOs have decided to maintain their independent status, they nonetheless have all been quite willing to have the BIML host the RT meetings on Zoom, and to also host a special RLMO RT page on the OIML website and a dedicated PG Workspace for RT members. The expanded agendas for the annual RT meetings have included not only reports on RLMO activities, but updates on the OIML-CS, CEEMS, e-Learning and, most recently, the Digitalization Task Group (OIML DTG) - all from an RLMO perspective. In addition, Open Forum discussions are regularly held on Focus Topics that have included Smart Meters and also Digitalization. EVSEs have just been added, with Road Traffic Safety next.

This April 2023 edition of the OIML Bulletin provides articles from three of the RLMOs, with articles from the other RLMOs anticipated in later editions this year. I appreciate the work of all of the authors, as well as Chris Pulham and his editorial team at the BIML for their excellent job in bringing the OIML Bulletin to us each quarter. It is quite an effort!

I hope that you enjoy reading these articles and learning more about the RLMOs and the activities in their regions. It has been my privilege to chair the RLMO RT since 2019, and I look forward to seeing it continue to grow!

# The checkpoint model for a confident choice of sample size

</div>

DR MANUEL BASTUCK, Wipotec GmbH, Germany DR YOSHITADA TANAKA, AIST, Japan

## Abstract

All measuring instruments that are put on the market must comply with certain regulations. Arguably, the most important aspect of this is the precision and accuracy of the actual measurement result. Testing for compliance is often done by measuring known objects and, from the measurement error, determining whether all the relevant parameters are within the prescribed limits. The number of such measurements, although crucial to the confidence in the result, is often not given much thought. It is then either too high, wasting resources, or too low, lowering the confidence. In this paper we present the checkpoint model which, given a tolerable risk for false decisions, increases the sample size gradually just until the decision can be taken with enough confidence. Applied to the real-world data of checkweighers, the sample size can be reduced from 60 with the classical approach to as low as around 20 with the checkpoint model.

## Introduction

It is a basic principle in measurement technology that the confidence $ ^{1} $ in the result of a measurement increases with sample size. Therefore, the larger the sample size, the better - at least in theory. In reality, increasing the sample size often requires considerable resources such as time, money, or effort. Thus, a compromise between the sample size and the confidence in the result must be found.

The resources required to collect data are usually considerable for the person performing a test or an experiment. The statistical concept of "confidence", on the other hand, often feels much more abstract and like a sidenote, so that the sample size is "intuitively" chosen to "feel right" (Figure 1) without any statistical justification. In most cases, the chosen sample size will either be too small to reach an (implicitly) assumed confidence, prompting false decisions, or too large, wasting resources.

The problem of choosing a sample size is not new and there are many different recommendations depending on the problem at hand. This paper focuses on the special problem of testing whether a measurand (of a sample) exceeds a predefined limit. For this case, we propose an approach which will enable the examiner to choose just the right sample size depending on an easily understandable interpretation of confidence. Our approach, the next iteration of Tanaka and Tanaka (2021) [2], is first introduced theoretically, but with a strong focus on application instead of statistical theory. Afterwards, it is applied to the practical application of testing checkweighers for compliance with the wellknown OIML Recommendation R 51 Automatic catchweighing instruments. This serves as an example of a much wider field of possible applications, such as testing utility meters for electric energy, gas, water, or heating.

## Dynamic sample collection

Let us consider, without loss of generality, an instrument which shall not exceed a certain maximum permissible standard deviation $ ^{2} $ (MPSD, generally "error limit") of its measurement errors. For example, and for easier wording in the following, it could be an automatic weighing instrument which measures the mass of objects. Its true standard deviation $ \mathrm{(SD_{real})} $ is unknown, but we can estimate it. To do so, a certain number of objects n with known masses are measured, and the measurement errors are recorded as the difference between known and measured mass. Computing the standard deviation of this sample of size n provides an estimate $ \mathrm{SD_{meas}} $ for $ \mathrm{SD_{real}}. $

The "classical" procedure to test whether the scale's measurement errors are below the MPSD is as follows:

1) Take a sample of measurement errors of predefined size n,

2) compute its standard deviation as an estimate $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ for the true $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{real}} $

3) decide "fail" if $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} > \mathrm{MPSD} $ , "pass" otherwise.

Being predefined, it is unlikely that the sample size n is the best compromise between confidence in the result and resource usage. But what is the best compromise i.e. the best sample size, in this case?

To answer this question, consider the worst-case scenario. This is not a measuring instrument with an extremely large $ \mathrm{S D}_{\mathrm{r e a l}} $ but one with $ \mathrm{S D}_{\mathrm{r e a l}}= \mathrm{M P S D}. $ This instrument should theoretically "pass", but is infinitely close to the border of "failing", so that any small disturbance could change the decision. Hence, the number of false decisions in this worst-case scenario will always be close to 50 %. The risk of a false decision will be lower for any other instrument with $ \mathrm{S D}_{\mathrm{r e a l}} $ above or below MPSD as this makes the decision "clearer".

We take the worst-case scenario as the basis for a Monte Carlo simulation. This means that we use a computer to simulate, for example, 100 000 normally distributed samples with a given sample size. The normal distribution is chosen because that is how random measurement errors are distributed. The $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ is computed on these 100 000 samples as if they were real data, resulting in 100 000 possible outcomes of an actual measurement. Figure 2a shows that even though the $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{real}} $ was 1 for all samples, possible $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ range from 0.4 to 1.6 if the sample size was as low as 10. This kind of uncertainty decreases with larger sample sizes, but never becomes zero for a finite sample size. This is true for any measurement.

It is impossible to pick a finite value and tell with 100% confidence that we will never measure any value above or below it. However, we can determine a value below which only 1% of all measurement results are located. This value is the maximum of the 1% smallest values (1000 in a simulation of 100000) and shown in Figure 2a in blue. It is also known as the 1st percentile because 1% of the data are smaller than this value. The same can be done to determine the (red) 99th percentile. 1% of the data are larger than this value. Both percentage values can be arbitrarily chosen.

If our aim is to test whether the $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{real}} $ of an instrument is below the MPSD, we could interpret the blue 1st percentile as an "acceptance boundary": if the $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ is smaller than the acceptance boundary, the instrument is "passed". Similarly, if $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ is larger than the red, 99th percentile "rejection boundary", the instrument is "failed". Any $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ between these boundaries does not prompt a decision. This interval thus acts as a kind of "safety margin".

If a measured value falls within this safety margin, more samples need to be taken. This influences the next comparison in two ways. First, both the acceptance and the rejection boundary move closer to the MPSD, as determined by the percentiles based on the worst-case simulation. Second, $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ becomes a better estimate for $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{real}} $ . The acceptance boundary $ b_{acc} $ (n) and the rejection boundary $ b_{rej} $ (n) are functions of the sample size n, which cancels out the improving estimate and maintains the same confidence in the decision independent of the sample size.

The confidence in the decision can now be quantified with this model. In the worst case, 1 % of tests will produce a false decision because $ b_{acc} $ and $ b_{rej} $ have been chosen accordingly. That also means that we can be at least 99 % sure that our decision is correct. The assumption of the worst case is very strict and provides a lower limit for the confidence. The performance will be significantly better, although hard to quantify, for real instruments.

Summarized, our approach works as follows:

1) start with an empty sample;

2) add a number $ n_{p} $ of measurement errors to the sample, resulting in a total sample of size n;

3) compute its standard deviation as an estimate $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}} $ for the true $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{real}} $;

4) go back to step 2) if $ b_{acc}(n)\leq \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}}\leq b_{rej}(n); $

5) decide "fail" if $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}}>b_{rej}(n) $ , or "pass" if $ \mathrm{SD}_{\mathrm{meas}}<b_{acc}(n). $

Step 4 can be interpreted as a checkpoint for the current sample size which only allows a decision to be made if it can be made with sufficient confidence. Hence the name "checkpoint model".

We would currently recommend limiting the number of checkpoints to a "reasonable" amount, e.g., not more than one checkpoint every ten measurements. In principle, each checkpoint is a new test, and in statistics, testing too often or indiscriminately easily produces wrong results. The amount of dependence between two following tests is yet to be investigated, so the checkpoints should neither be considered fully dependent nor fully independent from each other.

Figure 2b shows an example of using the checkpoint model for testing a (simulated) instrument with an $ \mathrm{S D_{r e a l}} $ of 0.6 and a worst-case false decision risk of 1 %. In roughly 25 % of cases, this instrument can already be "passed" with a sample size of only 10. Two thirds of all cases have been decided at a sample size of 20, and over 90 % at a sample size of 30. The average sample size is

around 20 which is a large improvement over the classical approach with a sample size of 60 as currently used in [3]. Both approaches produce no false decisions in this relatively easy case.

Even with 60 samples, there is still a very small number of cases (invisible in Figure 2b) in the safety margin and not passing the checkpoint. Instead of increasing the sample size over and over, it is often useful in reality to implement a maximum permissible number of samples. Then the decision can either be a default, e.g. "fail due to insufficient confidence", or the decision can be made according to the classical approach. This is easily incorporated into the model by setting both boundaries at the maximum permissible number of samples equal to the MPSD. Obviously, the desired confidence is void in this case.

## Practical application: checkweigher testing

This work was motivated by a practical issue in the revision of OIML R51:2006 [3], which currently requires that checkweighers for loads below 1 kg shall be tested by measuring 60 known masses. The "mean error" (ME, computed as the absolute mean) and standard deviation of their measurement errors are computed and individually compared against the maximum permissible mean error (MPME) and the maximum permissible standard deviation (MPSD). If both the ME and the SD are below their individual limits, the instrument passes the test, otherwise it fails.

Collecting 60 measurements, even if one intuitively knows that the instrument "will obviously pass" after 20 measurements, wastes resources. One initial proposal was to reduce the required sample size to 30. However, this comes with a seriously increased risk of false decision, as discussed earlier. Instead, the checkpoint model has been developed and proposed as an alternative approach for the next edition of R 51. Discussions about this are still ongoing at the time of writing this article.

Checkweighers must pass two independent tests, one for the mean error and one for the standard deviation, in order to be considered acceptable. Combining the tests in this way makes it less likely to pass both tests at the same time as compared to passing either one of the individual tests. This will increase the sample size at decision, but also add another layer of safety to the final decision. Any decrease in sample size can save a lot of resources as this test is done for a checkweigher at four different loads and potentially at two different speeds multiplying resource savings by a factor of eight.

## Conclusion

Given the problem of testing whether a measured parameter exceeds a certain limit, we have developed a statistical model which iteratively increases the sample size until it can take this decision with enough confidence. Iteratively increasing the sample size ensures that no resources are wasted by taking too many measurements but, at the same time, that the confidence in the result is high enough.

We have validated the model with synthetic and real data and shown that its decisions are almost at par with an approach which makes its decision on the maximum sample size. However, it needs a fraction of the maximum sample size for this kind of performance.

The model is easy to use as it only compares the measured values with two boundaries (acceptance or rejection). These values can be tabulated as factors of the actual error limit as has been done as an example in Table 2. The Monte Carlo simulation used to compute these boundaries is easy to implement and flexible enough to incorporate influences such as outliers which would be difficult analytically.

The main task of GULFMET is to enhance cooperation with the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM), the International Organization of Legal Metrology (OIML), and other well-established relevant international and regional organizations to benefit from their experience. GULFMET recognizes the role of the OIML in promoting the global harmonization of legal metrology procedures to reduce trade barriers and costs in a global market and to promote mutual recognition.

GULFMET, as a Regional Metrology Organization (RMO) established under the auspices of the GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council) Standardization Organization (GSO), brings together National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) of the United Arab Emirates, the Kingdom of Bahrain, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, the Sultanate of Oman, the State of Qatar, and the State of Kuwait. GULFMET works closely with the OIML to promote legal metrology in the GCC Countries.

The GULFMET Certification Scheme (GCC-CS) is an initiative supported by the GCC Standardization Organization that aims to facilitate, accelerate, and harmonize the work of national authorities in the GCC that are responsible for the type evaluation and type approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. This initiative relies on the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS), and discussions are ongoing with the OIML experts to determine how the OIML-CS can support GULFMET in implementing the GCC-CS for measuring instruments.

GULFMET TC Legal has developed guidelines on implementing the GCC-CS; these guidelines were approved by the GSO Technical Council in February 2023.

This Scheme allows manufacturers of measuring instruments or their representatives (who must obtain a type approval certificate before placing their measuring instrument products on the GCC market) to benefit from this Scheme by submitting an application to any of the National Metrology Authorities (NMA) in member states to issue type approval certificates. The type approval certificate confirms that the measuring instrument fulfills the GSO technical regulations or the relevant OIML Recommendations. Following the type evaluation, type approval, and issuance of the certificate, the type approval certificate issued by the member state is accepted (recognized) by the other member states.

The Scheme also aims to facilitate the initial verification of measuring instruments subject to metrological control and can help promote the manufacture, marketing, and use of measuring instruments that comply with the requirements of Gulf standards and technical regulations.

# Smart meters: The Brazilian point of view of legal metrology

</div>

JUAN CARLOS MATEUS SANCHEZ Technology Researcher National Institute of Metrology, Quality and Technology-INMETRO, Brazil Direction of Legal Metrology/Sector of Electrical Quantities Measurements

## Abstract

This paper presents the regulation challenges faced by the Brazilian Direction of Legal Metrology (DIMEL) in the last 30 years since the first appearance of electronic meters in the country. The concept of smart meter generations is presented in order to properly classify the technology deployments and thus help to define the term "smart meter". For each generation of smart meters new challenges from the legal metrology point of view appeared; therefore, this paper also discusses the practical solutions implemented in Brazil in terms of test procedures and regulation requirements.

## 1 Introduction: Historical regulation background

## 1.1 Early years with only induction meters

Since 1990, Brazil introduced a reform in the electricity sector in which vertical integrated companies began to split out into at least four different companies with dedicated scopes according to the electricity business:

generation, transmission, distribution and commercialization. Competition was introduced for generation and commercialization companies, while a regulated monopoly was maintained in the transmission and distribution areas. The new Brazilian model created an electricity market, a national power system operator (ONS), an electricity commercialization chamber (CCEE), and a National Agency of Electrical Energy (Aneel).

In this context, the metrological control of electricity meters in Brazil was one of the oldest controls in the country. Our records show type approvals since 1966 [1] and, since then, more than 800 type approval "certificates" (called Portaria in Brazil) have been issued by INMETRO/DIMEL, the Legal Metrology (LM) authority in Brazil.

Figure 1 shows a timeline from 1966 to 2005 comparing the regulations released by INMETRO in Brazil with the corresponding international publications related to electricity meters. The upper part of the timeline shows the set of well-known international standards concerning electricity metering equipment [2] - [7] and OIML Recommendation OIML R 46 [8]. In the lower part of this figure, it is possible to see a summarized scope of the regulations released by INMETRO and the Brazilian regulation number is in square brackets [1], [9] - [12]. Although not all the legal acts are included in the timeline, the most important regulations were included for the sake of space.

The green symbol in Figure 1 indicates the point at which static (electronic) meters where introduced in Brazil. It is interesting to note that static meters began to be installed in the country before any regulation or standards had yet been published. This is a very common situation for LM authorities around the world because, on the one hand, it is important to define the legal requirements in order to ensure an appropriate level of credibility of measurement results [13]. On the other hand, the LM authority cannot be so inflexible to the point of preventing the introduction of new technologies or discourage innovative new approaches to measurement [14]. For these reasons, the type approval of electronic meters was established in 2002 [12].

## 1.2 The fast "smart meter" deployment

Figure 2 shows a timeline between 2006 and 2009. This period was particularly critical for our country because it began with the introduction of the first "smart meters" in Brazil. In fact, in 2006 a new automated meter management (AMM) system was deployed massively in the metropolitan area of Rio de Janeiro to solve the problem of high non-technical losses that reached 22 % of the total amount of energy bought by one of the Rio's utility companies. Although there is evidence that before 2006 "smart meters" could already have been installed in Brazil, this year is considered a milestone from which the regulation process began.

The companies concluded that energy theft was the main cause of such a large amount of losses, so they developed a new system where the electricity meters was located as far as possible from the consumers, on the top of the medium voltage network. Thus, users could not access the meter anymore (see Fig. 4). Moreover, tasks such as remote readings, disconnection of in-debt consumers, and reconnection of regular ones were done automatically without the need for the physical presence of technical staff.

With the rapid installation of the AMM systems and without any legal metrological control covering this type of instruments, consumer claims rose very quickly. In 2007, the Legislative Chamber of the State of Rio de Janeiro opened a hearing in order to demand information concerning Authorities' actions regarding users' complaints. Most of them questioned the AMM System's accuracy, blaming it for the increase in users' payments.

In September 2007, INMETRO released a regulation for the type approval of AMM Systems [15] and shortly thereafter the first regulation for electronic meters [16] and a second version for electromechanical meters [17] were released. Regulation [16] had been studied since 2004 and when it was released it was very innovative because it included in one single document the technical and metrological requirements for all the metrological control steps of active and reactive energy meters with

four accuracy classes: A (2 %), B (1 %), C (0.5 %) and D (0.2 %). Because no OIML Recommendation was available for electronic meters in 2007, the first edition of this regulation was based on the IEC standards series for metering equipment [2] - [5].

In addition, INMETRO decided to carry out an in-service verification by selecting a random sample of 1305 consumers being measured by the AMM System. The results were analyzed using statistical tools that allowed us to identify the relative importance of factors (e.g., manufacturer, number of phases, and location) which affected the percentage of in-field defective units. This analysis also allowed us to assess global and local uncertainty estimates for the installed meters. This study produced vital information needed to make important policy and regulatory decisions about the use of the AMM systems installed throughout the country. One of the most important findings was the need for software requirements which in 2009 lead us to release a regulation on this subject for AMM Systems [18]. Other important findings are described in Section 3.

## 2 Legal metrology aspects of smart meters in Brazil

## 2.1 Definition of "smart meter"

To properly discuss the legal metrology aspects for smart meters, the term itself must first be defined.

Because there is not a consensus on the definition of what a "smart meter" is, different developments can claim to be "smart", which in the end turns the term into a commercial one. From the legal metrology point of view, the term "smart" meter confuses the scope of regulations and consequently affects the procedures for type approval, initial verification, and subsequent verification.

In Brazil many discussions took place on whether the Brazilian AMM System can be considered a "smart meter" or just a "clever" one. Because of this, the author of this paper proposes a definition based on the degree of automation involved in the deployment called smart meter generations [24], [25]. In this definition, "smart" meters are classified into four generations:

The first generation is also known as Automated Meter Reading (AMR) and corresponds to the first deployments of "smart" meters. The main characteristic of the instruments of this generation is that measurement data flows in a unidirectional way i.e. the consumption information goes from the meter to the utility company through communication technologies (such as Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, optical fibers, RF or PLC), dispensing, or in some cases just reducing, the need for meter reading staff.

The second generation is called Automated Meter Management (AMM). In this generation, the instruments, besides enabling AMR, also receive commands from the billing center, allowing tasks such as the disconnection of in-debt customers and reconnection when the debt is paid. Therefore, the main feature of AMM systems is that data transits in a bidirectional way using a communications network also known as Advanced Meter Infrastructure (AMI).

The third generation corresponds to a better and efficient use of the AMI in which additional features, such as the application of multiple rates and/or prepayment schemes, can be implemented in a reliable and secure way. Meters of this generation comply with complex software requirements such as

data protection, software integrity and authenticity among many others as described in OIML D 31 [26]. Even though the metering system does not execute multiple rates or prepayment tasks, if it complies with the software requirements it is more difficult to mislead it (by the user and/or the utility company), in other words it is more reliable and consequently it is not just a "clever" meter; now it can be called "smart".

The fourth generation is the true smart meter in which the customer has access to advanced functionalities. For example, the user can obtain their load curve through the meter. It allows communications with home appliances through IoT technologies, making possible demand-side management for residential consumers. It enables measuring of residential demand (called "interval metering" by the authors in [27]) and service quality indexes. It is easy to verify/calibrate by the metrological authorities, and it performs all these functions in a safe and reliable way through the compliance of software requirements.

With the definitions above it is easier to define the proper metrological controls (i.e. type approval, verifications, sealing plan, etc.) for each smart meter generation. Observe that the metrological requirements could be the same for all generations, but the software requirements are not necessarily the same. Metrological control procedures could also be different for meters from the 1st and 4th generations.

The smart meter classification described above is not a broad concept applied in Brazil. It is simply a proposal to help regulators understand and prescribe correct regulation requirements and procedures.

## 2.2 Brazilian AMM system

Figure 4 shows a schematic of a node in the Brazilian AMM system. This figure shows a medium voltage (MV) network (1 kV $ \leq $ V $ \leq $ 100 kV) supplying electricity to a transformer that subsequently reduces to low voltage (LV) levels (V $ \leq $ 1 kV). From the transformer, compact concentric cables (anti-theft cables) distribute the electricity to the houses passing through Metering Boxes (MB), which contain from 9 to 12 single-phase measurement modules (MM). Poly-phase consumers can be measured using two or three MM, but up to the maximum MB capacity between 9 MM to 12 MM (depending on the manufacturer).

Each user's property has an Individual Reading Terminal (IRT) installed to display the total energy consumption registered by the respective MM (or group of MMs for poly-phase consumers). Depending on the manufacturer, communication between the MB and IRT can be through either wireless Radio Frequency (RF) or Power Line Carrier (PLC) technology. Communication between MB and the billing center happens in a hierarchical way due to the large amount of information

## 2.2.1 In-service verifications results

In order to evaluate the measurement error of the Brazilian AMM system, in 2007/08 a metrological sampling was carried out in a total population of almost 300 000 consumers being measured by the AMM system installed in five regions of the metropolitan area of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.

The study focused on existing class A AMM systems (2% nominal error). The metrological verification process consisted of co-installing a higher-accuracy INMETRO-approved reference meter (class D = 0.2 % nominal error) on the customer's property. In parallel, these meters measured the customer's consumption for a period of one month (the usual billing cycle in Brazil). The measurement error ( $ \varepsilon $ ) between the AMM system and the reference meters was computed as follows:

$$
\varepsilon [ \% ] = \frac {R ^ {A M M} - R ^ {I n m e t r o}}{R ^ {I n m e t r o}} \times 1 0 0
$$

Where $ R^{AMM} $ is the IRT reading in one consumer being measured by the AMM system and $ R^{Inmetro} $ is the reading in the reference meter installed by INMETRO. Since the AMM systems were designed to have a maximum error of $ \pm2\% $ in nominal conditions, then (allowing for an in-field expansion factor of 2) an AMM system was declared as "defective" if the measured error of a consumer exceeded $ \pm4\% $ ,therefore, as one AMM system can measure electric energy from multiple consumers distributed geographically, if only one consumer appears to have an error outside this range then that AMM system is deemed to be defective.

Because the population of consumers being measured by the AMM system in the Rio area was large (approximately 300 000 clients) and hence it was prohibitively expensive to carry out complete testing, the population was divided into two groups by the manufacturer and a sampling plan was constructed, yielding a sample size of 800 consumers being measuring by the AMM system from manufacturer A and 500 from manufacturer B. Moreover, to yield a representative sample, the sample was integrated

proportionately into the total population in each locations (five places), and phase configurations (three types) resulting in samples that were also proportionately unbalanced.

During the verification period, the following four issues affected the comparative readings:

Incorrect consumption displayed on a customer's IRT. This case happens when an IRT shows the reading not of the customer, but of their neighbor. Such an anomaly is due to an installation error. These cases were detected after the first month period of verification. When detected, the faulty reading was discarded, the mistake was corrected, and a new comparison cycle was initiated. This situation led INMETRO to include authenticity requirements in the software regulation.

Incorrect meter constant. This case happens when the constant that converts the measured energy into electronic pulses has an incorrect value. These cases show that this parameter might be changed by software in the field or from the billing center. The solution for this problem led INMETRO to include requirements and procedures in the regulation ensuring the integrity of the parameters. Additionally, it was required that the system firmware version be displayed on the customer's IRT.

Faulty communication between the MB and the IRT. Various factors such as location, obstacles, communication module damage or even IRT damage were found to potentially contribute to absent readings on the IRT. These cases posed a considerable problem when testing was carried out at the one-month mark. When these cases occurred, the immediate solution was to extract the information directly from the MB through utility intervention. Because the MB retains these data in its internal memory before sending the information to the billing central, these cases were not considered as defects during the verification. Observe that from the LM point of view, this case could be considered as a defect, since the only way to provide customers with their consumption information without the utility intervention is through the IRT.

Emergency intervention by the utility. Some differences between the reference meter readings and the IRT readings were due to a by-pass in the MM done by the utility in emergency situations. In such cases the reading was discarded, the by-pass was removed, and a new cycle of testing was started.

Furthermore, it was identified that when the supply voltage drops below 60 V, due for example to a single-phase fault, the AMM system did not turn off; instead, it lost its reference and measured the power supply's frequency as a load, producing very incorrect measure-

ments. To solve this issue, billing with the first version of the AMM system was stopped and a global substitution plan replacing AMM Systems with INMETRO's type approval was requested.

Statistical tools also were used in order to evaluate the in-field performance of the AMM systems by three factors: two manufacturers (two brands installed in the field); three possible connection types (single, two or three-phase) and five locations (municipalities inside the metropolitan region of Rio de Janeiro). In-depth results can be found in [29].

## 3 Lessons learned

The statistical analysis showed that most defective AMM systems were poly-phase systems, which led INMETRO in a second moment to suspend any billing from poly-phase AMM systems until a new verification could produce lower defective levels. These results allowed INMETRO to optimize the staff and logistics resources for the second in-field verification, also allowing the utility to correct all the problems rapidly and revert to issuing bills to poly-phase users through the AMM systems.

This section is dedicated to discussing some of the lessons learned with the in-field verification of the AMM system and some regulatory aspects that the authorities in other countries might consider during a massive AMM systems deployment. From the author's point of view the aspects detailed below are the most important issues to be considered by an authority dealing with the deployment of a new AMM system.

## 3.1 In-depth analysis of the AMM system architecture

Each particular AMM system deployment has its own architecture because it was designed as a solution to resolve regional utility problems. Therefore, the requirements for type approval should be defined by identifying the components that are legally relevant and by defining

the aspects of the metrological control after the type approval (sealing plan, initial verifications, and subsequent verifications). The use of smart meter classification presented in 2.1 can be useful.

One of the difficulties faced by INMETRO was the fact that the Brazilian AMM system is an electronic meter "dismembered" into the distribution network (today called multi-part meters). The traditional electronic meter includes the sensor, the indicator (display), and the data logger in a single box, which is not the case for the Brazilian AMM system. For example, the test output (for verification/calibration) is located in the top of the posts making it difficult to run any subsequent verification without utility intervention.

Another issue is the delivery point problem: in the traditional measurement system there is no difference between the measuring point (MP) and the delivery point (DP) of electricity. The MP is a physical place where the measurement sensors are located, i.e. where the meter is located. In Brazil, the DP is the lot's border where the client's facility begins, i.e. where the cables enter into a building. The definition of DP is needed to establish who is responsible for the facilities before and after this point. This responsibility includes the payment of losses, maintenance, and protection against tampering. Due to technical limitations, the MP and DP of energy are not always at the same point as shown in Figure 5.

Figure 5 shows the following three situations:

(a) DP and MP are at the same location: This is the traditional case in which the responsibilities are clearly defined for both the user and the provider.

(b) The DP is before the MP: This is the case for users connected at high voltage levels and they own a transmission line from their property boundary to a substation or transformer, where the measuring equipment is placed.

(c) The DP is after the MP: This is the case of the Brazilian AMM system, in which the measurement elements are out of the sight of users in order to prevent potential tampering. Recently, modern AMM solutions have moved to using low power instrument transformers (LPIT) in which situations (b) and (c) also happen.

Note that in situations (b) and (c), although the supplier's responsibility is defined by the location of the DP, a mechanism is needed to compensate losses between the DP and the MP. In the specific case of Brazilian AMMs, these losses are negligible from the consumer's point of view. But from the utility company's point of view, electricity thefts that can potentially occur between the MP and the DP could be simply ignored. Today, Aneel has resolved the losses problem through a compensation charge to be paid by the utility company, but no clear solution was given for energy deviations among consumers; therefore, this matter is a subject for future research.

The first versions of the AMM system had no software separation between the legally relevant parts and the non-relevant parts, making software evaluation a difficult task. INMETRO spent too much time understanding software requirements and developing assessment procedures which include documentary evidence and source code evaluation.

Although many OIML D31 requirements are very subjective, INMETRO successfully unified the acceptable solutions among our software analysts and accredited third-party laboratories to carry out the software assessment. Even though Brazilian manufactures reluctantly accepted software evaluation, now they recognize that these requirements are helpful in designing better metering projects.

## 3.2 Ensure availability of in-home displays

The first version of the Brazilian AMM system had a collective terminal to display the MM readings. This situation made it difficult to verify whether the record on the invoice corresponds to the actual consumer's load, and also made impossible for users to monitor their consumption or verify if there was an error in the system such as that described in 2.2.

Some utilities argued that the IRT was useless because most users do not check it often and rarely read it, or do not even know where to read the measurement. They claimed that the IRT is just a means to view the measurement and that a damaged IRT does not involve loss of measurement data, and if the client wishes to verify their consumption they just have to call the billing center or even display the measurement results on their smartphone.

From the author's point of view such arguments are unacceptable because they ignore basic legal metrology principles of publication of the measurement results and also ignore the reality of remote and poor areas in Brazil. Therefore, after several discussions, INMETRO determined that the IRT should be installed on all the existing installed AMM systems, confirming the importance of the possibility to read individual terminals as being a relevant element of the AMM system.

INMETRO also carried out another rapid inspection only in IRT terminals, to determine the reading availability. Results showed that 68 % of the IRT made the reading available while 32 % did not. The identified problems were due to communication failures (22 %) and IRT damage (10 %). Among the available readings INMETRO identified that 38 % of the inspected IRTs do not display the date and hour of the last reading and only 30 % comply with all the desired requirements. The utility company promptly resolved all of these nonconformities.

Another discussion concerned the information that these devices should present to the user. In Brazil the following requirements are mandatory for IRTs:

to display the firmware version;

to show the date and the time of the reading on the display;

have a resolution of 1 kW.h; and

to refresh the reading at each 1 kWh consumed by the user.

INMETRO did not find any prescription or requirement in the applicable international standards about information that smart meters have to display; therefore, we strongly recommend that regulators consider it.

In general, the amount of information to be displayed by the IRT can become a limiting factor that increases the system cost. For example, INMETRO suggested that the IRT comes with the test output (the blinking calibration LED); this feature would be particularly useful for subsequent verifications or when the client wishes to see if there are any energy leaks in their installation. Today, none of the manufacturers have provided such desired a feature because this would increase the cost of manufacturing the AMM too much. In any case, the author would like to draw the attention of legal metrology authorities regarding the role of in-home displays in subsequent verifications.

## 3.3 Multi-rate meters case

As mentioned in 1.3, in 2010 Aneel was determined to establish multiple electricity rates for residential users (called "white tariff" in Brazil); therefore, from the legal metrology point of view INMETRO identified a new magnitude to be controlled: the time accuracy. First, the timekeeping capability of the electricity meter had to be evaluated for type approval in order to ascertain that it applies the correct tariff at the legal time and also the internal registers have to be reliable enough to not create distrust among the users.

The first version of multi-rate regulation had identified the following timekeeping requirements from the IEC 62052-21 tariff and load control standard [6] and prepayment systems [7]:

Clock accuracy:0.5 s/24 h or $ \pm 5. 7 8 $ $ \mu s / s $;

- Clock accuracy with operative reserve: 1 s/24 h or $ \pm11.57\ \mu s/s $;

Clock accuracy under temperature influence: $ \pm0.15 \mathrm{~s /^{\circ} C} $ in each 24 h;

Clock accuracy under electromagnetic disturbances: 0.5 s/24 h or $ \pm 5. 7 8 $ $ \mu s / s $ .

In 2013, laboratories which performed tests according to IEC 62052-21 took more than 30 days to carry out the clock accuracy test, which made it too expensive. In such a scenario, the challenge was to develop the procedures to carry out the tests with a shorter test time and acceptable uncertainty.

Many discussions took place in order to decide how the tests would be carried out and how to obtain the signal from meter's internal time base. It was also discussed whether verifying the internal time base frequency is enough to evaluate the time accuracy of the meter. Arguments against the real need for these tests were raised by manufacturers who claimed that smart meters can accurately compute the time via the internet or through a periodical connection to a high-accuracy clock, but the fact is that in Brazil not all areas of the country have a good connection to the internet. Besides that, even in large Brazilian cities internet connection still sometimes fails, so INMETRO concluded that the product has to comply with the timekeeping requirements even without synchronization with an external clock. In [27], additional discussion about time accuracy is presented for smart meters with a demand measurement feature (called "interval metering"); this shows the importance of timekeeping accuracy for stand-alone deployments.

Actually, for electronic meters these tests are very difficult to perform, firstly because the time interval to obtain the meter's clock accuracy is at least 24 h. Secondly, static meters are integrating instruments, which means that no significant fault shall occur during disturbances. Therefore, evaluating the clock accuracy during an EMC test, which typically takes 20 to 30 minutes, is also very difficult. Thirdly, most commercial meters in Brazil at that time were not equipped for time accuracy testing (i.e. they did not have any output to synchronize them with an external reference clock).

To resolve these issues, INMETRO developed a methodology to obtain the meter's clock accuracy in short time intervals, without opening the meter and totally based on the time displayed by the meter. The manufacturers implemented a test function in the meter's firmware so that, when enabled, the following tasks happen:

according to the meter's time indication, it shall emit light pulses periodically with a programmable time interval. The pulses could be emitted at the optical test output (used for energy calibrations) or at any other place in the meter such as the optical communication port. An electric output signal is not allowed because it can be susceptible during EMC testing;

while the function is enabled, the energy measurement cannot stop and shall be recorded. If a manufacturer opts to use the test output to emit the time pulses, the metrological energy pulses cannot interfere with the time pulses; and

if power faults happen, when the meter re-starts the function shall be restored. Of course, it is admissible that meters turn off, however, the time indication and pulses shall return when the power supply is restored.

The development of this function was a key factor to correctly test multi-rate meters in Brazil. It also can be used during subsequent verification and currently is a helpful tool to implement the initial verification of multi-rate meter batches. In-depth details about the performance results of the timekeeping function during several EMC immunity tests were presented in [30].

Until now, INMETRO has not received any claim from consumers regarding the rates charged by the utility companies using meters approved by INMETRO. Because the "white tariff" is optional since it was released in 2018, consumers' adoption has slowly grown. Data from September 2021 reports that more than 64000 consumers have adopted the "white tariff" scheme in Brazil. Nevertheless, INMETRO is proud to have contributed to offering Brazilian consumers different ways to optimize their own consumption.

## 4 Conclusions

Thirty years of legal metrological control of electricity meters in Brazil have been summarized in this paper. A timeline was presented that describes the main advances in regulation in Brazil compared with international standardization, in order to provide an overview of the smart meters deployment in the country. The challenges faced in carrying out type approval of smart meters was presented, showing that it is possible to clearly define what a smart meter is by classifying it into four different generations according to the degree of advanced metering functionalities. This classification demonstrated that it would be useful to define metrological control and prescribe better requirements in the applicable regulations.

A summarized case study of the Brazilian Automated Meter Management System (AMM system) was presented in order to illustrate the different aspects that have to be taken into account when regulating smart meters. Some polemic issues were briefly discussed in order to help readers to form their own opinions.

Finally, INMETRO's experience in regulating the metrological control of multi-rate meters and its particularly difficult way of timekeeping device testing was shared. A time test function requested by INMETRO was developed by Brazilian manufacturers in order to resolve testing difficulties. It proved to be useful to conduct the time accuracy tests even during EMC immunity tests.

There are still some regulatory challenges such as street lighting energy meters, electric vehicle charging stations and demand measurements, which we are currently working on. Nevertheless, all the actions taken by INMETRO have demonstrated that the fairness in the sale of electrical energy for both the consumers and the utility companies has been improved. Consumer confidence in smart meters approved by INMETRO also increased, while the possibility of fraud diminished.

## Brazil

Brazil has 202 768 562 inhabitants (IBGE, 2015) and covers a surface of 8 515 767 km $ ^{2} $ , which makes it the fifth largest country in the world. This makes metrological control a considerable challenge in a territory of such magnitude. The Brazilian National Metrology Institute, Quality and Technology (INMETRO) through its Legal Metrology Direction (DIMEL) is in charge of legal metrology work.

It can be said that metrological control in Brazil began with the country's adhesion to the OIML in 1984 although the first legal metrology regulations are from the Brazilian Empire age (XIX century). As of today, regulations have been issued for 27 different types of measuring instruments, including for example nonautomatic weighing instruments (NAWI), liquid fuel dispensers, evidential breath analyzers (EBA), public services meters, speed meters, and taximeters. There is a complete regulatory process which includes a study of the impact of regulation on society. The requests received from different actors (such as industry, government entities or consumers), and the Regulatory Impact Analysis, are decisive in defining the types of measuring instruments that are regulated in the Brazilian territory. For each regulated instrument, the conditions for carrying out type approval tests and subsequent verifications are established by regulation in order to allow the Brazilian Network of Legal Metrology and Quality (RBMLQ-I) to perform the relevant metrological control (A. Soratto, 2014).

Brazil has been a Member State of the OIML since 1989, however it does not accept certifications issued under the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS); all instrument models used in Brazil must be directly tested by INMETRO (INMETRO, 2022). In relation to the sanctioning power that Brazil has, offenders against the regulations can be punished in many ways including warnings, fines, interdiction, apprehension, destruction and cancellation of type approvals, among others. ## Ecuador

In 2001, the Ecuadorian population was estimated at 12156608 inhabitants (Republica del Ecuador, 2001). The World Bank estimated that by 2021 it had reached 17797737 inhabitants (Banco Mundial, 2023). This is about a third of the Colombian population and about three times the Costa Rican population. For the metrological control of the instruments in the Ecuadorian territory, there are eight technicians who cover the entire territory of the country.

Unlike the previous countries presented above, Ecuador only started its metrological control activities in 2015, initially in the main cities of the country and progressively covering the rest of the territory. The competent entity in matters related to legal metrology is the Ecuadorian Standardization Service (INEN). Currently, the INEN has the following regulations:

NAWI up to 200 kg, since 2015;

quantity of product in prepackages, since 2016;

- taximeter model approval (through documentary approval), since 2016;

type approval of equipment for the measurement of the speed of vehicles (through documentary approval), since 2018; and

gas analyzers, opacimeters, sound level meters (through documentary approval), since 2018.

INEN also plans to regulate moisture meters for grains and truck scales, within the framework of a project to strengthen the Ecuadorian quality infrastructure, with the support of the South Korean government (Calle & Calderon, 2022).

As a Corresponding Member of the OIML, since 2017, Ecuador accepts certifications issued under the OIML-CS. For some instruments for which approvals are issued, they are documentary since the measuring instruments are not tested in the Ecuadorian territory. Surveillance activities take place in the market and there is no border control for instruments imported into the country.

Regarding the sanctioning power, the fines for noncompliance with the metrological regulation can amount to up to 34000 USD (Presidencia de la (Republica del Ecuador, 2001). Among the needs identified for an optimal development of legal metrology is the increase of competent and trained personnel, added to the implementation of a more robust quality infrastructure within the country.

## SIM

Of the 33 countries that comprise the SIM region, only five are OIML Member States (Brazil, Canada Colombia, Cuba, and the USA). Having only three Latin American countries as OIML Member States has worked against the SIM region; supporting this is the case of OIML R 126, which considers the operating conditions for EBA and which states that the atmospheric pressures for correct operation are between 860 hPa and 1060 hPa (OIML, 2021). The capital cities of three South American countries (Bogotá in Colombia, La Paz in Bolivia and Quito in Ecuador) and many other intermediate cities in the region are located at heights above sea level with atmospheric pressures below 860 hPa, which would classify them as allegedly "inappropriate" for the correct functioning of the EBA covered in OIML R 126.

In most countries, with some exceptions such as Brazil and Mexico, certifications issued under the OIML-CS are accepted to demonstrate compliance. However, there is not a single OIML-CS OIML Issuing Authority in the region, which forces measuring instrument producers to look for OIML Issuing Authorities in Europe or Asia (OIML-CS, 2023).

The type of instrument that most demonstrates compliance with OIML-CS certifications in the region is NAWI followed by fuel dispensers, since these two are mostly regulated in the region and the corresponding OIML Recommendations are equivalent in most of the countries.

Countries including Guatemala, Guyana, Ecuador, and Costa Rica are carrying out work to update their regulations and train their personnel, with their own resources or those of third parties that promote the strengthening of the quality infrastructure in the region. The main needs associated with training and regulation are in relation to electric energy meters, which goes hand in hand with the widespread interest that is beginning to exist among countries with the regulation of EVSEs (Ehrlich, 2023).

## Conclusions

In Latin America, the culture of compliance is not as deeply rooted as it is in other regions of the world such as Europe. For this reason, it is not only important to regulate the legal metrology requirements in each country, but it is also crucial to establish a sanctioning regime. Border controls, with their respective technological tools, accompanied by robust regulations, are decisive in avoiding or reducing the entry of low quality measuring instruments into countries.

The development of each country in terms of legal metrology and in general of the quality infrastructure are related to the initiation of activities and regulation in this sense. However, in the case of the SIM region, the size of each economy (closely related to the number of inhabitants) plays a fundamental role for investment.

The work groups for the creation of transversal and unified regulations for several countries have proven to

be successful in other latitudes, such as in the European Union. However, they are not common in the SIM region and in general they do not have good results since issuing the regulation, modifying it or updating it, results in a traumatic process that consumes large amounts of time and resources for each economy.

Databases with information related to legal metrology, such as metrological verifications carried out and to be carried out, approved models, certifications, among others, are essential to make more effective controls. Countries that do not have these types of tools should prioritize their development, while countries that already have them should make an effort to analyze these databases in order to optimize their surveillance process.

Developing the legislative and institutional framework in a country with an emerging economy is not an easy task. Defining the scope of the metrological control body and the way in which it will relate to the other actors in the quality infrastructure is essential. Poor planning can restrict the scope of surveillance, make it dependent on other bodies to have a regulated framework or prevent a timely update of the standards used in metrological control.

It is a priority for the SIM region to have a greater number of full members (i.e. Member States as opposed

to Corresponding Members) in the OIML, as well as to interact with the different OIML Technical Committees and Project Groups that are of interest to each country. This will help draft new Recommendations and ensure that updates to existing Recommendations fit the reality and the needs of the region.

It has been seen that the countries in the SIM region have disparate economies, large technological development gaps between them, and also complex geographies. These characteristics have represented great challenges for the various legal metrology bodies; a solution path that has had good results with large coverage of territory and representative numbers of verifications, could be the figure of MVBs.

It is necessary to close the gaps that exist between countries, with support from the economies inside the region. An important obstacle to overcome is the bureaucracy of issuing legal metrology laws and the difficulty that some political sectors have in perceiving the importance of legal metrology in the economy of a country. Once the gaps between the various countries are overcome, it may be possible to think of unified databases, unified standards and everything else that could lead the SIM region into a digitized era, with interaction between countries, and their legal metrology bodies, within the framework of Industry 4.0.

## 1 Introduction

According to OIML R 87 Quantity of product in prepackages, a prepackaged product (also called prepackaged goods) is defined as:

A single item for presentation as such to a consumer, consisting of a product and its packing material, made up before being offered for sale and in which the quantity of the product has a predetermined value, whether the packing material encloses the product completely or only partially, but in any case in such a way that the actual quantity of product cannot be altered without the packing material either being opened or undergoing a perceptible modification [1].

Therefore, control of their net content is an important role of the legal metrology authority to protect consumers.

## 2 Development of the online trade of prepackaged goods

Over the last few decades, trade of goods has moved from a form of bulk commodities to prepackaged goods. Nowadays, approximately 95 percent of all goods sold in the market are prepackaged goods. Prepackaged goods

traded all over the world are estimated to account for more than 75 percent of the total value of all traded commodities [2]. In 2020, the COVID-19 outbreak had a big impact not only on daily life but also on commerce, i.e. from face-to-face transactions to commerce using online technologies and delivery systems. Distribution of prepackaged goods has significantly increased, especially through e-commerce. Prepackaged goods are easier to distribute across local regions, and also within countries. It is difficult to block the flow of prepackaged goods around the world. As part of consumer-packaged goods (CPG), more economies have expanded their market for prepackaged goods from local areas to the global market. McKinsey Global Institute reported that some economies within the Asia-Pacific Region had concerns about putting their products onto the global market, as shown in Figure 1 [3]. Asian countries such as Indonesia and Thailand are moving their packaged foods onto the global market, while other countries still focus on local markets. In line with this, the World Bank noted in 2021 that within the ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) countries, for instance, the exported value of selected prepackaged goods would reach USD 11.9 billion.

Interestingly, demand for prepackaged goods has tended to significantly increase in the aftermath of COVID-19. The pandemic altered consumers' behavior. Digitalization in the economy has also driven the market to shift from conventional in-store shopping to e-commerce. Some global companies reported in 2022 that in the South-East Asian Region, e-commerce was persistently growing despite the resumption of in-store transactions after the peak of the pandemic as shown in Figure 2 [4]. In fact, online retail channels are still gaining ground even if consumers have partially moved back to in-store shopping [4]. As for household goods, during the pandemic years the global market witnessed significant demand that increased almost 15 percent in 2021 compared to the quantity in 2020. Regarding the packaged foods and beverages sectors, the 2021 performance in the global market was generally increasing with modest increases in the production of rice, pasta, flour, coffee, milk, yogurt, butter and cheese. However, not all commodities showed a positive trend during the pandemic years. Sub-sectors for packaged water and soft drinks were undoubtedly impacted by a decrease in demand.

Interestingly, the global companies reported that about 40 percent of South-East Asian consumers care about sustainability issues when they purchase foods through e-commerce in the form of prepackaged goods as shown in Figure 3 [4]. Sustainability in the global environment is taken into consideration in the materials, methods and procedures employed for the production, packaging and transportation of the products.

Prepackaged goods are considered to provide confidence in availability due to their long shelf-life, and high quality at a relatively reasonable price guaranteed by the manufacturer. These positive factors will potentially lead to the growth of the prepackaged goods market. For instance, prepackaged foods such as frozen food, canned food, cereals, instant noodles, and others which are sealed in a box, bag, can or another container, are easily transported to the global marketplace and can be stored over a sufficiently long period of time. For environmental issues, consumers can also make purchases that best align with their values such as a preference for green and sustainable products.

However, although 40 percent of consumers care about sustainability, only 3-15 percent actually act on their intentions by purchasing prepackaged goods including foods which are considered to be sustainable (Figure 3). One reason of this behavior is the fact that consumers generally have insufficient information about the available sustainable options when they purchase prepackaged goods. In addition, they do not necessarily trust the sustainability claims provided by the manufacturer when they select the foods.

However, in local and international trading, some prepackaged goods are often rejected due to a nonconformity in the indicated net quantity. This is referred to as "short weight", where the actual net content is below the predetermined amount (declared net content minus an allowance), which leads to a non-conformity with the labelling. The main reasons for this are the limitations of government resources to control a wide range of prepackaged goods produced by a large number of packers operating both domestically and internationally. Hence, legal metrology authorities play a key role to control and regulate prepackaged goods in term of conformity with the quantity requirements. The scheme for regulating prepackaged goods around the

world is generally based on relevant domestic and international standards which are represented by OIML R 87 Quantity of product in prepackages [1] and OIML R 79 Labeling requirements for prepackages [5]. It is clear that regulations, or the development of a legal metrological control for prepackaged goods, aim not only at increasing consumer protection but also at establishing fair trade among the operators in the economy and the provision of value-added products [6, 7].

## 3 Control of prepackaged goods within the Asia-Pacific Region

Legal metrological control systems for prepackaged goods employed by the economies within the AsiaPacific Region are mostly based on the 2004 edition of OIML R 87. Some are aligned with the 2016 edition.

In order to ensure that prepackaged goods are aligned with the technical regulations (both labelling and quantity requirements), most legal metrology authorities within the Asia-Pacific Region conduct market surveillance (Figure 4). Metrological supervision of prepackaged goods is a control exercised over the manufactures or packers, and is performed in order to check the correctness of the net quantities contained in prepackages based on the laws and regulations on metrology [8,9]. Traditional metrological supervision is conducted based on both complaints from consumers and regular inspections in the market. Prepackaged goods are also inspected by inspectors at warehouses, factories, wholesalers, and retailers. If a non-conformity is found among the inspected products, the inspectors may trace back to the manufacturer or packer. In some cases of imported goods, the associated legal metrology authorities take collaborative action with the customs authority in order to control the prepackaged goods under cross border issues.

In the economies in this Region, there is not much statistical information related to the outcomes of inspection of prepackaged goods. However, major nonconformities are associated with either the labelling requirement in terms of the font size of the quantity indication, or a short weight below the declared net quantity. In Viet Nam and the Republic of Korea, for instance, there is a scheme where manufacturers or packers can declare a conformity and apply the quantity certification mark to prepackaged goods in certain categories. The quantity certification systems have the following objectives:

establishing rules and procedures for fostering confidence that the labeling and net quantity of prepackages comply with all the relevant legal requirements;

promoting the efficiency of the control of prepackages whilst maintaining confidence in the net contents and facilitating fair trade of prepackaged products; and

promoting the harmonization, uniform interpretation and implementation of legal metrological requirements for net quantities, labelling requirements and production systems for prepackages.

However, the actual application of a quantity certification system within the Asia-Pacific Region is challenging due to the fact that prepackages are produced by a number of micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs) in developing countries across the Region. This system therefore needs further consideration not only for providing consumer protection but also for increasing the values of the MSME's products in the market by assisting their businesses to overcome the current limitations.

## 4 Challenges faced by Legal Metrology Authorities concerning prepackaged goods

There are challenges in establishing a sound metrological control system for prepackaged goods. In this process, the lack of awareness among business sectors and consumers is the biggest challenge. Quantity labelling is not a priority aspect when consumers buy goods, compared with their price and quality. Consumers tend to consider the price as the highest priority when they select goods, followed by the quality. Quantity labelling attracts less attention and this means that consumers are less aware of the quantity. This tendency could be exploited by businesses (e.g. manufacturers or packers) which also do not pay attention to the quantity. However, many large companies cover this issue on quantity in a product innovation context by producing a

wide variety of packaged products which are intended to avoid any misinterpretation by consumers due to a small font size used for the net content on the label, for example.

These challenges are also faced by the economies in the Asia-Pacific Region. Due to resource limitations, the public relations role of the government in some economies has not yet covered a wide enough range of communities of businesses and consumers. Thus, educational programs and social campaign initiatives may sometimes not be organized systematically. The government still remains at the phase of exercising metrological control over the prepackaged goods on the market. Traditionally, the metrological control of prepackaged goods in most economies within the Asia-Pacific Region is carried out through a metrological supervision scheme. This scheme includes pre- and postmarket surveillance as illustrated in Figure 4 and references [6,8,9].

As an example, in the case of Indonesia there are more than 5 500 packers of "big companies" and more than 65 million micro, small and middle enterprises (MSMEs) as potential targets for the surveillance of prepackaged goods. Regular inspections are performed by central and local governments to ensure the correctness of the descriptions, including the net quantity, declared on the label. Some priority commodities such as rice, drinking water, milk, tea, sugar, cooking oil, and cooking gas are subject to regular annual inspection. Major challenges in controlling prepackaged goods are the labelling issues, and also the promotion of national regulations on prepackaged goods to society and to business entities, including MSMEs.

Indonesia also has concerns in promoting prepackaged goods to increase consumer awareness. Various activities aim to educate consumers and businesses, especially MSMEs, and help them to understand the requirements for prepackaged goods. As a result, a consumer survey conducted by the Ministry of Trade in 2022 showed that 59.9 percent of the respondents checked the information on the label concerning net quantity at the point of sale. The survey indicated various reasons for checking, such as ensuring the correct net content (59.3 percent), the required content (15.5 percent), and comparing with other similar products (1.6 percent). The survey also indicated that 85.8 percent of the respondents trusted the correctness of the actual net contents of the products with respect to the nominal quantities declared on the labels. About 86.2 percent of them also had confidence because the product had been manufactured by a trusted business entity. Some educational materials for consumers are presented in Figure 6.

## 5 Asia-Pacific Legal Metrology Forum's strategies under the Working Group on Goods Packaged by Measure

Vast quantities of goods are traded in the form of prepackages and accordingly, their metrological control becomes an essential part of a viable control system for the economies within the APLMF. This Forum, through its Working Group on Goods Packaged by Measure, has

strategies for promoting the harmonization, uniform interpretation and implementation of the legal metrological requirements for the quantities of products in prepackages including labelling requirements and production systems. These strategies are summarized below:

i) facilitation of the removal of technical barriers to trade through harmonization of the requirements for prepackaged goods;

ii) improvement of the legal metrological control system for prepackaged goods through training and capacity-building programs to strengthen the competence of the officers, inspectors and technicians of the legal metrology authorities;

iii) understanding and practical implementation of the OIML Recommendations and Guides regarding prepackaged goods; and

iv) collaboration for delivering the necessary information on prepackaged goods to local stakeholders, e.g., industry and businesses with a special focus on MSMEs.

The APLMF Working Group on Goods Packaged by Measure has conducted many training courses in this field (Figure 7). It is also developing an e-Learning module based on OIML R 87 and R 79. The contents of this e-Learning module were drawn from practical training experiences, including textbooks. The module is developed to be used by the APLMF economies for conducting online training courses and/or allowing selflearning to refresh the competencies of the legal metrology authorities.

## 6 Closing remarks

The metrological control system of prepackaged goods aims to protect consumers regarding the net quantity of products, and also helps international trade of prepackaged goods through maintaining confidence in the goods. Controlling prepackaged goods is not only a legal requirement for the packers, but it also helps consumers by enabling informed decisions to be made when purchasing prepackaged products based on the reliability of the declared net quantity. Therefore, governments, businesses, and consumers should work together to improve the metrological control system on prepackaged goods.

# Legal aspects of measurement traceability and some national practices in the Russian Federation

</div>

L.K. ISAEV and A.Yu. KUZIN VNIIMS, Russian Federation

## 1 Abstract

The authors of this article, who have been involved in legal metrology for many years, provide details of the legal aspects of metrological traceability and describe the background to this issue and its actual use these days. The development of legal aspects of metrological traceability at national level is related to the development of international legal metrology based on the activities of the OIML. Some advantages and disadvantages of establishing metrological traceability through verification and calibration are shown. From the point of view of legal metrology in regulated activities such as trade, health care, environmental monitoring, etc., the case with verification is preferable. In the authors' opinion, the worldwide metrological infrastructure created by the OIML provides the possibility of using both verification and calibration for metrological traceability.

## 2 Introduction

The need to emphasize the role of the State in socially significant areas such as trade, health, safety and environmental protection led to the establishment in 1955 of an intergovernmental organization, the International Organization of Legal Metrology (OIML). All measuring instruments related to these areas were subject to tests for the purpose of type approval and subsequent regular verification, i.e., state metrological control, which provided the "reference" to the national measurement standards of units.

accompanied by intense competition between manufacturing companies, and verification, as a function of public administration, began to act as a brake. However, the State was not inclined to interfere in these market relations, therefore, in order to promote the development and improvement of measuring equipment and to encourage competition, the State was forced to mandate part of its verification function to non-state organizations, while maintaining them under rigid state control. In those days most countries at the legislative level began narrowing down the nomenclature of regulated measuring instruments, leaving the rest of the fleet of measuring instruments to another form of "reference": calibration. As an alternative to verification, in the sense of voluntary activities, calibration turned out to be beneficial to manufacturing companies, allowing them to quickly obtain the required certificates of conformity of the metrological characteristics of the devices produced. Calibration soon became the dominant type of certification of measuring equipment, and providing links of the measurement results in the legislative spheres, i.e. the traceability link to national measurement standards, began to be provided by both verification and calibration.

At the turn of the 1950s and into the early 1960s, there was a growing development worldwide of measurement methods, and many new and different kinds of measuring instruments (MIs) were developed. This was

However, matters were different in Russia. Traceability was ensured through the linking of measurement results to the State measurement standards through verifications performed according to the State verification schemes, which contain the information on the ranges and accuracy characteristics that could be verified in the measuring instruments, the metrological characteristics of the measurement standards at each level of the transfer of a quantity value, as well as methods for transferring a quantity value. It should be noted that all the measuring instrument verification procedures (certification or attestation of measurement standards) at all stages of the verification scheme are tested and checked during the testing of measuring instruments or certification of measurement standards. The traceability of the measurement standards involved in the verification schemes is checked when undergoing approval by the State research metrology institute, the holder of the State primary measurement standard. All verification procedures are approved by the authorized governmental body, Rosstandart. They are published and are publicly available in the Federal Information Fund. Subsequent to their approval, the schemes can be used by any of the verification laboratories, thus undergoing numerous testing in practice, which is a good point. For most commonly used measuring instruments, the verification procedure is tested many times by different laboratories. And besides if any errors are detected in them (which sometimes may occur), this becomes known immediately to the authorized State body. It is also worth noting that the verification procedures are closely regulated by the State organs in

respect of amending them, for example, when a new measurement standard or a more efficient method for the transferring of quantity values is developed. The submission of a traceability scheme to prove traceability through verification is not required in the Russian Federation. It is sufficient to provide an indication in the documents of the appropriate State verification scheme.

Thus, since the times of Dmitri Mendeleyev $ ^{1} $ traceability was realized in Russia through linking to measurement standards and, accordingly, through verification schemes. Any measuring instruments not covered by the agreed nomenclature of the measuring instruments were subject to departmental verification.

This radically distinguishes traceability established through verification from that established through calibration.

From the 1960s onwards, the transfer of reference values or measurement results took place in the country only through verification either state or departmental verification- either state or departmental verification to which all operated measuring instruments were subjected under the State System for Assurance of Measurement Uniformity (SSM). The SSM was introduced into metrology practice as a system in the late 1960s, and in the early 1970s the regulatory part of the System became a part of the State Standardization System (SSS). Calibration, as we know it today, was not carried out in the same way in those days, because calibration was then understood to mean "calibration of a measure" (or a set of measures) such as verification of a measure (or a set of measures) by aggregate measurements. This definition was published in document GOST 16263-70 "Metrology. Terms and Definitions", para 11.23. Another definition of calibration was given in 1993, in the Law of the Russian Federation "On Assurance of Measurement Uniformity" [1], which read: "a set of operations performed to determine and confirm the actual values of metrological characteristics and (or) suitability for use of a measuring instrument that is not subject to state metrological control and supervision". As can be seen, no mention was made of the uncertainty of measurements in this definition.

Until 1979 there was not much motivation to start calibration of measuring instruments and introduce the assessment of uncertainty into metrological practice in Russia. At the international level it was only in 1979 that the International Committee of Weights and Measures (CIPM) adopted the Recommendation of the BIPM Working Group "Expression of Experimental Uncertainties". Then only in 1993, when the Law "On Assurance of Measurement Uniformity" was being enacted in the Russian Federation, at the international level the "Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement" (GUM) was developed under the guidance of seven international organizations, and was published for uniform application by metrologists worldwide.

This was the first time that the main organizational issues of calibration were explicitly described at the legislative level in Russia; however, neither traceability nor uncertainty was mentioned there. Moreover, for a long time some practical people presented calibration as a kind of departmental verification. Notwithstanding the creation of the Russian calibration system, calibration still remained an 'exotic' procedure, because the phrase in Article 23 stating that "measuring instruments that are not subject to verification may be subject to calibration" could be interpreted somehow that MIs both may and may not be subjected, which is confusing. With transformations taking place in the economy, the terms "branch" and "department" ceased to be widely used, and with them the term "departmental verification" almost stopped being used, too. The uniformity of measurements in the country was ensured by the State Metrological Service, but their efforts were hardly enough to compensate for the activities of the disappearing metrology services at enterprises that had previously been responsible for carrying out departmental verification of measuring instruments.

In 1999, the practice of measurement uncertainty assessment changed in Russia, with the establishing of the metrological foundations for the "Mutual recognition of national measurement standards and of calibration and measurement certificates issued by national metrology institutes". After the Mutual Recognition Arrangement (CIRM MRA) was signed in Paris on 14 October 1999, based on the results of the national measurement standards comparisons, unambiguous requirements were introduced to assess uncertainty for national measurement standards. Also, within the framework of this Arrangement, a BIPM key comparison database, KCDB, was launched containing the calibration and measuring capabilities (CMCs) of each country. The need to assess the uncertainties of the characteristics of Russia's primary measurement standards required certain efforts, as this should be initiated at the government metrological level, at which the world-accepted order is observed and

mutual international recognition of measurement and calibration results is ensured in Russia. It is characteristic that "traceability" appeared when ISO/IEC 17025:1999 was introduced in the Russian Federation by way of the national standard GOST R ISO/IEC 17025:2000. Para 5.6, "Traceability of measurements", read: "The concept of traceability in this International Standard is used as a binding to measurement standards in order to ensure the uniformity of measurements".

## 3 Traceability through verification and calibration

The ILAC policy, developed in accordance with the requirements of ISO/IEC 17025:2017, does not contradict the Russian Federal Law "On Assurance of Measurement Uniformity", adopted in 2008 [2]. The term 'traceability' is defined in Article 2, item 18 of the Law, as a "property of a measurement standard of a quantity unit $ ^{2} $ , a measuring instrument or measurement result, which consists in the documented establishment of their connection with the state primary measurement standard or national primary measurement standard of a foreign country of the corresponding quantity unit through the comparison of measurement standards of quantity units, verification, and calibration of measuring instruments. National legislation, which takes precedence, is focused on verification, while the ILAC policy on the traceability of the measurement result is a special case of the above-mentioned scheme.

ISO/IEC 17025 also provides for the possibility of using verification to confirm metrological traceability. Annex A, Para A.2.3 reads: "Measurement standards that have reported information from a competent laboratory that includes only a statement of conformity to a specification (omitting the measurement results and associated uncertainties) are sometimes used to disseminate metrological traceability... The technical basis for this approach is that the declared conformance to a specification defines a range of measurement values, within which the true value is expected to lie, at a specified level of confidence, which considers both any bias from the true value, as well as the measurement uncertainty" [3].

A positive assessment, based on the results of the verification of a measuring instrument used as a measurement standard, is an assessment of the conformity of the measurement standard provided by a

legal entity or an individual entrepreneur accredited in the national accreditation system. At the same time, the declaration of compliance with the specification contains detailed results of studies of the characteristics regulated by the verification methodology, which is undoubtedly an advantage in providing evidence of traceability. Those detailed results should include quality assurance/control measures taken to confirm the ongoing integrity of the measuring instrument between verifications or calibrations.

Para A.2.3 proceeds: "...This approach, in which the specification limits are imported as the source of uncertainty, is dependent upon:

the use of an appropriate decision rule to establish conformity;

the specification limits subsequently being treated in a technically appropriate way in the uncertainty budget..." provides opportunities for the application of verification results to demonstrate metrological traceability, because:

"the rules for decision-making to establish compliance" of the measurement standard are established in the Order of the Ministry of Industry and Trade of the Russian Federation, dated July 31, 2020, No. 2510 "On Approval of the Procedure for Verification of Measuring Instruments, Requirements for the Verification Mark and the Content of the Verification Certificate", and "the limited values in the uncertainty budget given in the specifications" are taken in state verification schemes and in verification procedures, established in accordance with the Order of the Ministry of Industry and Trade of Russia dated August 28, 2020 No. 2907 "On Approval of the Procedure for Establishing and Changing the Interval between Verifications of Measuring Instruments, the Procedure for Establishing, Canceling Verification Methods and Making Changes to Them, Requirements for Methods for Verifying Measuring Instruments".

It should be noted that calibration is specified in International Standard 17025 as the main procedure for ensuring metrological traceability.

## 3.1 Traceability through calibration

Traceability, ensured through calibration of measuring instruments, has both advantages and disadvantages when compared with traceability ensured through verification of measuring instruments.

The major advantage of traceability ensured through calibration is associated with the advantages of calibration itself, the main advantage of which should be attributed to its flexibility, i.e. the possibility of

developing a calibration procedure for particular ranges or a part of the measurement range of a measuring instrument, required by a laboratory, or a particular measurement task or the needs of an applicant. An individual calibration procedure allows to flexibly choose a traceability scheme, which, with sufficient qualifications of the developer of the calibration procedure, can provide the desired result. Actually, there are no established requirements for carrying out the calibration of measuring instruments, including the development and application of the calibration procedure. They are regulated by the internal rules of laboratories or organizations within which the laboratories operate. For the same measuring instruments, calibration procedures may be different at various laboratories, and so calibration may be performed differently, using different measurement standards.

Nonetheless, in every calibration the proof of traceability must be evident in the calibration certificate or report. The level of the evidence in each calibration largely depends on the qualifications of the calibrator. The lack of free access to the calibration procedures developed by laboratories complicates the calibration performance. Neither the compliance with the implementation of the relevant documents by laboratories, nor recognition of their activities, even by the International Laboratory Accreditation Organization (ILAC), guarantees metrological traceability. Even the most thorough control of accredited laboratories by accreditation bodies, carried out every few years, is unable to control the proper quality of each calibration procedure and each calibration certificate issued by an accredited laboratory. Accreditation experts check the compliance of laboratories with quality manuals, rather than compliance with the quality of the calibration procedure or the quality of traceability evidence specified in calibration certificates (this may be correct to a certain extent, since accreditation experts are specialists in the particular area, and not in general in the calibration of all measuring instruments).

Even in advanced and well-organized calibration laboratories the implemented traceability schemes sometimes lack information on the accuracy of the measurement standards and on the calibration procedure. The schemes can be considered to be informative documents rather than technical documents.

## 4 Legislation on metrological traceability and metrological infrastructure

On 13 November 2018, during the 26th General Conference on Weights and Measures held in Versailles, four international organizations (BIPM, OIML, ILAC and ISO) signed the Joint Declaration on Metrological Traceability [5], offering the opportunity to address the problem of traceability of measurement results on a global scale.

The Joint Declaration acknowledges the approach of the OIML as one of the three approaches to ensuring traceability, which "promotes the global harmonization of laws and procedures in the field of legal metrology and provides its Members with a guiding framework to be used for application in the development of national legislation, bearing in mind that measurements in trade and regulatory areas should be carried out on the basis of measurement standards with recognized traceability to the SI. The OIML has established a worldwide technical infrastructure that provides its Members with metrological guidance for the harmonization of national requirements for the production and use of legally regulated measuring instruments. Such infrastructure helps implement legal traceability in the regulated areas such as trade, health, safety, and environmental monitoring."

These concepts were presented in the document of the Asia-Pacific Forum on Legal Metrology (APLMF) named "National Metrology Infrastructure" [4], developed in 2012, which was finalized in 2016 jointly with the Asia-Pacific Metrology Program (APMP) and the participation of the PTB.

The aim of the metrology infrastructure is to guarantee confidence in measurements supporting regulation, trade, and manufacturing. This is realized by ensuring that measurements are fit for purpose, which thereby results in a reduction of transaction costs and, for measurements in trade, minimizes the risk of market failure. The metrology infrastructure comprising the elements of metrological control and traceability is shown in Fig.1[4]. The traceability elements are crucial for measurements in industry and in the community, and are disseminated to ensure that these measurements are of the required accuracy for their intended purpose. The traceability elements also underpin the legal control provisions. They are intended to ensure that measurements made for legal purposes (i.e. for regulation, trade or contractual matters) are within the maximum permissible errors prescribed for that purpose.

## 5 Conclusion

In the authors' opinion, calibration and verification can be equally applied to ensure metrological traceability. At the same time in the field of legal metrology the provision of metrological traceability by means of verification of measuring instruments is preferable. Legally validated metrological traceability demonstrated through verification certificates can be beneficial in regulated areas. The approach to addressing this issue may be to amend JCGM 200:2008 and ISO/IEC 17025 [6], after extensive discussion with fellow metrologists and

with their support, in order to clarify the concept of metrological traceability and describe how it can be achieved through verification of measuring instruments.

## Introduction

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

The OIML-CS comprises two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B. Competence of the OIML Issuing Authorities and their Test Laboratories is demonstrated through self-declaration under Scheme B and accreditation or peer assessment under Scheme A.

The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate. Participating in the OIML-CS commits, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS that are established in OIML B 18:2018 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS). Signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation and test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an OIML Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

The OIML-CS was launched on 1 January 2018 and has replaced the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

# Eighth meeting of the OIML-CS Management Committee

</div>

PAUL DIXON

OIML-CS Executive Secretary

BIML Assistant Director

## Introduction

The Eighth OIML-CS Management Committee Meeting was held at METAS, Bern-Wabern, Switzerland on 16-17 March 2023. This was the first time that the Management Committee had met in-person since March 2019, and it provided an excellent opportunity for Management Committee members to reacquaint themselves and to also meet new members in-person for the first time.

## Review Committee and Maintenance Group meetings

As in recent years, online meetings of the Review Committee and Maintenance Group were held on 3 February and 15 February respectively. However, in association with the Management Committee meeting, 'informal' meetings of the Review Committee and Maintenance Group were held at METAS on 15 March. In addition, meetings of the Assessment Requirements Working Group and the Promotion and Awareness Raising Working Group were also held on 15 March. Having in-person meetings of these groups led to very productive discussions and complemented the online Review Committee and Maintenance Group meetings held the previous month. These 'informal' meetings enabled all interested participants to attend as the meetings were not limited to the nominated participants in the Working Groups.

## Management Committee meeting

The Management Committee meeting was chaired by the Management Committee Chairperson, Mr Mannie Panesar, with 19 of the 22 Management Committee members from OIML Member States either present or represented. In addition, there were representatives from the following liaison organisations: GULFMET, CECIP, and ILAC/IAF.

At the meeting the Management Committee agreed to make a recommendation to the CIML to reappoint Mr Mannie Panesar as Management Committee Chairperson for a second three-year term. Mr Bill Loizides, the current Management Committee Deputy Chairperson, will complete his second three-year term at the end of 2023 so, in accordance with the rules of the OIML-CS, the Management Committee also agreed to undertake a process to select a candidate for appointment as Management Committee Deputy Chairperson. The Management Committee took the opportunity to thank Mr Loizides for his efforts and hard work supporting the OIML-CS as the Management Committee Deputy Chairperson, most notably when he deputised as Management Committee Chairperson in 2020.

Some of the other key items that were discussed and considered during the meeting were:

- feedback and experiences of participants in the OIML-CS;

- updates from the Review Committee, Maintenance Group and various Working Groups;

high priority publications and periodic reviews including a decision to include OIML R 111:2004 Weights of classes $ E_{1}, $ $ E_{2}, $ $ F_{1}, $ $ F_{2}, $ $ M_{1}, $ $ M_{1-2}, $ $ M_{2}, $ $ M_{2-3} $ and $ M_{3} $ in the OIML-CS;

applicability of the COVID contingencies that were put in place in 2020 to ensure the continued effective operation of the OIML-CS throughout the pandemic;

establishment of a Working Group to consider modular approvals;

use of the OIML-CS logo on test reports; and

working with the OIML Digitalisation Task Group to consider what implications digitalisation has for the OIML-CS.

## Development and future growth of the OIML-CS

As part of the Management Committee meeting a Workshop was held where the participants were split into groups to discuss and develop ideas regarding the topic of "Development and future growth of the OIML-CS". To stimulate the discussions and ideas, a guest speaker from the State Secretary of Economic Affairs in Switzerland gave a presentation on the importance and benefits of harmonised standards and the reduction of technical barriers to trade. The groups provided feedback on their discussions to the plenary, with a summary of the feedback collated for further consideration by the Management Committee. In addition, this feedback was used to support discussions regarding the development and future growth of the OIML-CS at a Presidential Council strategy meeting on 21 March 2023.

![](page=1,bbox=[104, 285, 1820, 1033])

Participants in the Eighth OIML-CS Management Committee Meeting, pictured at METAS, Bern-Wabern, Switzerland Photo courtesy of METAS

## Recommendations to the 58th CIML Meeting

The Management Committee will be recommending that the CIML re-appoint Mr Mannie Panesar as Management Committee Chairperson for a second three-year term with effect from 1 January 2024. On completion of the selection process mentioned above, the Management Committee will also be recommending that the CIML appoint the successful candidate for the position of Management Committee Deputy Chairperson for a three-year period with effect from 1 January 2024.

In addition, the Management Committee will recommend that OIML R 111:2004 Weights of classes $ E_{1}, $ $ E_{2}, F_{1}, F_{2}, M_{1}, M_{1-2}, M_{2}, M_{2-3} $ and $ M_{3} $ should be included in the OIML-CS, initially in Scheme B with a transition to Scheme A after two years.

## Looking forward

Although online meetings have proved to be a useful and effective tool in supporting the work of the Management Committee during the pandemic, significant benefits were achieved through meeting in-person. Due to this, and because of the number and importance of the items that are expected to be on the agenda at the next Management Committee meeting, the Management Committee has agreed that the Ninth Management Committee Meeting will be held as an in-person meeting in New Delhi, India in 2024. However, this decision does not preclude the possibility of the Management Committee holding online meetings in the future.

The President of the International Committee of Legal Metrology (CIML) convened two key meetings in the week of 20 March 2023: a Strategy Meeting, and a Presidential Council Meeting.

Regional Legal Metrology Organisations (RLMOs) and their Round Table, as well as the OIML Digitalisation Task Group (DTG), were also discussed.

The next Presidential Council Meeting will be held in Thailand in October 2023.

# OIML DTG / IMEKO TC6 Webinar on "Blockchain technologies in metrology"

</div>

SASCHA EICHSTAEDT

OIML DTG Chairperson

In recent years, several initiatives have been started to investigate the potential use of distributed ledger technologies, also known as blockchain, in metrology. Proposed applications range from making traceability in calibration hierarchies more transparent and efficient to distributed infrastructures for processes in the quality infrastructure. The general advantage of blockchains is that they are designed to store information such that it is immutable without the need for a central storage and processing entity.

The OIML "Digitalisation Task Group" and the IMEKO TC6 "Digitalisation" jointly organized an online webinar on this topic on 29 March 2023. The event brought together experts and interested colleagues from metrology, legal metrology, and the general quality infrastructure for an exchange and discussion on this topic. Presentations given were:

- "Digital transformation at NMI Australia" by Philip Mitchell, NMI-A, Australia

- "EBSI: Blockchain for public goods" by Jose Manuel Panizo, European Commission

- "Metroracle: Digital traceability pyramid and measuring device as blockchain oracle" by Kruno Milicevic, Random Red

- "Blockchain and the European Metrology Cloud / QI-Cloud" by Maximilian Dohlus and Jan Wetzlich, PTB, Germany

- "Blockchain and smart contracts for traceability in metrology and advanced manufacturing" by Michael Chrubasik, NPL, United Kingdom

- "Blockchain for conformity assessment documents in the international market" by Irache Visiers, EUROLAB

The presentations showed the wide spectrum of potential use cases and benefits of blockchain technologies in the quality infrastructure. Examples are the efficient and secured digitalisation of processes; infrastructures with trust-by-design with or without trusted entities; secured access to documents and information as a basis for trustworthy digital representations of products; and distributed systems with verified communication of data and information for traceability.

Whilst some presentations showed prototypes or demonstrators, others were in a conceptual stage. Hence, there is still a lot of work and research to be done. It is therefore intended to consider this webinar as a starting point for a regular exchange aiming to achieve harmonization and interoperability of developments in the long run.
