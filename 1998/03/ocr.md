Regional cooperation in legal metrology - Promising new developments

As in many other technical fields, international cooperation in legal metrology is increasingly and successfully being supplemented by cooperative actions at regional level.

This regional cooperation may take various forms including, for example, the development of regional regulations, exchange of information and expertise, cooperative training, intercomparisons and resource sharing.

The degree to which an ideal level of regional cooperation can be achieved does, however, vary somewhat depending on the number of countries in the region, their relative degrees of development, the state of their national economies, cultures, languages, and so on. Larger regions require a higher level of coordination and management, which is not always easy to accomplish.

So the size of a region, besides having a bearing on other economic and physical aspects (such as transportation facilities), is doubtless an important factor in defining to what extent it can form a regional cooperation structure; in certain cases it may be appropriate to establish subregional cooperation forums in order to ensure that the practicalities of implementing a regional cooperation policy are thoroughly explored.

This type of evolution is increasingly being developed on the African continent where, in addition to existing African

cooperation bodies (e.g. ARSO in the field of standardization) certain sub-regional cooperation bodies are now emerging, each with a very precise role and set of objectives.

In this issue of the OIML Bulletin, we were eager to publicize this initiative which is clearly designed to further metrological cooperation in the South-African sub-region an initiative which is developing through SADCMET for scientific metrology, SALMEC for legal metrology and SARAC for accreditation matters. In this way, other regions may be encouraged to follow suit and thus further the common objectives we have in our field of activity.

We have, moreover, found it particularly appropriate to open up this issue to Zimbabwe, a country which is very active within this region, though which is not yet an OIML Member. A comprehensive article is featured which describes the ongoing metrological structure and activities in this country, and which will, we hope, pave the way for an open exchange of metrological dialogue between Zimbabwe and existing OIML Members, desirous to impart their experiences and objectives.

In this way, and by increasing our cooperation with SALMEC, we expect to be able to attract the interest of even more countries in the region, and look forward to perhaps welcoming some of them as OIML Members in a not too distant future.

# Re-establishment of mass calibration in Slovenia

</div>

A. KOLAR, Standards and Metrology Institute of Slovenia (SMIS), Ljubljana, Slovenia

## Abstract

Slovenia, which has a population of about two million, gained its independence in 1991 and has since had to reestablish its metrological infrastructure. The decision to keep mass under state control rather than delegate it to national universities or institutes led to the creation of a mass laboratory within the scope of the Slovenian Standards and Metrology Institute (SMIS). However, it was more economical to maintain the other physical quantities in locations where a certain degree of knowledge and equipment already existed, a technique known as a "Distributed system of national standards".

## 1 Introduction

It is no longer sufficient for a state-owned laboratory merely to demonstrate its capabilities and reliability to both national and regional industry. Slovenia does have its own accreditation service, though not yet EA approved, and so the goal of the Slovenian team was to obtain accreditation which would be recognized both within and outside Europe; to this end the SMIS/PTB joint project was regarded as a key opportunity, but this also meant that Slovenia would have to link its traceability chain to that of Germany (Germany can provide a direct liaison to the BIPM artifact of 1 kg).

The SMIS bought four comparator balances, all produced by Sartorius AG, and currently dispose of:

- a C10000S for weights from 2 kg-10 kg;

- a C1000 for weights from 100 g-1 kg;

- a C50S for weights of 10 g, 20 g and 50 g; and

- a C5S for weights from 1 mg-5 g.

The laboratory consists of two rooms: one is used as an anteroom and the other is a laboratory equipped with balances placed on granite tables which are, in turn, fixed to a floating floor. Ambient conditions are kept to within the following limits:

Maximum variation in humidity Maximum variation in temperature

$$
\begin{array}{l} \pm 1 0 \% / 8 \mathrm {h}; \\ \leq \pm 0. 5 \mathrm {K} / 1 \mathrm {h}, \\ \leq \pm 0. 7 \mathrm {K} / 4 \mathrm {h}, \\ \leq \pm 1. 0 \mathrm {K} / 2 4 \mathrm {h}. \\ \end{array}
$$

The equipment and constant ambient conditions are sufficient for the calibration of weights of Class $ \mathrm{E}_{2} $ (according to OIML R 111 - see Reference [5]).

To achieve the goal of accreditation, a "bottom up approach was adopted. As soon as staff were able to use the new equipment, calibration of weights brought in by customers was started, together with that of weights belonging to the SMIS. Procedures were documented; besides the organizational procedures which are important for the quality system (QS), two technical procedures were also documented - Uncertainty calculations and Calculation of conventional mass. Both comply with the PTB recommendations for mass laboratories.

In June 1997, a formal application for accreditation was filed with the DKD and during this time the laboratory cooperated with the PTB mass laboratory which provided the necessary advice and technical support under the supervision of Dr. Michael Glaser (see Reference [2]).

$$

$$
\Delta m = \frac {\left(m _ {\mathrm {A 1}} - m _ {\mathrm {B 1}}\right) + \left(m _ {\mathrm {A 2}} - m _ {\mathrm {B 2}}\right)}{2}
$$

## 3 Organization procedures

The technique itself does not meet all the QS requirements. The procedures followed daily in the laboratory are specific for the equipment the SMIS disposes of and for the personnel employed.

Before attempting to improve a process, one must ensure that every team member thoroughly understands it (see Reference [3]). In the present case, staff were first trained in all the procedures, which were subsequently documented. It can nevertheless arise that certain passages in procedures are not fully understood; in this case, the author of the procedure is requested to supply additional information for the purposes of clarification.

The assessors who evaluated the SMIS QS were fully satisfied with the running of the laboratory and a special acknowledgment was awarded, stating that it was clear that not only procedures were correctly documented, but that they were actually implemented as well.

The Quality Manual (see Reference [4]) is composed of a main document, not destined to change very often, and three supporting documents:

- organization procedures (including technical procedures);

- working documents/instructions;

- forms used for the work.

Using this structure, the laboratory does not completely comply with the PTB document DKD-6 for preparing quality manuals, but the PTB is flexible enough to allow a different structure to exist.

# Classification and limits for interpolation errors

</div>

DR. D. KISETS, National Physical Laboratory of Israel (INPL), Jerusalem

## Abstract

The significant problem of evaluating the measurement uncertainty in the calibration of analogue measuring instruments is the determination of so-called interpolation errors and their consideration when calculating the combined uncertainty. As a development of the author's papers on traceability and improvement of traceable measurements, published in the April and October 1997 issues of the OIML Bulletin [3, 4], this paper presents the results of further investigation to successively determine these interpolation errors and classify them in the framework of that kind of traceability which is termed "observation traceability". Theoretically permissible limits of interpolation errors are proposed, these having been verified by an experiment carried out at the INPL. As in the author's previously published articles, the theses of this paper are based on Theory of Information and Qualimetry (the so-called IQ-criterion, developed by the author and described in [3], [4] and [5]); mathematical expressions and data are also suggested. The author believes that this paper may be of interest both to the OIML and to all those concerned with metrology and calibration of measuring instruments.

## 1 Introduction

The interpolation error, defined by [1] as "a reading error resulting from an inexact evaluation of the position of the index with reference to the two adjoining scale marks between which the index is located", is a notably important source of combined uncertainty when measuring with an analogue measuring instrument. The significance of this part of measurement uncertainty is particularly perceptible for the calibration of precise instruments, especially if the calibration is carried out

with analogue reference standards. The calibration of liquid-in-glass thermometers, for instance, is a very typical case in question. The development of a wellfounded method for determining the limits of this type of error is a vital issue, which had to be taken into account when the standard operational procedures applied by the INPL were updated.

Unfortunately, existing international documents on measurement uncertainty concern mostly digital instruments; e.g. the standard uncertainty (u) due to the resolution $ \left( \delta_{\mathrm{x}} \right) $ of a digital indication is $ u=0. 2 9 \delta_{\mathrm{x}} $ for any indication [2]. Nothing similar appears to exist for analogue instruments, so the present paper sets out to fill this gap by jointly applying the concepts of Theory of Information and Qualimetry, together with measurement traceability tasks.

It is shown in this paper that the principle of "information cyclicity" (developed and reflected on in the above-mentioned publications) is quite well suited for classifying and determining the interpolation error in practical application. Using this principle, the precise limits of interpolation error are proposed.

The task of determining the interpolation error can be considered as involving the TUR (permissible Tolerance-Uncertainty Ratio) or its inverse value: the accuracy coefficient ( $ \rho $ ) is of interest presently. In a sense the evident solution to this issue may be extended to encompass the problem as a whole, but in so doing one should take into account the fact that a systematic approach is mandatory. This means that any experiment aimed at proving the theoretically determined limits of the interpolation error must mainly involve the measurements carried out by professional observers.

## 2 Theory

In terms of the IQ-criterion the permissible error of observation $ \left( \Delta\delta_{\mathrm{x a}} \right) $ (excluding a parallax error) resulting

from an incorrect reading of the measuring instrument indication by the observer, i.e. the interpolation error, may be considered as that part of the resolution of an analogue instrument $ \left( \delta_{\mathrm{x a}} \right) $ which is equivalent to the optimum redundancy of information $ \left( \mathrm{R}_{0} \right) $ obtained within $ \delta_{\mathrm{x a}} $ irrespective of the index position.

The heuristic assumptions arising from a systematic approach and the principle of information cyclicity are presented below.

## 2.1 Classification premises

The values of the interpolation error are significantly dependent on the experience of the observer; requirements are determined by the level of measurement traceability sought. There exist widely differing requirements as to observation accuracy, ranging from very precise measurements (specific for calibration), often demanding the application of auxiliary magnifying devices, right down to approximate observations where merely reading the index position within two scale marks is sufficient. Classification of the values of these elements serves to indicate the traceability structure.

The classification of interpolation errors may be carried out with the sequence of groups of numerical values arranged with the regularity of $ 2\pi $ . If the special limitation concerning the number of groups (N) is absent, then its theoretical maximum number is calculated as:

$$
\mathrm {N} = \left(\delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 2 \pi 1 0 ^ {- 3}\right) / \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} = 1 / 2 \pi 1 0 ^ {- 3} \approx 1 5 9.
$$

Here the multiplying factor $ (10^{-3}) $ corresponds to the well-known practical limitation for determining the uncertainty of precise observations. Thus, the deviation $ \left( \mathrm{D}_{j} \right) $ of the values of the interpolation error for each group (j=1; N) may be determined as follows:

$$
\mathrm {D} _ {\mathrm {j}} = (2 \pi \pm \pi) \mathrm {j} 1 0 ^ {- 3} = 0. 0 0 1 \pi \mathrm {j} (2 \pm 1)
$$

## 2.2 Informative limits of interpolation error

In line with the permissible limits for the relative optimum redundancy $ \left( \mathrm{R}_{_{\mathrm{or}}} \right) $:

$$
(1 / 4 \pi) \leq \mathrm {R} _ {\mathrm {o r}} \leq (1 / 2 \pi)
$$

as well as respective optimum confidences:

$$
C _ {\mathrm {o}} = (1 - 1 / 4 \pi) \approx 0. 9 2 (\mathrm {m i n i m u m v a l u e}) \mathrm {a n d}
$$

$$
C _ {\mathrm {o}} = (1 - 1 / 8 \pi) \approx 0. 9 6 (\mathrm {m a x i m u m v a l u e}),
$$

the following hypothesis may be drawn:

the interpolation error $ \left( \Delta\delta_{\mathrm{x a}} \right) $ is limited as:

$$
\left(\delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 4 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 6}\right) \leq \Delta \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} \leq \left(\delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 2 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 2}\right)
$$

where $ \mathrm{k}_{9 6} $ and $ \mathrm{k}_{9 2} $ are the coverage factors for the above maximum and minimum $ C_{0} $ values respectively.

As regards the appraisal of the observer, expression (2) can be interpreted as follows:

i) $ \Delta\delta_{\mathrm{x a}}>(\delta_{\mathrm{x a}}/2\pi \mathrm{k}_{9 2})=\mathrm{the~zone~of~total~professional} $ incompetence of the observer;

ii) $ \Delta\delta_{\mathrm{x a}}<(\delta_{\mathrm{x a}}/4 \pi \mathrm{k}_{9 6})=\mathrm{the~zone~of~extreme~professional competence~of~the~observer~that~is~excessive~in~terms of unification.} $

It should be noted that expression (2) represents a sort of statistical idealization of a conventional distribution, which in terms of the principle of information cyclicity represents a distribution considered as the average of all possible distributions. The estimation reliability when changing from the real distribution to the conventional one may be determined with so-called accuracy coefficient [3] $ \rho_{\mathrm{o}}=1/2\pi $ . Thus the estimation error $ \left( \delta_{\mathrm{e}} \right) $ should not be more than $ 1/2\pi $ by absolute value, namely:

$$
\left| \delta_ {\mathrm {e} (9 6)} \right| = \left| 1 - \left(1 / 4 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 6}\right) / \left(1 / 4 \pi\right) \right| \leq 1 / 2 \pi
$$

$$
\left| \delta_ {\mathrm {e} (9 2)} \right| = \left| 1 - \left(1 / 2 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 2}\right) / \left(1 / 2 \pi\right) \right| \leq 1 / 2 \pi
$$

The application of this condition to a normal distribution $ \mathrm{k}_{9 6}=2. 0 6 $ $ \mathrm{k}_{9 2}=1. 7 4 $ , mostly used in measurement readings statistics, results in:

$$
\left| \delta_ {\mathrm {e} (9 6)} \right| = 0. 0 3 8, \left| \delta_ {\mathrm {e} (9 2)} \right| = 0. 1 5 1.
$$

Clearly, each of these results is less than $ 1 / 2 \pi $ , which proves the reliability of the above estimation.

The interpolation error consists of a systematic element (b) which is the average of the observations, and a double standard uncertainty $ \left( 2 u_{\mathrm{a}} \right) $ , which is the double standard deviation of observations:

$ \Delta\delta_{\mathrm{x a}}=2 \mathrm{u}_{\mathrm{a}}+|\mathrm{b}| $ . Therefore (2) may be rewritten as follows:

$$
\left(\delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 8 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 6}\right) \leq \left(\mathrm {u} _ {\mathrm {a}} + \left| \mathrm {b} / 2 \right|\right) \leq \left(\delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 4 \pi \mathrm {k} _ {9 2}\right)
$$

For a normal distribution with infinite degrees of freedom this theoretical condition may be simplified as follows:

$$
0. 0 1 9 \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} \leq \left(\mathrm {u} _ {\mathrm {a}} + \left| b / 2 \right|\right) \leq 0. 0 4 6 \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}}
$$

If experimental data are used, then coverage factors must be corrected according to the number of measurements used (degrees of freedom v). In the case of a normal distribution: $ \mathrm{k}_{9 6}=\mathrm{t}_{9 6}(\mathrm{v}) $ and $ \mathrm{k}_{9 2}(\mathrm{v}) $ , where $ \mathrm{t}_{9 6}(\mathrm{v}) $

$ t_{92}(v) $ are the t-factors as functions of degrees of freedom tabulated in [2]. Therefore, the following condition is true:

$$
\left[ \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 8 \pi \mathrm {t} _ {9 6} (\mathrm {v}) \right] \leq \left(\mathrm {u} _ {\mathrm {a}} + \left| \mathrm {b} / 2 \right|\right) \leq \left[ \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} / 4 \pi \mathrm {t} _ {9 2} (\mathrm {v}) \right]
$$

Clearly, if correction of the observer's readings is practicable, then b = 0 should be accepted in each of the above expressions.

The only way to prove the correctness of the hypothesis concerning the proposed limits of interpolation error is by experimental corroboration, and using uniform statistics is a condition to carry out the experiment with observers. In this sense the participating observers should be chosen from among people who (a) are somehow involved in measurements, and (b) have essentially varying experience, i.e. ranging from those involved in top level calibration activity to those who merely use analogue measuring instruments.

## 2.3 Unified classes of interpolation error

Clearly, N = 159 (as calculated above) expresses the excessive number of classification groups for applied metrology. Practical estimation of interpolation errors is limited to a much less practical number $ \left( \mathrm{N_{p}} \right) $ because of its proportionality to the limits of interpolation error. Proceeding in this manner and taking into account formula (5), the following number of informative classes is true:

$$
\mathrm {N} _ {\mathrm {p}} = \mathrm {N} \left(1 / 8 \pi - 1 / 4 \pi\right) = \left(1 / 2 \pi 1 0 ^ {- 3}\right) \left(1 / 8 \pi - 1 / 4 \pi\right) = 2 \pi \approx 6.

## 4 Discussion

1. The problem of unifying the interpolation error can be expressed:

i) as the averaged value (or values, if the aim is the classification level of calibration activity) practical for use as one of the sources of combined uncertainty, and

ii) as the criteria for judging the professional capability of an operator to carry out particular measurements depending on the reading accuracy required.

2. The averaged values both theoretical (5), i.e.:

$$
0. 5 \left(0. 0 1 9 + 0. 0 4 6\right) \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}} = 0. 0 3 3 \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}},
$$

and experimental, i.e.:

$$
\mathrm {m} _ {\mathrm {x}} = 0. 0 3 3 \delta_ {\mathrm {x a}}
$$

are approximately the same. Incidentally, this value indicates that for the same resolution of analogue and digital instrument, the first is significantly more accurate, i.e.:

$$
\delta_ {\mathrm {x a} /} \delta_ {\mathrm {x}} = 0. 0 3 3 / 0. 2 9 = 0. 1 1 \mathrm {o n a v e r a g e},
$$

and no more than $ 1 / 2 \pi=0.16. $

3. The data in Table 3 demonstrate that the systematic error (b) does not definitely depend on the random part $ \left(u_{\mathrm{a}}\right) $ of the interpolation error and, therefore, on the class of observations (Table 1). In the framework of this experiment the arithmetic mean of the systematic error is:

$$
\mathrm {b} _ {\mathrm {m}} = - 0. 0 0 6 \approx - 2 \pi 1 0 ^ {- 3}.
$$

At the same time $ u_{\mathrm{a}} $ decreases towards class 1. The evidence gathered from this case indicates that the systematic error is the minimum limit of interpolation error, which corresponds to the principle of information cyclicity itself. The following system of equations with the minimum values min $ D_{j} $ and min $ \left(u_{\mathrm{a}}+\left|b / 2\right|\right) $ may be considered based on expressions (1) and (6):

min $ \mathrm{D_{j}}=\min \left(\lim_{j=1}0.001\pi j\right)=0.001\pi; $

min $ \mathrm{u_{a}+|b/2|}=\lim_{u\rightarrow 0}\mathrm{u_{a}+|b/2|}=\mid b/2\mid $ The equation $ \min \mathrm{D_{j}}=\min \left( \mathrm{u_{a}+|b/2|}\right) $ results in $ \mid b\mid =0.002\pi\approx 0.006 $ and, therefore, this demonstrates the full correspondence to the above experimental result $ \mathrm{b_{m}=-0.006}. $

4. The proposed method for determining interpolation errors can enrich the activity of calibration laboratories with useful quality control (QC) practice as the part of activity to meet the requirements of ISO/IEC Guide 25 [6]. To this end the laboratory can prepare the internal QC procedures aimed at decreasing (within the above permissible limits) and monitoring operator errors, as well as training the operators for the purpose of increasing their capability of accurate reading. It also seems reasonable to suggest updating the OIML models for training programs in metrology [7] with appropriate requirements on the subject.

As for the general problem mentioned in the introduction, i.e. the results of the experiment carried out with the model of interpolation error:

(a) the correctness of the principle of information cyclicity is once more clearly proved, and

(b) to a certain extent this is in approximate conformity with the so-called "golden rule of measuring technique", i.e. $ \rho\leq0.1 $ ,proposed half a century ago as a value based on experience [8].

## 5 Conclusions

1. The interpolation errors are characterized by sufficiently narrow limits determined with the principle and method of providing the optimum measurement information.

2. The lower limit of the interpolation error practically always exceeds 0.019 of the length of the two adjoining scale marks of analogue measuring instruments.

3. The normal condition for a calibration laboratory is to meet the following requirement for an operator: the average and permissible levels of skill in question should correspond to the relative interpolation error of 0.033 and 0.046 respectively.

4. The scale of unified classes of interpolation error is developed and proposed in order to help calibration laboratories determine the professional qualifications of operators and for their training.

5. The experiment results prove the correctness of the principle of information cyclicity.

6. The sensible use of proposed limits of interpolation error is their unification in the framework of OIML Recommendations as one of the means for updating traceable measurements.

## Abstract

There is a growing requirement for an internationally accepted system of recognition of measurement capabilities and relationships within and among countries, to facilitate seamless global commerce and trade. As a result, metrologists worldwide have recently developed increased interest in the concept and definition of traceability. Classically, traceability provides a way of relating the results of a measurement (or value of a standard) to higher-level standards. Such standards are usually national or international standards, and the comparisons used to provide the traceability must have well-understood uncertainties. An additional complexity arises because all instruments and standards are subject to change, however slight, over time. This paper develops approaches for dealing with the effects of such time-dependent changes as a part of traceability statements. The use of metrological timelines provides a means of effectively visualizing these relationships in a statement of traceability. When the rate of change in the measurement process is sufficiently small, the approach proposed here is less important. However, documented measurement assurance procedures are required at all levels so that appropriate uncertainties may be estimated with confidence. When laboratory or national boundaries are crossed in the traceability process, other factors come into play, and the original concept of traceability can become obscure. It is becoming common to hear the term "equivalence" used to describe these more complex measurement relationships.

## 1 Introduction

Worldwide commerce requires a coherent measurement system within which the consistency of measurements is easily maintained and demonstrated. Buyers and sellers needed such a system in order to evolve from barter to patterns of trade which use specifications to describe such things as size or performance. Classically, traceability [1] provides a way of relating the results of a measurement (or value of a standard) to higher level standards. Such standards are usually national or international standards, and the comparisons used to provide the traceability must have well-understood uncertainties. There is growing interest in the practical use of traceability to demonstrate the integrity of comparisons, and for that matter, to define just what it is that is being compared. Since all instruments and standards are subject to change, however slight, over time, the use of metrological timelines (described below) greatly facilitates the visualization of measurement relationships in a statement of traceability. This

paper discusses these issues, and addresses the need for new terms to describe the concepts usually associated with traceability.

## 1.1 Requirements for traceability

Depending on the measurement requirements and the resources available, the need for traceability and the form it takes may vary considerably. Manufacturers want the benefits of traceability so that customers will know the available level of performance for instruments and materials. In government applications, regulators may demand traceability to help ensure that public safety requirements are met. Further, armed services use traceability to provide a coherent measurement system for protecting lives, including those of service personnel. Traceability rarely stands alone; rather it forms part of larger systems which call for such varied properties as interchangeability of manufactured parts, quality systems in production of pharmaceuticals, and safety in air traffic control systems.

Common to all of these requirements is a need to know the results and uncertainties of measurements: the reduction of measurement variability leads to better and more compatible products and services. This in turn leads to more equitable trade and more efficient economies.

It is typically the responsibility of a national metrology institute (NMI) to provide its nation's measurement infrastructure with access to accurate measurement capability. The comparability of measurements and associated uncertainties with those of other nations is determined through a variety of mechanisms, including bilateral comparisons and round robins of international measurements. As discussed below, such comparability does not necessarily constitute traceability [2].

Requirements for measurement accuracy translate into a need to know not only the results of measurements but the uncertainties associated with the results. If it were practical, for the sake of coherence and consistency, all measurements of a given type in a country would be made using the same national standard in every laboratory in which the measurements are made. However, this is clearly impractical because of the volume of measurements. When industrial measurements are made, it is critical that each be made with accuracy sufficient for its intended use. One way to ensure this is to establish the relationship of the result of a measurement made using an industrial instrument with that which would have been obtained using the corresponding national standard. In a real sense the goal is "accurate" measurement, that is measurement deviating with acceptable uncertainty

from a recognized standard, and traceability is a part of reaching that goal.

The formalism of traceability is the tool that provides these measurement relationships. It is the process by which acceptable measurements with wellunderstood uncertainties can be documented to the degree required by interested parties. At its root, the primary use of traceability is to answer the questions (of auditors, regulators, those with a need for the "right" answer, ...): "What correction should be applied to a measurement result obtained at a given time with my instrument to match the result that would be obtained using the instrument (standard) to which traceability is desired? What is the uncertainty of this corrected measurement result?"

It should be noted that the result of a measurement, and its traceability, may be useful even when the measurement uncertainty is relatively large. It is for the user of the measurement to state the allowable magnitude of uncertainty for specific applications.

## 1.2 Current definitions of traceability

Probably the most widely-used and accepted definition of traceability is given in the 1993 International Vocabulary of Basic and General Terms in Metrology (VIM) [1], as: "property of the result of a measurement or the value of a standard whereby it can be related to stated references, usually national or international standards, through an unbroken chain of comparisons all having stated uncertainties." There are variations of this definition, for example [3], [4] and [5], that introduce the important additional requirement that quality assurance systems be in place. However, while possibly implied, there seems to have been no formal explicit statement of the need to consider the role that time plays in the definition of traceability.

## 1.3 New definitions of traceability

In his presentation at the 5th US-Italy Bilateral Seminar [6], Dr. Robert Hebner, the Acting Deputy Director of NIST, presented the following definition of traceability: "The property of the result of a measurement or the value of a standard whereby it can be related to stated references, usually national or international standards, through an unbroken chain of comparisons all having stated uncertainties. It is noted that traceability only exists when scientifically rigorous evidence is collected on a continuing basis showing that the measurement is producing documented results for which the total measurement uncertainty is quantified." Note that the

first sentence repeats the VIM definition. The second sentence is new, and was meant to emphasize that a single measurement result is sufficient to establish uncertainty relationships only over a limited time interval, and that direct periodic comparisons are otherwise required. In accordance with this principle, we show here that internal measurement assurance, using control (check) standards [1], is required to fully demonstrate that uncertainties remain within stated acceptable levels when establishing long-term traceability relationships. An uncertainty cannot be stated rigorously without demonstrated traceability. The rigor and level of detail of the measurement assurance procedures required for traceability necessarily depend on the relative levels of uncertainty of the standards involved.

The next section details how such traceability can be achieved. In particular, it shows why it is important to explicitly include the timeline of all relevant measurement events (the "metrological timeline") that supports and constitutes the chain of comparisons in a "statement of traceability."

Note that in these definitions, it is the result of a measurement or value of a standard that possesses traceability. Strictly speaking, then, traceability is not a property of an instrument or a laboratory, but is a property of the outcome of a process which involves instruments and laboratories. Such shorthand designations are frequently used, however, and so it is important in such cases to specify the range of operation covered by the instrument, or the applicable metrological variables and ranges for the laboratory, for which the traceable condition applies. The fact that traceability is a property of the result of a measurement, and not of an instrument or a laboratory, emphasizes that each measurement result has its own associated uncertainty specific to the circumstances in which it was obtained.

## 2 Metrological timelines

The most important aspect of a measurement process is to "get the measurement right" to some level. However, defining what "getting the measurement right" means is not always clear or straightforward. This is especially true if what is being measured (the measurand) is changing significantly with time, if the instrument being used to make the measurement is changing significantly with time (e.g. drifting), or if the reference standard to which the measurement is to be traceable is changing significantly with time. A rigorous, comprehensive statement of traceability must be capable of defining the measurement process and the associated

measurement uncertainties clearly enough that the relatively "instantaneous" measurement result can be "gotten right," even when things change with time, as they frequently do. The use of metrological timelines greatly facilitates dealing with time variations of the measured values of quantities that are presumed to be stable when documenting traceability.

## 2.1 Measurement uncertainty

Assessing measurement uncertainty is at the core of establishing traceability of a measurement result, and so it is important to have an accepted, well-established technique for assessing measurement uncertainty under a variety of measurement conditions. The 1995 Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement [7], and the 1994 edition of NIST Technical Note 1297 [8] address the questions of how to assess and express measurement uncertainty, especially if what is being measured is not changing with time. In its scope, the Guide states that it "is primarily concerned with the expression of uncertainty in the measurement of a welldefined physical quantity - the measurand - that can be characterized by an essentially unique value". To demonstrate traceability, the principles of the Guide can be used to assess individual uncertainties at "discrete measurement events," defined here as those covering time periods that are relatively short in comparison with the time period over which the measurand might change. For any case where there is a known, time-dependent systematic error (such as a documented drift over time in the use of an instrument), a simple way of incorporating such a known, uncorrected error into uncertainty considerations has been suggested by Phillips et al. [9]. Note that the Guide recommends correcting for such systematic errors whenever possible, and including a component of uncertainty for this correction.

What follows in this section is a description of the key elements of measurement assurance systems and metrological timelines used to develop rigorous yet practical statements of traceability. The measurement assurance system in an NMI is used as an example to develop the concept of the metrological timeline. Monitoring the stability of the national standards is essential, but those standards are usually maintained at a level sufficient to minimize the concerns of those wishing to demonstrate traceability to national standards. Thus it is not ordinarily necessary to take into account the stability of the national standards when demonstrating traceability from lower levels. Only in rare cases, where extremely small uncertainties are required, such as development of a new International Temperature Scale, is it necessary to correct for

such changes. Laboratories below the national level need to document the measurement assurance they use to demonstrate traceability from their levels to the national level, just as the NMIs need to document the measurement assurance they use to demonstrate traceability to standards representing the International System of Units (SI). The techniques cited here for the national level can also be used to demonstrate traceability at lower levels.

Clearly those measurement applications that permit larger uncertainties can be supported with less attention to the documentation of measurement quality. By reducing uncertainties beyond immediate levels of need, NMIs bring overall economies to their fields of measurement: investments by the NMI produce benefits which are widely distributed across the user community in terms of ease of use and reduced field-level costs.

## 3 Statements of traceability

As discussed in the Introduction, the use of traceability is primarily to answer the questions: "What correction should be applied to a measurement result obtained at a given time with my instrument to match the result that would be obtained using the instrument (standard) to which traceability is desired? What is the uncertainty of this corrected measurement result?"

The examples given in earlier sections demonstrate how the clear and rigorous identifications of metrological events and measurement relationships help, for even the simplest traceability statements, to answer these questions. To demonstrate the unbroken chain of measurement and uncertainty relationships between the measurement for which traceability is claimed and the standard to which traceability is claimed, all the relevant parameters must be defined and described. Metrological timelines, control charts, and records detailing all relevant metrological parameters associated with use of the instruments or standards involved at each step are important tools which make it possible to describe unambiguously how the uncertainty associated with the measurement has been evaluated.

On the practical side, while it is important to address the issues discussed above, the degree to which measurements and relationships must be documented may vary considerably, in part based on considerations of cost. For instance, less attention can be given to a standard's instability if the changes are small when compared with the uncertainty of the measurement. However, it is not wise to ignore the performance of the reference standard, since it too may undergo a serious change in performance: it may, for example, be damaged.

## 3.1 Components of a practical, rigorous statement of "simple" traceability

While practical considerations cannot be disregarded, it is useful for completeness, and to ensure that nothing has been overlooked, to list all the metrological events that must be considered in documenting the traceability of a measurement result. The key elements of a general statement of traceability for the relatively simple examples presented above are:

a) Provision of a complete metrological timeline (similar to those in Figs. 4-6, with accompanying descriptive text) identifying all relevant physical components, including control standards, and measures used to demonstrate the traceability of the measurement result and the integrity of its uncertainty.

Note: It is useful to document how control standards are used to ensure that no instruments or systems, especially those that are moved or transported, experience a significant unpredicted change in their performance over time. In particular it must be demonstrated, by using the same control standards before and after an instrument or system is shipped, that it performs in essentially the same manner after it is received as before it was shipped. For completeness, acceptable performance of the instrument or system should be verified following a measurement for which traceability is being demonstrated. Usually, this is done by comparison with control standards, documenting the procedure and results.

b) Description of all of the metrological details associated with the measurement, including what was measured, the result of the measurement, the instrument(s) or system(s) used, when and where the measurement took place, the measurement environment, the results of all ancillary measurements and their estimated uncertainties, who performed and who was responsible for the measurement, what calculations, models or analyses were used to obtain the measurement result, and the uncertainty of the measurement result.

c) Definition of the standard to which the measurement result is to be traceable, including the point in time in the existence of the standard at which the traceability is being established, and all metrological details (as described in (b) above) of the standard that influence the uncertainty of the measurement result.

Note: If the uncertainty, and any change in uncertainty, associated with the standard is much smaller than the uncertainty of the measurement result for which traceability is being documented, then only a brief description of the measurement history of the standard is needed as long as the standard performed within expected limits when the lower laboratory's standard was compared with it.

d) Provision of the uncertainty analysis including supporting documentation, such as test results and control charts, used to calculate the uncertainty of the measurement result for which traceability is being demonstrated. If there is an "additional" component of uncertainty (such as $ \delta U^{C} $ above) associated with the point in time at which the traceability to a higher-level standard is being established, this uncertainty must be clearly identified, and its incorporation into the overall statement of uncertainty described.

As noted above, depending on specific requirements, statements of traceability need not include all of these components. However, if a statement of traceability omits one or more of the components, the statement should say which components have been omitted, and why. In general, there is no such thing as "partial traceability". If any aspect of the measurement chain is not given due consideration, the reported uncertainty of the measurement result in the statement of traceability is inherently suspect.

In order for the testing laboratory to assess the measurement uncertainty associated with the measurement result at time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ , the uncertainty $ U_{t_{\mathrm{mT}}}^{\mathrm{T}} $ associated with the standard T at time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ must be known. This latter uncertainty can be evaluated in the testing laboratory from the control charts maintained on the standard T during the time period from $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ , when the standard T was calibrated by the lower laboratory's standard L and then returned to the testing laboratory, to time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ . If the time-average of the measurement quantity $ X^{\mathrm{T}} $ (denoted $ X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{T}} $ ), used in the control chart as a control parameter, changes (e.g. drifts) by an amount $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{T}} $ between $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ and $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ , then the testing laboratory can either "adjust" the values assigned to measurements made using T, or adjust the uncertainty assigned to T [9]. The original uncertainty associated with the standard T at time $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ can be ascertained in the testing laboratory from the calibration report corresponding to calibration of the standard T against the lower laboratory's standard L at time $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ . This calibration report should be issued to the testing laboratory by the calibration laboratory around time $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ . The method used to incorporate $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{T}} $ into the uncertainty of the measurement result for which traceability is being established needs to be specified [9].

The uncertainty associated with the lower laboratory's standard L at time $ t_{\mathrm{c T}} $ can be assessed in a similar manner by the lower laboratory. This uncertainty can be estimated from the control charts maintained on the standard L during the time period from $ t_{\mathrm{c L}} $ , when the standard L was calibrated against the higher laboratory's reference standard H and then returned to the lower laboratory, to time $ t_{\mathrm{c T}} $ . The original uncertainty associated with the standard L at time

$ t_{\mathrm{cL}} $ can be derived from the calibration report, issued by the higher laboratory, corresponding to calibration of the standard L against the standard H at time $ t_{\mathrm{cL}} $ . As in the case of the testing laboratory, if the time-average of the measurement quantity $ X^{\mathrm{L}} $ (denoted $ X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{L}} $ ), used in the control chart as a control parameter, changes by an amount $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{L}} $ between $ t_{\mathrm{cL}} $ and $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ , then the lower laboratory can either adjust the values assigned to measurements made using L, or adjust the uncertainty assigned to L, when it is used to calibrate T.

A comprehensive statement of traceability of the measurement result at time $ t_{\mathrm{m T}} $ thus requires, in principle, documentation of the performance history of the test standard T, the lower laboratory's standard L, and the higher laboratory's standard H. If the traceability of the measurement result is to be to H as it exists at time $ t_{\mathrm{m T}} $ , then a quantity $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{H}} $ will be needed as a correction factor or as an additional component of uncertainty associated with the measurement result. The definition of $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{H}} $ is, as above, the change in the time-average of the measurement quantity $ X^{\mathrm{H}} $ (denoted $ X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{H}} $ ), used in the control chart as a control parameter, between times $ t_{\mathrm{c L}} $ and $ t_{\mathrm{m T}} $

The testing laboratory's difficulty in assembling a comprehensive statement of traceability is then twofold. First, the lower laboratory does not typically provide the relevant performance history of the standard L in its calibration report, but gives only the uncertainty associated with the standard L at time $ t_{\mathrm{cT}} $ . Better laboratories will be able to provide such information, however, covering a reasonable period of time, on request. Second, for a system of standards only slightly more complex than that indicated in Fig. 7, or for traceability paths covering a span of several years, it could prove difficult to identify the standard H in the higher laboratory, especially if it has undergone modification, and even harder to obtain access to the quantity $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{H}} $ thus adding to the difficulty of assembling a comprehensive statement of traceability.

Fortunately, in many cases requiring traceability, these issues do not materialize due to the increasing levels of acceptable uncertainty as one descends the laboratory hierarchy. For example, if the testing laboratory in Fig. 7 only requires modest uncertainties when using the reference standard T, and the variations in the reference standards in the laboratories higher in the hierarchy are significantly below this uncertainty over the time period, then the testing laboratory can exclude these variations from the uncertainty analysis. However, if either $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{L}} $ or $ \delta X_{\mathrm{ave}}^{\mathrm{H}} $ are similar in magnitude to the uncertainty associated with using T, then they must be applied as a correction or incorporated into the final uncertainty of the measurement result using T at time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ in the statement of traceability. In either case the traceability analysis needs to show that this issue has been addressed. It is clear that when

higher laboratories establish sufficiently small uncertainties, uncertainty analyses in lower laboratories are simplified and record-keeping may be reduced.

The hierarchy of measurement and testing laboratories (national laboratories, secondary laboratories, testing laboratories, etc.) has been established in such a way that, ordinarily, a laboratory need only go one level up the traceability path to obtain the necessary calibration uncertainty, but usually will need to have traceability to more than one level up. For instance, in the example of Fig. 7, the testing laboratory should only have to go to the lower laboratory to obtain the needed calibration uncertainty, but will have to obtain information from the higher laboratories to have traceability to national standards.

If the testing laboratory had required a smaller uncertainty than that obtained from the lower laboratory, however, it could have sent its standard T directly to the higher laboratory for direct calibration against the standard H shortly before the measurement result required at time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ was obtained. This is frequently not possible for reasons of cost and time. When possible, however, such a procedure would allow the testing laboratory to assess how closely the measurement results agree for the two traceability paths, as well as how realistically the uncertainty evaluation of the measurement result at time $ t_{\mathrm{mT}} $ was carried out using the original traceability path.

## 4 Other issues concerning statements of traceability

## 4.1 Traceability when more than one standard is used for a calibration

Sometimes a lower laboratory's measurement standard L, when sent to a higher laboratory for calibration, requires that more than one reference standard H be used by the higher laboratory to perform the calibration adequately. The reference standard H is taken here to be a measuring instrument. Such is the case, for example, when the operating range of L does not overlap well with any of the standards H in the higher laboratory. Ehrlich et al. [10] have described a statistical algorithm for deriving the measurement uncertainty for pressure standards in such a situation. Under these circumstances the traceability of a measurement result is not to an individual instrument, but rather to a complex measurement system whose elements are the measurement instruments used, and the program of analysis is used to derive the uncertainty statement in the calibration report associated with the lower labo-

ratory's standard L. Care must be taken to document the historical data (e.g. using control charts) associated with these measurement systems so that traceability statements may be attached to measurements based on subsequent use of the lower laboratory's reference standard L.

## 4.2 Intrinsic standards and traceability

Another complex issue concerning traceability is the use of intrinsic standards. The key question is whether or not intrinsic standards can serve as stand-alone, turnkey systems requiring no prior comparisons with other standards while still providing inherent traceability to the other standards. Intrinsic standards differ from ordinary transfer standards in that they may be characterized in much the same way as primary standards. The ANSI/NCSL Z540-1-1994, American National Standard for Calibration - Calibration Laboratories and Measuring and Test Equipment - General requirements [11], states that intrinsic standards are "based on well-characterized laws of physics, fundamental constants of nature, or invariant properties of materials and make ideal stable, precise, and accurate measurement standards if properly designed, characterized, operated, monitored, and maintained". However, the document goes on to state: "Where intrinsic standards are used, the laboratory should demonstrate by measurement assurance techniques, interlaboratory comparisons, or other suitable means that its intrinsic standard measurement results are correlated with those of national or international standards". The National Conference of Standards Laboratories, at their July 1997 meeting [12], readdressed the issue of describing intrinsic standards and provided the following draft definition:

## Intrinsic (measurement) standard:

standard recognized as having or realizing, under its prescribed conditions of use and intended application, an assigned value the basis of which is an inherent physical constant or an inherent and sufficiently stable physical property.

## Notes:

1. An intrinsic standard usually consists of a device or system based on the requirements of a documented, consensus method.

2. The value of an intrinsic standard is assigned by consensus and does not need to be established by calibration or comparison with another standard Its uncertainty is determined by considering two components: (a) that associated with its consensus value and (b) that associated with its construction and implementation.

3. To establish and ensure stability and/or traceability, the value of an intrinsic standard and the uncertainty associated with its construction and implementation should be verified at appropriate intervals. Verification may be carried out either by applying a recognized, consensus test method or by intercomparisons among comparable standards. Such intercomparisons may be accomplished with standards in a local quality control system or with external standards including national and international standards.

The debate continues as to whether or not measurement results using intrinsic standards can be considered traceable to other measurement standards without benefit of direct intercomparison.

## 4.3 Multiple routes to traceability

When a calibration laboratory requires direct traceability of a measurement result to a reference standard in an NMI for one of the SI base quantities (mass, length, time, etc.), the calibration laboratory must go directly to the organization in the NMI that provides appropriate measurement services. But if a calibration laboratory requires direct traceability to an NMI for a measurement result of a derived quantity (e.g. pressure), then that laboratory can select from two options based on practicality. One is to have a standard calibrated against a reference standard for the derived quantity maintained at the NMI. The second is to obtain calibrations from the NMI for all the base quantities needed to develop a primary standard for the derived quantity. In the second case, metrological timelines must be developed and maintained for each of the base quantities.

Elaborating further, the first, most straightforward option is to go to the organization in the NMI that provides measurement services for the derived quantity. The organization provides this capability by developing primary standards based on measurement results which are themselves traceable to reference standards, maintained by other organizations in the NMI, that are representations of the SI base units. These representations are in turn based on realizations of the definitions of these units. Note that, while traceability to a reference standard that is a representation of an SI unit is possible, it is not appropriate to describe measurements as traceable to the SI unit itself, except possibly to the unit of mass, for which the SI unit is defined in terms of an international prototype. The preferred form is to describe a measurement result as "expressible" in terms of SI units [13].

The second option is to characterize a primary standard at the calibration laboratory by obtaining calibration services for the base quantities directly from the NMI. Alternatively, the calibration laboratory could obtain a primary standard characterized by another laboratory using calibration services obtained directly from the organizations in the NMI that develop and characterize reference standards that are representations of the SI base units. Examples of both options can be found in practice.

While both these options may satisfy the requirement that a measurement result be directly traceable to an NMI, it is important to note that the options may provide final measurement results and associated uncertainties that differ widely. This is because the primary standard developed by the calibration laboratory may be different from that developed by the NMI. Even if these standards are of identical design, minor differences in the implementations or models used for assessment of the final measurement results may change the values and uncertainties ascribed to the standards.

The use of comprehensive statements of traceability that contain metrological timelines of the types described above should provide ample opportunity to understand which traceability path is being used by the calibration laboratory, and what assumptions are being made.

## 4.4 Recalibration intervals

Related to the issue of traceability is the question of the frequency with which instruments should be recalibrated. Natural wear on an instrument due to the "hardness" and frequency of use, mishandling, environmental factors such as corrosion, and even the way an instrument is used, all have a bearing on instrument performance. A significant change in performance, or an uncertainty not within desirable limits, will typically warrant recalibration of the instrument. However, as discussed at length in [14], the cost of frequent recalibration balances the economic (and other) advantages of keeping an instrument within desirable limits of uncertainty.

An appropriate recalibration interval can be established by reference to the control chart which results from routine comparison with control standards. Furthermore, abnormalities can be quickly spotted and corrected by means of repair and recalibration. Determining when to recalibrate an instrument by using control standards usually results in less frequent recalibrations, saving time and money. Of course, the cost of purchasing and/or developing, using and maintaining control standards must be considered in the cost

accounting, but when traceability is required, the control standards must be used and become part of the cost of doing business anyway.

## 5 Considerations at the international level

The explicit examples presented above, using metrological timelines to portray the unbroken chain of comparisons in traceability relationships, apply specifically within a nation. In the growing global economy, the questions at the root of domestic traceability requirements, "What correction should be applied to a measurement result obtained at a given time with my instrument to match the result that would be obtained using the instrument (standard) to which traceability is desired? What is the uncertainty of this corrected measurement result?", also apply to measurement relationships that cross national boundaries. That is, someone performing a measurement in one country may need to know or demonstrate how the result of that measurement relates to what the result of that measurement would be if carried out by the NMI (or a calibration laboratory) of another country. While the concepts of traceability discussed above may sometimes be applicable to this situation, this is frequently not the case since NMIs do not typically calibrate each other's instruments, but rather compare measurement capabilities through international intercomparisons or round robins using intermediate or transfer standards. Depending on the details, results from such comparisons could sometimes be used to establish traceability to standards of another nation.

It is becoming more common to hear the term "equivalence" used to describe the metrological relationship when national boundaries are crossed. In these cases, the measurement capabilities of the various NMIs have been demonstrated to be equivalent within a certain range. The term traceability is being reserved for the more common usage of domestic traceability described in the main text above. The term equivalence, then, links measurements or standards at the same level. There is no hierarchical relationship between equivalent measurements or standards. Traceability, on the other hand, links measurements or standards through a hierarchical chain. Each link in the chain is either above or below the one next to it, even if they are in the same organization. These two terms signify very different concepts [15].

A point mentioned above, but worth reemphasizing here, is that in the VIM definition traceability is the property of the result of a measurement or the value of a standard, but not the property of an instrument or a laboratory. Thus, within this conventional definition, it

is not proper to think of an NMI being traceable to another NMI, even though measurement results could, in some cases, be established as traceable to standards in another NMI. From this perspective also, the term "equivalence" of national laboratories in particular metrological areas is preferred.

As noted earlier, it is not within the scope of this paper to develop a formalism using metrological timelines for describing the metrological relationships corresponding to traceability across national boundaries. To minimize confusion, words other than traceability should probably be used to describe the relationship between the result of a measurement in one country's NMI and the corresponding (hypothetical) result of the same measurement carried out by the NMI of another country if a direct link of calibrations (or equivalent for chemical or other standards) cannot be demonstrated. The Bureau International des Poids et Mesures (BIPM) is in the process of developing a mechanism by which the abilities of nations to perform nominally identical measurements can be compared and the results published [16], as a basis for establishing equivalence for use in developing mutual recognition agreements.

# Contributions to uncertainty of measurement in EMC tests using radiated fields

</div>

T. SCHRADER, M. SPITZER, K. MUNTER and J. GLIMM, Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB), Germany

## Abstract

In electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) susceptibility tests using high frequency rf-fields, for the selection of test severity (here: electrical field strength), the actual test standard EN 61000-4-3 admits interpretations which might lead to a much increased (but avoidable) uncertainty of measurement. Isotropic field sensors with a large measurement uncertainty could be used to determine the field strength distribution on the surface under test. On the other hand, according to the measurement instructions, measuring points with extreme field strength values can be neglected, including even those in which the test object is placed for the test. As a result, the correlation between the test severity to be applied and the actual severity is only slight. In addition, EN 61000-4-3 does not provide testing of the effect of tertiary waves, which is caused by a wave scattered at the device under test and its reflection at the field generator. With the measurement and simulation methods presented in this paper, these deviations are quantitatively determined for a concrete test object, and their influence on the uncertainty of measurement is stated.

## 1 Introduction

Since January 1, 1996, it has been mandatory for all electric and electronic instruments to be submitted to an EMC test pursuant to an EU Outline Directive [1]. This test serves to investigate whether and with which amplitude the instruments radiate rf-fields; furthermore, it is to be tested whether the instrument function is influenced by radiated rf- or conducted interferences. For measuring instruments subject to mandatory verification it is, for example, to be investigated whether

errors of indication due to high-frequency interferences lie within a defined error-limit range.

As early as 1983, performance of the emission tests was required for measuring instruments subject to mandatory verification by the PTB Testing Instructions No.17[2] which provide methods to investigate the behavior of instruments with respect to rf-fields.

Standard EN 61000-4-3 describes the method by which electromagnetic compatibility is to be tested and how the test severity for each field generator is to be determined. Despite these provisions, tests of the same instruments in different field generators (anechoic chambers) lead to different results, because the standard allows different interpretations as regards the selection of the test severity and as uncertainty estimations are not required.

In 1991, the PTB Electromagnetic Fields Laboratory launched a research project supported by the Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft (DFG) aimed at determining the quantities which essentially influence the uncertainty of measurement of electromagnetic compatibility tests in high-frequency rf-fields, and at developing methods of measurement allowing the individual uncertainty contributions to be quantitatively determined.

## 2 Comparison measurement of the electromagnetic compatibility of an instrument in different field generators

It has been shown in preliminary investigations that in different field generators, different interference effects may occur in the electronic instrument investigated, even though the electric empty test-field strength is the same. To reduce the large number of test parameters right from the start, a special instrument was developed for these investigations which, owing to battery current supply and optical data transmission, can be operated without a mains cable and other metallic connecting

lines. The electronic circuit, consisting of measuring transducer, measuring amplifier, A/D converter and micro-computer, is made of standard components. This instrument was exposed in different field generators to a high-frequency field of known field strength, and the response of the instrument was recorded between 20 MHz and 1 GHz [4], depending on the frequency range in which the field generator was used. As field generators a TEM cell, a GTEM cell, a stripline and different antennae in an anechoic chamber were used. Each time, rf-power was adjusted in such a way that a defined empty field strength of 15 V/m could be measured with an isotropic, i.e. omnidirectional sensitive field sensor.

In some cases, the test series evaluated showed resonances of the instrument's electromagnetic susceptibility, with a considerable frequency shift, in different field generators. A comparison of the field strengths, which in different field generators caused the same interference effect on the instrument (for example, a deviation of the value displayed from the uninfluenced displayed value by a defined amount) exhibits rather different necessary empty field strengths. The empty field strength set with an isotropic field sensor thus does not appropriately describe the test severity in a field generator required for obtaining reproducible results. This requires additional and more profound knowledge of the field structure in the field generators used.

## 3 Reference field generators

The requirements to be met by an ideal field generator in which reproducible results are obtained can be formulated on the basis of theoretical considerations. If the characteristics of the field generator differ from those of the theoretical reference, and if they are not corrected or appropriately taken into account when the test severity is set, they contribute to the uncertainty balance for electromagnetic compatibility tests. To metrologically determine errors which occur, appropriate measuring methods and instruments must be available.

Electromagnetic waves propagating in the free space without reflecting components form plane phase fronts of defined polarization with identical field strength. Electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular to one another and form a TEM (transversely electromagnetic) wave and are perpendicular to the direction of propagation of the wave; together with the direction of the wave vector k, they form an orthogonal spatial coordinate system. A metallic object scatters the energy of the incident field into a wide space. The scattered

wave thus generated propagates in the free space into infinity without further reflections. An empty, infinite space having the characteristics mentioned is regarded as a reference field generator [5] and is referred to as free space. As it is generally not permitted to perform susceptibility tests outside shielded rooms, the ideal field generator is to be theoretically investigated, for example, by numerical methods of calculation.

Taking these considerations as a starting point, two additional objectives were pursued within the scope of the research project:

- to determine the empty field structure in the field generators available as exactly as possible;

- to investigate the interaction between the equipment under test (EUT) inserted and the field generators.

To reach these objectives, an adequate measuring technique had to be developed, which would allow measurement of amplitude and phase data in a narrow frequency grid at high speed and with sufficient dynamics. This allows complete information on a three-dimensional field structure to be obtained.

## 4 Measuring technique

As a part of the DFG project, the measuring system "probe head with optical data transmission" was developed [5]. This system is based on analog-optical data transmission and transmits information from the frequency range from 500 kHz through 1 GHz both on the magnitude and the phase of the signal to be measured. An electronic circuit compensates for any position-dependent attenuations in the optical fiber so that a constant total attenuation is maintained in the transmission link. For this measuring system, different floating measuring heads with suitable impedance converters are available so that electric and magnetic fields as well as surface current densities on metallic test objects can be measured. In connection with a vector network analyzer, this system allows the measurement values to be rapidly determined, offering at the same time high frequency resolution and good dynamics better than 40 dB).

## 5 Analysis of the empty field

The measuring system described was used to determine the amplitudes and phase relations for three orthogonal field components in the electric and magnetic field, on the basis of which the spatial definition of the field structure (polarization) and the energy flux density

(Poynting vector) can be calculated. Polarization is represented by the geometrical figure described by the peak of the field vectors during a phase cycle. A distinction is made between linear, elliptical and circular polarization.

No tangential electric and no vertical magnetic field components exist on the surface of an object of infinite electric conductivity inserted in the field space; continuity of the fields is ensured by charges and currents on the surface of the conducting object. The orientation of the field vectors and the polarization of the electric and magnetic fields therefore determine the current distribution on the surface of the test object. Different current distributions on the same test object can firstly be attributed to differences in the empty field structures in different field generators; as will be shown later, the surface charge and current distribution also depend on the interaction between test object and field generator.

The current distributions and directions which can be measured on a closed part in the case of elliptical polarization differ from those measured for linear polarization. If the test object has slot apertures, the true load of the test object depends on the direction and amplitude of the current, because slot-shaped openings which act as polarization- and frequency-dependent filters couple in more strongly under transverse flow conditions than in the presence of currents flowing parallel to the slot. This is why elliptically polarized fields can produce a charge differing from that of linearly polarized fields.

Elliptical polarization is found in the near field of radiating rf antennae and when various field components with different propagation times, i.e. phase relations, are superimposed. Waves with different phase relations occur when the primary wave radiated by the antenna is reflected inside the field generator and thus forms a secondary wave. An antenna with a dipole radiation pattern generates, in the free space and at a sufficient distance, a linear polarization in the direction defined by the orientation of the antenna. In the field generator used, deviations of the polarizations are caused by:

- insufficient attenuation of the reflection of the absorber in shielded chambers at low frequencies;

- too short a distance between the measuring point and the radiating rf antenna;

- standing waves on the antenna feed cable which are due to unbalanced or non-symmetrically loaded symmetrical antennae;

- unsuitable cable arrangement, i.e. a cable running parallel to the electric field vector; and

In principle, such an elliptical polarization cannot be identified by isotropic field sensors, because a value for the equivalent field strength

The Poynting vector S describes the direction and temporal behavior of the power flux density in the highfrequency field [6]. Calculation of this vector requires measurement values of the complex amplitudes in three coordinate directions for the electric and magnetic field.

$$
\overrightarrow {S} = \overrightarrow {E} \times \overrightarrow {H}
$$

The Poynting vector can be separated into a timeinvariant effective component plus an oscillating reactive component. Together they generate differently shaped geometrical structures, depending on the field structure as, for example, lines, fans and cones. With a suitable field structure, the Poynting vector can generate a cone, two opposite tangents to the cone (or fan) passing through the origin describing an apex angle. The maximum apex angle of the cone generated by the Poynting vector describes the kind of energy transport in the high-frequency field and can be compared with the power factor cos $ \varphi $ in power engineering.

In free space and far away from the field source, an electromagnetic wave only transports effective power; the maximum cone apex angle is $ 0^{\circ} $ . If the wave, however, vertically strikes a reflector, it is reflected in itself. The fraction of the transported effective power is zero, resulting in a maximum apex angle of the Poynting vector of $ 180^{\circ} $ . An apex angle other than zero is found, for example, when the absorber wall in front of an antenna shows insufficient attenuation of the reflection.

Depending on the location on the antenna axis extended into space, frequency-dependent minima and maxima of the field strength occur instead of a monotonic decrease of the field strength ~1/r.

In unfavorable cases, this field structure can lead to an incorrectly weighted rated test field strength, because the field sensor and a test object introduced later differently screen the field space behind them. If the sensor is located in a field space point coinciding with a wave node, a low field strength will be measured. If the sensor is replaced by a large test object which due to its dimensions (comparable to or greater than the wavelength) is capable of screening the rear wall of the chamber, despite the small empty field strength, high currents flow on the surface of the test object which can couple into the latter and thus produce a disturbing effect. If the sensor was positioned in the place of an antinode, only small currents would flow on the test object despite high empty-field strengths. As the minima and maxima of the field strength shift along the extended antenna axis with $ \lambda/4 $ as a function of frequency, both too high and too small loads of the test object can occur during the emission tests. The determination of the Poynting vector prevents such a false assessment of the field quality, as in the case of standing waves a noticeable maximum apex angle of the Poynting vector results.

In accordance with the standard [3], the field strength distribution (field inhomogeneity) in a vertical test plane of size 1.5 m x 1.5 m is measured in 16 points selected at a distance of 0.5 m from one another. Neglecting up to four points having extreme field strength values, the minimum field strength forms the rated test load; the remaining maximum field strength values may exceed the rated value by 6 dB at most. As the four measurement values of arbitrary space points can be dispensed with (including those closest to the subsequent position of the test object) an enormous contribution to the uncertainty of measurement is produced here. At a vertical difference of 1 m, the field strength can change only to a limited extent; this is why the uncertainty contribution remains finite when measurement results are left out. From experience this contribution is estimated at 6 dB. If the test surface is reasonably reduced by, for example, doing without measuring points at the upper edge, the uncertainty can be stated in the representation of the empty field strength considering all other measurement values. In good fully anechoic chambers and in TEM wave-guides, field strength variations smaller than 3 dB are thus measured in the empty field.

When the phase information obtained in the 16 measuring points for the electric and the magnetic fields is evaluated, the wave-fronts and the wave impedance can be stated. In a fully anechoic room with ferrite absorbers, at 490 MHz, at a distance of 3 m from

the antenna, a slightly spherical wave-front was determined, which approximately corresponds to a plane wave-front. The characteristic wave impedance in the 16 points varied between 340 $ \Omega $ and 410 $ \Omega $ and the field inhomogeneity was 2 dB.

## 6 Analysis of the field with the test object

Besides the empty field structure, the propagation of the scattered waves generated on the test object determine the load of the test object in a susceptibility test. If the absorbers incorporated in the field generator attenuate the reflection only insufficiently or if they are mounted only on the rear wall as, for example, in the GETM cell, the scattered wave is reflected from the metallic surfaces and is superimposed as a tertiary wave to the primary/secondary field (tertiary wave effect) [5]. This modified field structure, which occurs only in the presence of the test object and therefore can never be seen in the empty field generator, in turn influences the current distribution on the test object so that - despite nominally equal empty field strength for linear polarization and equal direction - the test object can be differently loaded. The tertiary wave effect can lead to resonance-like field enhancement between the test object and, for example, the chamber floor. Furthermore, it is to be taken into account that the field distribution and thus the test load are influenced by accessories at different locations such as tripods, cameras, etc.

The method of the field factor [5] allows the tertiary wave effect to be investigated. The frequency-dependent field factor $ E^{*} $ is the quotient of the magnitudes of two field strengths which are determined in the same space point with (field strength E) and without test object (field strength $ E_{0} $ ) at the same transmitter power.

$$
E ^ {*} = \left| E / E _ {0} \right|
$$

This field factor $ E^{*} $ is a measure of the change in the field strength in the location close to the test object with regard to the uninfluenced empty field strength and depends - for a certain test object and a space point only on the properties of the field generator. As a result, this factor can be used for comparing different field generators. Basically, this field factor can be determined for any location close to the test object, the individual frequency responses being characteristic of a space point. For comparisons, this is therefore to be specified in a suitable way.

It is to be taken into consideration that maximum coupling into a housing takes place when the slotted front plate of the otherwise closed housing faces the

antenna. This is why the field factor is determined in front of the test object. Furthermore, the change in the frequency response due to the introduction of the test object should contain at least one minimum and a maximum. The frequency position of the minimum allows the exact distance of the sensor from the housing to be determined. For a cubic test object with an edge length of 30 cm, a distance of 15 cm was selected. At frequencies below 100 MHz, a larger housing is to be used, and a greater distance between sensor and test object is to be set.

As there is no tertiary wave effect, the calculated free space field factor $ E_{F R}^{*} $ constitutes the curve of limiting values. The weaker the dependence of the field factor of a field generator on the curve of limiting values of the field factor in the free space, the better the reproduction of the free space by the field factor.

$$
E / E _ {\mathrm {F R}} ^ {*}
$$

The theoretical limiting value curve was numerically calculated by the moments method ("CONCEPT II" program [7]) and a finite integration technique method ("MAFIA" program [8]) and the curve obtained was compared with the values determined in a free-spacelike measuring environment. For this purpose, a test apparatus was set up at the antenna test assembly of the Technology Centre of the Telekom at Brück (near Berlin). The cubic test object with an edge length of 30 cm and the field sensor were arranged on a wooden tower at a height of 10 m, and the radiating rf antenna was operated in horizontal polarization, arranged on a vehicle-mounted mast pointing upwards at an angle of $ 3 0^{\circ} $ . The distance of the sensor from the test object was selected at 15 cm and also allowed for in the numerical calculations.

The field factor was determined not only in this low-reflection test set-up but also in 21 field generators operated by industry and public authorities. Anechoic chambers of different sizes and with different absorber designs were investigated, the test apparatus and the test set-up being, however, always the same. While the measurements carried out in the free space test assembly and numerical calculations yielded comparable results, the comparison between the curve of limiting values and the results of the comparison measurements in part exhibits considerable differences. Though the standard criteria for a measuring environment are complied with, the field factors determined differ from the ideal curve by up to 10 dB.

The different non-ideal properties of field structure and field generators such as, for example, elliptical polarization and tertiary wave effect, lead to loading of the test object different from that of the free space; this difference can be demonstrated by the field factor of a housing. The field factor essentially depends on the

reflectivity of the environment r and the reflectivity of the test object $ \gamma_{rcs} $ , r being a function of the location considered, the frequency and the angle of incidence of the wave and $ \gamma_{rcs} $ on the size, conductivity and shape of the test object investigated.

The field strength $ E_{m} $ which can be measured in front of a test object is composed of several field components, each consisting of three components of the electric and three components of the magnetic field strength with complex amplitude. The wave $ E_{in} $ incident in the measuring point consists of the primary wave $ E_{p} $ and the secondary wave $ E_{s} $ . The component $ E_{s} $ results also in the empty field generator from reflection of the primary wave, for example, at the reflecting floor and can be described by $ r\cdot E_{p} $ . It can be seen that all side walls as well as the floor and ceiling of the field generator should attenuate reflections as strongly as possible to reduce this component of the field load.

The incident wave is reflected at the test object and gives rise to a scattered wave $ \gamma_{rcs}\cdot E_{in} $ . This scattered wave propagates in the field generator and can be reflected at the metallic walls of the field generator and give rise to the tertiary wave $ r\cdot\gamma_{rcs}\cdot E_{in} $ if the reflection is not sufficiently attenuated or if absorbers do not exist at all. This tertiary wave in turn propagates in the field generator and changes the field distribution. As a result of the reflection at the test object, the partial wave $ r\cdot\gamma_{rcs}^{2}\cdot E_{in} $ is formed.

The phenomena shown above can be quantitatively described in an equation assuming that the sensor itself does not reflect. The influence of the sensor which practically is only small was determined with a very small transfer sensor. In the general form this equation reads as follows:

$$
\vec {E} _ {m} = \left[ 1 + \underline {{r}} + \underline {{r}} \cdot \gamma_ {r c s} + \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {\infty} \sum_ {j = i - 1} ^ {\infty} \underline {{r}} ^ {j} \cdot \underline {{\gamma}} ^ {i} \right] \cdot \vec {E} _ {p} (f, g)
$$

where:

$$
\begin{array}{l} r = \mathrm {r e f l e c t i v i t y} \mathrm {o f t h e e n v i r o n m e n t}, \mathrm {a n d} \\ \gamma_ {r c s} = \mathrm {r e f l e c t i v i t y} \mathrm {o f t h e t e s t o b j e c t}. \\ \end{array}
$$

This equation takes all likely field components into account, for example also all multiple reflections of the incident wave (waveguide). As, unlike the amplitude of the tertiary wave, the amplitudes of the higher-order waves decrease due to losses caused by multiple reflections, the series is terminated after the second term neglecting all with an exponent greater than two.

In this case, equation (6) describes the field strength which can be measured in front of the test object more clearly, in particular when equation (7) which is valid for the free space is considered for comparison. The influence of the tertiary wave effect is described, for example, by the sum in equation (6).

$$
\vec {E} _ {m} = \left[ 1 + \gamma_ {r c s} + \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {2} \underline {{r}} ^ {i} \left(\underline {{\gamma}} _ {r c s} ^ {i} + \underline {{\gamma}} _ {r c s} ^ {i + 1}\right) \right]. \left[ (1 + r) \cdot \vec {E} _ {p} (f, g) \right]
$$

In the case of an ideal test environment, the field strength which can be measured in front of the test object is formed only by the superposed fractions of the primary wave and the scattered wave generated by the test object. Equation (6) is simplified as follows:

$$
\overrightarrow {E} _ {m} = \left(1 + \underline {{\gamma}} _ {r c s}\right) \cdot \overrightarrow {E} _ {p} (f, g) ]
$$

## 7 Surface current densities

The scattered waves emanating from the test object during susceptibility tests are caused by the current and charge distributions occurring on the surface of the test object. In the case of metallic housings preventing field penetration or direct field incidence, the surface currents and charges couple the energy into the shielded housing. As the coupling which takes place through openings of different shapes and sizes can be calculated and is thus known, the comparability of emission measurements can be investigated through the current density or the charge distribution on the surface of closed bodies.

The current distribution on conducting bodies can be determined according to the following formula from the tangential magnetic fields:

$$
\overrightarrow {J} = \overrightarrow {n} \times \overrightarrow {H} _ {\tan}
$$

When a suitable half-loop-shaped magnetic field sensor with optical data transmission is permanently mounted on a sphere, the current distribution can be measured in any space point and in any direction due to the invariance of the reflection characteristics with respect to incident fields when the sphere is rotated [5]. For the free space, in the case of a plane incident wave, the current distribution on a sphere can be calculated analytically [7] or numerically, for example by the moments method [8] or the finite integration technique [9]. The test load can be indicated for any space point on the sphere from the data obtained when the current distribution measured in a field generator is compared with the current distribution calculated for the same test object. For a cubic test object, comparisons between surface current densities - normalized to the empty field strength - measured in a stripline and numerically calculated for a free field basically show the same frequency response, but in detail a much more strongly rippled frequency response is measured due to the tertiary wave effect in the stripline [10].

## 8 Measurement of parasitic currents on cables

Another contribution to the uncertainty of measurement is caused by a scarcely reproducible arrangement

of test object cables and leads in field regions whose strength is almost unknown. The unknown orientation of the cables relative to the field vectors, different cable lengths and a non-defined system impedance lead to differences in the frequencies and qualities of resonance locations on the cables connected to the test object. Small changes in the position of cables in the HF field result already in deviations of the coupled-in conduction current of several dB.

The influence of the test environment on the HF current coupled onto test object cables was also investigated with the aid of the ToM system. A mains cable 0.5 m in length was inserted into a metallic cube via a coupler for non-heating appliances. Inside the test object the cable was connected to the metallic housing with a short-circuiting link. A current probe arranged on the link with optical data transmission measures the HF current in the frequency range up to 400 MHz. On the mains side, the cable was operated in open circuit and defined for different directions, and the change of the HF current was recorded at unchanged emitted power.

Tests in the small anechoic chamber of the PTB and in the GTEM cell 1500 show, for example, different amplitudes of the current at the resonant frequency of 125 MHz on the vertically oriented cable although the same empty field strength was set with an isotropic field sensor. Owing to vertical polarization of the electric field in the GTEM cell with only small secondary components, a higher amplitude of the conduction current is achieved than in the anechoic chamber with longitudinal field components in the direction of propagation. Thus a field sensor indicating a field strength according to equation (1) would not be suitable for setting the test load for magnetic compatibility tests even if it were isotropic.

## 9 Uncertainty budget for radiated electromagnetic compatibility tests

Thus the essential parameters limiting the reliability of EMC susceptibility tests are known. A phase-sensitive optical-analog transmission system was used to develop measuring procedures for the frequency range up to 1 GHz, allowing the individual contributions to the uncertainty in the representation of quantities disturbing the tests to be determined. To limit the influencing parameters, only the uncertainty in the representation of a surface current density on the front plate of a cubic test object with an edge length of 30 cm due to radiated fields is investigated. The test object is electrically isolated, i.e. the influence of connected cables on the uncertainty is therefore not taken into account. All

individual contributions of the additional influence quantities $ X_{i} $ to the overall uncertainty are to be determined in order that the uncertainty in the representation of a result quantity might be stated [11]. In this case, the uncertainty analysis for a disturbing quantity to be produced in electromagnetic compatibility tests is carried out. As the current density distribution J on the surface of test objects is a measure of the rf fields stressing of a test object in different field generators, which can be easily compared, the surface current density is investigated as the quantity to be realized. The factors influencing the field generator, the calibration of the sensor and the empty field are considered as sources of the uncertainty of measurement with respect to the reference surface current density $ J_{FR} $ determined in free space:

$$
J = J _ {F R} + \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {1 1} X _ {i}
$$

All input quantities are considered to be uncorrelated, as they were determined in different test set-ups.

## 10 Conclusions

Due to tolerances in the interpretation of the measurement instructions, emission tests according to the standard admit a simple uncertainty in the representation of the quantity disturbing the tests "surface current density" of $ \pm 93\% $ . This clearly shows that an uncertainty consideration is necessary in any case and that the uncertainty should be stated as an essential part of the test result.

When the knowledge acquired is taken into account, test results of substantially enhanced reliability can be achieved even with the existing standard and without considerably changing the field generators (see Table 1).

## Introduction

Metrology activities are generally classed into scientific, industrial and legal metrology. Until recently in Zimbabwe, there was no formalized structure responsible for scientific and/or industrial metrology, and the Trade Measures Department (TMD) of the Ministry of Industry and Commerce is responsible for legal metrology.

Realizing that metrology has a key role to play in social and economic development, efforts have been made to develop and formalize metrology via the projects described below, which are at varying stages of implementation.

(1) Establishment of an NMI with capabilities in terms of legislation, staff and physical facilities to ensure the accuracy, reliability, credibility and traceability to the SI units;

(2) Establishment of the National Metrology System composed of the NMI, under which measuring and calibrating facilities operate, including commercial calibration centres duly accredited by the NMI which in turn provide metrology services to all sectors;

(3) Establishment of the necessary legal measures, the development of laws, regulations, standard instructions and guidelines for metrology institutes, industrial enterprises etc. and the provision of a legal basis for the adoption of the International System of Units.

## Legal metrology in Zimbabwe

The TMD is responsible for legal metrology within the ambit of the Trade Measures Act (TMA) of 1974 and its amendments, which provides for the following:

- definition and authorization of the units of measurement which may be used in trade;

- provision for the uniformity of measuring equipment;

- provision for the assizing and reassizing of measuring equipment;

- provision for consumer protection regarding the sale of articles (and other transactions) by measurement or number.

Thus, the TMA's primary objective is to control measuring and weighing instruments used in trade as well as to ensure consumer protection. To this end, the TMD is tasked with:

## (a) Maintenance of measuring standards

The TMD maintains a standard weight set and standard volumetric glass pipettes from 10 ml to 5 l. In

the past the testing and verification of these standards have been carried out by the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) in South Africa. The Zimbabwe standards are used for the verification of local standards which are, in turn, used to verify working standards. The latter are used for assizing and re-assizing trade weighing and measuring instruments; in this manner local traceability is guaranteed. The TMA and its amendments is "silent" as relates to international traceability - an issue which is necessary for the standardization and harmonization of intra-state and inter-state trade.

## (b) Pattern approval, verification certification of weighing/measuring instruments used in trade

These activities are provided for in the different sections of the TMA. Issuing of a certificate with regard to the suitability for use in trade of any measuring equipment of a specified design or pattern is undertaken. Measuring equipment deemed not suited for trade weighing and measuring are issued with a written rejection. Assizing and reassizing of all trade weighing and measuring equipment is carried out periodically by the Superintendent from the TMD. In the case of dispute a document issued by the TMD is considered prima facie evidence in legal proceedings.

## (c) Aspects of packaging control

The TMA prescribes requirements for the marking of pre-packed items - measurement of the item to be marked on the wrapper or container in the prescribed manner by reference to an authorized unit. Accuracy of net contents is also a requirement. The TMA, however, does not make provision in respect of the standardization of pack sizes for certain mass-consumption items.

## Scientific and industrial metrology in Zimbabwe

These specific metrology activities are to be provided by the SIRDC through the NMI-a proposal which has been endorsed by the Government. Investment will take the form of buildings, equipment and human resources:

## Metrology buildings

The planned metrology laboratories are to be of an internationally acceptable standard having been so designed after intense local and international consultations. A major part of the investment is directed towards ambient control - temperature and humidity monitoring and controlling and real-time pressure measurement Such temperature control as $ 2 0 \pm0. 5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 2 3 \pm1^{\circ} \mathrm{C} $ and humidity $ 4 5 \pm5 \% $ will be easily achievable. Where required, special fittings have been specified, such as for example pressurized water, compressed air, etc. Construction work is in progress.

## Metrology equipment

The equipping of the NMI has been planned such that it will have the capacity to provide services and

facilities to include, at least, the following:

- maintaining certified national standards of measurement traceable to SI units;

- calibration and metrology/ measurement services;

- assisting in the provision of accurate facilities for implementing quality control, testing certification marking schemes laboratory accreditation activities and research activities;

- promotion of measurement accuracy, reliability and credibility through access to scientific and technical literature, conducting training programs and through the dissemination of information and promotional materials;

- undertaking prototype approval by type tests of measurement technology manufactured in the country or imported.

The above is only realizable by having available several measuring and calibration facilities duly accredited according to internationally recognized criteria. The following measurement laboratories are to be made available:

## Human resources development

The cost-intensive investment in setting up and equipping environmentally controlled metrology laboratories is to be complemented by the development of a highly qualified and well trained scientific and technical staff. ## Conclusion

The need for a formalized metrology infrastructure cannot be overemphasized but with today's global emphasis on ISO certification, traceability of measurements, harmonization of trade practices and procedures are being considered as high priority in Zimbabwe. It is realized that rapid industrialization, expansion of industrial production, self-reliance, socio-economic development is best achieved with the strengthening of infrastructures of metrology, standardization, testing and quality control (MSTQ) and other associated services.

## 1 The Southern African Development Community (SADC)

The SADC, established in 1992 to help member countries move towards economic integration as a trading block, came into existence when the Southern African Development Coordination Conference (SADCC) was transformed into the SADC. Until recently the SADC comprised 12 member countries (Angola, Botswana, Lesotho, Malawi, Mauritius, Mozambique, Namibia, South Africa, Swaziland, Tanzania, Zambia and Zimbabwe), and during an SADC summit in 1997, the SADC heads of state agreed to accept the Democratic Republic of Congo (previously Zaire) and Seychelles as members.

The SADC secretariat, headed by the Executive Secretary, is located in Botswana and financed by members' contributions. The SADC is in the process of simultaneously implementing the two basic models for regionalism, sectoral cooperation and trade integration. Various sectors have been defined, each coordinated by a single SADC country, in which sectoral cooperation is being pursued. One of these sectors is Trade and Industry, coordinated by the SADC Industry and Trade Coordinating Division (SITCD) attached to the Tanzanian Department of Trade and Industry in Dar-es-Salaam.

## 2 New Regional Accreditation, Metrology and Standards Infrastructure for SADC

The SADC expert group on Standardization, Quality Assurance and Metrology (SQAM) commissioned a study on the state of SADC metrology and approved the

recommendation contained in the resulting "Report on the Study for a Program on an SADC Metrology System", G. B. Makando, 1994, to establish an SADC regional metrology infrastructure. As a consequence, a Project Steering Committee on the Establishment of a SADC Metrology Network was formed, which held its first meeting prior to the 1996 SQAM meeting in Pretoria. The committee consisted of SITCD representatives and delegates from South African (NML, SANAS) and Zimbabwean (SIRDC) metrology and accreditation organizations. It considered and finalized a detailed project proposal for an SADC planning workshop, at which the SADC strategy, an action plan and a timetable for the establishment of the SADC metrology infrastructure would be developed. This proposal was approved by the SQAM.

At the invitation of the South African NML, a regional planning session of SADC member countries was held in Pretoria from October 7-11, 1996. It decided to create three regional bodies to represent the national organizations responsible for the top-level measurement standards (SADCMET), accreditation bodies (SARAC) and legal metrology organizations grouped in other trading blocks.

This decision was endorsed at a meeting of the SADC Expert Group on SQAM in Port Louis, Mauritius from 17-21 April 1997. At the same meeting it was decided to form a fourth regional body, the SADC Standardization Cooperation (SADCSTAN), to deal with regional standards and standardization matters.

All four regional organizations consist of a governing body, in which all SADC member countries are represented by one member each. The SITCD in Dar-es Salaam is also represented and acts as the interface to the official SADC structures, for example the SADC Sectoral Committee of Ministers. The chair of each

governing body rotates amongst member countries on a regular basis. Members of each of the governing bodies have observer status at meetings of the governing bodies of the other three regional organizations.

The executive portion of the regional bodies is a secretariat headed by a regional coordinator. The secretariat, which carries out the day to day running of the organization concerned, is staffed and financed by one of the national member organizations, which volunteers to carry out this task at any particular time. It is responsible for progress with the approved working program, for which it also has access to the support of Technical Committees, which draw their members from the pool of technical experts in the region.

From 1998, the activities of the four regional bodies are reported annually at a meeting of a Coordinating Committee chaired by the SITCD secretariat. All SADC member countries are represented at this Committee by a delegation reflecting their national organizations involved in standardization, accreditation and metrology. This Committee also deals with regional issues in the area of conformity assessment and the existing SADC Expert Group on SQAM is disbanded.

## 13 Recognition of the equivalence of the national standards of the USA and the Republic of South Africa for the SI unit of luminous intensity

An agreement recognizing the equivalence of the candela as realized by the NIST and the NML was signed between the two institutions on the opening of the NML's 50th anniversary Metrology Week on 26 August 1997. An excerpt of the text of the agreement is given below.

As adherents to the Meter Convention, the United States of America and the Republic of South Africa realize their units of measurement in accordance with the definitions of the International System of Units (SI) adopted by the General Conference of Weights and Measures.

The [...] NIST and the CSIR collaborate in research to verify and improve measurement standards, both bilaterally and through the BIPM.

On the basis of this collaboration, the Directors of NIST and the CSIR recognize that the national standards for the practical realization of the SI unit of luminous intensity in the USA and in the Republic of South Africa are equivalent to 7.4 parts in $ 1 0^{3} $ , within a combined standard uncertainty of 3.3 parts in $ 1 0^{3} $ .

This recognition of equivalence will remain in effect for an unlimited period, but may be withdrawn by either party at any time. While acknowledging the equivalence of national standards, [...] neither NIST nor CSIR accepts any responsibility for the correctness of any particular measurement referred to the relevant national standard maintained by the other organization.

# Legal Metrology in Indonesia

</div>

Legal metrology activities in Indonesia started with the passing of the 1923 Ijk Ordonnantie (Weights and Measures Law), which was revised and in 1981 the Legal Metrology Law came into force, largely based on OIML Document D1. The 1981 Law replaced the intermediate 1949 Verification Law, which no longer conformed to present-day conditions. Measures against criminal activities in weights and measures also include the Criminal Law, and to implement the Legal Metrology Law various Government Decrees have also been published.

The main activities of the Directorate of Metrology (which is a division of the Ministry of Industry and Trade) and its regional offices are to conserve measurement standards, supervise, verify and re-verify measuring instruments used for legal purposes, and promote metrological information.

Indonesia became an OIML Member State in September 1960

and has also been a Member State of the APLMF since its establishment in 1994. OIML Recommendations are used as a basis for national laws.

The Directorate is the national Issuing Authority for type approval certificates for imported measuring instruments and pattern approval certificates for domestic instruments.

Besides verification and reverification activities, the Directorate is a member of the National Calibration Network and provides calibration services for industrial measuring instruments.

# Metrological assurance of uniformity of measurements in the Russian economy

</div>

PROF. DR. L.K. ISSAEV, Vice-President, Gosstandart of Russia and CIML Member

In 1993 the new Russian Federation Law on "Assurance of measurement uniformity" was passed by Parliament, thus establishing the legal basis for ensuring uniformity of measurements and regulating the relations between the Russian Federation State administration bodies and all those concerned with the activities of production, utilization, repair, sale and import of measuring instruments (see Fig. 1). This Law aimed to protect citizens' rights and interests and to establish legal order in the Federation's economy in the light of the previous negative consequences of inaccurate measurement results [1, 2].

Three all-Russian scientific and technical Conferences entitled "Metrological Assurance of the Russian National Economy", held in 1994 and 1995 in Penza and 1997 in Moscow, covered the problems of implementing the Law and aimed to encourage the exchange of experiences gained. During the 1997 Conference (17-21 November), 237 leading metrological specialists from factories, organizations, establishments, federal bodies of executive authorities, bodies of the Russian territories' State metrological service, state scientific metrological centers and institutes took part. Delegates came from a number of CIS countries, including the Republics of Ukraine, Belarus, Armenia, Moldova and Kirghizstan.

During this Conference 42 lectures were delivered and debated, relating to:

- the Russian measurement system [3,4];

- state metrological supervision;

- traceability;

- uncertainty of measurement results;

- implementation of ISO 5725 and ISO 4259 standards;

- international cooperation in certification and calibration of measuring instruments;

- certified reference materials and certified reference data;

- development and certification of measurement procedures;

- training, etc.

Among the problems discussed were those encountered in assuring measurement uniformity applied to health care, ecology, safety, industry, and other fields (see Fig. 2). Special attention was paid to the peculiarities of implementing the Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement [5] in Russia. This was the second time that the Guide was discussed in detail - the first occasion was at the May 1996 Saint Petersburg joint meeting of the Russian Metrology Academy Coordinating Council, the Scientific-Technical Section on metrological problems of the Gosstandart of Russia, and the Scientific Council of VNIIM D.I. Mendeleyev.

The joint meeting proceedings were published in the Russian Journal "Izmeritelnaya Tekhnika" and its supplement "Metrologiya" No.11997.

It was noted that many new documents related to the measurement procedures (GOST R 8.56396 [6]) and to tests (GOST R 8.56897 "State System of Assurance of Measurement Uniformity - Attestation of Test Equipment - General Provisions") and some others related to the accreditation of metrological services for the right to certify and expertise metrological documents, etc. were developed and implemented. In preparation for implementing GOST R 8.563-96 from July 1, 1997, four regional conferences were organized during 1997 in addition to the all-Russian Conference.

It was recalled that in accordance with the President of Russia's April 1996 Decree, the State Award to the "Honored Metrologist of the Russian Federation" was introduced. At the present time, 53 metrologists from various fields have been awarded this title.

During the 3rd Conference on Metrological Assurance of the Russian National Economy it was decided:

- to consider it appropriate to announce 20th May as being Metrology Day in commemoration of the signing of the Meter Convention in 1875;

- to prepare a number of changes and additions to the Law on "Assurance of Measurement Uniformity" for acceptance by Government and Parliament as a result

of the 4-year experience gained in the economy;

- to establish the special scientifictechnical commission for CIS interstate measurement standards at the Gosstandart of Russia;

- to anticipate the development of normative and metrological support for each federal program;

- to implement the ISO 10012 standards;

- to elaborate during 1998 a Practical Manual for using the Guide [5], primarily taking into account those measurements related to scales of reference values which do not use primary measurement standards for the units of physical values (e.g. analytical measurements and some types of testing);

- to complete the development of the National Accreditation System of measurement laboratories;

- to pay much attention to the implementation of GOST R 8.563-96 as a basis for assurance of measurement results uniformity;

- to carry out the metrological inspection of accredited test laboratories and the metrological expertise of documents for the Services Certification System;

- to revise GOST 27384-87 "Water. Rates of measurement error of characteristics of of composition and properties" with regard to reference uncertainties for water quality control by a joint team of specialists from the Gosstandart of Russia, the State Committee for Ecology, the Ministry of Health and experts from the System of Accreditation of Analytical Laboratories [7];

- to request the Ministry of Higher and Professional Education to ensure that metrology is taught in all Russian technical universities.

Despite the national character of the Conference, some foreign firms also sent representatives, and

this event was all the more important for scientists and all those involved in metrology for another reason - namely that it was held in 1997 as part of the World Quality Day Session program.

It was decided to organize the 4th Conference in October 1999 in the ancient Russian city of Yaroslavl'.

## Secretariat: USA

## TC 9/SC 3 Weights

The Office of Standards Management of the United States Department of Commerce's National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) hosted a meeting of TC 9/SC 3 Weights from May 19-22, 1998 in Gaithersburg, Maryland to discuss the requirements and test procedures for weights of Classes $ \mathrm{E}_{1} $ to $ \mathrm{M}_{3}. $

Chairperson: Deborah McGann Ripley, US Technical Advisor, Office of Standards Management, NIST.

Participation: 22 participants; 13 delegates representing 8 of the 21 P-members, one delegate from Brazil (as an observer) and one delegate from the BIML. In addition, 5 delegates representing two manufacturers, one consultant and one observer were present. None of the 5 O-members attended.

## Main points

## General

In 1996, the OIML Task Force Group composed of Norway, Sweden, Denmark and Finland was created to develop test procedures and a test report format to supplement the requirements of Recommendation R 111 Weights of Classes $ E_{1}, $ $ E_{2}, $ $ F_{1}, $ $ F_{2}, $ $ M_{1}, $ $ M_{2}, $ $ M_{3}. $

In September 1996, this "Nordic Group" had already produced its $ 7^{\mathrm{th}} $ drafts of R 111-2, Part 1: Testing Procedures for Weights and Part 2: Pattern Evaluation Report. The Nordic Group and the BIML then organized the workshop Practical Test Procedures for Classes $ E_{1} $ to $ M_{3} $ Weights held in Borås, Sweden (October 2-4, 1996), at which questions and concerns regarding R 111, R 111-2 Part 1, R 111-2 Part 2 and other related Recommendations were raised.

After circulating the $ 8^{\mathrm{th}} $ drafts of R 111-2 Parts 1 and 2 and compiling the comments received, the Secretariat

for TC 9/SC 3 prepared a First Committee Draft (1 CD) of R 111-2 Parts 1 and 2 and decided to hold an International Working Group meeting on May 19-22, 1998 in Gaithersburg, where the above documents would be discussed. It was also felt essential to review and modify Recommendations R 33, R 47, R 52 and even perhaps some sections of R 111 to ensure that requirements are consistent throughout these Recommendations.

## Topics covered

The main issues discussed over the four-day meeting can be presented as they appeared in the Agenda circulated prior to the meeting, i.e.:

1 Open issues in OIML R 111-2 Part 1, First Committee Draft;

2 Open issues in OIML R 111-2 Part 2, First Committee Draft;

3 Proposed revisions to OIML R 111;

4 Proposed consolidation of all parts of OIML R 111 into one document; and

5 Secretariat's review and proposal regarding OIML R 33, R 47 and R 52.

The greater part of the meeting was devoted to reviewing the extensive comments and proposals compiled relating to item 1 above.

An informative statistical process control section was reviewed and accepted in principle by the participants. A full section on calculation of uncertainties was discussed, expanded and clarified. Furthermore, it was decided that the creation of an Annex or supplementary informative document providing examples of uncertainty calculations for R 111 would be useful and should be developed once the final version of R 111-2 Part 1: Testing Procedures for Weights is completed.

Extensive discussions then took place on the test procedures and associated calculations for the measurement of test weight density, magnetization, magnetic

susceptibility and surface roughness. A table was developed to establish when such tests should be performed i.e., on all or a sample of weights, only on initial verification or also on subsequent verifications, etc. In addition, another table was presented and refined, showing which density determination method was best suited for the different classes and capacities of weight.

Regarding item 2, an interesting dilemma arose: What would the OIML certificate issued under R 111 pertain to? This question was central to the subsequent exchanges since it is necessary to know what is required by the certificate to decide how information is to be treated. For example, would the certificate be recognized as the international standard format for a calibration report? Is the certificate a type approval document for a given weight or a set of weights or a type approval document for a given model of weight or set of weights? Furthermore, would it be allowable to have a set of weights composed of weights of different classes as is often the case when small weights of Class $ \mathrm{E}_{2} $ or $ \mathrm{F}_{1} $ are included within a $ \mathrm{F}_{1} $ or $ \mathrm{F}_{2} $ kit, etc? The Secretariat requested that the BIML investigate this problem.

Other topics such as requirements for temperature stability within the calibration laboratory and/or verification area were revised. Also, the pertinence of applying conventional mass values when the correction due to buoyancy and the associated uncertainty is too large was questioned and different proposals were studied. Germany proposed a solution where the current table of allowable density ranges would be modified to take into account upper limits of geographic altitude above which the errors due to buoyancy would be too great and the weights would not be allowed to be used; the Secretariat will provide an alternative proposal giving the option of using true mass at altitudes where conventional mass presents a problem.

Regarding items 3 and 4, the Secretariat proposed to transfer the R 111-2 Part 1 and the R 111-2 Part 2 documents into an Annex to R 111, where sections would either be included in mandatory or informative annexes.

It was agreed that R 33 should be revised and renamed from "International Recommendation" to "International Document". This work will be accomplished by a small working group composed of Germany, USA and France.

The Secretariat proposed to withdraw R 47 and R 52. The International Working Group decided that R 47 should not be withdrawn but should be reviewed modified and possibly incorporated into R 111. It was also decided that a ballot should be circulated to Member States to identify if hexagonal weights (R 52) are still in use or if this Recommendation is now obsolete and should be deleted.

## Meeting decisions

Since only 8 of the 21 P-members were present, decisions made at the Gaithersburg meeting will probably be circulated for a ballot vote.

No future meeting has been planned.

## TC 9/SC 3 Poids

## Secrétariat: États Unis d'Amérique

La réunion de TC 9/SC 3 s'est tenue au Office of Standards Management of the United States Department of Commerce's National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) à Gaithersburg, Maryland du 19 au 22 mai 1998. Le but principal de la réunion était de discuter les exigences et les méthodes d'essais pour les masses des Classes $ \mathrm{E}_{1} $ à $ \mathrm{M}_{3}. $

Présidente: Deborah McGann Ripley, US Technical Advisor, Office of Standards Management, NIST.

Participation: 22 participants; 13 délégués représentant 8 des 21 membres-P, un délégué du Brésil (entant qu'observateur) et une déléguée du BIML. Étaient également présents 5 délégués représentant deux fabricants, un consultant et un observateur. Aucun des 5 membres-O n'était présent.

## Points principaux

## Général

En 1996, le groupe de travail OIML Task Force Group composé de la Norvège, de la Suède, du Danemark et de la Finlande a été créé afin de développer des méthodes d'essais et un format du rapport d'essais supplémentaires à la Recommandation R 111 Poids des classes $ E_{1}, E_{2}, F_{1}, F_{2}, M_{1}, M_{2}, M_{3}. $

En septembre 1996, ce "Groupe nordique" a complété le septième projet de R 111-2, Partie 1: Testing Procedures for Weights et Partie 2: Pattern Evaluation Report. Le groupe nordique et le BIML a ensuite organisé l'atelier Practical Test Procedures for Classes $ E_{1} $ to $ M_{3} $ Weights qui s'est tenu à Borås, Suède, octobre 2-4, 1996 où plusieurs questions ayant trait à R 111, R 111-2 Partie 1, R 111-2 Partie 2 et autres Recommandations pertinentes furent soulevées.

Après circulation du huitième projet de R 111-2 Parties 1 et 2 et après compilation des commentaires reçus, le Secrétariat pour TC 9/SC 3 a préparé un premier Projet de Comité (1 CD) pour R 111-2 Parties 1 et 2 et a décidé de convoquer une réunion du Groupe international de travail du 19 au 22 mai 1998 à Gaithersburg afin de réviser les documents ci-dessus. Il semblait aussi essentiel de réviser et de modifier les Recommandations R 33, R 47, R 52 et peut-être même certaines sections de la R 111 afin d'assurer une application consistante des exigences à travers ces Recommandations.

## Sujets évoqués

La majorité des points discutés tout au long des quatre jours de réunion peuvent être divisés tels qu'ils étaient présentés à l'Ordre du jour pour les réunions. C'est-à-dire:

1 Points de discussions portant sur OIML R 111-2 Partie 1, Premier Projet de Comité;

2 Points de discussions portant sur OIML R 111-2 Partie 2, Premier Projet de Comité;

3 Révisions proposées portant sur OIML R 111;

4 Consolidation proposée de toutes les parties de OIML R 111 dans un document unique; et

5 Révision du Secrétariat et suggestions ayant trait à OIML R 33, R 47 et R 52.

La plus grande part de la réunion fut dévouée à réviser le grand nombre de commentaires et suggestions amassés portant sur le premier point ci-dessus.

Une section sur des méthodes de contrôle statistique de l'etalonnage fut révisée et acceptée en principe par les participants. Une section complète sur le calcul de l'incertitude a été discutée, modifiée et clarifiée. De plus, il a été jugé qu'il serait bénéfique de développer une annexe ou un document supplémentaire afin de donner des exemples clarifiant le calcul de l'incertitude pour la R 111. Ce document ne sera élaboré qu'une fois la R 111-2 Partie 1 finalisée.

Plusieurs discussions ont suivi ayant trait aux méthodes de détermination de la densité de la masse, de la détermination de la magnétisation, de la susceptibilité magnétique et de l'état de surface, ainsi qu'aux calculs s'y rattachant. Un tableau fut éventuellement développé pour établir quand ces essais devraient être exécutés; c'est-à-dire: sur toutes les masses ou un échantillonnage, seulement lors de la vérification primitive ou aussi lors des vérifications subséquentes, etc. De plus, un autre tableau a été présenté et raffiné; celui-ci proposant quelle méthode de détermination de la densité serait préférable à employer selon la classe et la valeur nominale de la masse.

En ce qui a trait au point 2, un dilemme intéressant s'est posé: à quoi s'appliquera le certificat OIML pour la R 111? Cette question était centrale aux échanges qui ont eu lieu par la suite puisqu'il est nécessaire de savoir qu'est-ce qui sera exigé par le certificat afin de décider comment l'information sera traitée. Par exemple, est-ce que le certificat sera un certificat international d'étalonnage? Est-ce que le certificat représente l'approbation de type pour un seul poids ou un ensemble de poids, pour un modèle de poids ou pour un modèle d'ensemble de poids? De plus, est-ce qu'il sera permis d'avoir un ensemble de poids composé de différentes classes de poids tel qu'il est le cas lorsque des petits poids $ \mathrm{E}_{2} $ ou $ \mathrm{F}_{1} $ sont inclus dans des ensembles $ \mathrm{F}_{1} $ ou $ \mathrm{F}_{2} $

etc. Le Secrétariat a demandé que le BIML étudie ce problème.

D'autres sujets tels les exigences pour la stabilité thermique dans les laboratoires d'étalonnage et les salles de vérification ont aussi été revus. La pertinence de l'utilisation de la masse conventionnelle lorsque les corrections dues à la flottaison et les incertitudes cirattachant sont trop larges a été mise en question. Diverses propositions ont été étudiées dont une de l'Allemagne où le tableau qui existe présentement pour la variation maximale de la densité serait modifié pour ajouter des limites maximales sur l'altitude où au-delà de ça, les erreurs seraient trop grandes et les masses ne pourraient plus être utilisées. Le Secrétariat proposera une autre solution où la masse réelle serait utilisée là où la masse conventionnelle pose un problème.

En ce qui a trait aux troisième et quatrième points de la liste, le Secrétariat a proposé de transférer les R 111-2 Partie 1 et R 111-2 Partie 2 en annexes à R 111, où certaines sections seraient obligatoires et d'autres seraient à caractère informatif.

Il a été décidé que la R 33 devrait être révisée et renommée de "Recommandation Internationale" à "Document International". Cette tâche sera accomplie par un petit groupe de travail composé de l'Allemagne, des Etats-Unis et de la France.

Le Secrétariat a proposé de retirer la R 47 et la R 52. Les participants ont suggéré que la R 47 ne soit pas retirée mais plutôt révisée, modifiée et peut-être incorporée à R 111. Il a aussi été décidé qu'un scrutin de vote devrait être envoyé aux États Membres afin

d'identifier si les poids hexagonaux (R 52) sont encore utilisés ou si la Recommandation est maintenant désuète et devrait être retirée.

## OIML Certificate System

The OIML Certificate System for Measuring Instruments was introduced in 1991 to facilitate administrative procedures and lower costs associated with the international trade of measuring instruments subject to legal requirements.

The System provides the possibility for a manufacturer to obtain an OIML certificate and a test report indicating that a given instrument pattern complies with the requirements of relevant OIML International Recommendations.

Certificates are delivered by OIML Member States that have established one or several Issuing Authorities responsible for processing applications by manufacturers wishing to have their instrument patterns certified.

OIML certificates are accepted by national metrology services on a voluntary basis, and as the climate for mutual confidence and recognition of test results develops between OIML Members, the OIML Certificate System serves to simplify the pattern approval process for manufacturers and metrology authorities by eliminating costly duplication of application and test procedures.

## Système de Certificats OIML

Le Système de Certificats OIML pour les Instruments de Mesure a été introduit en 1991 afin de faciliter les procédures administratives et d'abaisser les coûts liés au commerce international des instruments de mesure soumis aux exigences légales.

Le Système permet à un constructeur d'obtenir un certificat OIML et un rapport d'essai indiquant qu'un modèle d'instrument satisfait aux exigences des Recommandations OIML applicables.

Les certificats sont délivrés par les États Membres de l'OIML, qui ont établi une ou plusieurs autorités de délivrance responsables du traitement des demandes présentées par des constructeurs souhaitant voir certifier leurs modèles d'instruments.

Les services nationaux de métrologie légale peuvent accepter les certificats sur une base volontaire; avec le développement entre Membres OIML d'un climat de confiance mutuelle et de reconnaissance des résultats d'essais, le Système simplifie les processus d'approbation de modèle pour les constructeurs et les autorités métrologiques par l'elimination des répétitions coûteuses dans les procédures de demande et d'essai.

- the trend of privatization of legal metrology activities;

- the need for improved communication between interested parties;

- the relationship between initial certification, market surveillance and inspection;

- the future impact of the MID drafted by the European Commission;

- consumer protection and links with DG XIV of the European Commission;

- the needs of CEE countries for advice and guidance; and

- the future role and development of WELMEC in harmonizing legal metrology enforcement activities.

# Eighth UN/ECE Working Party Session 18-20 May 1998, Geneva (Switzerland)

</div>

The eighth session of the Working Party on Technical Harmonization and Standardization Policies of the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UN/ECE) was held from 18-20 May 1998 at the Palais des Nations, Geneva.

The session was attended by over 50 representatives from thirty countries, including delegates from the BIML, ISO, IEC, WTO, EU, EFTA, CEN, EOTC, EOQ and EA.

The following topics were discussed:

- the report of the World Trade Organization (WTO) on the First Triennial Review of the Operation and Implementation of the Agreement on Technical Barriers to Trade;

- coordination of standardization activities - ECE Standardization List;

- the role of international standards in international harmonization;

- developments in conformity assessment at international, regional and national levels and problems experienced by economies in transition;

- metrology - guide on standardization of test methods;

- UN/ECE study on the role of standards and regulations in international trade; and

- the 1998-2002 work program.

Representatives of various organizations (WTO,ISO, European Commission, EA, COOMET, etc.) and of national institutes reported on developments in the fields of standardization, conformity assessment, metrology and related activities since the seventh session of the Working Party in May 1997.

The Working Party considered the information given by the OIML which was included in the document Harmonization. Implementation and Application of International Standards submitted by the Rapporteur for Harmonization; the BIML representative also gave an oral report on regular surveys carried out by the OIML to control implementation of OIML Recommendations at national level.

The proposal by the Rapporteur for Metrology to prepare guidelines on methodology for measuring processes was accepted, together with the BIML representative's suggestion that before these guidelines are prepared, interested international (ISO, IEC, OIML) and regional (CEN, CENELEC) organizations should be consulted on its scope and content.

The program element on aspects of metrology related to testing activities is included in the 1998-2002 work program and the growing economic importance of metrology was underlined by several participants of the session.

The final version of an information booklet on the activities of the Working Party was approved by the session and will soon be available.

Discussion of most of the subjects under consideration will be continued during the ninth session of the Working Party which is planned to be held in Geneva in May 1999.

24-26 February 1999

## Topics:

1 Advances in metrology

2 Relevance of metrology to the environment, safety health, chemistry and socio-economic development

3 Role of metrology in quality improvement, quality assurance and the ISO Standards

4 Mutual recognition agreements and laboratory accreditation

5 Regional and global metrology network and equivalence of national measurement standards

6 Traceability of measurements, calibration and test certificates; barriers to trade

7 Metrology in developing countries

8 International cooperation

9 Future strategy of metrology concerning global trade

## CALL FOR PAPERS

The OIML Bulletin is a forum for the publication of technical papers and diverse articles addressing metrological advances in trade, health, environment and safety - fields in which the credibility of measurement remains a challenging priority. The Editors of the Bulletin encourage the submission of articles covering topics such as national, regional and international activities in metrology and related fields, evaluation procedures, accreditation and certification, and measuring techniques and instrumentation.

Authors are requested to submit a double-spaced, titled (typed) manuscript and accompanying visual materials (photos, illustrations, slides, etc.), together with a disk copy in one of the following formats: WordPerfect 5.1, Word 6 or 7 (or previous versions for PC or Macintosh), or Quark XPress for Macintosh. Authors are also requested to send a passport-size, black and white identity photo for publication.

Papers selected for publication will be remunerated at the rate of 150 FRF per printed page, provided that they have not already been published in other journals. The Editors reserve the right to edit contributions for style and space restrictions.
