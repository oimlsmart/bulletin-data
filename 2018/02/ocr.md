STEPHEN PATORAY BIML DIRECTOR

## A world of change

Although we live in a world with well-defined terminology and constants that seem, well-constant, we also experience a great amount of change. We encounter many technological changes in our daily lives, and the constant evolution of the SI is a change we are all witnessing now; this is reflected by the 2018 World Metrology Day theme. We also see change on an ongoing basis in many different areas of the OIML.

Recently the most significant change was the election of Dr. Roman Schwartz as CIML President at the 52nd CIML Meeting in Cartagena. Dr. Schwartz provided us with a vision of what he expects to accomplish over his six-year term. He has also appointed a number of new members to the Presidential Council who will add a new dynamic to this key advisory group.

In January 2018 the OIML Certification System (OIMLCS) was fully implemented and replaced the OIML Basic and MAA systems. This change has already led to a significant increase in the number of participants in the OIML-CS and participation is expected to continue to grow in the future.

We have seen increasing activity and indeed much improvement in the work of various project groups after the Secretaries and Conveners participated in specialized training related to the changes in OIML B 6 and the use of the PG Workspaces on the OIML website. It is my hope that the CIML will see the significant benefit of such training and initiate a change to make this type of training not only ongoing but also mandatory for all Secretaries and Conveners in the future.

The DCMAS Network participants, with the OIML as chair, have developed a new website and have also made significant progress in creating a definition for a Quality Infrastructure. It is now planned to rename the Network as the International Network on Quality Infrastructure. There has also been an increasing level of participation and new members are joining the Network.

There has been continued progress in the area of training and the OIML Training Center. A number of Training Center events have already taken place and more are planned for the near future. These have all been well organized and very well attended. This is a result of the change in focus toward CEEMS and the resolution that was passed in 2015.

As we look toward future changes, the CIML will be electing a new First Vice-president at its 53rd meeting in Hamburg. The call is now out and this is an excellent opportunity for all CIML Members to take a very big step and submit their application to help the OIML continue to evolve to meet the needs of all of its members.

At the 53rd CIML Meeting, the CIML will also be appointing a new BIML Director. The selection committee is now working to identify the best candidate from the many applicants. The 53rd meeting will also mark the last meeting for me as BIML Director; this will be a very significant change for me personally as I return to my home in the USA and start a new chapter in my life with my wife and family.

There is indeed a world of change in and around the OIML. It has been my great privilege to have had the opportunity to play a small part in it. I also expect great things of and for the OIML in the future as you all move forward together in this world of change.

## Abstract

One of the ways to assure the quality of measurement results is represented by participation in interlaboratory comparisons (ILC) or proficiency testing (PT). In this way the laboratory's performance, as well the quality of their measurement system (method equipment, personnel) can be assessed and demonstrated. In 2016, a proficiency testing in the field of legal mass metrology (verification of Non-automatic Weighing Instruments, NAWI) was organized in Romania for the first time. This paper presents the results obtained by nine participating laboratories. The performance of the participating laboratories was evaluated according to the statistical methods used in quantitative proficiency testing and was based on the "z" score.

## 1 Introduction

In Romania, the only accredited provider for ILC/PT in the field of metrology is the ROLAB-IC Association (Association of Romanian Laboratories). Thus, ROLAB organizes ILC/PT schemes which aim to assess performance of laboratories performing calibrations or metrological verifications in different fields.

According to [2], the measurement results obtained in the inter-laboratory experiment were evaluated for outliers. Firstly, the Cochran test was applied. Because the results were not conclusive, the Grubbs test was used for the extreme values of the string data (maximum and minimum). The critical values of these statistics for significance levels $ \alpha=5\% $ and $ \alpha=1\% $ for p laboratories and n repeated measurements in the laboratories, are provided in the respective ISO standard.

The "z" score of the participating laboratories was calculated for every test performed (accuracy, repeatability, eccentricity and tare) and for each load provided in the PT protocol.

## 4 Calculation of the performance criteria

The performance of the participating laboratories was calculated according to the statistical methods used in the quantitative proficiency testing and was based on the "z" score which was calculated for every test (accuracy, repeatability, eccentricity and tare) and for each load provided in the PT protocol using the following formula:

$$
z = \frac {x _ {i} - X}{\hat {\sigma}}
$$

## Where:

$ x_{i} $ represents the result (or the average of the results) reported by a participant for the measurement,

X is the assigned value, and

$ \hat{\sigma} $ is the standard deviation for the proficiency assessment.

Depending on the tests performed on the balance, the calculation for X and were carried out as follows:

a) for accuracy and tare tests, where 2 measurements were performed for each load, the basic method for determining the repeatability and reproducibility of a standard measurement method according to [2] was used. In this case, the standard deviation for the proficiency assessment, $ \sigma_{\mathrm{pt}} $ was calculated according to [3], as follows:

$$
\sigma_ {p t} = \sqrt {\sigma_ {R} ^ {2} - \sigma_ {r} ^ {2}} (1 - 1 / m)
$$

where:

$ \sigma_{\mathrm{R}} $ and $ \sigma_{\mathrm{r}} $ represent the reproducibility and repeatability standard deviations,

m is the number of replicate measurements each participant is to perform in a round of the proficiency testing scheme. The assigned value for this case was based on the results reported by all the participants and consisted in their average.

b) for the eccentricity and repeatability tests, the standard deviation for the proficiency assessment was estimated from "expectations based on experience". In this case, the value attributed to the standard deviation for the proficiency assessment was calculated using the maximum permissible error (MPE) of the balance at the considered loads, in accordance with 8.1.2 of [3], as follows:

$$
\hat {\sigma} = \frac {M P E}{3}
$$

The factor "3.0" represents an action limit for an evaluation with a "z" score and the MPEs of the balance are shown in Table 1.

The assigned value X for case b) was calculated from the average of the RL/PL values obtained for each test (eccentricity and repeatability).

## 5 Performance evaluation of participating laboratories

According to [3], section 9.4.2, the performance evaluation of the participating laboratories is based on the following criteria:

- $ |z|\leq2 $ the performance is considered "acceptable" and do not generate any signal. Result: "correct".

- 2 < |z| < 3 the performance is considered "questionable" and generates a "warning signal".

- $ | z | \geq 3 $ , the result is considered unacceptable, generating an action signal to remedy the situation. Result: "incorrect".

For the repeatability test, even if the "z" score is not normally distributed, the same criterion was used according to [3], section 9.4.2, note 6.

## Abstract

Weighing instruments with a minimum capacity of 4900 kg require a special verification vehicle to transport the 200 kg and 500 kg mass standards. In Brazil, these instruments are widely used by the agro-industry sector, responsible for a significant part of the national GDP. Due to the national industry infrastructure in Brazil, most of the calibration laboratories are located in south/southeast regions, in contrast to the agro-sector, in the west/north. This study shows the implementation of a mobile laboratory to support calibrations where calibration laboratories are sparse. We observed a reduction of 60.5 % in costs mainly due to the logistics issues.

This paper is an extended version of the presentation given at the 9th Brazilian Congress on Metrology, 2017.

Keywords: traceability; legal metrology; calibration

## 1 Introduction

Metrology can be described as part of human history itself, and measurement systems are observed from ancient societies to modern days. Legal metrology was initially introduced as a necessity to control food in trade [1] and evolved into several different areas that impact either the economy, society, the environment or health as well as security. Measuring instruments are regulated in order to provide confidence in measurements that affect society in various ways, providing more benefits than

non-regulation. Especially in trade, the regulation processes are more cost-effective to the economy than deregulation [2].

Legal metrology can also be considered as providing a competitive advantage in international trade by overcoming technical barriers, thus protecting the national economy in the import/export of goods, the values of which are based on a measurement. Traceability plays a major role in metrology and consequently its application, once an unbroken chain of measurements of known uncertainties provides the necessary confidence to adjust measuring instruments that affect society as a whole.

As an example of benefits involving legal metrology, NIST's investments of approx. USD 0.5 million in Saudi Arabia resulted in an increase in exports between USD 300-500 million [3]. In Japan, a study proposing a model to evaluate the economic loss involving differences in standards showed that a divergence in 0.05 mg in 1 kg would generate USD 77 000 of losses for the weighing industry [4].

In Brazil, the National Institute of Metrology, Quality and Technology-Inmetro-is responsible for regulation in legal metrology, as well as for accreditation maintenance of the national standards and traceability of the standards of the delegated bodies.

In 2016, the agro-industry sector produced 184 697 696 T of cereal, oilseed and grain legume crops. The projection for 2017 shows a production of 238 627 189 T [5]. The agro-industry sector, in which trade is mostly weighing based, is also an important component of Brazil's international trade.

Heavy load weighing instruments are an important component of several important sectors of the economy. Consequently, as a continental country occupying an area of 8 515 759.090 km $ ^{2} $ and 5570 municipalities [6], Brazil's legal metrology system is decentralized and the legal control comprising verifications and surveillance of measuring instruments is conducted by 26 delegated bodies (one per State) across the national territory [7], under Inmetro's coordination.

A verification truck transporting both 200 kg and 500 kg working mass standards is responsible for the verification of heavy load weighing instruments. Consequently, those working mass standards require calibration in order to provide traceability to the agroindustrial sector in Brazil.

The logistics costs as well as well as the period out of service of the verification trucks due to the long distances to be covered in order to calibrate the working standards are two significant aspects in the area, involving costs to meet the demand of verification issues.

To overcome this difficulty, this study presents the implementation of a mobile laboratory of mass calibration (ML), which provides the necessary infrastructure demanded for calibration of the mass standards

used to verify and inspect heavy load scales. We also present the financial benefits of the ML, when compared to calibrations conducted by accredited laboratories.

## 2 The mobile calibration laboratory

For this study, automatic instruments for weighing road vehicles, rail-weighbridges and non-automatic weighing instruments with a minimum capacity of 4900 kg are described as heavy load weighing instruments, hereafter called weighing instruments for simplicity.

The delegated bodies for legal metrology activities in Brazil are responsible for verification and inspections. In 2016, 19 555 scales were verified (initial, periodic and after repair). Each delegated body has an adequate number of verification trucks containing the necessary standards for the verifications. The verification truck contains mass standards of 20 kg, 200 kg and 500 kg, necessary for the verification of weighing instruments according to the tests based on the relevant OIML Recommendations. Figure 1 shows the trucks used in the verification procedure of weighing instruments.

Regardless of its continental area, Brazil concentrates most of its industry in the south/south-east territories. As a consequence of this polarization of the industry, the calibration laboratories are also located mostly in the south/south-east regions. However, as the north and the west account for most of the agro-industrial sector, the area contains a large number of weighing instruments Therefore, the delegated bodies working in the agroindustry area demand calibration for their mass standards.

## 1 Introduction

This article deals with a method for the calibration of systems for measuring the mass of liquids in tanks in their place of operation after calibration.

The method for measuring the mass of liquids is described in OIML R 125 [1], ISO 15169 [5], ISO 11223-1 [8] and DCTY 7094 (ГОСТ 8.587) [9]. The terminology used in these documents varies, but a generalized name of this method could be "a method for measuring the mass of liquids contained in storage tanks using the hybrid hydrostatic principle". The systems consist of automatic level gauges (ALG), automatic tank thermometers (ATT) and pressure transmitters (transducers) for measuring the hydrostatic pressure of liquid, etc.

Temperature corrections are not considered in this article.

The density of the liquid in the tank is estimated from the measured pressure and the height of liquid above the sensor, measured with an ALG. The volume of liquid in the tank is obtained from the capacity (calibration) table and the liquid level above the dipoint. The mass of the liquid is obtained by multiplying volume and density.

The uncertainty of the measurements of the mass of the liquid is related to the main requirements of the System:

1 All measuring instruments used in the System must have appropriate metrological characteristics according to [5] and conform to the necessary uncertainty of 0.5 % for mass measuring (see, for example, [1] and [9]).

2 The tank must have a capacity (calibration) table relative to the dip-point with an uncertainty of 0.1 % (see, for example, [3], [6] and [10]).

4 During operation, the reference height of the automatic level gauge and the reference height of the pressure sensor must have minimum deformations.

3 The reference height of the automatic level gauge and the reference height of the sensor of the pressure transmitter must be measured with the maximum achievable accuracy relative to the dip-point.

The authors did not find a recommendation of the relative accuracy when fulfilling point 3 above. But it is logical to assume that its maximum permissible error (MPE) must be better than $ \pm4 $ mm for measuring the level of the liquid, as indicated in [2]. This is a difficult task for an empty tank and it is an impossible task using direct measurement for a tank filled with liquid during exploitation. From experience, we know that during exploitation tanks suffer a significant deformation due to the liquid pressure and in time, point 4 above is not fulfilled. These were the two main reasons for developing a new method for the calibration of the system.

The calibration process consists of:

measuring the level of liquid from the dip-point, and

- simultaneously estimating the density of the liquid by measuring the height of the liquid surface above the reference point of the automatic level gauge and the hydrostatic pressure of the liquid by the System and by measuring standards.

Adjusting the measurement results by the least square method (LSM) for the geometrical and other parameters (biases) obtained for the System is a very difficult part of the calibration process. The parameters (biases) are entered into the System as corrections. The uncertainty evaluation is performed for the parameters of the System and for the mass of the liquid, when the System is operating, taking into account the correlation between the parameters and the results of the measurement strictly using the least square method.

## 2 Analysis of the sources

The general metrological and technical requirements are given in OIML R125 [1] concerning the relative determination of the mass of the liquid by systems for measuring the mass of liquids in tanks. The testing and verification procedures for the systems and their parts are also described in [1]. The new calibration method described in this article develops general procedures from [1]. In [1] very simplified formulas are described for processing the mass of the liquid and the relevant uncertainty evaluation, but in [1] the correction of the mass of the liquid from the calibration results is not dealt with. The uncertainty evaluation is carried out strictly by the LSM method.

In OIML R 125 [1] and DCTY 7094 (ГОСТ 8.587) [9] the maximum permissible error given for the mass of the liquid in the tank is 0.5 %.

In ISO 15169 [5] the requirements are described in more detail relative to obtaining the volume, density and

mass of the liquid in vertical and horizontal cylindrical and spherical tanks by means of hybrid tank measurement systems. In [5] the maximum permissible errors of the liquid level measurements, pressure, and temperature are normalized, but for mass only formulas for uncertainty evaluation are given. This is correct, but in this case the calibration results are not used in addition.

For the non-stratified liquid in [5] it is recommended to directly compare the liquid density measurement from the System with the liquid density from laboratory research or with the density measured by an online densitometer into or out of a tank. But [5] has no calculation method for this density comparison.

All the sources have a verification procedure for the separate parameters of the System concerning conformity to their individual requirements. In [5] it is recommended to verify the automatic level gauge using ISO 4266-1 [7], but not simultaneously when verifying the density.

In [1], [5] and [9] great attention is paid to evaluate the uncertainty of the mass of the liquid, but correlations between the measurement results and the mass correction from the calibration are not used.

According to [5] it is necessary to define the zero error and the linearity error for the pressure transmitter. But in [5] there is no precise division of biases (corrections) and their uncertainty.

## 3 The calibration measurements process

From experience it is known that the verification or calibration of each part of the System does not guarantee that the error of the measured mass of the liquid is within the maximum permissible error range. One of the reasons, for example, is the deformation of the tank wall due to the liquid pressure. Therefore, fulfillment of the calibration of the System during operation is a very important task.

To realize the new method it is necessary to have two measurement standards:

a transportable level measuring standard, and

a transportable density measuring standard.

Both of these standards may be combined in one device. One or both of these standards may also have a temperature standard.

Before calibration, the transportable level measuring standard should be installed on the reference flange [7] above the dip-point. The transportable density measuring standard should be installed on any other flange. During the calibration it is necessary to pour liquid into or out of the tank. This process is stopped, for

example, at ten or more points. On these points the liquid level from the dip-point and the mean density given by the transportable measuring standard are measured. Simultaneously the height of the liquid surface above the reference point is measured with the automatic level gauge, and the pressure is measured with the transmitter sensor. Simultaneously the temperature is measured with the temperature standard and the automatic tank thermometer.

For a non-stratified liquid the density may be measured using a sample of liquid in the laboratory or by densitometer for samples out of a tank [5].

## 4 The mathematical model

Taking into account the above, a mathematical model for measuring the mass of petroleum or liquid petroleum products in the tank can be written as:

$$
M _ {i} = \rho_ {i} \cdot V _ {i}
$$

where:

$ \rho_{i} $ is the density of the liquid measured by the System;

$ V_{i} $ is the volume of liquid from the capacity (calibration) table, which was interpolated by the System using the measured level of liquid in the tank.

This volume is reduced to the average temperature of the liquid in the tank by the equations from [5].

The density of the liquid in the tank is calculated from the measurement results of the pressure and the level of liquid above the sensor of the pressure transmitter. Theoretically, the density of the liquid in the tank $ \rho_{i} $ must be calculated by the formula:

$$
\rho_ {i} = \frac {P _ {i}}{g \cdot H _ {i}}
$$

where:

$ H_{i} $ is the level of liquid measured by the automatic level gauge, adjusted on the tank, above the sensor of the pressure transmitter;

$ P_{i} $ is the pressure of the liquid measured by the pressure transmitter adjusted on the tank, when the level of the liquid was ;

g is the acceleration due to gravity which is equal to 9.8066 m/s $ ^{2} $

Taking into account the reasons mentioned above, equation (2) is not suitable for calculating the liquid density in the tank with sufficiently low uncertainty. Therefore, with the aim of obtaining better accuracy for measuring the mass of the liquids, we propose the following mathematical model:

$$
\widetilde {\rho} _ {i} = \frac {P _ {i} - \left(P _ {0} + c _ {P} \cdot P _ {i}\right)}{g \cdot \left(\left(H _ {L} + c _ {H _ {L}} \cdot L _ {i}\right) - \left(H _ {P} + c _ {H _ {P}} \cdot L _ {i}\right) - L _ {i}\right)};
$$

$$
\tilde {H} _ {G _ {i}} = \left(H _ {L} + c _ {H _ {L}} \cdot L _ {i}\right) - L _ {i}
$$

where there are two groups of values:

## 1) Measured for each level of liquid height:

$ \tilde{H}_{i} $ is the level of liquid in the tank which was measured by the level measuring standard from the dip-point (in our case corresponding to the datum-point) [6];

$ \tilde{P}_{i} $ is the mean density of the liquid in the tank which was measured by the density measuring standard, when the level in the tank was $ \tilde{H}_{i} $;

$ L_{i} $ is the distance measured from the automatic level gauge reference point to the surface of the liquid;

$ P_{i} $ is the pressure of the liquid measured by the pressure transmitter.

## 2) The unknown parameters of the System

$ P_{0} $ is the additive bias of the pressure measurement results by the pressure transmitter in the tank (called zero error in [5]);

$ C_{P} $ is the multiplicative bias of the pressure measurement results by the pressure transmitter in the tank (called the linearity error in [5]);

$ H_{L} $ is the reference height of the ALG;

$ C_{H_{L}} $ is the multiplicative bias of the reference height of the reference point of the automatic level gauge;

$ H_{p} $ is the reference height of the sensor of the pressure transmitter;

$ C_{H_{p}} $ is the multiplicative bias of the reference height of the pressure transmitter sensor;

i=1...n is the number of the liquid level where the measurements were fulfilled;

n is the amount of the liquid levels at which the measurements were fulfilled.

Unknowns $ C_{H_{L}} $ and $ C_{H_{p}} $ characterize the changing of the reference height of the automatic level gauge and the pressure transmitter from the height of the liquid in the tank by the linear law.

Equations (3) and (4) are not fulfilled due to errors that the measurements have and due to the unknown parameters of the System. Equations (3) and (4) are interdependent. The distance measured by the automatic level gauge to the liquid surface, the reference distance of the automatic level gauge and its multiplicative bias are common for the two equations. Thus we have difficulties in aligning the measurement results using the LSM.

## 6 Uncertainty evaluation by LSM

The value $ V^{T}WV $ in the numerator of the right part of equation (17) is calculated using equation (16):

$$
V ^ {T} W V = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} \left(\left| v _ {\tilde {\rho} _ {i}} \quad v _ {\tilde {H} _ {i}} \quad v _ {P _ {i}} \quad v _ {L _ {i}} \right| \cdot \left| \begin{array}{c c c c} w _ {\tilde {\rho} _ {i}} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & w _ {\tilde {H} _ {i}} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & w _ {P _ {i}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & w _ {L _ {i}} \end{array} \right| \cdot \left| \begin{array}{c} v _ {\tilde {\rho} _ {i}} \\ v _ {\tilde {H} _ {i}} \\ v _ {P _ {i}} \\ v _ {L _ {i}} \end{array} \right|\right)
$$

In [4] the simplest case for evaluation of the measurement results uncertainty is described. The uncertainty evaluation using the LMS is a more difficult case, but it is only adequate for mathematical models (3) and (4).

The uncertainty evaluation using LSM starts with the evaluation of the standard deviation weight unit $ \sigma_{u} $ by the equation:

$$
\sigma_ {u} = \sqrt {\frac {U ^ {T} W _ {U} U}{2 n - k}} = \sqrt {\frac {V ^ {T} W V}{2 n + n m - k}}

$$
w _ {i} = \frac {1}{q _ {i}} = \frac {\sigma^ {2} \left(x _ {0}\right)}{u ^ {2} \left(x _ {i}\right)} = 1
$$

This standard deviation comprises the errors of the measurement standard, the errors of the measuring System, the nonlinearity of the tank deformation due to the liquid pressure, other nonlinear characteristics, etc. The uncertainty evaluations of the System parameters are calculated by the equations:

$$
\sigma \left(\tau_ {j}\right) = \sigma_ {u} \cdot \sqrt {Q _ {j j}}
$$

where $ Q_{jj} $ are the diagonal terms of the matrix inverted to the matrix of the normal equations from (13).

The parameters of the System are the biases calculated from the decision based on the equations above. The biases must be introduced into the System and so must the corrections. The mathematical model during operation must be identical to the mathematical model in calibration. The mathematical model (equations (3) and (4)) corrected during operation gives:

$$
\rho_ {t} = \frac {P _ {t} - \left(P _ {0} + c _ {P} \cdot P _ {t}\right)}{g \cdot \left(\left(H _ {L} + c _ {H _ {L}} \cdot L _ {t}\right) - \left(H _ {P} + c _ {H _ {P}} \cdot L _ {t}\right) - L _ {t}\right)};

## 7 Summary

1) The calibration method in operation has been developed to evaluate the parameters of the System for measuring the mass of the liquid in the tank and its uncertainty.

2) The mass of liquid mathematical model for calibration must be similar to the mathematical model for the mass of liquid in operation, which uses the parameters of the System from calibration and considers the correlation between the parameters.

3) The new mathematical model developed does not consider the tank temperature deformations, the temperature drift of the pressure transmitter, etc.

4) The parameters of the System derived from calibration are their biases, which are entered into the System as corrections. This procedure improves the measurement accuracy of the mass of the liquid in operation.

5) Another measurement model may be proposed, but the method of deciding by the least square solution will be similar.

6) In the authors' opinion, the proposed method can be used as a mathematical background for developing International Recommendations for the calibration and operation of measuring systems for the mass of liquids in tanks based on the hybrid hydrostatic principle.

The OIML Bulletin is a forum for the publication of technical papers and diverse articles addressing metrological advances in trade, health, the environment and safety - fields in which the credibility of measurement remains a challenging priority. The Editors of the Bulletin encourage the submission of articles covering topics such as national, regional and international activities in legal metrology and related fields, evaluation procedures, accreditation and certification, and measuring techniques and instrumentation. Authors are requested to submit:

- a titled, typed manuscript in Word or WordPerfect either on disk or (preferably) by e-mail;

- the paper originals of any relevant photos, illustrations, diagrams, etc.;

- a photograph of the author(s) suitable for publication together with full contact details: name, position, institution, address, telephone, fax and e-mail.

Note: Electronic images should be minimum 150 dpi, preferably 300 dpi.

Technical articles selected for publication will be remunerated at the rate of 23 $ \epsilon $ per printed page, provided that they have not already been published in other journals. The Editors reserve the right to edit contributions for style, space and linguistic reasons and author approval is always obtained prior to publication. The Editors decline responsibility for any claims made in articles, which are the sole responsibility of the authors concerned. Please send submissions to:
