CEEMS: The powerful force of continuous development of the OIML

## Significance

World leaders from 193 UN Member States adopted the ambitious "2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development" in 2015. This agenda has been the driving force not only for individual countries but also for a number of International Organizations to reach these ultimate goals. Of course, the OIML is among them, and the OIML mission perfectly matches the core target: to facilitate trade, establish mutual confidence and harmonize the level of consumer protection worldwide.

One of main aims of the OIML is to support Countries and Economies with Emerging Metrology Systems (CEEMS) in developing sound legal metrology systems to fit in with the notion of a modern quality infrastructure which plays a fundamental role in sustainable development and ensuring that challenges can be faced.

## CEEMS work progress

The CIML decided to establish the CEEMS Advisory Group (AG) at its 48th Meeting in 2013 in order to promote CEEMS work, to enable the CEEMS community to actively participate in OIML activities, to study and draw up OIML support policies and plans for CEEMS, and to provide guidance for the OIML's support activities for CEEMS.

After more than four years of development, the AG - led by its Chair Mr. Pu Changcheng and Vice-Chair Mr. Peter Mason with support from many other individuals - has continued to contribute new vitality to the OIML. Through the OIML Pilot Training Centers (OPTC), various CEEMS-oriented training courses and seminars have been organized to promote CEEMS capacity building. Significant achievements have been made, leading to the work of the CEEMS AG becoming one of the pillars of the OIML's strategy.

Under the guidance of the CIML, the AG will continue to make efforts to increase the enthusiasm and breadth of CEEMS activities by

- further strengthening research on the strategic development of international legal metrology cooperation,

- widely publicizing and raising the awareness of legal metrology,

- actively leading the revision of International Documents (e.g. OIML D 1),

- developing the OIML Training Center strategy and framework, and organizing international metrology seminars and trainings,

- supporting CEEMS to establish and implement the OIML-CS,

- establishing an OIML CEEMS expert database and conducting consultancy work,

- strengthening communication with RLMOs and developing strategic cooperation on projects of common interest, and

- other activities specified in B 19, which defines the terms of reference for the CEEMS Advisory Group.

## Future outlook

Facing the future and meeting the requirements of modern times, the OIML will continue to support the work of CEEMS and assist CEEMS in jointly establishing a global metrology cooperation system to better implement the OIML mission and promote global sustainable development. Meanwhile, CEEMS also need the OIML to make better use of the legal metrology system to promote fair trade, boost technological innovation, support government supervision, protect consumers, and achieve social goals. The OIML welcomes Corresponding Members as well as non-members to join as OIML Member States, so that our CEEMS project can continue to solve metrology needs worldwide.

# Traceable calibration of automatic weighing instruments operating in dynamic mode

</div>

MATEJ GRUM

Metrology Institute of the Republic of Slovenia

## Abstract

This article presents the progress made in the development of methods for calibrating automatic weighing instruments (AWIs) operating in dynamic mode. The calibration specificities are highlighted. For automatic catchweighing instruments and automatic gravimetric filling instruments, basic information about the measurement method, error model and measurement uncertainty contributions are given. Several approaches are presented for determining the reference mass of the test loads on the control weighing instrument, including the related analysis of the air buoyancy correction and the influence of the density of the test loads. The use of harmonized and validated methods will, when finalised, enable traceable calibration of the AWIs to be carried out in dynamic operation mode.

## 1 Introduction

Automatic weighing instruments, which perform measurements in dynamic mode, are extensively used in the preparation, production and quality assurance of prepackaged products as well as for products whose content or composition is determined by weighing.

While non-automatic weighing instruments (NAWIs) are routinely calibrated by accredited calibration laboratories according to EURAMET Calibration Guide for NAWIs [1], the calibration of AWIs is not as well defined, because there is a significant difference between the static measurement mode of operation of NAWIs and the dynamic measurement mode of operation which is typical for the majority of AWI applications.

The growing dissemination of AWIs emphasises the need to confirm their metrological quality by calibrations and the reliable estimation of the measurement uncertainty in order to judge the accuracy of the weighing result. Thus, the development of calibration methods for dynamic measurements with AWIs is the scope of the EMPIR project 14RPT02 "Traceable calibration of dynamic weighing instruments operating in the dynamic mode" (AWICal), funded by EURAMET [2]. This project will develop harmonised calibration methods and uncertainty evaluation models for the three selected categories of AWIs, i.e. automatic catchweighers, automatic instruments for weighing road vehicles in motion, and automatic gravimetric filling instruments, selected to represent the most commonly used instruments.

## 2 Calibration method

The first step in the process is the establishment of the measurement model, which is the mathematical relationship between the measurand and all the quantities involved in the measurement. The actual value of the weighing is affected by various dynamic effects of the instrument operation, the load type and its properties, and the rate of operation of the instrument. Due to the dynamic behaviour of their operation, the functional relationship between the weighing result, the operational parameters such as the rate of operation, the type of material and the nominal value of the test load is very complex and currently out of the scope of the calibration procedure.

Because of the dynamic behaviour, it will usually not be possible to calibrate a measurement range for such instruments. The calibration will deliver a measurement error of the instrument in comparison to a reference mass value under the conditions of the calibration such as the speed of the load transport system and the load properties.

In agreement with the client, the calibration is performed at individual nominal values defined by the mass of the test loads, and is only valid for the specified test loads (with a small bandwidth for mass, volume and shape).

Calibration is performed at the location at which the instrument is being installed. Test loads are made of the type of product, which is normally weighed on the calibrated instrument. A calibration is performed at the rate of operation requested and specified in advance by the client. Normally these conditions are the same as the conditions during the actual weighing process.

The documents for the calibration of automatic instruments should include at least guidance on:

- determination of the mass of the test loads,

measurements to be performed,

calculation of the measuring results,

evaluation of the uncertainty of measurement, and

- contents of the calibration certificates.

In the following two subsections, basic details are given concerning the calibration procedures for automatic catchweighing instruments and automatic gravimetric filling instruments.

## 2.1 Automatic catchweighing instruments

The object of the calibration for the automatic catchweighing instrument, which is symbolically shown in Figure 1, is the indication of the instrument I in response to an applied load with a reference value of the mass of the test load, $ m_{ref} $ The error of indication E is:

$$
E = I - m _ {r e f}
$$

![](page=7,bbox=[253, 1186, 1008, 1922])

<div align="center">

Fig. 1 Automatic catchweighing instrument

</div>

Due to the effects of air buoyancy and other effects such as convection, drift and others which may lead to minor corrections in the term $ m_{x} $ $ m_{ref} $ is not exactly equal to $ m_{cTL} $ and so the conventional mass value of the load is:

$$
m _ {r e f} = m _ {c T L} + \delta m _ {B} + \delta_ {m} \dots
$$

$ \delta m_{\mathrm{B}} $ is the air buoyancy correction for the test load used for calibration, and $ \delta m $ represents further corrections that it may be necessary to apply under special conditions.

Tests are performed to determine the errors of indications, repeatability of indications, and the effect of eccentric application of a load on the indication.

## 2.1.1 Test for errors and repeatability of indication

The test consists of repeatedly passing the same load over the middle of the load receptor, under identical conditions of handling the load and the instrument, and under constant test conditions. The purpose of this test is to appraise the accuracy and repeatability of the instrument at least at the points of usual application of the instrument. Each test point is characterized by its own repeatability.

For the test load $ L_{\mathrm{T}} $ the mean error of indication E is calculated as follows:

$$
E = \bar {I} - m _ {r e f}
$$

where $ \bar{I} $ is the mean of several indications. Section 3 provides further information on how $ m_{ref} $ can be obtained.

From the n indications $ I_{\mathrm{i}} $ for a given test load, the standard deviation $ s(I_{\mathrm{i}}) $ is calculated as:

$$
s (I) = \sqrt {\frac {1}{n - 1} \sum_ {1 = 1} ^ {n} \left(I _ {i} - \bar {I} _ {T L}\right) ^ {2}}
$$

with

$$
\bar {I} _ {T L} = \frac {1}{n} \sum_ {1 = 1} ^ {n} I _ {i}
$$

## 2.1.2 Test for effect of eccentric loading

The effect of the eccentric application of the load on the indication is tested when applicable. The effect of eccentric loading shall be determined using the selected test load $ L_{ecc} $ using the middle of the load transport system, the portion of the load transport system that is halfway between the centre and the back, and repeated with the same test load using the portion of the load transport system that is halfway between the centre and the front.

For each tested band k of the load transport system the average difference $ \varDelta_{ecc,k} $ is calculated as follows:

$$
\Delta I _ {e c c, k} = \bar {I} _ {k} - \bar {I} _ {m}
$$

## 2.1.3 Standard uncertainty of the error of indication

To account for any sources of variability of the indication, i.e. the effect of the resolution of the indication, the repeatability of the instrument and the error due to an off-centre position of the test load, is amended by correction terms as follows:

$$
\bar {I} = \bar {I} _ {T L} + \delta I _ {d i g T L} + \delta I _ {r e p} + \delta I _ {e c c}
$$

The standard uncertainty of the indication of the catchweigher $ u(\bar{I}) $ then equals:

$$
u ^ {2} (\bar {I}) = \frac {d ^ {2}}{1 2} + \frac {s ^ {2} (I)}{n} + \frac {\left| \Delta I _ {e c c , k} \right| _ {m a x}}{2 L _ {e c c} \sqrt {3}} I ^ {2}
$$

Where d is the resolution of the instrument.

The standard uncertainty of the indication and the standard uncertainty of the reference value of the mass $ u(m_{ref}) $ contribute to the standard uncertainty of the error of indication u(E):

$$
u (E) = \sqrt {u ^ {2} (\bar {I}) + u ^ {2} \left(m _ {r e f}\right)}
$$

Section 3 provides further information about the standard uncertainty of the reference value of the mass.

## 2.2 Automatic gravimetric filling instruments

The object of the calibration of automatic gravimetric filling instruments, which is symbolically shown in Figure 2, is the preset value error of the instrument, $ E_{p} $ determined at different preset values, $ F_{p} $ i.e. values, expressed in units of mass, preset by the operator, in order to define the nominal value of the fills. The preset value error for the i-th test fill equals:

$$
E _ {P, i} = m _ {r e f, i} - F _ {P}
$$

Due to the effects of air buoyancy and other effects, which may lead to minor corrections of the term $ m_{x} $ the reference values of the mass of the test fill, $ m_{ref,i} $ are not exactly equal to $ m_{cF,i} $ the conventional mass of the test fill. The conventional mass of the test fill cannot be determined directly, but by determining the conventional mass of the filled container $ m_{cB} $ (gross value) and the conventional mass of the empty container $ m_{cT} $ (tare value):

$$
m _ {r e f, \mathrm {i}} = m _ {c B, \mathrm {i}} - m _ {c T, \mathrm {i}} + \delta m _ {B, F} + \delta m \dots
$$

![](page=8,bbox=[1097, 282, 1603, 1123])

<div align="center">

Fig. 2 Automatic gravimetric filling instrument

</div>

$ \delta m_{B} $ is the air buoyancy correction for the test fill (refers to the net value of the test fill), and $ \delta m $ are further corrections that it may be necessary to apply under special conditions.

## 2.2.1 Test for preset value error and repeatability

The test consists of repeatedly filling the fills with the same nominal value, and under constant test conditions. The purpose of this test is to evaluate the accuracy and repeatability of the instrument at least at the points of usual application of the instrument. Each test point is characterized by its own repeatability.

For the preset value $ F_{p} $ the preset value error $ E_{p} $ is calculated based on measurement of n test fills as follows:

$$
E _ {P} = \frac {1}{n} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} m _ {r e f, i} - F _ {P}
$$

When the test fills are weighed on the calibrated control instrument (cf. section 3), equation (12) can be modified by introducing the mass of a single fill $ F_{i} $ in the equation, which is determined based on the weighing results for filled and empty containers $ W_{B} $ and $ W_{T} $ respectively:

$$
F _ {i} = W _ {B i} - W _ {T i}
$$

Finally:

$$
E _ {P} = \bar {F} + \delta m _ {B T o t} - F _ {P}
$$

where $ \bar{F} $ is the average mass of the fills:

$$
\bar {F} = \frac {1}{n} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} F _ {i}
$$

and $ m_{B T o t} $ is the total contribution of the air buoyancy correction. Section 3 provides more information on how $ m_{B T o t} $ is obtained.

From the n fills $ F_{i} $ for a given preset value, the standard deviation s(F) is calculated:

$$
s (\mathrm {F}) = \sqrt {\frac {1}{n - 1} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} \left(\mathrm {F} _ {i} - \bar {\mathrm {F}}\right) ^ {2}}
$$

## 2.2.3 Standard uncertainty of the preset value error

To account for the sources of variability of the preset value error, i.e. the repeatability of the instrument and the air buoyancy correction, equation (14) is amended by the correction terms as follows:

$$
E _ {P} = \bar {F} + \delta F _ {r e p} + \delta m _ {B T o t} - F _ {P}
$$

The standard uncertainty of the preset error $ u ( E_{P} ) $ equals:

$$
u \left(E _ {P}\right) = \sqrt {u ^ {2} \left(W _ {B}\right) + u ^ {2} \left(W _ {T}\right) + u ^ {2} \left(\delta F _ {r e p}\right) + u ^ {2} \left(\delta m _ {B T o t}\right)}
$$

where $ u ( W_{B} ) $ and $ u ( W_{T} ) $ account respectively for the standard uncertainty of the weighing result for the filled and the empty container, $ u ( \delta m_{B T o t} ) $ for the air buoyancy correction, and $ u ( \delta F_{r e p} ) $ accounts for the repeatability of the test fills, which is estimated as:

$$
u \left(\delta F _ {-} r e p\right) = s (F) / \sqrt {n}
$$

Further information about the standard uncertainty of the air buoyancy correction is provided in Section 3.

## 3 Test loads, control instrument and air buoyancy correction

The calibration of AWIs in dynamic operation mode cannot be performed directly with standard weights.

To determine the errors of an instrument, test loads are applied. The test loads should preferably be the type of articles which are normally weighed on the calibrated instrument. For the purpose of calibration, the traceability of the test loads to the SI unit of mass shall be demonstrated using a control weighing instrument. The control instrument may be either separate (i.e. a weighing instrument other than the instrument being calibrated) or integral (i.e. when a static weighing mode is provided by the instrument being calibrated). The control instrument should ensure the determination of the mass of each test load to an accuracy which is appropriate to the expected uncertainty of calibration of the calibrated instrument.

The process of determining the reference mass value of the test load and its associated measurement uncertainty is one part of the whole process of AWI calibration. An appropriate method of determining the test load reference mass value needs to be selected bearing in mind the required measurement uncertainty and complexity of the method. Some of possible approaches are summarised below:

A. The test load is calibrated prior to calibration of the AWI. Its conventional mass is reported together with the uncertainty, e.g. in a calibration certificate.

B. The mass of the test load is determined by comparison with a standard weight at the time and place of calibration of the AWI on a weighing instrument, which is used as a mass comparator.

C. The mass of the test load is determined by weighing on a control weighing instrument at the time and place of calibration of the AWI and the control instrument is also calibrated at the same time as the calibrated AWI.

D. The mass of the test load is determined at the time and place of calibration of the AWI by weighing on a control weighing instrument, which was calibrated prior to the calibration of the AWI. The results of the calibration of the control instrument were reported in the certificate.

E. The mass of the test load is determined at the time and place of the calibration of the AWI by weighing on the calibrated control instrument, which satisfies a given specification, e.g. tolerances of [3].

The conventional value of the mass of the test load $ m_{cTL} $ is a priori not known, its density $ \rho_{TL} $ normally differs significantly from the conventional value $ 8000\mathrm{kg / m^{3}} $ and the air density $ \rho_{a} $ at the time of calibration is normally different from $ \rho_{0} $ $ (1.2\mathrm{kg / m^{3}}). $ The reference value of the mass of the test load $ m_{ref} $ therefore depends on the air buoyancy correction $ \delta m_{\mathrm{B}} $ and $ \delta m_{B T o t} $ respectively:

$$
m _ {r e f} = m _ {c T L} + \delta m _ {B} = W _ {C I} + \delta m _ {B T o t}
$$

where $ W_{CI} $ is the result of weighing of the test load on the control instrument and

$$
\delta m _ {B T o t} = - m _ {c} \left[ \left(\rho_ {a} - \rho_ {0}\right) \left(\frac {1}{\rho_ {C I}} - \frac {1}{\rho_ {s}}\right) \right]
$$

$ \rho_{s} $ and $ \rho_{CI} $ are the densities of the standard weights used for adjusting the control and calibrated instruments, respectively.

It can be seen that the correction is very small and that usually it is not necessary to apply the correction, i.e. $ \delta m_{B T o t}=0 $ . If the conformity of the standard weights used for adjustment of the AWI and calibration of the test load to [4] is established and if the AWI is not adjusted before the calibration, the relative uncertainty is evaluated as [1]:

$$
u _ {r e l} \left(\delta m _ {B T o t}\right) \approx \left(0, 1 \rho_ {0} / \rho_ {c} + m p e / \left(4 m _ {N}\right)\right) / \sqrt {3}
$$

Case A could be applicable only in a case when comparable dynamic behaviour of pre-prepared test loads with loads normally weighed on the instruments is achieved. In this case a drift mass of the test load within a time is relevant. The density of the test load needs to be known. Since it is expected that the density considerably differs from $ 8 0 0 0 \mathrm{~ k g / m^{3}} $ , air buoyancy correction is not negligible.

In Cases B to E the mass of the test loads is determined at the time and place of calibration of the AWI. Consequently, no significant drift of the test load is expected. The density of the test load does not need to be known. The air buoyancy correction is very small or negligible and does not depend on the density of the test load.

A critical point of Cases D and E is the evaluation of the uncertainty of the control instrument in use.

In general, if the same control instrument is used, the uncertainty of the reference mass value increases from Case B to Case E.

## 4 Validation

Validation of the measurement methods, error models and measurement uncertainty budgets developed is necessary, both experimentally and on-site. It is also necessary to check the reproducibility of the proposed calibration methods and uncertainty budgets developed for the calibration of the selected categories of AWIs with the interlaboratory comparisons.

For this purpose, validation plans were prepared for each group of AWI concerned. The plans take into account instrument selection, test loads selection,

determination of the test load mass, preparation and execution of measurements, monitoring of the environmental conditions, calculation of the measurement results, and reporting.

The results obtained using the calibration methods developed for automatic instruments operating in dynamic mode will be compared with the reference values obtained by static measurements of test loads on a non-automatic weighing instrument and with the measurements obtained using an automatic instrument calibrated in the static operation mode, each case taking into account the uncertainty of measurement.

## 5 Conclusion

The expected impacts of the EMPIR AWICal project are to provide a basis for traceable dynamic measurements for three groups of AWIs based on the draft guides submitted to EURAMET and improved weighing process control in different industries such as production prepackaged products and transport. Consequently, calibration laboratories for the calibration of AWIs could be accredited by accreditation bodies based on the harmonised guidance. Supporting information on measurement uncertainty will be available to conformity assessment bodies for AWIs.

For AWIs the uncertainty of measurement depends significantly on the properties of the calibrated instrument itself, the characteristics of the test loads, the control instrument, and not only on other capabilities of the calibration laboratory. The reference mass of the test load is usually determined in-situ. Several methods for determining the reference mass are available, however it is necessary to look for a suitable compromise between the accuracy and simplicity of the selected method. Additionally, the density of the test load does not need to be known without significant influence on the measurement uncertainty.

Finally, the capabilities developed in emerging EURAMET members during the project will support their local industry and the weighing sector, and will also help to develop the conformity assessment competence of these members in order to support the implementation of the MID and Prepackages Directives.

# The role of control instruments in the calibration of automatic gravimetric filling instruments in dynamic mode

</div>

SEVDA KAÇMAZ

TübitakUME National Metrology Institute

Gebze-Kocaeli, Turkey

## Abstract

In order to determine the preset value error of an automatic gravimetric filling instrument, the reference value of the mass of the test fills needs to be known. This value can be determined in various ways at the time and place of calibration of the instrument in dynamic mode. This article describes the effect of the control instrument in three different methods used to determine the reference value of the mass of the test fills and the estimated measurement uncertainty to be attributed to the results.

## 1 Introduction

It is a well-known fact that a weighing instrument, which serves to determine the mass of a load by using the action of gravity on it, is classified as automatic or non-automatic according to the operation method [1].

With the development of weighing technology, the number of Automatic Weighing Instruments (AWIs), which carry out measurements in a dynamic mode has increased. Although AWIs generally cost more than Nonautomatic Weighing Instruments (NAWIs), AWIs are more effective and efficient for their users in the long term. Improvements in the accuracy of AWIs mean that they are now used in an increasing number of applications from micro to macro weighing. Automatic catchweighers and automatic gravimetric filling instruments are by far the two most common groups of AWIs, and are used extensively in the preparation, production

and quality assurance of pre-packed products and other products, where their content or composition is determined by weighing. Also concerned are automatic instruments for weighing road vehicles in motion, which are mostly used for weighing trucks for trade and law enforcement purposes.

While NAWIs are routinely calibrated by accredited calibration laboratories (based on the Guidelines on the Calibration of Non-Automatic Weighing Instruments EURAMET/cg-18), the calibration of AWIs is not as welldefined and there is no standard approach for their calibration. At present automatic weighing systems are often only calibrated in a static way, rather than dynamically as they are used in practice. In practice the approximation is generally made that the measurement uncertainty of the AWI's calibration in dynamic mode is equal to the uncertainties determined for the static calibration. This is misleading for all parties concerned, since dynamic operation can introduce additional sources of errors and influences that may not be apparent when an instrument is calibrated statically. The same applies to the determination of the performance of automatic weighing instruments at varying operating speeds, as there are currently only limited reliable and traceable data for the relation between speed and weighing performance. Therefore, there is a need for validated reproducible calibration methods and measurement uncertainty evaluation models for different groups of AWIs operating in a dynamic measurement mode.

The calibration methods and the uncertainty evaluation models for the three selected categories of AWIs (i.e automatic catchweighers, automatic instruments for weighing road vehicles in motion and automatic gravimetric filling instruments) have been developed and validated via on-site tests at end-users / manufacturers by the 14RPT02 AWICAL Project "Traceable calibration of automatic weighing instruments operating in dynamic mode" [2].

In this study, the effect of the control instrument was investigated in three different methods developed by the AWI CAL Project to determine the reference value of the mass of the test fills and to estimate the measurement uncertainty for the calibration of automatic gravimetric filling instrument in dynamic mode. Also, the methods applied were compared.

## 2 Determination of the reference value of the mass of the test fills

An automatic gravimetric filling instrument (AGFI) is utilized to fill containers with a predetermined and virtually constant mass of product from bulk by

automatic weighing. In dynamic mode, the calibration consists of weighing the empty containers, applying the material tests to the instrument under specified conditions, weighing the filled containers, determining the reference value of the mass of the test fills determining the preset value error at dynamic weighing, and estimating the uncertainty of measurement to be attributed to the results.

A control instrument used to determine the reference value of the mass of the test fills based on weighing filled and empty containers can be either separate (a weighing instrument other than the instrument being calibrated) or integral (when a static weighing mode is provided by the instrument being calibrated). In order to ensure the determination the reference value of the mass of the test fills, the control instrument's resolution should be equal to or less than one third of the maximum permissible error of the automatic gravimetric filling instrument to be calibrated at the calibration points agreed with the client.

The reference value of the mass of the test fills can be determined by one of the three different methods as given below [3].

## Method B - Test fills weighed on the previously calibrated control instrument

The test fills are weighed at the time and place of calibration of the AGFI, but the control instrument has been calibrated previously. If the values of the filled and empty containers are close to the calibration point of the control instrument, the error of the control instrument is taken into account in the weighing results $ W_{CI}. $

$$
W _ {C I} = E _ {C I} - E _ {C I}
$$

If the value of the filled and empty containers are not close to the calibration point of the control instrument, the error of the control instrument is not applied to the weighing results $ W_{CI} $ and also the air buoyancy correction factor $ \delta m_{B T o t} $ since both have a negligible effect on the results.

$$
W _ {C I} = R _ {C I}
$$

$$
m _ {r e f} = W _ {C I, B} - W _ {C I, T} + \delta m _ {B T o t}
$$

where

$ W_{CI,B} $ - weighing result for the filled container (gross),

$ W_{CI,T} $ - weighing result for the empty container (tare).

Method C - Test fills weighed on the previously verified control instrument

The reference value of the mass of the test fills is determined by weighing the filled and empty containers on the previously verified control instrument and no correction is applied to the weighing results $ W_{CI} $:

$$
W _ {C I} = R _ {C I}

Based on the considerations in section 3, the Author proposes the following recommendations:

# How to ensure the future quality of utility meters

</div>

KATY KLAUENBERG and CLEMENS ELSTER Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB), Germany

## Abstract

OIML Guide 20 [1] states that the key elements of metrological quality control for utility meters are initial verification, followed by subsequent verification at periodic intervals.

Focusing on subsequent verification, the PTB has developed a procedure that guarantees with high confidence that 95 % of the measuring instruments fulfil conformance standards at all times. This procedure exceeds the scope of OIML Guide 20, current practices and former German regulations that merely guarantee that measuring instruments fulfil conformance standards at the time of inspection.

The procedure developed by the PTB consists of two complementary statistical methods that combine standard statistical sampling methods with the assumption of a linear decrease in the number of conforming meters over time. Without further knowledge, standard sampling plans [2] need to be applied to reliability levels higher than 95% due to the linear transformation. The second of the two statistical methods permits application of the more efficient sampling plans contained in OIML Guide 20 (which ensure 92% reliability), when more stringent conditions are tested and additional criteria are fulfilled.

This procedure will be enforced in Germany beginning in 2019. As a result, the level of consumer protection in utility billing will increase.

## 1 Introduction

## 1.1 Legal background and current practice

tion of a variety of measuring instruments is harmonized in the MID Directive [3]. This directive establishes requirements that utility meters must satisfy when placed on the market or put into use in the EU; Annexes F and F1 of this directive describe conformity assessment procedures that may include statistical verification (as supported in WELMEC Guide 8.10 [4]).

Metrological quality control for utility meters is regulated in many countries. In Europe, initial verifica-

Subsequent verification of utility meters is not harmonized in the EU (see section 1.3). Typically, only a subset of all meters is selected according to statistical principles. Based on the result of such a sample, an entire lot is to be removed from use or may be retained. In Germany the Measures and Verification Ordinance [5] regulates subsequent verification. This ordinance was revised in 2014 and now states the following in section 35:

(R) The period of verification is to be extended when, according to statistical principles, it can be expected, that at least 95 % of the devices in the lot conform to essential requirements [...] during the entire period of extension.

A new feature of this regulation is its goal of ensuring the future quality of meters. By contrast, the former German regulation (section 14 in [6]) aimed only to prove the correctness of utility meters at the time of their verification. The same is true of OIML G 20 [1]. End users expect utility meters and the bills issued based on their use to be correct at all times - and not merely to have been correct in the past. The new German regulation takes this expectation into account.

A second feature is the fact that the regulation refers specifically to the quality requirement. The former regulation referred instead to lower-order provisions that directly specified sampling plans. These sampling plans, in turn, guarantee compliance of 92 % of utility meters at the time of inspection.

The two new aspects of subsequent meter verification have clear advantages: they lead to a higher level of consumer protection when implemented and increase transparency and flexibility when sampling procedures are adapted to new market requirements. Such requirements may arise from the so-called energy transition ("Energiewende"), from digitalisation or from technical developments.

However, regulation (R) also increases the requirements on meter quality which may result in meter surveillance entailing additional effort and expense. This may prove to be of crucial importance, as the replacement of in-service utility meters is costly (introduction to [1], [7,8,9,10]). In addition, no procedure was available to enforce the regulation after it took effect.

## 1.2 New developments

The PTB has developed two statistical methods to implement section 35 of the Measures and Verification Ordinance [5]. The first method [11] can be applied as a default method when little knowledge is available on the utility meters tested. The second method [8] employs the more efficient sampling plans in [1]. However, these sampling plans need to be implemented under stricter specifications and linked to evidence verifying certain quality characteristics.

This article summarizes how the future quality of utility meters can be guaranteed based on these two statistical methods. Section 2 recapitulates the background of these methods, provides details of requirements and describes and provides examples of their implementation. Look-up tables are given in the Appendix for simple application. Recently, verification authorities have developed procedural instructions [12] based on the PTB's research and guidance. Starting in 2019, these procedural instructions will take effect, thus allowing regulation (R) to be enforced in Germany. Section 3 gives an outlook of how evidence supporting the second method can be provided as well as of conceivable alternative methods.

In the future, European and other countries will also be able to benefit from these developments taking place in Germany. Section 1.3 describes some international aspects of legal quality control. The statistical approaches themselves may also be of interest outside legal metrology - in particular concerning sampling whose objective is to ensure future conformance or high reliability levels.

## 1.3 International context

This work describes how the quality of in-service utility meters is monitored in Germany. Other countries have different or, in some cases, no regulations for subsequent verification. For example, Austria, Sweden, the UK and Canada have voluntary or required sampling schemes to demonstrate that meters are continuing to conform to regulations [13, 14, 15, 16]. A comprehensive overview is challenging due to language barriers and to modifications to laws, regulations and practice.

Austria's regulation for subsequent verification (section 18 in [13]) is similar to Germany's and requires that trueness and reliability of the measuring devices are ensured for the entire validity extension period. However, lower-order provisions in Austria [17, 18, 19] refer only to conformity at the time of inspection, for which purpose sampling plans [1] are directly applied for electricity meters and, under somehow stricter conditions, for gas and heat meters.

Often, in smaller countries, fewer utility meters are operated, and homogeneous lots tend to have smaller

sizes. Efficient sampling plans are therefore even more important in these countries.

OIML G 20 [1] states, "one generally accepted aim of legal metrology is that a high percentage of the utility meters installed in the utility service networks respect the maximum permissible errors (MPE) in service during use". Apart from those in Germany and Austria, the authors are not aware of regulations in place in other countries that cater explicitly to this aim.

## 2 The procedure

Standard sampling methods [20, 2, 21] verify the compliance of measuring instruments at the time of inspection. They do not verify compliance during an extension of the period of validity (as acknowledged in the introduction to [1]). To predict future compliance based on a sample of devices, a so-called reliability function needs to be assumed. Section 2.1 describes the linear reliability function, which allows the future requirement of 95 % to be transformed into a higher requirement at the time of verification.

Based on this transformation, the method in section 2.2 (called the Default Method here) applies standard attribute sampling methods [2]. Application of this method relies on minimum assumptions. Section 2.3 relies on the linear transformation as well, but makes use of a more efficient method for meters whose measurement deviation is known to take a certain form and for which measurement deviation is the only cause of failure. A theorem [8] has shown that verifying stricter thresholds for the measurement deviation then guarantees regulation (R) when the well-known sampling plans in [1] are applied.

These two alternative methods are combined in section 2.4.

$$

$$

The linear reliability function is the simplest such function and is approximately equivalent to the wellknown exponential reliability model, which has a constant failure rate. (Cf. [11] for detailed reasoning and limitations.)

When the time periods T and $ t_{i} $ are not known exactly, [11] also generalizes the linear transformation (1). For example, when authorities know the date of the next inspection only in terms of the calendar year, and when in addition the lot is composed of meters with different ages (where d is the maximum age difference in a lot, and $ t_{i}=t_{1}+T(i-1) $ composed of the initial period of validity $ t_{1} $ and (i-1) extensions of length T), then the required proportion of compliant meters increases to

$$
p _ {i} = 1 - 0. 0 5 \left(1 + \frac {T + 1}{t _ {1} + T (i - 1) - d}\right) ^ {- 1}.
$$

In summary, to apply transformation (2), the following assumptions must hold:

## Assumptions 1: Linearity

- the number of functioning devices decreases linearly with time;

- instead of exact times, only whole calendar years are known, with

- meters of d different years of initial operation in one lot,

the initial period of validity being $ t_{1} $ years

inspections 1,...,i having extended (or extending) the period of validity by T years.

Procedures to verify assumptions (1) are currently being developed (see section 3). As discussed in [11], utility meters showing some specific non-linear behaviour still may be conservatively covered by the linear approach. For short validity extension periods, assumptions (1) continue to hold approximately after the inspection. For example, in Germany the reliability of utility meters is trusted to still be linear when the validity period is extended by only two years.

## Example 1: Linearity

If meters in a lot are inspected in the calendar year in which the initial verification period of 6 years is completed $ ( t_{i}=t_{1}=6 $ , such as meters for cold water in Germany), if the meters were all put into service in the same calendar year (e.g. in 2001, such that $ d=1 $ ), and if the meters are to be inspected in the calendar year in which the two-year extension period is completed $ ( T=2 ) $ , then verifying the reliability

$$
p _ {1} = 1 - 0. 0 5 \left(1 + \frac {2 + 1}{6 - 1}\right) ^ {- 1} = 0. 9 6 9
$$

at the time of inspection according to transformation (2) guarantees regulation (R).

## 2.2 The Default Method

Regulation (R) can be guaranteed by verifying the transformed reliability $ p_{i} $ with standard sampling procedures. For utility meter surveillance, the attribute sampling plans in [2] are usually applied (e.g. [1,17,18,19,22], as well as Germany's former provisions); these sampling plans were expanded to include intermediate reliability levels to increase their efficiency (cf. Table A1 in [11]). These plans rely on the following assumptions:

## Assumptions 2: Sampling

- the lot is homogeneous with respect to the factors which affect reliability;

- the lot is of a finite size and is sampled from without replacement;

- the classification of sampled meters as non- conforming is error-free; and

the statistical test properties are specified. $ ^{1} $

Specific sampling plans may be calculated efficiently via software or may be retrieved from look-up tables. Table 1 in the Appendix contains an extract of typical settings for ease of application. In brief, the default procedure for guaranteeing regulation (R) is summarized as follows:

## Procedure 1: Default Method

- provide evidence verifying assumptions (1) and (2);

- calculate $ p_{i} $ according to equation (2);

- choose the column of Table 1 such that $ 1-p_{i}\leq \mathrm{LQ}/100 $ and the row according to the lot size;

- verify n randomly selected utility meters; and

- the lot is accepted when c or fewer devices are nonconforming, and is not accepted otherwise.

## Example 2: Default Method

Continuing Example (1) with 1201 to 3200 meters in the lot, verifying the reliability $ p_{i}=0.969 $ at the time of inspection requires the sampling plan in the column $ \mathrm{LQ}=2.7 $ of Table 1 and thus $ (n,c)=(141,1) $ . That is, when no more than one out of 141 randomly sampled meters is non-conforming, the lot meets regulation (R) with high confidence.

The sample sizes in Table 1 are larger than those in [1] because the requirements are distinctly stricter $ ( p_{i} > 0. 9 2) $ . As a result, consumers are better protected against malfunctioning meters and incorrect utility bills especially between inspections.

The main advantage of these default sampling plans is their simplicity: look-up tables can be used, while few assumptions are needed. The course of action for Procedure 1 is similar to the course of action formerly used in Germany and similar to the one in [1]. Finally, this Default Method is safer for consumers than any method not containing a prediction.

## 2.3 The Efficient Method

When, in addition to the minimum assumptions of the Default Method, knowledge about the quality of utility meters is available, more efficient sampling plans are conceivable. The high costs associated with replacing in-service utility meters may be reduced, allowing effort to instead be directed towards improving specific quality characteristics of meters and obtaining evidence verifying these characteristics.

A quality characteristic whose quantitative impact was proven in [8] is measurement deviation. Specifically, when the following assumptions hold, it is now known that at least $ 1 0 0 p_{i} \% $ of a population meets specification $ \Delta $:

## Assumptions 3: Measurement deviation

The measurement deviation (or its maximum over a range)

- is the only criterion to judge the lot,

- is known to have a Normal distribution (with arbitrary parameters), and

- is smaller than specification $ \Delta /\gamma $ for at least 92 % of the lot, where

$$
1 / \gamma = \frac {\Phi^ {- 1} \left(\frac {0 . 9 2 + 1}{2}\right)}{\Phi^ {- 1} \left(\frac {p _ {i} + 1}{2}\right)} \quad \mathrm {(w i t h} p _ {i} > 0. 9 2 \mathrm {a n d} \gamma > 1)
$$

That is, instead of requiring a large sample to verify the high reliability $ p_{i} $ it is sufficient to apply a smaller sample to verify the reliability of 92 % at tighter specifications. Figure 2 illustrates this relation.

To verify the stricter specification above, standard sampling procedures such as [2] can again be applied. Consequently, the more efficient sampling plans in [1] (which were formerly used in Germany) guarantee regulation (R) when the assumptions (3) apply. These sampling plans are more efficient than the Default Method yet also provide a higher level of protection to consumers against malfunctioning meters and incorrect utility bills than with previous practice. That is, only with the mathematical proof in [8] and only with

For ease of application, the single and double sampling plans of [1,2] are reproduced in Tables 3 and 4 in the Appendix.

In brief, the efficient procedure for guaranteeing regulation (R) is summarized as follows:

## Procedure 2: Efficient Method

- provide evidence verifying assumptions (1), (2) and (3);

- look up $ 1 / \gamma $ in Table 2 (or use equations (2) and (3));

- decide on single or double sampling;

- choose the row in Table 3 or 4 according to the lot size;

- verify n randomly selected utility meters;

the lot is accepted when

all meters are compliant with respect to all criteria other than measurement deviation, and

no more than c devices have a measurement deviation larger than $ \Delta /\gamma $;

otherwise, the lot is not accepted.

Procedures for verifying the additional assumptions (3), so-called qualification procedures, are currently being developed. See section 3 for some key aspects.

## Example 3: Efficient Method

Verifying the reliability $ p_{i}=0.969 $ in Example (1) consists in verifying the reliability of 92 % when assumptions (3) hold. For the lot, conformance then focuses on measurement deviations being smaller than $ \Delta /\gamma $ according to equation (3). With 1 201 to 3 200 meters in the lot, sampling plan $ (n,c)=(80,3) $ (cf. Table 3) enforces regulation (R). That is, no more than three out of 80 randomly sampled meters may be non-conforming; furthermore, these three failures must all be due to measurement deviations that are larger than 0.812 $ \Delta. $

Compared to the sampling plan (141,1) in Example (2), the Efficient Method reduces the number of devices under test considerably and allows for more failures although under stricter specifications.

The main advantages of these sampling plans are their efficiency and the fact that they continue current practice. Fewer samples are needed than with the Default Method. The sampling plans previously applied

in Germany and in [1] are retained. The course of action is similar as well, and look-up tables can still be used. Finally, the Efficient Method is also safer for consumers than any method that does not contain a prediction.

## 2.4 Combination of both methods

It is clear that the Default Method and the Efficient Method are complementary. If the quality of a lot can be proven by verifying assumptions (3), the method in section 2.3 can be applied. That is, smaller sample sizes and larger acceptance numbers are sufficient to guarantee regulation (R), but stricter specifications need to be applied as well. If evidence verifying these assumptions is unavailable, the method in section 2.2 can be applied. That is, standard sampling procedures at higher reliability levels guarantee regulation (R).

Both methods implement section 35 of [5] and form the basis for procedural instructions [12]. These instructions, issued by the state verification authorities, will enforce section 35 of [5] starting in 2019. Consequently, meter users will be better protected against noncompliant meters; the authors hold the opinion that higher-quality meters will dominate the German market in the future. If needed, other aspects of meter quality may enable alternative efficient sampling plans. (See section 3 for an outlook.)

## 3 Future developments

To benefit from the Efficient Method (section 2.3), evidence is needed that reliability decreases linearly and that measurement deviations (or their maximum over a range) are Normally distributed. Currently, Germany's associations involved in the meter market are jointly developing qualification procedures for this purpose. The standards [23] (and in future [24]) may provide a basis to test for departure from the Normal distribution, and parametric or non-parametric estimators (such as [25]) to test for departure from linearity. In general, small samples will rarely lead to a detection of non- Normal distributions or non-linear reliability, whereas large samples may lead to the detection of very small departures from the hypothesized Normality or linearity.

Furthermore, evidence is needed that measurement deviations are the only cause of non-conformance. In practice, support for this assumption is gained with each sample. Meters that are clearly non-conforming are removed from service immediately (i.e. before sampling) under good quality management systems.

The German regulation for subsequent verification is not confined to the two sampling procedures introduced above, but leaves room for alternative or additional methods in the future. For example, more efficient sampling could be performed at shorter time intervals. A series of smaller samples could contribute more information (possibly at an earlier stage) about future insufficient reliability. To this end more flexible reliability models may be needed which may also decrease the risk to consumers of non-linearly aging meter populations. Bayesian methods and variable sampling methods may include valuable additional information. Future research could explore these approaches, quantify their benefits and weigh them against practical possibilities.

Currently, regulation (R) does not account for the economic value of mis-measured goods. If desirable, future research could also design sampling procedures to balance the costs incurred by replacing a meter with the costs incurred by a meter that obtains incorrect measurements (e.g. similarly to [9, 26]).

For metrological control of the quality of in-service utility meters, we have described two methods that guarantee with high confidence that 95 % of the measuring instruments fulfil conformance standards at all times. Both methods are simple and supported by lookup tables; the general course of action is similar to the one currently used. The two methods represent a choice between minimum assumptions and maximum sampling efficiency.

## Abstract

Based on work developed by the multidisciplinary Health Metrology Task Group (HMTG), this paper depicts new approaches for best practices in health metrology. Aiming to enhance metrological traceability, a case study is considered in the field of infusion technology where translational research is emphasized. The specific role of education in metrology is highlighted, focusing on the framework of hospitals' policy and the related background of health professionals. The discussion is structured around the ongoing project EMPIR 15SIP0.

## 1 Introduction

Although metrological tools are well established for measurements in technical fields such as economic transactions, the environment, road safety and food, they remain comparatively insufficient for measurements in the medical field.

Interest in metrology for health [1] has grown over the last few years and has led to debates and discussions about the key role played by metrological and statistical principles when applied to healthcare and health monitoring systems. One of the major challenges is the enormous diversity of measurements and the strong interdisciplinary nature of the topic.

Evaluating technological progress in the field of measurements and measuring instruments over the last years, it is readily understandable that a lot of work remains to be done with respect to metrology applied in health service facilities.

On the one hand for the medical device industry and applications in medicine, including medical equipment manufacturers, periodic testing and calibration of

equipment must be performed as part of a quality control regime that guarantees the reliability of medical instruments.

On the other hand, healthcare professionals assume that the results obtained using the instruments are accurate, reliable and comparable over time. This assumption implies that measurement results are metrologically traceable. Nevertheless, there should be awareness that measuring instrument errors, uncertainties and incorrect handling procedures could lead to incorrect diagnoses and inadequate treatments.

Although measurement errors are present and quantified in a wide range of medical measurements, healthcare professionals generally do not realize that metrological traceability is one of the causes of the most serious medical measurement errors.

When this topic is applied in the framework of healthcare, there is often no broad consensus.

Therefore, the growing awareness of the key role of metrology in healthcare creates new metrological opportunities in the area of health quality assurance.

In this context and under the framework of metrology within the Portuguese healthcare organizations, a study was performed in 2014 involving Portuguese hospitals to identify and analyze issues concerning the concept of metrological traceability [2]. One key output highlighted the lack of awareness of healthcare professionals regarding metrology as a topic requiring further evaluation. Moreover, it was also considered that the interoperability between metrology, quality assurance and healthcare was not contextualized within the current European legal framework [3]. Therefore, under the framework of the Sectoral Commission for Health - CS/09 in the Portuguese Quality System, a multidisciplinary Health Metrology Task Group (HMTG) emerged hosted by the Portuguese Institute for Quality (IPQ).

## 2 Method

Metrology in health is relatively new and many potential topics exist. Based on this approach, it has been observed that the meaning of calibration and metrology are not known by many users of medical instruments. In addition, the testing, measurement and calibration of bio-medical instruments are becoming increasingly significant, and accuracy in diagnosis and effectiveness of treatment are required. Until now, the traceability of medical measuring instruments has not been addressed by the health organizations in a systematic manner. Therefore, the metrology community needs a strong partnership with clinical experts and with the health-related industry.

Considering the key role of best metrological practices in healthcare facilities, the ad-hoc HMTG Working Group consists of engineers, chemists, nurses, physicists, pharmacists and other technical experts from several official organizations.

Public hospitals, institutions from the Portuguese Ministry of Health and other top level stakeholders are members of the HMTG, and they are also active on other technical committees which ensures a better flow of information.

Given the need to achieve best practices in healthcare facilities and taking into account clinical practices, the main goal of this multidisciplinary Task Group (TG) is to improve the knowledge of metrology among non-metrology professionals. To achieve this, HMTG developed a set of guidelines in metrology for several different measuring instruments with the aim of ensuring the application of metrological best practices by health professionals in Portuguese hospitals [4], [5], [6], [7].

The first guide published [4] is a transversal document applied to all fields of metrology, focusing on the specific field of health metrology. The subsequent guides are technical documents that cover the key tools of metrological and technical requirements for blood pressure devices [5], clinical thermometers [6] and infusion pumps [7]. These guides are freely available for download from the IPQ website $ ^{1} $

As the medical community and government authorities are considered to be the target recipients of these reference documents, the task group organizes discussions, workshops and conferences with stakeholders such as clinical experts and health professionals. All the activities developed following the publication of these guides have been in response to a strong demand from the healthcare professionals and from those who are engaged in the use of medical instruments and measurements. The users of medical instruments and devices must be given training in order to make them aware of the role of metrology, as well as the methodologies of measurement and calibration which are described in the guides listed above.

From a metrological point of view, the national task force has been a key factor in establishing and supporting confidence in medical measuring instruments and clinical measurements.

## 3 Infusion technology

The complexity of some medications and the multiple steps involved in their preparation, administration and

monitoring create a scenario that demands close attention to detail and relies heavily on human intervention to detect and mitigate errors. In order to support the use of infusion technology, medical infusion pumps are widely used as they are fundamental for primary health care, namely for providing drugs, nutrition and hydration to patients such as syringe pumps and peristaltic pumps. Hence, it is crucial that the volume and flow delivered by these instruments are as accurate as possible. To ensure this, it is necessary to have appropriate calibration methods and standards. These instruments present different measuring features and capacities according to their use and therapeutic application [8].

Two different calibration methods can be used to determine the flow error of infusion pumps [9]. One is the gravimetric method, considered to be the primary method and commonly used by the National Metrology Institutes. The other is a comparison method where an Infusion Device Analyzer (IDA) is used as a flow generator, and is typically used in hospital maintenance offices.

## Introduction

The OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) came into operation on 1 January 2018 with the participation of 20 countries. The aim of the OIML-CS is to harmonize the work of national bodies that are responsible for the type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, manufacturers of measuring instruments, who are required to obtain type approval in countries in which they wish to market their products, may benefit from the OIML-CS as it provides evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation.

The objective of the OIML-CS is to avoid unnecessary re-testing when obtaining national type evaluations and approvals. OIML Issuing Authorities approved by the OIML-CS Management Committee issue OIML Certificates and OIML type evaluation reports for measuring instruments according to the relevant OIML Recommendation. National or regional authorities then utilize these OIML Certificates and/or OIML type evaluation reports to issue national or regional approvals. By this approach, the measuring instrument type needs to be tested only once by an approved test laboratory.

At present, ten OIML Issuing Authorities and 21 Test Laboratories are approved under the OIML-CS as competent bodies to perform tests and examinations according to the relevant OIML Recommendations. These authorities are located in Australia (NMIA), Denmark (Force), France (LNE), Germany (PTB), Japan (NMIJ), Netherlands (NMi), P.R. China (NIM), Slovakia (SLM), Switzerland (METAS) and the United Kingdom (NMO). These countries, plus Belgium, Cambodia, Canada Colombia, Cuba, India, Korea (R.), Latvia, Namibia,

New Zealand, South Africa and the United States, have committed to utilize OIML Certificates and/or OIML type evaluation reports for issuing national type approvals.

The OIML-CS covers 18 categories of measuring instruments such as utility meters, automatic and nonautomatic weighing instruments, taximeters and fuel measuring instruments (including fuel dispensers).

## OIML Certification System development

The OIML Basic Certificate System was developed in 1991. As an intergovernmental organization, the OIML aims to harmonize the regulations and metrological controls applied by national metrological services. In order to establish international harmonization of metrological requirements for different kinds of measuring instruments, OIML Recommendations are developed by Project Groups under Technical Committees or Subcommittees. Currently there are more than 100 Recommendations available. These Recommendations are intended as model regulations for a number of categories of measuring instruments. OIML Member States are morally obliged to implement these as far as possible. For example, these Recommendations are recognized by the European Commission as "normative documents" to demonstrate compliance with EU Directives. Corresponding tables are developed by the European Cooperation in Legal Metrology (WELMEC) between the OIML Recommendations and the European Directive 2014/32/EU (MID) for different categories of instruments. Using this approach, OIML Recommendations serve as normative documents for automatic weighing instruments, utility meters and taximeters under the 2014/32/EU Directive. Also other countries/regions align their national regulations with OIML Recommendations and accept OIML test results for national approval, sometimes with some additional national requirements.

Manufacturers have already used OIML Certificates for many years to issue national type approvals for measuring instruments. The main approach of the OIML-CS is that the instrument is tested according to the relevant OIML Recommendation only once and then accepted by national authorities worldwide. This acceptance was in several cases supported by Mutual Recognition Arrangements (MRA) between the OIML Issuing Authorities.

## One-stop testing, global access

Although the Basic Certificate System functioned correctly, improvements were needed. Whereas a number of Issuing Authorities performed their work under accreditation, some self-declared their competence. No requirements were laid down concerning accreditation, and the application of manufacturer test results was not visible. These shortcomings endangered the acceptance of OIML type evaluation/test reports and OIML Certificates for a number of countries.

To improve the system, the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA) was established in 2005 which led to the creation of a Declaration of Mutual Confidence (DoMC) between different countries for each category of measuring instrument. The MAA was

based on accreditation or peer assessment of the testing laboratory according to ISO/IEC 17025. By December 2017 over 4000 certificates had been issued under the Basic System and the MAA, of which more than 1000 were issued under the MAA. However, the MAA was limited to only three categories of measuring instruments - water meters (R49), load cells (R60) and non-automatic weighing instruments (R76). Having two systems in parallel, with a limited number of categories of instruments under the MAA and having no requirements for determining the competence of the Issuing Authority led to the need to revise the complete system.

On 1 January 2018 the OIML-CS was launched. The OIML-CS consists of two schemes: Scheme A (comparable with the MAA) and Scheme B (comparable with the Basic Certificate System). Furthermore, OIML Issuing Authorities shall comply with ISO/IEC 17065 requirements and the Test Laboratories shall comply with ISO/IEC 17025. The OIML-CS was launched with non-automatic weighing instruments (R76) and load cells (R60) in Scheme A. All other categories of measuring instruments started in Scheme B and will be transferred to scheme A within two years. In 2020, all instruments shall be under Scheme A, meaning that Scheme B Certificates will no longer be issued. By this approach, the OIML-CS will have succeeded in upgrading all certificates to a level that meets the

## Benefits for Utilizers and Associates

Countries with emerging economies currently do not always have sufficient capabilities to assess the conformity of complex measuring instruments that are under legal control. Smart utility meters can be of great benefit for these countries, but the testing of these meters can be complicated. National authorities that are responsible for the approval of these instruments have to rely on the testing capabilities of their designated test laboratories before the instruments are placed on their market. These laboratories are not always equipped with adequate technologies to test these instruments, or do not have sufficient knowledge available. Nevertheless, these countries have a great need in putting these instruments into use in their countries.

Smart utility meters are already widely installed in different regions. These meters have numerous advantages for both energy suppliers and users when compared to mechanical meters. Smart and also prepaid meters can be of great benefit for other regions, such as large countries with remote areas. Remote reading of the meter is a major advantage. Additionally, fraud can be detected more easily. However, these meters contain electronics and software with new technologies. Software especially plays an essential role. National test

laboratories in countries with emerging economies which are designated to perform independent examinations of these instruments have limited resources to evaluate the software. Consequently manufactures encounter long waiting times to obtain approval of their products.

Countries such as Colombia, Cambodia, Cuba and India now draw full benefit from the OIML-CS as Utilizers to overcome this obstacle. They trust the competence of approved OIML Issuing Authorities which perform type examination of measuring instruments. Their trust in the OIML Certificates and associated type evaluation reports is based on the quality infrastructures of the OIML Issuing Authorities and accompanying Test Laboratories.

## Transition arrangements from OIML Basic/MAA to OIML-CS

In October 2017 at the 52nd CIML meeting in Colombia, CIML Members approved a resolution to replace the OIML Basic System and the MAA with the OIML-CS from 1 January 2018. OIML certificates (and associated type evaluation reports) issued under the previous Systems remain valid. However, transition arrangements are still needed to enable the use of "old" test data for certification under the OIML-CS.

As previously mentioned, the OIML-CS consist of two schemes, A and B, which are comparable to the MAA and Basic systems. The main difference between Scheme A and Scheme B is that a Test Laboratory has to demonstrate its competence by peer assessment or accreditation in order to participate in Scheme A. The participation of a Legal Metrology Expert in these assessments is mandatory. To give the opportunity to Test Laboratories to demonstrate their compliance for a specific instrument (the specific OIML Recommendation), Test Laboratories are allowed to issue Scheme B Certificates for a two-year period. Thereafter, no Scheme B Certificates may be issued for that category of instrument. According to the OIML-CS, test data obtained under the Basic System cannot be used for Scheme A. Hence, the instruments that were tested under the Basic System have to be retested if the manufacturer wants a Scheme A Certificate. Both OIML Issuing Authorities and manufacturers agreed that this is an unfavorable situation and the test data should be trusted when this data is obtained under certain conditions, e.g. accreditation.

OIML MAA certificates, including underlying evaluation and test reports, are accepted under the OIML-CS for establishing Scheme A and B Certificates. The MAA already provided mechanisms that are

equivalent to the OIML-CS for reviewing and approving Test Laboratories. Therefore, OIML Issuing Authorities can simply use the test data obtained under the OIML MAA to establish Scheme A Certificates.

Similarly, Scheme B Certificates can be established for the test data obtained under the OIML Basic System since both systems are based on self-declaration of competence. However, the aim of the OIML-CS is to provide utilizers of OIML-CS Certificates with sufficient trust in the competence of Test Laboratories. Therefore, self-declaration is insufficient for Scheme A Certificates. Moreover, OIML Recommendations transition into Scheme A within two years. Thereafter, no Scheme B Certificates can be issued, meaning that old test data can no longer be used for Scheme A Certificates.

For these reasons, the OIML-CS Management Committee reviewed the transition arrangements. Considering that the MAA was limited to three categories of measuring instruments, it is obvious that there is a need to use "old" test data under the OIML-CS. Members of the MC agreed that the test data obtained before 1 January 2018 may be used by OIML Issuing Authorities (under certain conditions) for issuing an

OIML type evaluation report and OIML Certificate under Scheme A. In addition, a Working Group was established to develop the conditions for the use of this test data and to revise the OIML-CS documentation accordingly.

## The OIML Certification System (OIML-CS)

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

It is a single Certification System comprising two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B (see the OIML website).

The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate. Participating in the OIML-CS commits, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS. OIML B 18:2017 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) establishes these rules whereby signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation and test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an OIML Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

The OIML-CS was launched on 1 January 2018 and replaces the OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

## The OIML Basic Certificate System

The OIML Basic Certificate System for Measuring Instruments was introduced in 1991 to facilitate administrative procedures and lower the costs associated with the international trade of measuring instruments subject to legal requirements.

The System provided the possibility for manufacturers to obtain an OIML Basic Certificate and an OIML Basic Evaluation Report, indicating that a given instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML International Recommendation. The Basic System was described in OIML B 3 OIML Basic Certificate System for OIML Type Evaluation of Measuring Instruments, which was withdrawn when the OIML Certification System was introduced in January 2018.

## The OIML MAA

GUO Su, Secretary, CEEMS Advisory Group

An OIML Pilot Training Center (OPTC) event, the "OIML/APLMF Training Course on Prepackaged Goods" was held in Nanning, Guangxi, P.R. China from 10-13 April, 2018. This training course was jointly organized by the OIML, the Asia-Pacific Legal Metrology Forum (APLMF) and the State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR), P.R. China. It was supported by the Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB) and the OIML Pilot Training Center (OPTC).

The aim of this training course was to help improve and harmonize metrological capability in the OIML Member States and in the Asia-Pacific region and facilitate trade development through the removal of technical barriers and the application of consistent regulatory practice. It covered OIML R 79 Labelling requirements for prepackaged goods and R 87 Quantity of product in prepackages in addition to providing valuable insights into 'best practice' in this area of legal metrology, including guidance on system requirements for a certification system for prepackages.

Participants also visited the Guangxi Institute of Metrology and a Shuanghui Group for on-site practice. Through the lectures by trainers, open discussions and face-to-face exchanges of experiences, each participant acquired the skills and knowledge to understand and apply the requirements of OIML R87 and R79, inspect prepackaged goods using internationally accepted AQS methodology, determine whether their economy's current inspection systems meet OIML requirements, and develop an action plan to implement OIML requirements.

# In Remembrance of Ferenc Petik (1928-2018)

</div>

by ATTILA SZILVASSY - Former BIML Assistant Director

Ferenc Petik was born in 1928 in Hungary where he completed his studies. He later obtained a doctorate degree at the Hungarian Academy of Sciences (MTA).

He began his career at an industrial firm for material testing machines. In 1963 he joined the National Office of Measures, Hungary (OMH) where he led the field of force and hardness standard measurements and the evaluation of material testing machines. He developed measurement standards as well as calibration methods especially for hardness measurements.

About a quarter of the 100 publications he authored were published in international scientific journals and he gave numerous presentations at conferences and symposiums.

From 1973 - as head of the OMH General Metrology Secretariat - he had the responsibility to extend the scope of activities to international and regional metrology organizations (OIML, BIPM, COMECON Metrology Section, etc.) and also to regional and bilateral cooperation.

In 1980 in Washington he was appointed BIML Assistant Director and joined the International Bureau of Legal Metrology in October 1981.

He brought his scientific knowledge and laboratory practice in the fields he was active in at the OMH, as well as his knowledge of French, English, Russian and German. He contributed to the development or revision of several OIML Recommendations on testing blocks for hardness measuring machines, weighing instruments based on resistance of materials, and also the quadrilingual vocabulary on hardness measurements. He also helped maintain connections between various international and regional organizations and the OIML, and helped develop the working methods of the OIML Pilot and Reporting Secretariats as they were called at that time.

After two five-year mandates at the BIML he returned to his native country to end his career.

He suffered the pain of losing his wife Maria Petik née Erdödi in 2013 and passed away himself in May 2018.

The BIML, together with its past Director Bernard Athané, present their sincere condolences to the daughter, son and grandchildren of Ferenc and Maria Petik, and also to their whole family.

## Background

Many countries and economies with emerging metrology systems suffer from a lack of resources for the operation of a sound legal metrology system. Although these resources cannot be provided by the OIML, the Organization supports initiatives for the development of legal metrology. To highlight the importance of metrology activities in CEEMS, and to provide an incentive for their improvement, in 2009 the OIML established an Award for "Excellent achievements in legal metrology in Developing Countries".

Following the establishment of the Advisory Group on matters of Countries and Economies with Emerging Metrology Systems (CEEMS), and an increased focus on OIML activities in this area, it was decided to rename the Award the "OIML CEEMS Award" from 2018.

The OIML CEEMS Award is intended to raise the awareness of, and create a more favorable environment for legal metrology and to promote the work of the OIML.

The Award intends: "to acknowledge and honor new and outstanding activities achieved by individuals, national services or regional legal metrology organizations contributing significantly to legal metrology objectives on national or regional levels."

## How can candidates be proposed?

Nominations may be made by any individuals or organizations concerned with legal metrology, including the individual or organization seeking the Award.

Nominations should be sent to Ian Dunmill at the BIML and must contain facts documents and arguments explaining why the candidate deserves the Award. The closing date is 18 August 2018.

## Selection procedure

The BIML will prepare a list of candidates highlighting the importance of the achievements. The Award winner will be selected by the CIML President and announced at the 53rd CIML Meeting in October 2018.

## Selection criteria

The criteria which will be used to assess the candidates' contribution or achievement will include:

- its significance and importance;

- its novelty;

- its attractiveness and adaptability for other legal metrology services.

## The Award

The Award will consist of:

a Certificate of Appreciation signed by the CIML President;

a token of appreciation, such as an invitation to make a presentation of the Award-winning achievement at the next CIML Meeting or OIML Conference at the OIML's expense;

an engraved Award trophy.

Of the 14 personalities who have to date headed the PTB and its forerunner, the Physikalisch- Technische Reichsanstalt, Dieter Kind stands out because of his 20 years in office. Not only nationally, but also internationally he made an outstanding contribution to metrology. He served as President of the highest body in the world of metrology, the International Committee for Weights and Measures of the Metre Convention (CIPM), from 1984 to 1996.

Dieter Kind studied electrical engineering in Berlin and Munich and received a doctorate in high-voltage technology before being appointed President of the PTB in 1975. He mastered this responsibility magnificently, which was reflected in the large number of honors and prizes he was awarded - including the Grand Cross of Merit of the Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany.
