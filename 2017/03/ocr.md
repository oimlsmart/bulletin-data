OIML technical work

In recent years, the OIML has made extensive efforts to improve the procedures used in its technical work. This has meant significant changes to the Directives for OIML technical work (OIML B 6), resulting in revisions in 2012, 2013. A further revision, based on the experience gained over the last three years of operation, will be proposed for approval by the CIML in October this year.

Over the same period, the OIML website has undergone a complete overhaul, particularly as far as the facilities available to Members are concerned. All Project Group membership and management is now handled through secure parts of the website, and tools have been developed to allow Project Group conveners to communicate and consult with their members as well as to archive all information relating to their work. Additionally, all Project Group and CIML voting now takes place via the website.

The BIML is committed to ensuring that all those responsible for the technical work receive adequate training to be able to use these website tools effectively and to fully understand the technical work procedures and OIML structures described in B 6. A pilot training course was conducted in Germany last year, and in this edition of the

OIML Bulletin you will find a report on a highly successful training course held in Saint Petersburg, Russian Federation in early June. Within the next year, it is our intention to run similar courses elsewhere in Europe, in the Americas, in Africa and in Asia.

The replacement of the existing OIML Basic Certificate System and Mutual Acceptance Arrangement by the new OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) is also creating a need for training to ensure that all stakeholders can take full advantage of the new System.

At the same time, the OIML has been working with the ACP EU TBT Programme to develop a comprehensive e-learning package on legal metrology. The platform is now in place on the OIML's servers, and can be freely accessed. The courses are currently available in English, but French translations will be added shortly, and new courses will be made available in the future.

All these developments show that training and keeping our Members updated has become a significant activity for the OIML at the moment, and is likely to remain so in the near future.

# Performance based method for determining the calibration frequency of upright cylindrical tanks in custody/royalty service

</div>

JAMES CLYDE HASSELL, Consultant Engineer, Saudi Aramco, P&CSD/ PASD/CMU

LUIZ GUSTAVO DO VAL, Metering Engineer, Saudi Aramco, P&CSD/ PASD/CMU

## Abstract

This paper presents the results of a performance based method for establishing calibration frequencies of vertical upright cylindrical tanks in custody/royalty transfer service. This performance based method will allow tank operators to adjust calibration intervals to maintain volumes losses under 0.02 % of the calibrated volume. This number is 10 times less than the number stated by OIML R 71 [1] for the maximum permissible capacity table uncertainty of vertical cylindrical tanks.

Volume comparisons performed by the authors at terminal facilities have identified tanks with volume variations around 0.05% (5 times the ISO recommended limit). As an example of potential financial impact, a one million barrel crude oil tank with this rate of expansion would distribute an additional 500 barrels of unaccounted crude per tank transfer. At a terminal with six million barrels per day delivery, the unaccounted crude per transfer may be 3,000 barrels per day. At USD 50 per barrel this equates to USD 150,000 of unaccounted crude per day or USD 54,750,000 per year.

variations in capacity can result from physical changes made to the tank structure, prolonged environmental exposure and the stresses experienced during normal use.

## 1 Introduction

Large storage tanks will vary in capacity from their original manufacturer specifications. These

Per the American Petroleum Institute (API) Manual of Petroleum Measurement Standards (MPMS) guidelines and the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) [2,3], tank calibration should be performed when a new tank is placed into service, as well as a total recalibration every 15 years for custody transfer services. API guidelines also allow tank owners to increase or decrease re-calibration frequencies based on the evaluation of historical data, field tests and performance records.

The International Organization of Legal Metrology (OIML) [1] mentions that the national authorities shall determine the frequency of tank re-calibration, and the requirements. Tank re-calibration shall also be carried out if deformation of the tank causes a change in its metrological qualities (including reference gauge points, floating roof, and other modifications). Subsequent verification, when carried out, shall include examination of the construction and of its external appearance. For tanks used in custody transfer service, the bottom course diameter, bottom course plate thickness and tank tilt shall be verified.

In addition to bottom course plate thickness and tank tilt, one method specified by API and ISO for evaluating tank volume capacity change over time is to perform manual strapping (external circumference measure using a calibrated steel tape) of the lower tank course every 5 years. The external circumference obtained from this strapping is mathematically converted to internal circumference under unstressed condition (empty tank). This standardized internal diameter can then be compared to the previous tank diameter corrected by the same method. The difference in corrected internal diameters can then be compared against Table A-1 and Table B-1 of references [2] and [3] respectively. Tables 1 and 2 have been compiled from Tables A-1 and B-1 of API and ISO, respectively, and are presented below.

In the case of tilt as per ISO [3], the recommended criterion is a change of 10 mm/m of tank height. Although this will affect the apparent tank capacity to only a relatively minor extent (less than 0.01 % of the volume change), the change in tilt is considered more important than the capacity effect. A significant degree of tilt can be an indication of structural problems in the tank's foundations and should be investigated. The same effect is applicable for the reduction of the plate thickness in the range of 1.5 mm to 3 mm stated by reference [3]. Also, based on the authors' experience, changes presented in the tank's tilt and plate thickness are small when compared with the measure of

the bottom course circumference, and do not impact the capacity table.

This paper will outline a methodology to establish calibration frequencies of vertical upright cylindrical tanks in custody/royalty transfer service through the measure of the circumference of the bottom tank course.

This method will allow tank operators to keep losses due to changes in tank volumes under 0.02 % of the calibrated volume, which is 10 times less than the maximum permissible uncertainty for vertical cylindrical tanks stated in OIML R 71 [1].

Also presented are the bottom course diameter variation results for five different tanks over 10 years of operation. These tanks are used in crude oil and refined products custody transfer at terminal locations.

## 2 Methodology

## 2.1 Determination of the reference circumference

- Use a master strapping tape to measure the circumference of the tank. Preferably, use a tape with the same coefficient of linear expansion as the tank shell material to minimize any tape correction and its associated uncertainties.

- Take the reading of the liquid level in the tank.

- Take a measurement of the bottom course plate's thickness. The plate thickness must be measured at eight points circumferentially on the bottom courses and averaged as per reference [3]. Or, acquire the plate thickness from the last inspection report of the tank or from the tank drawing [2,3].

- Determine the density of the liquid in the tank.

- Take the measurement of the reference circumference at a position where working conditions allow reliable measurements. Strap the tank at one of the following levels, [4]:

1) 1/5 to 1/4 of the course height above the ground for the first course, or

2) 1/5 to 1/4 of the course height below the upper horizontal seam of the first course.

Note 1: Once one of the above levels is selected, the same level shall be used for the next verification.

Note 2: In this work the strapping level selected was at 80 % of the course height.

- Repeat the circumference measurement to achieve measurements agreeing within the tolerances specified in Table 3.

- Apply the corrections from the tape's calibration certificate for each reading performed on the item above.

- Take the arithmetic average of the corrected values of the circumferences determined on the item above. This average is the external circumference of tank $ ( x_{l} ) $ to be used in the calculation to determine the unstressed internal bottom course diameter.

## 2.2 Determination of the internal diameter

## 2.2.1 Internal circumference of the bottom course under strapping conditions

The internal circumference of the bottom course is determined by Eq.(1):

$$
x _ {4} = x _ {1} - 2. \pi . x _ {2} - x _ {3}
$$

Where:

$ x_{4} $ is the internal circumference under the strapping conditions of the bottom course (mm).

$ x_{l} $ is the external circumference under the strapping conditions of the bottom course (mm).

$ x_{2} $ is the plate and paint thickness of the bottom course (mm).

$ x_{3} $ is the obstruction to the tape due to vertical seams (mm) determined by Eq. (2), [3].

$$
x _ {3} = \frac {2 \times N \times t \times w}{d} + \left(\frac {8 \times N \times t}{3}\right) \times \sqrt {t / d}
$$

Where:

N is the number of butt straps or projections per course.

t is the amount of rise (thickness of straps or projections) (mm).

w is the width of the straps or projections (mm).

d is the nominal diameter of the tank (mm).

## 2.2.2 Internal circumference of the bottom course under unstressed condition

If the tank is not empty at the time of the circumference measurement, the average circumference shall be corrected to unstressed condition (empty tank) by the correction value given by Eq. (3), [3].

$$
\Delta C = \frac {g \times x _ {4} ^ {2} \times (\rho - 1 . 2) \times H}{2 \times \pi \times E \times x _ {2} \times 1 0 0 0}
$$

Where:

$ \Delta C $ is the circumference correction to obtain the empty or unstressed condition for the bottom circumference (mm);

g is the acceleration due to gravity $ \mathrm{(m / s^{2})} $; A value for $ g=9.806 $ 65 $ \mathrm{m / s^{2}} $ is used in this work [3].

$ \rho $ is the density of the liquid at the observed temperature at the time of strapping $ \mathrm{(k g / m^{3})}. $

H is the head of liquid above the level of the strapped circumference during strapping (mm). It is the difference between the height of the liquid $ \left(h_{l}\right) $ at the time of measurement and 80 %

of the bottom course height $ ( h_{c} ) $ . The head H can be calculated by Eq. (4), [3].

E is the Young's modulus of elasticity $ \mathrm{N / m^{2}} $ of the tank. A value for $ E=200 \, \mathrm{x} \, 10^{9} \, \mathrm{N / m^{2}} $ is used in this work [3] for tanks constructed with carbon steel.

$$
H = h _ {I} - \left(0. 8 \times h _ {c}\right)
$$

If we use a value for $ \pi=3,1 4 1 $ 593, [3], Eq. (3) can be reduced to Eq. (5):

$$
\Delta C = \frac {0 . 7 8 0 3 9}{1 0 ^ {1 4}} \times x _ {4} ^ {2} \times (\rho - 1. 2) \times \frac {H}{x _ {2}}
$$

So, the internal circumference of the bottom course under unstressed condition (empty tank) can be determined by Eq. (6):

$$
x _ {5} = x _ {4} - \Delta C
$$

Where:

$ x_{5} $ is the internal circumference of the bottom course under unstressed condition (empty tank) (mm).

## 2.2.3 Internal diameter of the bottom course under unstressed condition

Once the internal circumference of the bottom course under stressed condition has been determined, the average internal diameter of the bottom course (D) can be determined by Eq. (7), as:

$$
D = \frac {x _ {5}}{\pi}
$$

## 2.3 Tank bottom course inside diameter variation

To use Table 1 or Table 2, the internal diameter variation $ (\Delta D) $ of the bottom course under unstressed condition shall be determined by Eq. (8), as:

$$
\Delta D = (D) _ {L V} - (D) _ {L C}
$$

Where:

$ \Delta D $ is the variation of the internal diameter of the bottom course (mm).

LV is the subscript that means "last verification." It is the internal diameter of the bottom course under unstressed condition calculated with the last verification data.

LC is the subscript that means "last calibration". It is the internal diameter of the bottom course under unstressed condition calculated with the last calibration data of the tank to generate the capacity table.

The combined standard uncertainty of the variation of the internal diameter of the bottom course under unstressed condition can be determined by Eq. (9):

$$
u _ {\Delta D} = \left[ \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {1 4} \left(c _ {i} \times u _ {i}\right) ^ {2} + 2 \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {7} \sum_ {j = i + 7} ^ {1 4} c _ {i}. c _ {j}. \left(u _ {i}. u _ {j}. r _ {i, j}\right) \right] ^ {1 / 2}
$$

Where:

$ u_{\Delta D} $ is the combined uncertainty of the variation of the internal diameter of the bottom course (mm).

$ r_{i,j} $ is the correlation factor between the standard uncertainties $ u_{i} $ and $ u_{j} $ . It may be determined by engineering judgment or based on simulations or experiments. The value is a number between -1 and 1, where $ r_{i,j}=0 $ represents uncorrelated quantities, and $ |r_{i,j}|=1 $ represents fully correlated quantities. In this work a value of $ |r_{i,j}|=1 $ is used for all correlated input quantities [5]. In this work, the input data used to determine $ \Delta D $ is taken over 10 years, so the only correlation quantities used in this work are: $ x_{2}, x_{3}, h_{c} $ and E due to the use of the same values and uncertainties during the evaluation of the tank diameter at LV and LC conditions.

The fourteen (14) sensitivity coefficients of Eq. (9) can be determined from Eq. (10) to Eq. (24), as:

$$
c _ {1} = \frac {\partial D}{\partial x _ {1}} = \frac {1}{\pi} - \frac {g \times x _ {4} \times (\rho - 1 . 2) \times H}{\pi^ {2} \times E \times x _ {2} \times 1 0 0 0}
$$

$$
c _ {2} = \frac {\partial D}{\partial x _ {2}} = - 2 + \frac {g \times x _ {4} \times (\rho - 1 . 2) \times H}{\pi \times E \times x _ {2} \times 5 0 0} + \frac {g \times x ^ {2} _ {4} \times (\rho - 1 . 2) \times H}{\pi^ {2} \times E \times x _ {2} ^ {2} \times 2 0 0 0}
$$

$$
c _ {3} = \frac {\partial D}{\partial x _ {3}} = - \frac {1}{\pi} + \frac {g \times x _ {4} \times (\rho - 1 . 2) \times H}{\pi^ {2} \times E \times x _ {2} \times 1 0 0 0}
$$

$$
c _ {4} = \frac {\partial D}{\partial \rho} = - \frac {g \times x ^ {2} _ {4} \times H}{\pi^ {2} \times E \times x _ {2} \times 2 0 0 0}
$$

$$
c _ {5} = \frac {\partial D}{\partial h _ {l}} = - \frac {g \times x ^ {2} _ {4} \times (\rho - 1 . 2)}{\pi^ {2} \times E \times x _ {2} \times 2 0 0 0}

## 2.4 Criteria for deciding the volume variation of a tank

After the tank calibration, the second requirement in assuring the quality of the volume results is in-service checks between tank calibrations. Normally, companies define the time between calibrations based on their internal policies, on international standards or on national regulations applicable where the tank is installed.

API [2], for example, recommends that a total recalibration should be undertaken every 15 years, even if the five-yearly verification checks do not show any variation for a given tolerance chosen in Table 1.

ISO [3] mentions, as a general rule, that a variation in tank volume of 0.01 % or greater should be considered to be significant. The authors recommend a tolerance of 0.02 %, due to the following reasons:

1. The use of a tolerance of 0.01 % will reduce the time between verifications by half, and may become an unfeasible process for tank operators to implement.

## 4 Conclusions

This paper has outlined a methodology to establish calibration frequencies of upright cylindrical tanks in custody/royalty transfer service through the measure of the circumference of the bottom tank course.

The methodology will allow tank operators to keep losses, due to changes in tank volumes, under 0.02 % of the calibrated volume, which is 10 times less than the maximum permissible uncertainty for upright cylindrical tanks stated in OIML R 71 [1].

Based on the results presented, each tank shall be treated as an "individual instrument" to have its performance monitored within 0.02 % of volume variation. For example, tank A presented a time between calibrations of 2.4 years. So, the authors recommend that each tank shall be monitored in intervals of every 2 to 3 years. The average value is half of that actually used by the petroleum industry, which is 5 years.

Also, the authors recommend that Table A-1 of API [2] should be changed to the same format

of Table B-1 of ISO [3] to implement this methodology easily. The API table does not show variation in tank capacity for a specific tolerance, for example, 0.02 %. It presents the tolerances by ranges. In addition, the API table should be expanded for larger nominal tank diameters. Some tanks analyzed presented nominal diameters higher than those shown in the API table.

Finally, this work presents a methodology to calculate the expanded uncertainty of the tank's diameter variation. The results presented values in the range of $ \pm1 $ to $ \pm2 $ mm for k=2. These figures show the effectiveness of the strapping method to monitor the tank volume variation using Tables 1 or 2 compiled from API and ISO.

## Abstract

Being able to control the actual quantity of product in prepackages (see Figure 1) is indispensable in order to ensure fair trade, maintain competitive markets, and protect consumers against under-filled prepackages. In many countries, governmental authorities check packers or conduct market surveillance to ensure compliance with metrological requirements concerning the quantity of product in prepackages.

The 2016 edition of OIML R 87 Quantity of products in prepackages [1] superseded the 2004 edition with significant improvements, for example a sound statistical basis. This Recommendation also proposes a new stepwise sampling plan for the statistical control of prepackages. The aim of this procedure is to decrease the burden on packers by requiring fewer samples to be taken for control inspections, especially in cases of noncritical prepackages under high quality control. In addition, this new procedure also saves time and costs of both inspectors and packers since fewer prepackages have to be opened and destroyed for control purposes.

This article explains the basic concepts and statistical background of the single step sampling plan proposed in the main text of OIML R 87:2016. Then, it introduces the new stepwise sampling plan proposed in its Annex H.

## 3 Reference test for metrological requirements with sampling procedure

## 3.1 Introduction of sampling inspection

In those countries in which OIML R 87 is applied, legal metrology officials including inspectors conduct tests to determine whether prepackages comply with the requirements introduced in 2.1-2.3. The tests are carried out by statistical means based on random sampling of an inspection lot. This inspection lot with a size of N is determined based on an hourly production number in many cases. Use of such a sampling method is highly practical to save time and cost required by the inspectors.

From an inspection lot size N, an inspector randomly selects a sample which is composed of a group of products with a size of n. The acceptance or rejection of the production lot is based on a test on the n items of the sample. The number of n is usually much smaller than N, what means that only a small portion of the products is inspected. This statistical approach inherently introduces a certain risk of incorrectly rejecting a "good lot" (i.e. a correct lot fulfilling the criteria). This risk is referred to as a "producer's risk" (PR). On the other hand, there is also a certain risk to incorrectly accepting a "bad lot" (i.e. an incorrect lot not fulfilling the criteria). This risk is called a "consumer's risk" (CR) [5].

## 3.1.1 Average requirements for sampled lots

Due to such an uncertain nature of a sampling inspection, OIML R87 specifies several probabilistic criteria based on statistics. A set of criteria arose from the average requirement in 2.1. Among them, one criterion specifies that the probability of incorrectly rejecting a good inspection lot satisfying equation (1) shall be no more than 0.5 % (PR). Another one specifies that the probability of correctly rejecting a bad inspection lot with an average quantity less than $ Q_{\mathrm{nom}}- $ 0.74 $ \sigma $ shall be at least 90 % (CR), where $ \sigma $ is the standard deviation of the actual quantity of prepackages in the inspection lot.

## 3.1.2 Individual requirements for sampled lots

Another set of probabilistic criteria arose from the individual requirement in 2.2. One criterion specifies that the probability of incorrectly rejecting a good lot, where 2.5 % of prepackages has T1 errors, shall be

smaller than 5 % (PR). Another criterion specifies that the probability of correctly rejecting a bad inspection lot, where 9 % of prepackages has T1 or T2 errors, shall be at least 90 % (CR).

On the basis of the given metrological requirements above and the specified producer's and consumer's risks, the associated statistical tests can be generated. It has to be noted that both the average test and the individual test are independent of each other. This fact means that they will follow separate requirements as outlined below.

## 3.2 Statistical principles of control by sampling

To ensure practical application of the criteria in 3.1.1 and 3.1.2, OIML R 87 proposes the following statistical procedures to conduct a sampling inspection:

## 3.2.1 Test of average requirement

R 87 requires rejecting the lot if:

$$
e _ {\mathrm {a v e}} / s + S C F < 0 \quad \mathrm {w i t h} \quad e _ {\mathrm {a v e}} = \frac {1}{n} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} e _ {i}
$$

Here, $ e_{\mathrm{ave}} $ is the average of errors, $ e_{i} $ are the individual prepackage errors defined by $ e_{i}=q_{i}-Q_{\mathrm{nom}} $ taking into account their signs, $ q_{i} $ are the measured actual quantities of prepackages in the sample, n is the sample size of prepackages, s is the sample standard deviation of the individual errors, and SCF is the Sample Correction Factor always with a positive sign. The criterion in equation (4) guarantees that the probability of incorrectly rejecting a good inspection lot is less than 0.5 %.

An appropriate value of SCF, which fulfills the requirements in OIML R 87 for the identified lot size, is calculated using the formula in equation (5):

$$
S C F = - \frac {t _ {0 . 0 0 5 , n - 1}}{\sqrt {\frac {n (N - 1)}{N - n}}}.
$$

Here, $ t_{0.005,n-1} $ is the quantile of the Student's $ t $ inverse cumulative distribution function with the two parameters, the probability $ p=0.005 $ and the degree of freedom $ f=n-1. $

On the other hand, in order to fulfill another criterion, which specifies that the probability of correctly rejecting a bad inspection lot with an average quantity less than $ Q_{\mathrm{nom}}-0. 7 4 \sigma $ shall be at least 90 % (CR), the criterion in equation (6) shall be satisfied:

$$
\sqrt {\frac {n (N - 1)}{N - n}} \geq \frac {t _ {0 . 9 , n - 1} - t _ {0 . 0 0 5 , n - 1}}{0 . 7 4}
$$

Based on a statistical analysis however, this criterion actually holds in most of the sampling inspections using OIML R 87. Therefore, there is effectively no need to consider this criterion.

## 3.3 Stepwise sampling plan

In order to reduce inconveniences of the packers and inspectors, a new stepwise sampling plan has been introduced in Annex H of the new OIML R 87. This plan is especially appropriate and effective for controlling so-called "low-risk" prepackages under high quality control with high hourly production rates. In a sampling inspection of such a 'good' inspection lot, an inspector may easily know empirically that the inspection lot will likely pass the test after a small group of prepackages has been measured. The normal inspection method specified in Table 2, however, requests the inspector to inspect all items in the sample regardless of the quality of the lot.

The stepwise sampling plan consists in interrupting the measurement on a 'good' lot and making a decision at an early stage using other numerical criteria provided only for this plan. In principle it starts with a limited number of measurements and proceeds with additional steps of measurements only if the test result is not conclusive. At each step, an acceptance criterion for the lot is defined with acceptable numbers of prepackages with T1 error $ \left(n_{\mathrm{ac}}\right). $

Unlike the single step sampling plan of Table 2, however, the calculation procedures for developing the stepwise sampling plan were not simple and it was impossible to solve it with an analytical method. A lengthy numerical simulation using iterative calculations was therefore employed. In this procedure, many values of probabilities against different combinations of parameters N, n, $ n_{\mathrm{T1}} $ $ n_{\mathrm{T2}} $ and $ n_{\mathrm{ac}} $ were simulated, and an assessment was repeated if each of the values fulfilled the statistical criteria in 3.1.2.

The simulation was conducted for the most critical cases with an average quantity equivalent to the nominal quantity $ \mu=Q_{\mathrm{nom}} $ based on the criteria specified in OIML R 87. We conducted the simulation for the cases with the lot size of every number from 100 to 10000. For the cases from 10000 to 100000, the number of every 100 was simulated for efficiency (e.g. 10100, 10200, 10300, etc.).

## 4 Conclusion

Being able to control the quantity of product in prepackages is important in order to ensure fair trade, maintain competitive markets and protect consumers Traditionally, such controls are performed by using a single step sampling scheme or a double sampling plan according the EU legislation (Directive 76/211/EEC).

The revised OIML R87 presents a new stepwise sampling plan that ensures the same level of confidence in terms of both the consumer's and the producer's risks. More importantly, fewer packages have to be measured in most cases. This is particularly beneficial for inspectors in checking high volume production of prepacked goods.

# Measuring the benefits of legal metrology to place it in the National Quality Infrastructure

</div>

BRUNO AMADO RODRIGUES FILHO, D. Sc. National Institute of Metrology, Quality and Technology - INMETRO, Brazil

## Introduction

One of the biggest problems in order to be able to measure the importance of metrology activities (especially legal metrology activities) is the difficulty in measuring their benefits quantitatively. It is well known that legal metrology provides confidence in measurements, affecting both the economy and society by ensuring conformity, fair trade and fair competition. However, measuring these benefits to demonstrate their importance is not trivial.

The infrastructure required to support consumers and the general public is very expensive since it involves measurement standards, specialized workforces research, logistics and accreditation - in other words all the components of the National Quality Infrastructure For example, in 2014 in Brazil, the budget of the Brazilian National Metrology Institute, INMETRO, was around USD 245 million (taking into account the exchange rate at that time).

Society and governmental funding bodies constantly question national metrology institutes about their budgetary allocation. Then, those who make investments in the sector demand results that can be expressed quantitatively. As early as in 1832 Charles Babbage stated that "The cost to the purchaser is the price he pays for any article, added to the cost of verifying the fact of its having that degree of goodness for which he contracts".

Therefore, the costs incurred due to the legal metrology component are part of the price of the products the activity aims to control, and so they are financed by society, directly or indirectly. Consequently, the benefits provided by legal metrology must surpass the costs. Although the costs are simple to compute, the benefits are not.

Finally, in order to contribute to answering the question regarding the place of legal metrology in a quality infrastructure, we present the results from a range of quantitative impact studies that demonstrate the relevance of metrological activities to the economy.

## Measuring the economic impact of legal metrology

Since there are several studies on the impact of either metrology or legal metrology, we decided to mention only a few of them. We consider their quantitative results, the different methodologies used, as well as whether they are used as references in other/new studies. Although some of the studies evaluated metrology as a whole, they also show the impact on the economy, and in fact on society as a whole.

Akerlof, Nobel Prize laureate, discussed what he called the "costs of dishonesty" in his studies about quality and uncertainty; in some cases, governmental intervention may increase the welfare of all parties (Akerlof, 1970). In our case, we aimed to evaluate the benefits reaped in return for expenditure in the infrastructure to prevent market asymmetry. This asymmetry, which is also understood as being a lack of knowledge of good quality products in a given market, may lead to their exclusion due to the societal inefficiency of the asymmetric information flow (Stiglitz, 2002). An economic approach connected the measurement deviations to an amount in monetary units, where this money at risk can be treated as a lack of information about the product.

The intention to evaluate the economics of legal metrology is not a new subject. In 1973, Stiefel proposed a model to measure the monetary value of goods which incorrectly change hands due to inaccuracies caused by device errors, or so-called economic distortion. His model computes the product of the error derived from the error distribution of devices, and the monetary value of the products traded by the device. However, the main limitation was the availability of data at the time.

Additionally, proxy-models have also been extensively applied, as presented by Don Vito, who estimated that measurement-related activity comprised 3.5 % of the US GNP, considering the measurement costs incurred in all the sectors of the US economy (Don

Vito, 1984). Another example is the estimate conducted by the American business community in Saudi Arabia, describing NIST's efforts to harmonize standards which resulted in an increase in exports to the country of between USD 300-500 million per year, at a cost of about USD 0.5 million to the Institute (Semerjian; Watters, 2000).

Moreover, studies carried out in 2001 in Canada by KPMG Consulting for the Institute for National Measurement Standards applied a model that measured the public component of maintaining primary metrological standards using the costs of registration for ISO 9000 and ISO 14000 as an indicator of companies' willingness to pay for traceability (KPMG, 2001).

Temple and Williams (2002) also discussed whether measurement activity constitutes an economically important area for policy concern, considering the infratechnology from the United Kingdom, which comprises the structure that includes measurements and test methods and standard reference materials. The study presents a formulation to estimate the aggregate impact of measurement R&D, which link R&D expenditures to improvements in technology, resulting in increases in factor productivity and GDP. The authors concluded the study estimating that the measurement activities related to R&D amounted to over 2 % of the GDP.

The UK Department of Trade and Industry used a mapping measurement impact model (MMI) to assess the economic benefit to industry of different research projects funded as part of the UK National Measurement System, showing total economic benefit measures (EBM) of around £2010 million (Bowns et al, 2003).

As more accurate and precise methods and standards provide a competitive advantage in the market place, at an international trade level, in 2002 Usuda and Henson proposed a methodology that can be applied to any industry and any market considering the deviations of measurement standards between two parties, e.g. manufacturer and consumer/exporter and importer. The case test, applied to national measurement of mass, achieved an uncertainty of around 0.05 mg at 1 kg, showing that the economic loss for the weighing industry in Japan is around USD 77,000. However, an uncertainty of 0.5 mg would cause an economic loss of around USD 2 million.

An input-output approach, based on Leontief's 1975 IO Model, was also conducted; it considered all the interconnections within the economy. As this model describes economic reality as closely as possible (Davar), this approach considers all the aggregations and interconnections among the various sectors of the economy due to the relation between the output of a sector and its input to other sectors of the economy. This allows identification of the way in which any distortion in an input affects the entire economy. The model was applied to measure the impact of metrological fraud in

the fuel market in Brazil, using empirical data; the result showed that the loss to consumers is around USD 300 million for 1 % of fraudulent devices in the market, considering a metrological fraud of 10 % (Rodrigues Filho; Goncalves, 2017).

## Conclusions

Legal metrology enforces requirements in measuring instruments commonly used in trade, for measurements that cannot be checked by one of the parties, ensuring fair competition, minimizing losses to the economy. The studies presented demonstrate the vast economic impact of legal metrology activities, representing in some cases a significant amount of the GDP of some countries. Thus, legal metrology controls can be classified as a major part of a quality infrastructure; they are thus not merely a "minor box on a graph", but rather contributing equality to provide confidence in measurements. The metrology entities rely on each other, contributing to national reliability. Scientific metrology research into new methods, as well as the maintenance of national standards and accreditation, is responsible for spreading measurements with known uncertainties due to an unbroken chain of calibrations. Legal metrology enforces requirements in vital areas.

It is also worth noting that only the economic impact is presented here; the contribution of legal metrology to health and the environment must also be considered as being benefits of legal control.

The OIML plays a major role in this system, guiding countries towards conformity and known maximum permissible errors, thus ensuring acceptable losses for the markets, and ensuring fair trade and competitiveness. As a key player, the author believes that the OIML should also invest in impact methodologies to harmonize studies concerning the impact of legal metrology systems, allowing comparisons between systems. This would permit the creation of indicators to measure the maturity of legal metrology systems, thus allowing analysis and continuous improvement for countries.

# OIML Training Workshops for PG Conveners and TC/SC Secretariats

</div>

GILLES VINET, BIML

A series of OIML training workshops has been launched to assist OIML Project Group (PG) conveners and Technical Committee/Subcommittee (TC/SC) secretariats. A first training workshop had been conducted by Mr. Ian Dunmill, BIML Assistant Director, in 2016 in Germany.

At its 51st annual meeting in Strasbourg, the CIML directed the BIML to intensify such training activities in order to assist countries assuming convenership/ secretariat responsibilities for OIML PGs or TCs/SCs.

The need for such training has increased over the last few years, as several new persons have been appointed as PG conveners. In addition, the OIML website has been significantly modified. For example, online voting tools have been improved and PG workspaces have been created to allow PGs, TCs and SCs to conduct work and share information in their own dedicated work area (PG Workspace) on the OIML website. Furthermore, OIML B6 (which establishes the rules for conducting technical work) was revised in December 2013 and further revisions are expected to be approved by the CIML in October 2017. These rules clarify how PG conveners and members, as well as TC/SC secretariats and members, are expected to conduct OIML technical work.

The increasing need for such training, combined with an OIML budget surplus, led the CIML, at its 51st meeting, to instruct the BIML to conduct such training over the next 24 months and to fund training expenses for PG conveners and TC/SC secretariats.

A two-day training workshop was conducted on 6-7 June 2017 in St. Petersburg, Russia. Ian Dunmill and Gilles Vinet delivered this training workshop to 25 participants from Russia.

OIML structure and publications;

The workshop covered the following aspects of OIML technical work:

Role and responsibilities of PGs and TCs/SCs;

- Role and responsibilities of PG conveners and members;

- OIML rules, processes and tools that PG conveners need to know to lead an OIML project;

- OIML website - where to find things and how to use it; and

PG workspace - how to use it.

The training was very successful and well organized by our Russian colleagues who also arranged a visit to the VNIIM Institute of Metrology laboratories and to their Metrology Museum which was a short 10-minute walk from the training venue.

Additional training workshops will be conducted in 2017 and 2018 for all OIML Member States which have a Project Group convenership or Technical Committee/ Subcommittee secretariat. In order to proceed in a costeffective manner for the OIML (i.e. reduce overall travel costs), training workshops will be conducted in Asia, Europe, and the USA.

PAUL DIXON, BIML

## Introduction

Following the article on the new OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) in the April 2017 edition of the OIML Bulletin this article provides a report on the activities of the provisional Management Committee (prMC) on the implementation of the OIML-CS, including the outcomes of the second prMC meeting that was held on 13-14 June 2017.

## Activities since the first prMC meeting

A significant amount of activity has taken place to address the outcomes of the first prMC meeting that was held in February 2017. The first key decision that was taken by the prMC was the approval of the new OIMLCS logo which is shown in Figure 1 opposite. Approval of the logo was seen as a key step in establishing a recognizable brand for the OIML-CS and will be used on all materials associated with the OIML-CS.

The change to the OIML-CS structure - removing the Advisory Panel and incorporating a Review Committee as a sub-committee of the Management Committee that was agreed at the first prMC meeting has been incorporated into the OIML-CS documentation.

The OIML-CS documentation has also been further improved and final versions of the Working Drafts of the revision of OIML B 18 and the Operational Documents (ODs) and Procedural Documents (PDs) were developed for approval at the second prMC meeting.

A "temporary" area on the OIML website has been established to provide information on the OIML-CS and work has started on developing the full OIML-CS website which will go live later this year. Further consideration was also given to the transitional arrangements under the OIML-CS, and proposals were developed regarding the revision of existing OIML Basic and MAA certificates from 1 January 2018 as well as the use of test data from those certificates for issuing OIML-CS certificates for consideration by the prMC at its second meeting.

Nominations for legal metrology experts to participate in accreditation and peer assessments have been received and the nominated experts have provided information on their experience and capabilities. The Review Committee established at the first prMC meeting will use the information provided to make a recommendation to the prMC on the suitability of the nominated experts.

Work has also taken place with ILAC regarding the revision of the existing Joint OIML-ILAC Assessment Procedure for test laboratories in accordance with ISO/IEC 17025, and a proposal has been made to IAF to develop a similar Joint Assessment Procedure to cover OIML Issuing Authorities under ISO/IEC 17065.

## Second prMC meeting

The prMC held its second meeting on 13-14 June 2017 in Shanghai, P.R. China. Representatives from 13 OIML Member States (Australia, Czech Republic, France, Germany, India, Japan, Netherlands, New Zealand, P.R. China, South Africa, Switzerland, United Kingdom and United States) and one Organization in Liaison (CECIP) participated in the meeting. Representatives from Cambodia, Indonesia, Iran and the Philippines also attended as observers.

At its meeting the prMC made a number of decisions and agreed on a set of actions to progress with the implementation of the OIML-CS so that it may become effective from 1 January 2018, replacing the current OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

## Next steps

The prMC report to the 52nd CIML Meeting in 2017 will make a series of recommendations and proposals to enable the OIML-CS to become effective on 1 January 2018.

The draft revision of OIML B 18 will be submitted to the CIML for approval in October 2017 and the ODs and PDs will be published on the OIML website. A number of Working Groups have been established to address the actions that were raised at the second prMC meeting.

The BIML will update the "temporary" OIML-CS pages on the OIML website to provide further information for key stakeholders, and will continue to work on the development of the full OIML-CS website.

Members of the prMC and BIML staff will identify further opportunities to promote and raise awareness of the OIML-CS amongst key stakeholders such as potential OIML Issuing Authorities, Utilizers and manufacturers. One such event is the seminar on the OIML CS that will be held in Cartagena de Indias, Colombia in conjunction with the CIML meeting in October 2017.

Further information is available at: http://cartagena.oiml.org/seminar.html
