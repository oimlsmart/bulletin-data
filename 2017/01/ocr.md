Happy New Year!

PETER MASON CIML PRESIDENT

The beginning of a new year is naturally a time of looking forward. But our hopes and expectations are framed by our experience of the past. And after the surprises which 2016 brought to many parts of the world, we can be forgiven if our hopes and expectations for the coming year involve more than the usual element of uncertainty.

Uncertainty is of course a familiar concept to metrologists. And for the legal metrology community in particular one of our most important roles is to reduce uncertainty in the areas we are involved in so that markets and society can function efficiently, to the benefit of all. But can there be a wider significance as we move into increasingly uncertain times? I believe there can, for three reasons.

Second, the tools that we use - setting standards and conducting conformity assessment - are ones which have been taken up in many other areas of regulation and our experience of using such tools is something which we can readily share with other regulators. The new Certification System is an excellent example of how an organisation like the OIML can prove a model for others.

First, the regulation of weights and measures was one of the first areas of regulation to evolve as civilisation developed, and it is still one of the easiest for most of us to appreciate. We are in a unique position to speak up for the value of regulation in a rapidly changing world.

Finally, we have a long history of international cooperation. Some of that is through formal structures such as the OIML. But much is also achieved by more informal initiatives, as demonstrated by the work stimulated by the CEEMS Advisory Group and reported to the 15th Conference. By continued participation in such initiatives we can demonstrate the practical value of such cooperation.

So as we move into 2017 I see grounds for confidence in the contribution that legal metrology can make to securing a less uncertain future.

Happy New Year.

# The impact of BIPM amendments on Romanian mass dissemination

</div>

ADRIANA VALCU

BRML - National Institute of Metrology, Romania

## Abstract

From January 2014 to January 2015 an extraordinary calibration using the IPK (International Prototype of the Kilogram) was carried out at the BIPM. After this calibration campaign, it was concluded that the results obtained for the set of BIPM working standards indicate the existence of an offset from the IPK of 35 $ \mu g $ over 22 years. Therefore, the Consultative Committee for Mass and Related Quantities (CCM) recommended that all mass calibrations of national prototypes and of mass standards issued by the BIPM during the period 2003-2013 need to be amended in line with this value. During this time, the Romanian National Prototype of the Kilogram (NPK), together with the stainless steel kilogram Ni81, were calibrated twice at the BIPM: in 2005 and in 2013. After receiving the BIPM amendments, the first measure taken by the Mass Laboratory was to perform a comparison between the NPK and the stainless steel reference standards, which represents the main step in the dissemination of the mass unit in Romania. This paper describes the results obtained from this comparison, the impact of these new values on the Romanian mass dissemination and the actions taken in consequence.

## 1 Introduction

All mass measurements must be traceable to the IPK which is held at the BIPM. This traceability is achieved via copies of the IPK held by National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) and maintained by periodic reverification of these copies by the BIPM. Therefore, the prototypes serving as national standards of mass are returned periodically to the BIPM for calibration either on an individual basis, which can be done anytime, or as

part of a simultaneous recalibration of all the prototypes known as "periodic verification" [1].

During its lifetime, the NPK - which is copy no. 2 of the IPK - was calibrated six times at the BIPM: three "periodic verifications" occurred between 1899-1911, 1939-1953 and 1988-1992 and there were three additional calibrations in 1976, 2005 and 2013.

In Romania, the most important standard after the NPK is represented by a stainless steel kilogram, Ni81. In 2005 and 2013, this mass standard was sent together with the NPK to the BIPM for calibration and received a new mass value. The first task after receiving these standards back from the BIPM was to perform comparisons between the NPK, Ni81 and all the reference stainless steel mass standards (a set of two 1 kg mass standards and two sets of disc weights from 50 g to 500 g). From January 2014 to January 2015 an extraordinary calibration using the IPK was carried out at the BIPM. After this calibration campaign, it was concluded that the results obtained for the set of BIPM working standards indicate the existence of an offset from the IPK of 35 $ \mu g $ over 22 years. Therefore, the Consultative Committee for Mass and Related Quantities (CCM) recommended that all mass calibrations of national prototypes and of mass standards issued by the BIPM over the period 2003-2013 need to be amended with this value.

## 2 The impact of amended mass values on the Romanian mass dissemination and actions taken in consequence

After receiving the new certificates from the BIPM, the questions that arose were whether a new campaign of calibration for the Romanian reference standards was required, and what measures should be taken for the next level of mass dissemination, namely the weights of accuracy class $ \mathrm{E}_{1} $ . Should customers in possession of such weights be informed?

Given these circumstances, our Mass Laboratory had two options: either to make the necessary corrections only by simply changing the calibration certificates of the reference standards, or to assign new mass values by performing additional comparisons starting from the NPK and the reference mass standards. The laboratory opted for the second option.

Even if the amended mass difference lies within uncertainty values of the reference mass standards (having a magnitude of one half of the expanded uncertainty given in the calibration certificate), it was considered that it was safer to perform additional comparisons at this level of mass dissemination. In this way, as a first stage, three reference kilogram standards

All the measurements are carried out under controlled environmental conditions. For accurate

## 6 Estimated mass values and uncertainties

## 6.1 Estimated mass values

The theory used for estimating mass values is the least squares approach. The measurement model is:

$$
X \beta = Y - e
$$

where:

$ \beta $ (k,1) is the vector of the k mass values of the standards;

X(n,k) is the design matrix;

e (n,1) is the vector of the deviations from the expected values of the mass differences;

Y(n,1) is the vector of n mass differences obtained by weighing (including buoyancy corrections). Each element "y" of the vector is calculated as follows:

$$
\begin{array}{l} y = \Delta \mathrm {m} + \rho_ {\mathrm {a}} \left(V _ {T \mathrm {t}} - V _ {S \mathrm {t}}\right) + C _ {\mathrm {m l}} = \Delta \mathrm {m} + \rho_ {\mathrm {a}} \left\{V _ {T 2 0} ^ {*} \left[ 1 + \alpha_ {T \mathrm {t}} \times \right. \right. \\ (t - 2 0) ] - V _ {S 2 0} ^ {*} \left[ 1 + \alpha_ {S t} \times (t - 2 0) \right] \} + C _ {\mathrm {m l}} \tag {2} \\ \end{array}
$$

where:

$ \Delta m $ is the difference of balance readings calculated from the weighing cycles (ABBA);

$ \rho_{\mathrm{a}} $ is the air density at the time of the weighing;

$ V_{\mathrm{Tt}} $ $ V_{\mathrm{St}} $ are the volumes of the weights involved in a measurement at temperature "t";

$ \mathrm{V}_{T 2 0}; $ $ \mathrm{V}_{S 2 0} $ are the volumes of the weights involved in a measurement, at $ 2 0^{\circ} \mathrm{C} $;

$ \alpha_{T \mathrm{t}} $ $ \alpha_{S \mathrm{t}} $ is the volume expansion coefficient of the weights, temperature dependent;

$ C_{\mathrm{m l}} $ is the correction due to different center-of mass heights calculated as:

$$
C _ {\mathrm {m l}} = m \times \gamma \times \delta h / g
$$

with: m nominal value of the weight, $ \gamma $ gravitational gradient $ g $ acceleration due to gravity, $ \delta h $ represents the distance between the centers of mass of the two artifacts being compared.

The expected values of the unknown masses are calculated using formulas [4]:

$$
\langle \beta \rangle = \left(\mathrm {X} ^ {\prime} ^ {\mathrm {T}} \times \mathrm {X} ^ {\prime}\right) ^ {- 1} \mathrm {X} ^ {\prime} ^ {\mathrm {T}} \times \mathrm {Y} ^ {\prime}
$$

The terms from formula (4) are determined according to [4].

The mass of the check standards was measured and compared to the accepted values using T-test statistics according to [1].

## Abstract

Several developments, such as information technology tools, new processes and fast technological evolution, have changed the activities conducted under the legal metrology umbrella in order to bring symmetry and fair competition to the market as well as incorporating sustainable and renewable practices.

One of the main aspects of research and development relies on sharing information and data in order to allow international comparisons, which enables the correlation of process results among countries. OIML International Recommendations are an important instrument in achieving harmonization. However, the literature regarding the results of these standardized processes is sparse, and countries consequently have no tools to measure the maturity level of their processes regarding legal metrology activities. This article shares the results regarding the Brazilian metrology activities, such as verifications, inspections, non-conformities, as well as giving a brief overview regarding the Brazilian legal metrology system, and the most recent developments, in order to enable international comparisons and discussions.

## 1 Introduction

metrological control has expanded from trade to the environment, health, consumer protection and significant areas that impact society.

Commerce is recognized as being one of the most ancient activities attributed to humanity and a range of historical evidence demonstrates its importance to the development of societies (Birch, 2003). Similarly to commerce, weights and measures (since weighing devices date back about ten thousand years) (Euler, 2015) were initially introduced for food control in commerce (Birch, 2004). In modern society this

Regulations are applied to imperfect markets in order to control the asymmetries among the parts, for example fair trade between buyers sellers in commerce, and legal metrology is the process that embraces the application of statutory regulation and enforcement activities in order to control weights and measures (VIML, 2013), bringing conformity to society.

Although there is growing literature about legal metrology, researchers generally do not publish national results regarding their legal metrology activities. A systematic literature review considering the period 2004-2014 (Rodrigues Filho; Goncalves, 2015), regarding legal metrology control shows that only Germany, Thailand and Jordan published studies reporting information on their legal metrology results, such as the number of verifications and non-conforming measuring instruments (Kochsiek, 2005; Kochsiek; Schulz, 2004; Melhem, 2009).

Science and development relies on reproducibility and sharing of information, which in turn allows research in different regions to reproduce the results of a formulation, research or any other methodology. When control processes are applied to different regions, different results might be expected, since there are several reasons that can affect the results such as social and cultural characteristics. Then, only a deep investigation can explain different results. However, information is necessary in order to compare processes and understand the differences providing improvements to increase effectiveness.

Moreover, due to globalization, economies are interconnected while remaining interdependent, especially in free trade areas and among parties in common trade policies where non-conformities in measurements might affect more than one country. Therefore, knowledge about the indicators of the legal metrology processes is an important tool for conformity.

Additionally, most measuring instrument manufacturers commercialize their products globally, and the information about non-conformities is an important indicator to identify the harmonization of the instruments put into local markets, and it can also help to identify bad commercial practices, when for example a producer sells non-conforming instruments to one country and good quality ones to another (in market surveillance, for example).

The present article shares the results and developments of the legal metrology activities concerning measuring instruments on the market in Brazil. We also discuss the publication of information regarding legal metrology allowing future comparisons and discussions concerning efficacy and effectiveness of the legal metrology processes.

## 2 Legal metrology in Brazil

Brazil is the fifth biggest country considering its total area and the ninth economy in the world (World Bank, 2016). Although the country is divided into five microregions (North, Northeast, Central, Southeast and South), comprising 26 states and 5,570 municipalities the majority of the population (56 %) is located in the South and Southeast regions (IBGE, 2016). Therefore, the Brazilian metrology system has to be designed to cover this structure, providing consistency and fairness to the national market.

The National Institute of Metrology, Quality and Technology (INMETRO) is the National Metrology Institute in Brazil and is the governmental body responsible for legal metrology in the country. Brazil adopted the metric system in 1862 and it is one of signatories of the 1875 Metre Convention; Brazil has been both a BIPM and an OIML member since 1983.

So, in order to conduct its legal metrology activities INMETRO delegates verification and inspection activities to public bodies known as Weights and Measurements Institutes (WMI), since these activities

can be only conducted by public agents according to Brazil's legislation. The WMI forms the Brazilian Network of Legal Metrology and Quality (Rede Brasileira de Metrologia Legal e Qualidade - INMETRO, RBMLQ-I, in Portuguese), and some delegated bodies are also divided into regional branches in order to cover the region more effectively. Despite this delegation, the Institute is still responsible for regulation, planning and control regarding legal metrology activities, surveillance, type approval and research. INMETRO also has two branches that belong to the RBMLQ-I.

INMETRO also authorizes manufactures and importers to conduct self-verification for utility meters, chronotachographs and sphygmomanometers. The RBMLQ-I is also responsible for authorizing repairers of measuring instruments. Regardless of the repairs, after repair verifications are also conducted in order to comply with national legislation.

In 2015, the Brazilian infrastructure regarding metrological verifications and inspections comprised 2,562 metrologists, 561 vehicles and 57 trucks in order to cater for national demand. Figure 1 summarizes the national infrastructure and also gives an overview regarding the country's geography.

## 4 Research and developments

Research and development carried out by INMETRO regarding legal metrology has mainly focused on enhancements of processes and fraud control.

The information technology tools have also been extensively explored in order to provide intelligence and optimization to legal metrology activities.

An Integrated Management System (IMS) has been introduced in order to organize all the information gathered from the delegated bodies in the states providing real time information regarding measurement activities, instrument deviations as well as prepackage tests in the market, and supporting decision makings (Pohlmann Filho et al., 2014). The implementation of the system also enables data mining and a knowledge management approach.

Moreover, an online platform (Platform of INMETRO on the States-PSIE) has been introduced as an interface of the Institute to society, providing

certificates, status of verifications, and other information, as an instrument of transparency (Rodrigues Filho; Soratto; Goncalves, 2015).

Additionally, the Institute has developed a system called SIMCS to support the planning and execution of metrological verifications in Brazil. SIMCS provides the Coverage Ratio of metrological verification services of each of the INMETRO delegated bodies, in several types of economic activities (Soratto et al., 2014). The system has provided information regarding measuring instruments under legal control as well as regions of the country requesting more intensive legal metrology actions.

In the last five years, INMETRO has also invested heavily in technological tools to avoid metrological fraud, especially electronic tools (Leitao; Vasconcellos; Brandao, 2014). The Institute has dedicated an entire division to creating countermeasures to difficulties encountered in implementing these counter-fraud devices such as training, equipment, software requirements, etc.

Recently, the Institute has conducted a project in order to measure the economic impact of legal metrology activities due to an Input-Output Model, aggregating all the interconnections of the economy and allowing deviations in products that affect interdependent sectors (and consequently the economy) to be evaluated (Rodrigues Filho; Goncalves, 2016).

## 5 Conclusions

Brazil is a continental country with several distinct regions, from densely populated areas to difficult access ones. The legal metrology system in Brazil faces several challenges in order to bring conformity to such a diverse country, once a regulation must be followed in the entire country. However, the number of verifications and inspections shows the robustness of the system as well as the low non-conformity percentage in most of the instruments in the country.

In order to face these difficulties, INMETRO has invested intensively, in the last years, in information and communication technology tools and they have showed some improvements to the Brazilian legal metrology system.

The investigation of the economic distortion indicator also follows the state of the art in the field providing a strong indicator to improve regulations and surveillance activities as well as improving measurements on the main products of the country that contribute to the Brazilian international trade balance.

Finally, we hope to encourage other National Metrology Institutes to publish their results in order to provide data and information on international comparisons among countries. These comparisons can provide indicators for self-analysis regarding their national metrology system and its maturity, allowing the improvement of their processes and activities in the legal metrology field, and thus contributing to harmonization at an international level.

The past year has been quite a challenging one for our Organisation. As an organisation, our Members are at the heart of everything we do, and it has been a great pleasure to see Thailand join us as our sixty-first Member State. In addition to welcoming Miss Wiboonlasana Ruamraksa as Thailand's CIML Member, I also extend a welcome to our other new CIML Members:

I have already mentioned the sad passing of Willem Kool, and this has of course placed considerable strain on the staff resources within the Bureau. As I noted in my Report last year, staff within the Bureau have a tremendous record of being willing and able to cover for each other, but the impact of this way of working for any length of time is significant. Work has to be reprioritised and individuals have to become familiar with new subjects and new issues. I should like to pay tribute to all the staff of the Bureau for the way in which they have dedicated themselves to carry on our Organisation's business under the circumstances.

Our current procedures allow us very little flexibility when there is a need to replace an Assistant Director but it was clear from an early stage that additional resources would have to be brought in on a temporary basis. Another focus of activity this year has been the project to introduce a single, integrated OIML Certificate System. Once again, in Arcachon we adopted a very ambitious timescale for completing this project and I am pleased that it has indeed been possible to get to a point where we have a new Draft Basic Publication ready for CIML approval. The approach which we have adopted, based on models which have been found to be successful in other international organisations, is one which will place much of the responsibility for running the System in the hands of a new Management Committee. Nevertheless, it is also important that the right balance is struck between proper oversight by the CIML and the practical authority which the Management Committee needs to operate effectively. I am sure this will be a major topic of our discussions in Strasbourg. As a result of these efforts, we can be confident that if the new Basic Publication is approved at our meeting in Strasbourg the new System will be able to start operating provisionally next year and come into full operation in 2018.

The third major topic discussed in Arcachon led to the adoption of CIML Resolution no.2015/10 on "addressing the needs of countries and economies with emerging metrology systems" (CEEMS). As foreshadowed in Resolution 2015/10, a lot of effort has gone into developing the concept of Pilot Training Centres and two very successful centres were initiated in Beijing and in Guangzhou in July and August this year. One of the most important features of the approach we adopted last year was the focus on co-operation with others, not

least bilateral co-operation with individual Member States. This is clearly illustrated by the initiatives in China. Another excellent example occurred in Bangkok in March where Manfred Kochsiek and I organised a joint PTB/OIML workshop on different approaches to type approval as part of the PTB's programme of support to the Association of South East Asian Nations (ASEAN). Nevertheless, there is still much to do in this area and one of the things we will have to discuss in Strasbourg is how we organise oversight of our work on CEEMS matters going forward.

In various conversations I have had with colleagues who are keen to develop the legal metrology systems in their own countries, the approach adopted in Arcachon has generated a lot of interest. This was particularly evident at the Annual SIM Meeting in the Dominican Republic which I attended in November 2015. It is also clear how important OIML Certificates are in establishing our visibility in so many parts of the world. All of this is additional confirmation of the benefits which we can hope to see when the new and improved scheme is introduced.

In my last two reports I made reference to the OIML's participation in the OECD's study of the role of international organisations in regulatory cooperation. In fact, the OIML has featured in this work as a case study, carried out under my supervision by Marc Loesewitz, a student at the Nanterre Centre of International Law (CEDIN), who served as an intern at the Bureau. This study has in turn contributed to a major OECD Report which will be published at the beginning of November. As well as giving us the opportunity to share with colleagues in other organisations our experience of introducing reform and modernisation into our processes and procedures, this work has ensured that the OECD Report acknowledges the wide diversity of models for international cooperation, not least our own model which depends so heavily on involving our Member States. It has also been a useful opportunity to raise the profile of legal metrology with other important international organisations. Marc will be present at our meeting in Strasbourg and will be able to provide additional information on his work.

The OECD study has confirmed the importance of the bilateral relationships which we have with a number of other international organisations, most notably the BIPM. Last year I noted the scope there was for our two organisations to work even more closely together in promoting the role which a modern metrology infrastructure, designed to support scientific metrology industrial metrology and legal metrology, can play in economic development. This is one of the areas that we have not been able to give much attention to this year, due to the need for re-prioritisation, but it remains an important element in our approach to the CEEMS community and I hope the discussions at the 15th Conference will provide an opportunity to explore further how these ideas can be taken forward.

In preparing for the 15th Conference, I have naturally looked again at what we have previously identified as the three main challenges for our Organisation over the next few years. The past year has confirmed the relevance of those priorities:

First, speeding up and making more efficient our processes for producing and revising our Recommendations and other publications is vital if we are to remain relevant in an ever-changing world. That means embracing new technology, amending our procedures to reflect that new technology and training ourselves on how best to operate those procedures.

Second, our Certificates are a major part of how we are seen in many parts of the world. Potentially they are one of the most important elements we can offer to countries which want to improve their levels of metrological control but which lack the resources to operate full-scale type approval controls. Simplifying and modernising our Certificate System is key to responding to those needs.

Third, meeting the needs of our Members - including our Corresponding Members - with emerging metrology systems is an essential part of remaining relevant in a world which is increasingly globalised and where the resources available to public authorities are under pressure everywhere. The comprehensive package of proposals we adopted last year is an excellent start, and has been very well-received, but delivering that package will involve several years of effort.

Finally, on a personal note, many of you will be aware that I and my family have faced some difficult issues of our own this year. I would like to express my thanks to you all for the kindness and support I have received from the two Vice-Presidents, other members of the Presidential Council, many other CIML Members and the Director, Stephen Patoray, and his staff. I am grateful to everyone who makes this the very special organisation which it is.

I look forward to a very successful week of meetings and, together, to taking our Organisation into the future.

# Update on the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS)

</div>

PAUL DIXON, BIML

## Introduction

At its 51st meeting in Strasbourg the CIML took an important decision by approving the Framework for a new OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) that will replace the existing OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA) on 1 January 2018. This article describes the reasons for that decision, the activities that have taken place since October 2013, and the objectives and major elements of the new OIML-CS.

An OIML Seminar "OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA)" was held prior to the 48th CIML Meeting in October 2013 to discuss the OIML MAA (see OIML Seminar Report S7). The discussions at the Seminar and the subsequent 48th CIML Meeting resulted in the adoption of three CIML Resolutions (2013/15, 2013/16 and 2013/17) and the creation of an Ad-hoc Working Group (AHWG) to review the OIML MAA.

The AHWG was tasked with addressing concerns raised during the Seminar relating to

there being two systems (Basic and MAA) in parallel,

a management structure that it was felt could be improved,

issues regarding the acceptance of certificates, and

a lack of awareness of the MAA amongst potential users and beneficiaries.

However, during the course of its work it became apparent to members of the AHWG that a more fundamental overhaul of the OIML Certification Systems (Basic and MAA) was required.

Following an update on its activities at the 49th CIML Meeting (see Resolution 2014/18) the AHWG presented a proposal at the 50th CIML Meeting in October 2015 containing the structure and principles for a new OIML-CS. The CIML adopted Resolutions 2015/17 and 2015/18, approving the structure and

principles of the new OIML-CS, disbanding the AHWG and creating an OIML Certification System Project Group (CSPG).

In accordance with Resolution 2015/18 the CSPG was tasked with preparing the relevant documents for the new OIML-CS:

an OIML Basic Publication containing the Framework for the OIML-CS, and

- working drafts of the necessary operational and procedural documents,

with the intention that a Final Draft Basic Publication Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) would be submitted for approval at the 51st CIML Meeting in October 2016 and that the new OIML-CS would become effective from 1 January 2017.

## Development of the Framework for the OIML-CS

The CSPG, chaired by the CIML First Vice-President, Roman Schwartz, was formed in late 2015 with representatives from 15 countries (14 P-members and one O-member). An Intermediate Project Draft of the Framework document which is now OIML B 18 was developed and was circulated to the CSPG in February 2016.

Following a discussion on the work of the CSPG and the Intermediate Project Draft at the Presidential Council Meeting in March 2016, an updated Project Roadmap was developed along with a revised Draft. One of the key changes made to the Project Roadmap was to reflect the decision taken by the Presidential Council to delay the implementation of the new OIML-CS until 1 January 2018 to allow sufficient time for the development of the Operational and Procedural Documents that would underpin the Framework.

The revised Draft was circulated to the CSPG for vote and comment and, following approval of the Draft by the CSPG, a Draft Basic Publication was submitted for CIML Preliminary Online Ballot (POB) on 18 April 2016. Following a successful POB, a Final Draft Basic Publication was circulated to the CIML for approval along with a set of Working Drafts of the Operational and Procedural Documents that had been developed. The Working Drafts of the Operational and Procedural Documents were provided to give information on the detailed documentation that would underpin the Framework.

A meeting of the CSPG was held in August 2016 to discuss comments and concerns regarding the Final Draft Basic Publication that had been raised by some CSPG members and to review the Working Drafts of the Operational and Procedural Documents. The outcome

of the meeting was an amended Final Draft Basic Publication (Version 2) which was submitted for CIML approval. This was accompanied by an Additional Meeting Document which provided supplementary information to the CIML in support of the approval of the Final Draft Basic Publication (Version 2).

At the 51st CIML Meeting, Resolution 2016/16 was adopted approving the Final Draft Basic Publication (Version 2) which has since been published as OIML B 18:2016 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS).

## Principles of the OIML-CS

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation/test reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

It is a single Certification System comprising two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B which will replace the current OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML MAA. The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate or not. Participating in the OIML-CS and signing the OIML-CS Declaration will commit, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS. OIML B18:2016 establishes these rules whereby signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation/test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

## Objectives of the OIML-CS

The objectives of the OIML-CS are

logical requirements for measuring instruments and/or modules,

to promote the global harmonization, uniform interpretation and implementation of legal metro-

to avoid unnecessary re-testing when obtaining national type evaluations and approvals, or recognition of measuring instruments and/or modules under legal metrological control, while achieving and maintaining confidence in the results in support of facilitating the global trade of individual instruments, and

to establish rules and procedures for fostering mutual confidence among participating OIML Member States and Corresponding Members in the results of type evaluations that indicate conformity of measuring instruments and/or modules, under legal metrological control, to the metrological and technical requirements established in the applicable OIML Recommendation(s).

## OIML-CS scope and participation

As mentioned above, the OIML-CS is a single Certification System with two Schemes:

Scheme A, and

Scheme B.

Those categories of measuring instruments (including families of instruments, modules, or families of modules) for which the relevant OIML Recommendation specifies the metrological and technical requirements, the test procedures, and the OIML test report format will automatically be included in the OIML-CS. A category of measuring instrument will initially be placed in Scheme B, with the intention that all categories of measuring instruments in the OIML-CS will transition to Scheme A two years after first being included in the OIML-CS.

The requirements for the participation of Issuing Authorities and their associated Test Laboratories in Scheme A or Scheme B are the same, but the method of demonstrating compliance is different. Issuing Authorities are required to demonstrate compliance with accepted international standards, e.g. ISO/IEC 17065, and Test Laboratories are required to demonstrate compliance with ISO/IEC 17025. For participation in Scheme B, it is sufficient to demonstrate compliance on the basis of "self-declaration" with additional supporting evidence. However, for participation in Scheme A, compliance shall be demonstrated by peer evaluation on the basis of accreditation or peer assessment.

## Introduction

ISO CASCO WG 44 is responsible for the revision of ISO/IEC 17025 General requirements for the competence of testing and calibration laboratories. OIML D 30 Guide for the application of ISO/IEC 17025 to the assessment of Testing Laboratories involved in legal metrology gives guidance for the application of ISO/IEC 17025 to the assessment of Testing Laboratories involved in legal metrology and will be affected by the revision of ISO/IEC 17025. One objective is to have the additional requirements of the current OIML D 30 included in the revision of ISO/IEC 17025.

## Meeting

The ISO CASCO WG 44 held its fifth meeting in Geneva on 20-23 September 2016. The aim of the meeting was to discuss the comments received on the Second Committee Draft (CD2) that the WG 44 Drafting Group had been unable to resolve in order to prepare a draft for the Draft International Standard (DIS).

The OIML had submitted comments on the CD2 ISO/IEC 17025 which were a compilation of those received from members of the OIML mirror committee.

Major changes in the revised standard will be the new structure as a consequence of the CASCO document QS-CAS-PROC/33 "Common elements in ISO/CASCO standards". This structure is similar to other recent ISO/CASCO standards, (e.g. ISO/IEC 17065:2012) and will facilitate the integration of different CASCO standards in one Management System. The revised standard will also integrate the principles of the new ISO 9001:2015. Furthermore, the revised standard will contain an informative Annex A on metrological traceability.

Over seventy ISO CASCO WG 44 members attended the meeting, with representatives from Standardization Bodies, Liaison Organizations, Accreditation Bodies, Testing Laboratories and Calibration Laboratories (including Federations of Testing and Calibration Laboratories).

Even though 96 % of ISO CASCO Members voted in favour of the CD2, over 100 pages of comments were received. The WG 44 Drafting Group had addressed the majority of the comments so only those comments that had not been addressed by the WG 44 Drafting Group were discussed. Some of the key issues that were discussed were:

use of the term "measurement uncertainty" rather than "uncertainty of measurement";

- revisit the use of the terms "verification" and "validation";

text regarding laboratory facilities and environmental conditions;

review of the clause on equipment;

- clarify "calibration" versus "comparison" for laboratories maintaining primary standards;

- requirements for securing a sample, and addressing uncertainty in sampling;

- requirements for evaluation of measurement uncertainty;

- requirements for reporting the decision rule in statements of conformity; and

information on principles of Metrological Traceability in Annex A.

Finally, the WG 44 decided to advance to the next step in the standardization process and to issue the Draft International Standard (DIS) ISO/IEC 17025. The ballot on the DIS is due to start at the beginning of January 2017 and in this phase the DIS will be accessible to the public and discussed in the national mirror committees.

# The OIML and international regulatory cooperation

</div>

MARC LOESEWITZ

## Introduction

Back in 2014 the OECD started a series of case studies on the work of international organizations in the field of international regulatory co-operation (IRC). The intention was to provide detailed overviews of the structure, governance, instruments and processes of such organizations, in particular as they related to international rule-making and standard-setting. Even though IRC is a key ingredient of regulatory quality and plays an important role creating common rules of globalization, the detailed operation of IRC remains largely unstudied. That is why the OECD chose to gather evidence from 50 international organizations on their IRC mechanisms. Within this process the OECD recently decided to analyze the IRC mechanisms of 5 international organizations closer through case studies.

As a major standard-setting organization, the OIML was naturally interested in participating in order to improve the regulatory quality of its work. Having written the case study on the OIML, it is my intention in this article to introduce the key findings of my work. The complete version of the case study is available on the OECD website under the following link:

http://www.oecd.org/gov/regulatorypolicy/OIML_Full%20Report.pdf

## Forms of international regulatory cooperation

There is a variety of ways to achieve regulatory cooperation, ranging from formally binding mechanisms to the mere exchange of information between regulators. How an international organization contributes to IRC and in which forms depends to a great extent on the

governance structure and operational modalities of the international organization itself.

The OIML is characterized as a very decentralized organization with a small secretariat, with most of the work being performed by the Member States. The CIML and its subsidiary bodies are therefore the main platform for the different forms of IRC. In those bodies, the Member States can work together, share experience, discuss technical work and seek solutions to common problems. Based on Article I of the Convention, three main forms of IRC are employed by the OIML:

exchange of information;

discussions on good practices in joint studies; and

the development of technical standards (International Recommendations).

These three main forms can be further divided into more specific policy tools in the form of e.g. technical guidance, certificate systems and support in the field of legal metrology, which illustrate the valuable contributions to IRC by the OIML.

The work of the OIML therefore involves activities prior to standard-setting and the actual development of standards. It is an evidence-based bottom-up approach with extensive use of upstream activities of the policy cycle. The OIML pursues those forms of IRC without the use of any legally binding instruments. It is a fundamental characteristic of the International Recommendations, even though usually designed to be directly transposed in the domestic systems of Member States, that they are voluntary and non-binding.

## Ensuring the quality of OIML instruments

In the assessment of the tools and instruments used to ensure the quality of regulatory co-operation, again the specific nature of the OIML has to be taken into account. The burden of ensuring the quality of OIML instruments is, because of its highly decentralized nature, shared between the organization and its Member States. Due to the use of non-binding instruments, there are no sanctions which can be used to ensure regulatory quality and consistency.

The nature of OIML also makes it difficult to use techniques that would often be used by national administrations to assess the quality of initiatives that have been introduced - the OIML is not in a position to use either ex ante impact appraisals nor ex post evaluation in a systematic way to review the implementation of Recommendations in the Member States.

However, the OIML has addressed the challenge of quality control in other ways, notably by four specific measures:

promoting stakeholder engagement;

monitoring take-up of certification systems;

producing technical guidelines; and

conducting periodic reviews of its Recommendations.

Stakeholder engagement take places in a number of ways through national mirror committees, through the presence of technical experts in TCs/SCs and Project Groups or through public consultation. The frequent consultations with private stakeholders facilitate the implementation of Recommendations and are a strong feature within the OIML system. Due to the fact that industry representatives and other private stakeholders are designated as technical experts by CIML Members or Corresponding Member Representatives, OIML Members can try to balance competing group demands amongst the different private stakeholders.

Another tool used by the OIML is the registration of every OIML certificate issued by the Issuing Authorities located in the Member States, and a list is published both on the OIML website and in the Bulletin in order to monitor the take-up and implementation of Certificates. Implementation of a uniform and effective regulatory framework is further ensured by the use of external expert reports. These reports on specific legal metrology issues are chosen by the BIML, but written independently by the experts. Through this tool, the OIML deepens the understanding of detailed regulation and aids the gathering of information about the implementation of the standards.

Finally, the review of Recommendations promises to be a useful tool in order to keep up in the never ending race of technology. The quality of the standard-setting activities is likely to be greatly improved by the five-yearly review of each Recommendation after publication, particularly since a criterion used in the review process is the actual use of the Recommendation in the light of technological developments and its importance for national regulations.

The second aspect to ensuring the quality of the standards and other outputs from the OIML is the role of the Member States themselves. It is the Member States which register and assume jurisdiction in respect of administrative, technical and social matters concerning legal metrology and thus the responsibility of demonstrating regulatory quality belongs first and foremost to them. Most perform some kind of monitoring of the standards which they adopt. By these means the Member States play their part to ensure the regulatory quality of the OIML instruments, which is very important for successful implementation and shows once again how Member State-driven the Organization is.

## Assessment of impact and challenges and success

An evaluation of the impacts and benefits of regulatory co-operation through the OIML remains an undeveloped field. The lack of monitoring capacity in an organization as small as the BIML can be considered as the main reason for this. A more systematic exchange of information amongst the Member States would be needed in order to paint a clear picture of costs and benefits.

The benefits of regulatory co-operation, such as the reduction of transaction costs, consumer protection effective stock control, reduction in fraud, enforcement of fair trade practices as well as knowledge flow, have been established in a number of studies. But even though added value to society and especially trade is widely acknowledged, the OIML faces challenges in extending its work. The biggest challenges identified by the case study are the "buy-in" from Member States, the lack of regulatory flexibility, difficulties to catch up with rapid technology changes and implementation issues.

Given that the OIML is a highly decentralized, Member State-driven organization with non-binding instruments, facing these challenges can be difficult. How does the OIML effectively deal with the challenges and what aspects make the regulatory co-operation of the OIML successful?

The OIML's greatest strength is its position as a widely accepted international standard-setting organization with a growing membership and strong non-member engagement in its activities. The voluntary strategy it has chosen relies on three main pillars for the improvement of implementation:

first the OIML exercises a high degree of transparency and accessibility in its work; every Document and Recommendation is freely accessible on the website for download;

second, because the OIML's work is technical and specific, it is easier for stakeholders to become involved and this also fosters common interest amongst both Members and non-members;

third, the consistent dialogue engaged with the Member States, stakeholders and non-members is also very fruitful.

Also the recognition by the user-community is also a source of strength: OIML Recommendations and Documents find a broad application beyond the OIML membership. Good examples are the International Vocabulary of Legal Metrology and the Recommendation on the pre-packaging of goods.

Naturally, overlaps occur between those international organizations which regulate metrology

and standardization when the standards of the various organizations are related. It is therefore important that there be inter-agency coordination. Fortunately, there is a good record of co-operation between the main organizations in the form of consultations and incorporation of different policies and information provided by various organizations (e.g. IEC, ISO, BIPM and ILAC). Joint project groups and Memoranda of Understanding help to avoid duplication and propel the technical knowledge flow.

But more importantly than the measures taken by the OIML, the endorsement of OIML Recommendations by the WTO through Article 2.4 of the TBT Agreement is a key factor for successful implementation. To the extent that OIML Recommendations can be considered as "relevant international standards" according to the TBT agreement, their use in domestic technical regulations may fall under the obligations of WTO Members. This consequently results in the applicability of the stronger enforcement mechanisms of the WTO which provides a strong incentive for Member States to implement Recommendations and is indeed the main driver for implementation overall.

## Conclusion

The case study has identified the factors leading to success within the structural architecture in the OIML including the open consultations with stakeholders, the flexibility of voluntary technical standards and the broad acceptance of Recommendations and definitions developed by the Organization.

Success in regulatory co-operation is therefore due to the strong interplay of public and private incentives

as well as other international organizations in the field. In addition, the practical approach by the development of technical assistance, training programs and expert reviews is evidence that the instruments of the OIML have been successful. Overall, international regulatory co-operation in legal metrology can therefore be considered a relative success.

Nevertheless, the case study also identifies a number of challenges faced by the OIML. The technological change propelled by globalization leads to the increasing need to speed up the standard-setting process in order to keep up with rapid developments. The flexible institutional structure of the OIML is generally a good basis in order to address this challenge. However, there is still scope for the procedures to be optimized to reach their maximum potential.

It will therefore be necessary to keep the structures and policies which govern the OIML's technical work under review. Also, systematic and structured evaluation through Expert Reports could create greater momentum for Member States to implement OIML Recommendations into their national legislation. Bearing in mind the limited resources available to the OIML and its Member States, concentrating efforts where the greatest added value can be achieved would lead to mutual benefit and more successful implementation of OIML Recommendations.

These have converged into completely new technology fields such as the industrial internet and Smart Services. So, we think it is high time to once again bring together all the stakeholders in Legal Metrology to discuss the challenges and opportunities that the "Digital Single Market" offers. The Workshop will be chaired by Florian Thiel from the PTB, convener of WELMEC Working Group 7 Software, and assisted by Marko Esche from the PTB, secretary of OIML TC 5/SC 2 Software, as well as other colleagues from the PTB.

The OIML, WELMEC and Helmholtz-Fonds e.V. have confirmed their support.

The aim of this Workshop is to again bring together experts from different technical disciplines as well as representatives from industry, science, testing authorities, notified bodies and regulatory authorities to discuss challenges, approaches and solutions concerning IT-related metrological topics.

At its 51st meeting in October 2016, under Resolution no. 2016/20 (Agenda Item 9.1.2.2) the CIML approved as a new project in TC 5/SC 2 Software the revision of OIML D 31:2008 General requirements for software controlled measuring instruments. This Workshop offers the opportunity to identify and discuss items that are considered relevant enough to be introduced in a revision of WELMEC Guide 7.2 Software and a revision of OIML D 31.

## Stakeholder needs

The Workshop will be oriented directly towards stakeholders' needs since its form and topics are directly derived from the result of a survey among WELMEC members. Many experts have already confirmed they will contribute actively, such as industry representatives (e.g. CECIP and CECOD), Notified Bodies, the European Commission, EURAMET, etc. All these contributions, together with contributions from the PTB, will form the skeleton of the Workshop.

The Editors of the Bulletin encourage the submission of articles covering topics such as national, regional and international activities in legal metrology and related fields, evaluation procedures, accreditation and certification, and measuring techniques and instrumentation. Authors are requested to submit:

- a titled, typed manuscript in Word or WordPerfect either on disk or (preferably) by e-mail;

- the paper originals of any relevant photos, illustrations, diagrams, etc.;

- a photograph of the author(s) suitable for publication together with full contact details: name, position, institution, address, telephone, fax and e-mail.

Note: Electronic images should be minimum 150 dpi, preferably 300 dpi. Technical articles selected for publication will be remunerated at the rate of 23 $ \epsilon $ per printed page, provided that they have not already been published in other journals. The Editors reserve the right to edit contributions for style, space and linguistic reasons and author approval is always obtained prior to publication.
