Bonne année !

Le mois de janvier est le moment idéal pour réfléchir aux réalisations de l'année écoulée et se projeter vers l'année à venir.

Dans la perspective de 2023, la 58ème Réunion du CIML et le Séminaire OIML L'avenir de l'OIML à l'ère numérique à Chiang Mai ont été des grands moments pour notre Organisation. Après des années de réunions en ligne en raison de la pandémie, une réunion en personne était très attendue et nous a certainement aidés à retrouver notre élan. L'hospitalité chaleureuse de la Thaïlande, notre pays d'accueil, a fait de cette semaine à Chiang Mai un événement remarquable. Je tiens à exprimer une nouvelle fois ma gratitude à la Thaïlande pour son organisation et son accueil impeccables de la réunion.

La 58ème Réunion du CIML et le Séminaire OIML ont été très productifs et réussis. Les présentations faites lors du Séminaire OIML par divers orateurs sur un éventail de perspectives ont stimulé de riches débats et discussions entre les délégués lors des sessions en petits groupes. Un bref rapport est inclus dans cette édition du Bulletin.

Cette réunion du CIML a également marqué la fin du mandat du Dr Roman Schwartz, qui a occupé le poste de président du CIML pendant six années impressionnantes. Les membres se sont unis pour exprimer leurs remerciements et leur appréciation au Dr Schwartz pour son leadership, et il a été nommé Membre d'Honneur du CIML en reconnaissance des services rendus à l'Organisation.

De nombreuses décisions importantes ont été prises par le CIML, notamment l'élection de M. Bill Loizides comme Deuxième Vice-président du CIML. Le CIML a également accueilli favorablement la Déclaration de Chiang Mai, qui est un programme destiné à renforcer la collaboration entre les

différentes autorités de métrologie légale (voir le rapport dans cette édition).

A l'horizon 2024, notre priorité reste de récolter les bénéfices de la digitalisation pour notre communauté mondiale de la métrologie. A la suite du Séminaire OIML, le Groupe de Travail OIML sur la Digitalisation s'appuiera sur les contributions fournies par les délégués à Chiang Mai et travaillera à la formulation d'un Plan d'Action.

Continuer à promouvoir le Système de Certification OIML (OIML-CS) et augmenter le nombre d'économies participantes sont clairement des priorités clés pour nous en 2024. La réunion du Comité de Management de l'OIML-CS à Delhi, en Inde, sera une excellente occasion d'élaborer davantage sur la façon de relever ce défi.

La Déclaration de Chiang Mai exigera également du Groupe Consultatif du CEEMS qu'il entreprenne des actions pour créer un impact. En outre, nous devons nous assurer que notre travail technique est effectué de manière efficace et efficiente afin de répondre aux attentes de nos différentes parties prenantes. En même temps, nous ne devons pas oublier que la plupart des ressources pour le travail de l'OIML sont fournies par nos Membres. Je suis convaincu que nous pouvons relever ces défis, si nous exploitons l'engagement et l'énergie dont nous avons fait l'expérience ensemble à Chiang Mai et si nous utilisons l'esprit positif qui y a été généré pour accomplir nos tâches.

Je me réjouis de travailler avec vous et j'ai bon espoir qu'ensemble, nous obtiendrons davantage pour la communauté de la métrologie légale dans toutes les régions. Je souhaite à tous nos membres et lecteurs une très bonne et prospère année.

## Happy New Year!

January is the ideal time to both reflect on the achievements of the past year and look forward to the year ahead.

Looking back at 2023, the 58th CIML Meeting and the OIML Seminar The future of the OIML in the digital era in Chiang Mai were definitely highlights for our Organisation. Following the years of online meetings due to the pandemic an in-person meeting was eagerly anticipated and it certainly helped us regain momentum. The warm hospitality of Thailand, our host country, made the week in Chiang Mai a remarkable event. I would like to again express my gratitude to Thailand for their impeccable organisation and hosting of the meeting.

Both the 58th CIML Meeting and the OIML Seminar were very productive and successful. The presentations given at the OIML Seminar by various speakers on a range of perspectives stimulated rich debate and discussions between delegates during the breakout sessions. A short report is included in this edition of the Bulletin.

This CIML meeting also marked the end of the term of Dr Roman Schwartz, who served as CIML President for six impressive years. Members joined in expressing their thanks and appreciation to Dr Schwartz for his leadership, and he was made a CIML Member of Honour in recognition of his service to the Organisation.

Many important decisions were taken by the CIML, notably the election of Mr Bill Loizides as CIML Second Vice-President. The CIML also welcomed the Chiang Mai Declaration, which is a programme designed to enhance the

collaboration between different legal metrology authorities (see the report in this edition).

Moving forward into 2024, it remains our priority to reap the benefits of digitalisation for our worldwide metrology community. As an outcome of the OIML Seminar, the OIML Digitalisation Task Group will build on the feedback provided by delegates in Chiang Mai and will work on formulating an Action Plan.

Further promoting the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) and increasing the number of participating economies are clearly also key priorities for us in 2024. The OIML-CS Management Committee meeting in Delhi, India, will provide an excellent opportunity to elaborate more on tackling this challenge.

The Chiang Mai Declaration will also require the CEEMS AG to initiate actions to create an impact. Further, we should ensure that our technical work is carried out efficiently and effectively to meet the expectations of our various stakeholders. At the same time, we should not forget that most of the resources for the OIML's work are contributed by our Members. I am confident that we can rise to these challenges, if we harness the engagement and energy we experienced together in Chiang Mai and use the positive spirit generated there to accomplish our tasks.

I look forward to working together with you and I am optimistic that together we will achieve more for the legal metrology community in all regions. I wish all our Members and Readers a very happy and prosperous New Year.

CHIANG MAI

BUREAU INTERNATIONAL DE METROLOGIE LÉGALE (BIML)

Summary report

Fifty-eighth meeting of the International Committee of Legal Metrology

17-19 October 2023 Chiang Mai, Kingdom of Thailand

The Fifty-eighth meeting of the International Committee of Legal Metrology (CIML) was held on 17-19 October 2023 in Chiang Mai, Kingdom of Thailand, as an in-person meeting. 54 Member States were present or represented so the quorum was reached. In addition, there were representatives from five Corresponding Members and one Organisation in Liaison.

The OIML was warmly welcomed to Chiang Mai by Mr Wattanasak Sur-iam (Director General of the Department of Internal Trade and CIML Member for Thailand), Mr Wiraphong Rhitrot (Vice Governor of Chiang Mai) and Mr Keerati Rushchano (Permanent Secretary of the Ministry of Commerce).

The CIML President, Dr Roman Schwartz, reported on the key activities and membership changes that had taken place during the year. At the end of his report he handed over the presidency to Dr Bobjoseph Mathew (Vice-Director of the Federal Institute of Metrology METAS, Switzerland), who was elected for a six-year term at the 57th CIML Meeting in 2022. The Committee thanked Dr Schwartz for his presidential leadership during the period 2017-2023, awarded him an OIML Medal, and made him an Honorary Member.

As there were no candidates for the position of CIML First Vice-President following the call for candidates made in November 2022, another call for candidates will be circulated in November 2023. Mr Bill Loizides (Australia) was appointed as CIML Second VicePresident for a six-year-term with immediate effect.

The BIML Director, Mr Anthony Donnellan, reported on the activities and achievements of the BIML and its staff. For the 58th CIML Meeting, Mr Donnellan's report focussed on the extensive and critical role that the International Bureau of Legal Metrology (BIML) performs to raise awareness of, and to promote, international legal metrology.

As a result of the significant efforts and resources deployed to manage Member debt, again this year no OIML Member States nor Corresponding Members were delisted.

The five-year terms of Mr Donnellan and Mr Dunmill (BIML Assistant Director) were renewed by the Committee.

The accounts for 2022 were presented and were approved by the CIML. Mr Donnellan reported that the Organisation continues to be in a strong financial position, although late payments still present an underlying risk to the OIML and rising inflation continues to be a challenge. He stated that expenses remain generally within expectations, and that the OIML Operating Reserve had not been accessed during this period.

A summary of the detailed discussions held during the Regional Legal Metrology Organisations Round Table (RLMO RT) held on 26 September 2023 was presented by the RLMO RT Chairperson, Dr Charles Ehrlich. Organisations in liaison (BIPM, CECIP, IAF, ILAC) submitted written reports on their activities and CECIP gave an oral presentation.

The BIML gave a report on the progress of the technical work of the Organisation, and lists of highpriority projects and publications were approved by the Committee. The Committee also thanked the French and German co-conveners of TC 9/SC 1/p 1 for their work on the revision of R 76:2006 Non-automatic weighing instruments and approved the temporary transfer of the convenership of the Project Group to the BIML.

The following publications were approved by the Committee:

- Revision of OIML B 6:2019 Directives for OIML technical work (Parts 1 and 2);

- Amendment of OIML B 17:2012 Policies and rules for the reimbursement of travel expenses incurred by CIML Members of Honour and invited guests in attending OIML events;

- Revision of OIML B 19:2022 Terms of Reference for the Advisory Group on matters concerning Countries and Economies with Emerging Metrology Systems (CEEMS AG);

- New Basic Publication OIML B 22 Policy and rules for the reimbursement of travel expenses incurred by the CIML President in attending legal metrology related events;

- Revision of OIML D 31:2019 General requirements for software-controlled measuring instruments (as amended in 2020).

- The following new projects were approved by the CIML:

- Revision of OIML D 31:2023 General requirements for software-controlled measuring instruments;

- New Recommendation DC electricity metering.

Mr Chris Pulham spoke about communication with OIML stakeholders and gave a presentation on the OIML Bulletin, focussing on the highly successful concept of "Mentors" and plans for future editions, including adding a "Letters to the Editor" section and exploring possibilities to introduce more technical, peerreviewed articles. Members were encouraged to submit topics and articles for future editions.

The OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) Management Committee (MC) Chairperson, Mr Mannie Panesar, reported on the activities of the OIML-CS,

Rapport succint

Cinquante-huitième Réunion du

17-19 octobre 2023 Chiang Mai, Royaume de Thailande

La Cinquante-huitième Réunion du Comité International de Métrologie Légale (CIML) s'est tenue du 17 au 19 octobre 2023 à Chiang Mai, Royaume de Thaïlande, en tant que réunion en personne. 54 États Membres étaient présents ou représentés, de sorte que le

quorum a été atteint. En outre, il y avait des représentants de cinq Membres Correspondants et d'une organisation en liaison.

L'OIML a été chaleureusement accueillie à Chiang Mai par M. Wattanasak Sur-iam (Directeur Général du Département du Commerce Intérieur et Membre du CIML pour la Thaïlande), M. Wiraphong Rhitrot (ViceGouverneur de Chiang Mai) et M. Keerati Rushchano (Secrétaire Permanent du Ministère du Commerce).

Le Président du CIML, le Dr. Roman Schwartz, a présenté un rapport sur les principales activités et les changements de Membres qui ont eu lieu au cours de l'année. À la fin de son rapport, il a transmis la présidence au Dr. Bobjoseph Mathew (Vice-Directeur de l'Institut Féderal de Métrologie METAS, Suisse), qui a été élu pour un mandat de six ans lors de la 57ème Réunion du CIML en 2022. Le Comité a remercié le Dr. Schwartz pour sa présidence pendant la période 2017-2023, lui a décerné une médaille OIML, et l'a fait Membre d'Honneur du CIML.

En l'absence de candidats pour le poste de Premier Vice-Président du CIML suite à l'appel à candidatures lancé en novembre 2022, un nouvel appel à candidatures sera diffusé en novembre 2023. M. Bill Loizides (Australie) est nommé Deuxième Vice-Président du CIML pour un mandat de six ans avec effet immédiat.

Le Directeur du BIML, M. Anthony Donnellan, a présenté les activités et les réalisations du BIML et de son personnel. Pour la 58ème Réunion du CIML, le rapport de M. Donnellan s'est concentré sur le rôle étendu et essentiel que le Bureau International de Métrologie Légale (BIML) joue pour faire connaître et promouvoir la métrologie légale internationale.

Grâce aux efforts et aux ressources considérables déployés pour gérer la dette des Membres, cette année

## 1 Introduction: terms, vocabulary, and the role of the DTG

The Gartner Glossary $ ^{1} $ defines 'digitisation' as the "process of changing from analog to digital form", in contrast to 'digitalisation' which it defines as "the use of digital technologies to change a business model" and "the process of moving to a digital business". The term 'digital transformation' can then be defined as "a business process (a digitalization) enabled by a technical change (a digitization)". These definitions from the business consulting company Gartner need to be interpreted from the OIML's perspective to enable the conclusion of steps towards the future of the OIML in the digital era. That is, one needs to identify what "business model" means for the OIML as an international organisation based on treaties and memoranda of understanding. In a similar way, this interpretation and discussion is taking place across OIML Member States, stakeholders of legal metrology, and within all pillars of the international quality infrastructure.

The OIML DTG was founded in 2022 to support the OIML in addressing the many questions and challenges arising from digitalisation and in investigating potential benefits for the Organisation and its Members. It was rapidly concluded in the first DTG meeting that there is a need to develop a common understanding of basic vocabulary and terminology. In this context it is important to note that the DTG does not intend to duplicate work being carried out in other OIML Project Groups (PGs) and committees related to vocabulary. Instead, the DTG aims to summarise and complement the existing work with the vocabulary needed for a strategic discussion of the role of and challenges for legal metrology in the digital era.

There are several existing sources for terms and vocabulary in OIML publications:

- OIML V1 International vocabulary of terms in legal metrology (VIML);

- OIML G 18 Alphabetical list of terms defined in OIML Recommendations and Documents; and

- OIML D31 General requirements for software-controlled measuring instruments.

When it comes to terms related to "digital", the existing OIML work basically covers the technologies in products and services investigated under the realm of legal metrology. Examples are "digital data processing device", "digital display" or "digital indication".

An exception to this is OIML D 31, which covers a wider range of terminologies related to digital technologies, because of the topic of the PG. That is, the D 31 vocabulary list contains terms such as "audit trail", "cryptographic certificate" or "data domain".

Another exception is the new OIML Guide 23 Guide to the use of online technology for conducting CEEMS activities in a post-COVID world. This Guide introduces terms such as "e-Learning" and "online technology", but without providing a concise definition as is usually given for other vocabulary in OIML publications. Moreover, in its Chapter 8 this Guide clearly states that terms such as "digitalisation", "digital transformation", "digitisation" and "the digital agenda" have not been defined within the OIML and that no consensus even exists as to their exact meaning.

The DTG decided to take the definitions from Gartner as its orientation and starting point. In particular the definition of "digital transformation", however, requires further investigations and concrete use case examples from legal metrology. The "transformation" may refer to processes in legal metrology that are evolving towards end-to-end digital processes by replacing physical (i.e. analogue) steps with digital ones. The term "transformation" may also refer to changes in technologies or processes to enable a better handling in some way. It is important to note that this transformation does not necessarily have a defined final state. Instead, it includes an ongoing adoption of new (digital) technologies considered as "facilitators to make metrological services even better" $ ^{2} $

Digitalisation in legal metrology is an essential part of the wider digital transformation in the quality infrastructure. Processes in legal metrology rely on measurement standards, accreditation, conformity assessment, market surveillance, and standardisation. Thus, digitalisation in legal metrology is closely related to digitalisation within the rest of the quality infrastructure. At the same time, industries and economies are progressing rapidly, which results in challenges for the quality infrastructure. Moreover, the expectation from customers and end-users in the digital era is that all information and processes are making use of digital technologies in a reasonable way for their benefit. Some examples of topics that need to be addressed by the quality infrastructure are as follows:

- large quantity of data sources underpinning new digital services, and novel approaches for data analysis, based on artificial intelligence and machine learning;

- interconnected, versatile, and often inexpensive sensors, combined in so-called sensor networks as in the "Internet of Things" (IoT); and

- fundamentally new approaches in production, such as additive manufacturing.

In 2022, the Task Group "SI Digital Framework" of the International Committee for Weights and Measures (CIPM), together with the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM) initiated the "Joint Statement of Intent on the digital transformation in the international scientific and quality infrastructure $ ^{3} $ , now signed by 10 international organisations - including the OIML $ ^{4} $ . This statement is a commitment to collaborate in the implementation and further development of the international system of units (SI) for the digital world as well as collaborating in the wider digital transformation. The role of the OIML DTG is to translate the Joint Statement of Intent into concrete recommendations and actions for the OIML. The DTG is also the group within the OIML which is actively collaborating with other institutions of the international quality infrastructure under the Joint Statement.

## 3 Benefits and opportunities of digitalisation in legal metrology

The digital era has brought about unprecedented changes in all aspects of our lives, and legal metrology is no exception. Consider the potential advancements in accuracy, efficiency, and accessibility that digitalisation can bring to legal metrology. In principle, the existing technologies enable all countries and economies to leverage these advancements for the improvement of insights and general trust and confidence in products and services. However, the list of possibilities is almost endless, making guidance and collaboration extremely important. The OIML DTG is considered to support this by initiating projects within the OIML, establishing collaborations with other organisations, and preparing recommendations for the CIML. In the breakout group discussion, it was important to consider expectations about major opportunities for legal metrology in the near future, and to discuss where the DTG should direct its focus.

The discussion outcomes of the Seminar regarding benefits and opportunities can be grouped as follows:

cost reduction and increased effectiveness;

- access and insights into information and data; and

- new services and processes in legal metrology.

The attendees saw opportunities for cost reduction by moving from paper to digital documents and processes. This could speed up approvals and inspections, enable real-time communications, as well as automation for calibration and testing. Digitalisation of entire procedures and the implementation of harmonised digital tools can also help to reduce human errors, as it avoids typing in information manually. Moreover, the use of digital processes, documents and platforms can also improve consumer protection and lead to more objectivity and impartiality and thereby increase the effectiveness in legal metrology.

Effectiveness of processes in legal metrology can be increased by improving access and insights into information and data for approvals, inspections, and verifications. The Seminar attendees mentioned sharing of data between organisations for improved access to services and more efficient processes. With appropriate access to data and information, a higher transparency, and further insights into devices under legal metrological supervision is possible and could enable the application of intelligent software tools. Although cybersecurity and privacy are always an important issue, the attendees argued that digital data can be secured more effectively than paper against fraud and misuse provided that appropriate security measures are put in place. Effective insights into digital information are

enabled by using machine-readable information, enrichment of data records with metadata, and provision of extended information, such as historical data and the incorporation of influence factors.

Digitalisation as "the use of digital technologies to change a business model" (Gartner) was also discussed by the Seminar attendees. Digital technologies can be used to enable remote testing and verification, leading to novel types of services and processes in legal metrology. For instance, remote testing could help to reduce administrative burdens and travel time, making legal metrology services more efficient. A "Remote OIML Laboratory" could be a potential new business model resulting from such digital technologies.

Users of legal metrology services expect digital solutions, such as electronic payment and digital certificates / reports. For the OIML, these technologies could offer new possibilities for the application of artificial intelligence, software control and collaboration. In several use cases and pilot developments, access to information is already provided via a unique ID and QR-code.

Overall, the Seminar attendees considered a wide range of potential application areas for digital technologies, demonstrating the complexity of the topic: machine-readable standards; information portals and platforms; process automation; blockchain-based data bases for verification; remoting testing; e-Learning platforms; digital product passport. The challenge for legal metrology in general and for the OIML in particular is to prioritise these topics in such a way that innovations can be supported whilst not overwhelming regulations, laboratories and technical verification with requirements and ground-breaking changes.

## 4 Risks and challenges

With all the potential benefits and opportunities in mind, the various risks and challenges must not be ignored. A general challenge seen by the Seminar attendees was how to handle the growing gap between technological advances of products and the capabilities in legal metrology. "Are we moving fast enough?", is a frequently asked question when discussing digital transformation challenges. Several companies, including measuring device manufacturers, have started transforming their workforce to incorporate digital skills such as programming, information technology and data science. These skill sets are not yet developed with the same speed within legal metrology organisations. Thus, the gap in knowledge, experience and capabilities is increasing.

Digital transformation includes relying on information technology. This ranges from fundamental

## 5 Expectations towards support from the OIML as an organisation

The Seminar attendees envisioned the OIML to become an international "centre of excellence" for digital

transformation in legal metrology and to serve as a recognised information hub on related topics. To achieve this visionary role for the OIML, the Seminar attendees considered various types of training, collaboration and networking activities.

With the development and provision of training material on topics related to digitalisation in legal metrology, legal metrology organisations, regulators and verification authorities could benefit from the combined expertise and experience of experts within the OIML. This material could be combined with a technical capacity building programme, training activities, and workshops. Overall, the Seminar attendees emphasised the requirement to leave no one behind and for the OIML to meet the needs of all its stakeholders.

The OIML training material should include insights into future digital technologies to raise awareness as well as guidelines for integrating digital technologies with existing workflows and systems. The Seminar attendees noted the importance of communication across all pillars and stakeholders to promote a general digital culture in legal metrology. In addition to workshops, training and e-Learning, communication material may include publications in the field of digitalisation in metrology, best practices, and practical examples that are easy to understand. Therefore, a communication plan and survey on digital maturity across OIML Members was considered to be a suitable starting point.

Given that the technical work of the OIML makes up a fundamental pillar of international harmonisation in legal metrology, the Seminar attendees considered the further developments in this direction as contributing to supporting new application areas and types of measurements. Discussions and ideas were the development of a common "Digital VIM" vocabulary, setting standards for the digitalisation of legal metrology systems, and the moderation of harmonisation activities. It was well noted that harmonisation also means establishing elementary requirements and setting standards for interoperability rather than requiring all technical aspects to be "identical". Moreover, aspects of harmonisation should always be taken into account in the communication and planning within the OIML pillars and activities. For instance, developments and activities presented in meetings of the Regional Legal Metrology Organisations Round Table (RLMO RT) may consider potential harmonisation needs to enable international interoperability and compatibility. The OIML could thus serve as a communication and collaboration hub.

Similar to the development of machine-readable documents such as "smart standards", as presented by Roberta Gerasymchuk in her keynote presentation, the Seminar attendees realised a great opportunity in fostering the development of "smart OIML Recommendations". Therefore, the OIML Technical Committees (TCs) should be guided to produce machine-readable content and transition everything provided by the OIML to become machine-readable. Two example applications mentioned explicitly were to support and promote digital test results referring to OIML Recommendations, and the incorporation of digital information controls in OIML publications to enable more efficient processes in legal metrology and verification.

The BIPM plans to transition all its services and "products" to become digital and machine-readable. The Seminar attendees saw a similar plan for the OIML and expected the OIML to provide corresponding infrastructures and digital processes. Especially the provision of information, e.g. about approved instruments, test results and the authenticity of certificates for automated verification and testing was considered of great benefit to foster digitalisation in legal metrology. The existing OIML-CS could be a valid starting point for such a development. For instance, the OIML-CS could be provided as an additional attribute of an electronic signature to be machine-readable. An OIML-CS service (or app) could check whether an individual instrument complies with the relevant OIML Recommendations and the underlying OIML-CS certificate. In this way, automated verification and testing could be enabled, leading to more efficient and effective processes in legal metrology.

## 6 Fostering collaboration of the OIML with regional organisations

A growing number of national and regional initiatives address digitalisation in legal metrology. The underlying principles as well as the general motivation of these initiatives have a lot in common. This offers several opportunities for collaboration, mutual developments, and sharing of expertise. The OIML can benefit substantially from these initiatives, provided that suitable pathways and formats are identified. The Seminar attendees discussed several ideas on how the OIML may benefit from this. Basically, three areas were identified:

active knowledge sharing;

- strengthen partnerships; and

utilising digital technologies.

Knowledge and experience sharing can be based on several elements:

- promotion of national and regional workshops within the OIML;

- organisation of OIML webinars and seminars; and

- provision of training material and case studies.

Thus, these activities have a lot in common with the communication and training initiatives expected from the OIML by the Seminar attendees. The OIML has the unique international role of bringing together expertise experience and examples from legal metrology practice. This role should be strengthened to raise awareness increase understanding, and enable internationally accepted good practices in digitalisation for legal metrology. Therefore, the OIML may integrate existing regional and national activities across its Members gather best practices, and establish a pool of experts that can be consulted if needed.

An important task of the OIML is to foster and maintain international partnerships with other organisations of the quality infrastructure. The Seminar attendees recognised this and envisioned for the OIML to become an even more important international player when it comes to digitalisation of the quality infrastructure. The attendees noted the role of the OIML to promote relevant aspects of digitalisation to ministries and policy makers, engage and collaborate with RLMOs, and to raise awareness in the different stakeholder groups. In particular the collaboration with the CIPM could be strengthened further based on the Joint Statement of Intent and the newly founded CIPM Forum "Metrology and Digitalisation".

However, it was also noted that the OIML could make use of existing connections, e.g. with the CIML and experts from Member countries, to support all its

Members in digitalisation. This may require modernising existing technologies for connecting the various information and discussion places within the OIML itself as well as within the RLMOs. A first step could be to establish aspects of digitalisation in all interactions, technical work, and collaborations across international legal metrology. Together with suitable technologies in place to aggregate this information properly, insights may be provided that would ease the uptake and use of regional resources and experiences. It is worth noting that digitalisation as a process does not necessarily follow other development processes. That is, an organisation may leapfrog developments in the uptake and implementation of novel digital technologies. Especially countries and economies with emerging metrology systems (CEEMS) would benefit from this kind of bypassing steps in digitalisation. Moreover, the transformation of smaller organisations towards automation and digitalisation may be easier than for large ones with a variety of established legacy systems and workflows.

## 7 Conclusions and outlook

The OIML Seminar showed a huge interest and motivation on the subject of digitalisation in legal metrology. The attendees saw clear benefits and opportunities, provided that digital technologies are implemented and incorporated properly in legal metrology systems. However, the wide range of digital maturity and literacy across the OIML Member countries proved to be a significant challenge. The lack of easily approachable training and guidance material oriented at legal metrology was considered a serious hurdle especially for smaller and emerging organisations. Technology advances at an increasing pace with the amount of integrated digital technologies increasing rapidly. The expectations from manufacturers, policy makers and end-users towards legal metrology is to

support innovations in digital technologies whilst maintaining the established level of confidence in the quality and safety of products and services.

The OIML DTG acts as hub within the OIML bringing together experts and collaborating with external partner organisations. Hence, the DTG itself is not able to develop and implement all the ideas discussed in the Seminar. However, the DTG will pick up the needs, requirements, and ideas from the Seminar by initiating novel activities, prioritising its work, and establishing collaborations.

In the past, the DTG has participated in various workshops, webinars, conferences, and meetings. Based on the outcomes of the Seminar "The future of the OIML in the digital era", these activities will be increased to communicate the needs and ideas from within the OIML to all its Member organisations and partners. The DTG will establish a Sub-committee to organise the development of e-Learning material on digitalisation in legal metrology. This activity will be carried out in close collaboration with the BIML, the CEEMS Advisory Group (CEEMS AG), and experts from RLMOs. Moreover, the DTG will organise a webinar series for sharing and discussing developments and ideas on digital transformation of legal metrology systems.

In 2022, the DTG founded a Sub-committee for preparing recommendations for progressing the OIML technical work towards machine-readable Recommendations, test reports and other publications. This work will be continued and combined with the efforts of the DTG to collaborate with OIML TC 1 Terminology and the OIML-CS. These collaborations, as well as partnerships with CEEMS and the RLMOs, are important pillars for the DTG and for the OIML in its digitalisation journey.

The DTG considers its role to be a role of supporting collaboration, knowledge sharing, and the joint development of recommendations for all Members and stakeholders of the OIML to enable global progress in digitalisation.

## Good regulatory practices

Good regulatory practices (GRP) are principles and methods that aim to ensure the quality, effectiveness, and efficiency of regulations and regulatory systems. GRP can help to promote strong, sustainable, and inclusive economic growth, as well as protect public health, safety, and the environment.

A Quality Infrastructure (QI) is the system of organizations, policies, legal and regulatory frameworks, and practices that support and enhance the quality, safety,

and environmental soundness of goods, services, and processes. QI is essential for the effective operation of domestic and international markets, and for achieving public policy objectives.

GRP and QI are closely related, as both seek to improve the performance and outcomes of regulations and standards. Some of the benefits of applying GRP in relation to QI components are:

- reducing unnecessary regulatory burdens and costs for businesses and consumers, by avoiding duplication, inconsistency, and complexity in QI services and requirements;

enhancing transparency, accountability, and stakeholder participation in the design, implementation, and evaluation of QI policies and regulations;

improving the quality and credibility of QI services and institutions, by ensuring their competence, impartiality, and international recognition;

- fostering innovation and competitiveness, by enabling access to new markets and technologies, and supporting the development of new products and services that meet quality and safety standards;

- strengthening the alignment and harmonization of QI regulations and standards with regional and international best practices, and facilitating trade and cooperation;

economic stability: They create a stable economic environment by fostering fair competition and preventing harmful activities;

public safety and environmental protection: Safeguard public health, safety, and the environment through regulations on pollution, waste management, healthcare, and food safety;

fairness and consumer protection: They ensure a level playing field for businesses and protect consumers from harm and deception;

legal certainty: They provide clear rules and procedures, mitigating legal disputes and fostering business confidence;

facilitating social change: They can be used to drive positive social change by influencing societal behaviours and attitudes.

Without good regulatory practices, a country can face numerous challenges:

increased corruption and economic instability;

- decline in public safety and environmental quality;

unfair business practices and consumer harm;

lack of clarity and legal certainty;

- reduced transparency and accountability;

obstacles to social progress;

The initiatives required to facilitate and promote achieving the objectives and benefits of a GRP are:

overlaps, frictions and chaotic operations of government agencies.

developing a National Quality Policy, NQP, that defines the vision, objectives, and roles and responsibilities of QI stakeholders, and that is aligned with the national development strategy and priorities;

establishing a sound legal and institutional framework for QI that clarifies the mandates, functions, and coordination mechanisms of QI institutions, and that ensures their independence, accountability, and sustainability;

applying regulatory impact assessment and stakeholder consultation tools to assess the costs and benefits of QI regulations and standards, and to solicit feedback and inputs from relevant parties;

adopting a risk-based approach to QI regulation and enforcement, that focuses on the most critical areas and sectors, and that balances the level of assurance with the level of intervention;

building the capacity and competence of QI service providers, such as metrology, standardization, accreditation, and conformity assessment bodies, and ensuring their compliance with international standards and best practices;

- promoting the awareness and use of QI services and regulations among businesses and consumers, and providing them with information, guidance, and support to comply with quality and safety requirements;

monitoring and evaluating the performance and impact of QI policies and regulations, and using the results to inform decision-making and continuous improvement.

## Conclusion

The focus of this study is orientated towards having a healthy regulatory environment in Nigeria. Technical regulations are essential elements of a good quality infrastructure. Standards are similar to technical regulations, but differ in their implementation. Standards are voluntary in nature, while technical regulations are mandatory, and failure to comply with them may lead to penalties, sanctions or prosecution.

It is crucial to establish good regulatory practices in a country to ensure economic stability, public safety, fair play, consumer protection, legal certainty, transparency accountability, and good governance. Without good regulatory practices, a country may face increased corruption, economic instability, societal harm and insecurity, which can impede its overall development. Establishing a healthy competitive environment for technical regulations enforcement is a key component of a robust National Quality Infrastructure. For decades, Nigeria has been bedevilled by the consequences of the lack of a good, uniform, internationally recognized regulatory framework. This means that running government businesses would be difficult, opening up the country to corrupt tendencies.

## Recommendations

To further strengthen the NQI in Nigeria, several recommendations were proposed. Firstly, the establishment of the Office of Technical Regulation (OTR) is essential. The OTR would serve as the administrative arm of the National Quality Council (NQC) and be responsible for ensuring the establishment of a good regulatory environment in Nigeria and the implementation of the provisions of the National Quality Policy (NQP). The OTR would also coordinate the development and implementation of technical regulations, ensure harmonization and alignment with international standards, engage stakeholders, provide capacity building, and participate in international forums. The National Quality Council (NQC) is the engine room in ensuring transparency, accountability, and good governance. This is achieved through the implementation of good regulatory policies and practices, which are essential for a nation's sustainable growth and development. Therefore, the NQC's commitment to good regulatory practices is essential for Nigeria's continued success in achieving its development goals. By fostering a stable, fair, and transparent environment, the NQC can contribute to a prosperous and thriving nation where all citizens can benefit.

## Introduction

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

The OIML-CS comprises two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B. Competence of the OIML Issuing Authorities and their Test Laboratories is demonstrated through self-declaration under Scheme B and accreditation or peer assessment under Scheme A.

The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate. Participating in the OIML-CS commits, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS that are established in OIML B 18:2022 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS). Signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation and test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an OIML Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

The OIML-CS was launched on 1 January 2018 and has replaced the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

## OIML certificates

OIML certificates issued under Scheme A and Scheme B can be downloaded from the database on the OIML website at https://www.oiml.org/en/oiml-cs/certificat_view.

The database also includes certificates issued under the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the MAA. Although these two systems are no longer in operation, the certificates remain valid.

## The OIML Bulletin is one, if not the only, international publication dedicated to legal metrology topics.

In accordance with CIML Resolutions 2019/30 and 2020/21, there is a clear desire amongst OIML Members for the Bulletin to continue to be an attractive publication for legal metrology worldwide, and for it to be an excellent advertisement for our Organisation.

This has been achieved through long-term planning of the future editions and identification of key topics of high interest, for instance, legal control of measuring instruments in the fields of energy, health and the environment, where important aspects such as new technology, legal requirements, or test/verification procedures will be addressed.

In addition, support has been sought from CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who have taken on the responsibility of acting as "Mentors" for future editions, certain key topics, or specific technical articles. The role of "Mentors" is to support the Editor of the Bulletin by coordinating the development of articles for a key topic through the identification of authors / experts. It is not necessarily expected that articles are written by the "Mentors" themselves, but by experts that a "Mentor" has identified and contacted.

In order to identify key topics of significant interest for future editions and "Mentors" to lead them, the BIML prepares, and makes available on the OIML website (www.oiml.org/en/publications/bulletin/future-editions), a plan for the upcoming four to six editions of the Bulletin.

The table on the following page is intended to be "dynamic", i.e. proposed key topics may be moved to other editions depending on available "Mentors" and authors for technical articles.

All CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives are encouraged to support the OIML Bulletin, to share their legal metrology experiences with the legal metrology community worldwide, and to take responsibility either as a "Mentor" for one of the next editions of the Bulletin, or by promoting it at TC/SC/Project Group meetings, RLMO meetings, CEEMS AG meetings, and other opportunities.

CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who would like to be a "Mentor" for a specific edition / key topic, or who would like to suggest that a new key topic be added to the list, are asked to contact the BIML (chris.pulham@oiml.org).

# Looking back on 40 years in metrology

</div>

DR ROMAN SCHWARTZ

I am pleased to be able to take this opportunity to say goodbye to all the readers of the OIML Bulletin and to my many colleagues in international metrology after retiring on 1 November 2023.

Looking back on 40 years as a public servant in the field of metrology, I am very grateful for the extraordinarily varied, interesting and challenging work, but also for the many contacts and excellent collaboration with many metrology colleagues from industry, society and research in Germany, Europe and around the world.

As a young physicist, I started my career at the Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt, PTB, in May 1983, acquainting myself with scientific metrology in the field of precision mass measurement, where I obtained my PhD in 1993, before switching to the Weighing Section in 1994 and learning the testing and approval of weighing instruments and weighing modules from scratch. This prepared me well to take over responsibility for the Weighing Section in 1996 and to represent it in external committees, for example in WELMEC Working Group 2, dealing with nonautomatic and automatic weighing instruments, and also as Chairman of WELMEC Working Group 7 "Software" from 1996 to 2006. A highlight in 2005 was the publication of the first software guide for instruments under the European Measuring Instruments Directive (MID), WELMEC Guide 7.2 "Software Guide (EU Measuring Instruments Directive 2014/32/EU)". a model for other software guidelines, such as OIML D 31 General requirements for software-controlled measuring instruments.

I became familiar with the technical work of the OIML in 2001 when I took over the TC 9/SC 1 co-secretariat; I have fond memories of the good cooperation with my French and many other colleagues, in particular with Mrs Corinne Lagauterie, to name just one, on the revision of OIML Recommendation R 76, which was published in 2006.

As a physicist, it was a very special privilege and highlight to work on the redefinition of the kilogram in

the CIPM Consultative Committee of Mass and Related Quantities (CCM) from 2006 to 2016 and to personally participate in the 26th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) in Versailles, where the important resolution "On the revision of the International System of Units (SI)" was unanimously adopted in November 2018.

Another personal highlight was my appointment as an honorary professor at Leibniz University Hannover in May 2017, in recognition of the long-standing lectureship "Metrology of Mechanical Quantities", which I held from 2003 to 2019 to teach students the basics of scientific and legal metrology, a voluntary side activity that gave me a lot of pleasure.

As CIML Member for Germany since March 2006, member of the Presidential Council since 2007, CIML Second Vice-President from 2010 to 2012 and CIML First Vice-President from 2012 to 2017, I have fond memories of the many advances that have been made in field of technical work by many project groups who were able to develop important new, or revise older, OIML publications.

As a particular highlight of this period, of course, I see the further development of the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA), launched in 2006, into a comprehensive OIML Certification System (OIML-CS), valid for all relevant measuring instrument categories, which was launched on 1 January 2018. I am convinced that the OIML-CS will continue to grow and develop further because it offers many advantages to all the parties involved. Another important achievement of this period is the establishment of an "Advisory Group on matters concerning Countries and Economies with Emerging Metrology Systems" (CEEMS AG) under the leadership of my predecessor, CIML Past President Mr Peter Mason; in my opinion, the CEEMS AG can play a very important role in the establishment and further development of legal metrology systems in OIML member countries.

After the special in-person CIML meetings in Hamburg (2018) and Bratislava (2019), my CIML

![](page=7,bbox=[1249, 1968, 1644, 2518])

presidency (2017-2023) was particularly marked by the challenges of the corona pandemic, by the efforts, together with the entire BIML team, to maintain the Organization's business continuity by introducing online meetings and introducing online voting tools. I am very grateful that this was achieved very successfully with the first online CIML meetings (2020-2022) and the first Conference (2021) in the history of the OIML, including the adoption of the budget for the period 2022-2025, the successful continuation of the OIML-CS and CEEMS AG activities, an expanded format of RLMO Round Table meetings, thanks to the efforts of the RLMO RT Chair and CIML First Vice-President Dr Charles Ehrlich, and not least the successful continuation of the technical work, especially of the high priority project groups, which has led to many new or revised publications being approved since 2020.

Also the new OIML Digitalisation Task Group (DTG) could be founded in 2021. This is in response to the growing need for metrology to become part of the international activities in the field of digital transformation of industrial and economic processes, to identify the particular aspects and needs of legal metrology, and to bring these into the relevant international committees. I sincerely wish this important OIML Task Group every success.

Finally, I am very grateful that my CIML presidency ended with an in-person CIML meeting in Thailand in

October 2023, so that I could meet the CIML Members personally after a break of four years, hand over the baton to my successor, Dr Bobjoseph Mathew, and enjoy the outstanding hospitality of our Thai colleagues. I would like to thank the great Thai hosts, who organized the 58th CIML Meeting extraordinary well, and thank all colleagues who gave me an unforgettable farewell by awarding me the OIML medal and OIML honorary membership. I would particularly like to thank Dr Mathew and wish him every success and the same great support I have received during my term.

It was great to serve the OIML for more than 20 years, to have met and worked together with so many great, knowledgeable and motivated colleagues over many years and to have been CIML President for the last six years of my career. Thanks for every kind support I have received from many colleagues in the PTB, notably Mrs Susanne Ludwig, in the OIML and beyond, and for the excellent cooperation I could enjoy with other international organizations, especially with the BIPM/CIPM, ISO, IEC, ILAC/IAF and WELMEC.

My wish is that the OIML can keep pace with the rapid developments in technology and other challenges, and I wish everyone who reads this happiness, peace and a good sense of humor to withstand all adversities.

## Second GULF Metrology Forum

The Second GULF Metrology Forum, Metrology for Sustainability will be held from 22-23 April 2024 in Dubai, United Arab Emirates. This highly anticipated event is set to take place under the patronage of the Ministry of Industry and Advance Technology (MOIAT), United Arab Emirates, and is organized by MOIAT and the GULF Association for Metrology GULFMET.

The event aims to provide a platform for experts to share their knowledge and ideas in the field of metrology to promote the exchange of experience among executives, experts, specialists, and technical personnel directly involved in the field of metrology.

The Second Gulf Metrology Forum has been organized in response to the latest international developments in metrology. It aims to align Gulf policies, practices, and trends in metrology with global developments in measurement techniques and their industrial applications under the theme "Metrology for Sustainability".

The Forum, divided into four sessions, will feature a wide range of activities including keynote speeches, expert panel discussions, and technical presentations. The event will bring together professionals, scientists, researchers, and regulators to discuss the latest developments and innovations in metrology. The four sessions are:

- Opening ceremony and keynote speakers

Metrology for Sustainable Environment

Metrology for Sustainable Manufacturing Industries

Metrology for Sustainability: The Strategic Direction of the Gulf Countries.

For more information, please contact Eng Omar Kanakrieh okanakrieh@gso.org.sa

GULFMET is the Gulf Association for Metrology, a Regional Metrology Organization. Its members are the National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) of seven countries: the United Arab Emirates, the Kingdom of Bahrain, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, the Sultanate of Oman, the State of Qatar, the State of Kuwait, and the Republic of Yemen.
