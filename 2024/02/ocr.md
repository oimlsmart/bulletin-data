Journée mondiale de la métrologie 2024 : La durabilité

La Journée mondiale de la métrologie est la célébration annuelle de la signature de la Convention du Mètre le 20 mai 1875. La célébration de cette année marque la reconnaissance officielle par l'UNESCO du 20 mai de chaque année comme Journée internationale de l'UNESCO.

Cette désignation crée de nouvelles opportunités pour promouvoir la métrologie et est en accord avec la mission de l'UNESCO de construire un monde meilleur par la science et l'éducation. Cette année, la Journée mondiale de la métrologie met l'accent sur la façon dont la métrologie contribue à la durabilité de l'économie mondiale et de l'environnement.

Dans cette édition du Bulletin de l'OIML, nous présentons une variété de points de vue du monde entier sur le rôle crucial que la métrologie peut jouer dans la réalisation des objectifs de durabilité.

L'amélioration de l'efficacité énergétique est notamment au cœur des objectifs de durabilité. Un des articles, par exemple, montre comment la recherche métrologique et la collaboration avec d'autres parties prenantes peuvent contribuer à l'optimisation de la consommation d'électricité.

Grâce aux mesures, nous pouvons à la fois quantifier avec précision l'utilisation des ressources et contribuer à réduire leur gaspillage. Un autre article illustre, à l'aide de l'exemple du recyclage du phosphore à partir des boues d'épuration, l'importante contribution que la métrologie peut apporter à la transition vers une économie circulaire. Il apparaît clairement que les nouvelles technologies et les nouveaux produits destinés à l'économie circulaire peuvent bénéficier de la recherche métrologique dans leur domaine.

L'importance de mesures précises ne peut être sousestimée. Un autre article « fait la lumière » sur les impacts significatifs de la pollution lumineuse sur la sécurité et la santé humaines, ainsi que sur l'écologie et la biodiversité. Il

devient évident que la capacité de mesurer et de quantifier correctement ces impacts est d'une importance capitale. L'utilisation d'un système métrologique solide et normalisé est nécessaire pour surveiller les indicateurs clés et documenter les effets, et aidera ainsi toutes les parties prenantes à améliorer l'environnement.

Il est en effet important de travailler avec d'autres organisations pour relever les défis du développement durable. Dans ce Bulletin, l'Organisation internationale de normalisation (ISO), avec laquelle l'OIML entretient un partenariat de longue date renforcé par un protocole d'accord, illustre comment les normes peuvent avoir un impact tangible et précieux sur le changement climatique.

À l'OIML et en collaboration avec d'autres organisations internationales, nous visons à diriger et à promouvoir le développement de méthodes de mesure plus durables par le biais de nos Recommandations (qui sont considérées comme des « normes » au sens de l'accord OTC de l'OMC) et à soutenir ainsi les pays et les économies dans leur ambition d'atteindre les objectifs de développement durable.

L'OIML et les métrologues du monde entier doivent poursuivre leur travail quotidien pour assurer la confiance dans les mesures et soutenir ainsi les économies dans leurs efforts pour devenir des sociétés durables.

Les différents articles de ce numéro illustrent de manière très attrayante et claire comment le travail de la communauté de la métrologie légale dans le domaine du développement durable et du changement climatique contribue à la réalisation de l'objectif du développement durable.

Le slogan de cette année est donc tout à fait valable : Mesurons aujourd'hui pour un avenir durable.

J'espère que vous apprécierez cette édition du Bulletin.

# World Metrology Day 2024: Sustainability

</div>

World Metrology Day is the annual celebration of the signature of the Metre Convention on 20 May 1875. This year's celebration marks the official recognition by UNESCO of 20 May each year as a UNESCO International Day.

This designation creates new opportunities to promote metrology, and is in accordance with UNESCO's mission to construct a better world through science and education. This year's World Metrology Day focuses on how metrology contributes to the sustainability of the global economy and the environment.

In this edition of the OIML Bulletin we present a variety of views from across the globe on the crucial role metrology can play in achieving the sustainability goals.

Energy efficiency improvement is notably in the focus of the sustainability goals. One article, for instance, demonstrates how metrological research and collaboration with other stakeholders can support the optimisation of electricity consumption.

Through measurements we can provide both accurate quantification of the use of resources, and also help reduce their waste. A further article illustrates, by using the example of phosphorus recycling from sewage sludge, the important contribution that metrology can make to the transition to a circular economy. It becomes clear that new technologies and products for a circular economy can benefit from metrological research in their field.

The importance of accurate measurements cannot be understated. An additional article "sheds light" on the significant impacts of light pollution for human safety and health, as well as ecology and biodiversity. It becomes

evident that the ability to measure and correctly quantify such impacts is of key importance. Using a solid and standardised metrological system is needed to monitor key indicators as well as to document the effects, and will thus help all stakeholders to improve the environment.

It is indeed important to work together with other organisations to tackle the challenges of sustainable development. In this Bulletin, the International Organization for Standardization (ISO), with whom the OIML has a long-standing partnership strengthened through a MoU, illustrates how standards can make a tangible and valuable impact on climate change.

At the OIML and in collaboration with other international organisations we aim to lead and promote the development of more sustainable measurement methods through our Recommendations (which are considered as "standards" in the sense of the WTO TBT Agreement) and thus support countries and economies in their ambition to deliver on the sustainable development goals.

The OIML and metrologists around the world must continue with their daily work to provide confidence in measurements and thereby support economies in their drive to become sustainable societies.

The various articles in this edition illustrate in a very engaging and clear way, how the work of the legal metrology community in the area of sustainable development and climate change, is an enabler for the achievements of the SDGs.

This year's strapline is therefore truly valid: We measure today for a sustainable tomorrow.

# Metrology of light pollution on a legal basis: Current and future challenges

</div>

CONSTANTINOS A. BOUROUSSIS $ ^{*} $ , FLORIAN STUKER, PETER BLATTNER

Federal Institute of Metrology METAS, Bern, Switzerland

## 1 Introduction

Light pollution is a generic term which is frequently used to describe the negative effects of artificial light at night. According to the International Lighting Vocabulary (ILV) [1] published by the International Commission on Illumination (CIE), light pollution is defined as the "sum total of all adverse effects of artificial light". Thus, it includes all aspects of artificial light that create issues with or disturb the surrounding environment. The term originates from the astronomy branch describing the increase in the background radiance of the sky at night which reduces the contrast and thus limits the observation capability. Even though photons or light are not pollutants per se, the term "light pollution" has been widely used by various stakeholders, mainly to increase awareness, create impact, for activism, and for political pressure.

Some of the most common effects of light pollution are

- sky glow (rise of sky radiance at night) due to upward and reflected light (Fig. 1a),

- glare produced by strong and concentrated light sources under a relatively darker background (Fig. 1b),

- spill light from outdoor and indoor installations directed towards areas that are not intended to be lit (Fig. 1c), and

- intrusive light that illuminates indoor spaces that emanates from highly illuminated vertical surfaces in buildings and dwellings (Fig. 1d).

All these effects and many more also have a directional, spectral and temporal character which complicates the issue even more. The source of these is mainly the mal-practices that are employed in lighting engineering such as their design, the use of inappropriate equipment, the unnecessary illumination of areas and facilities, and the limited use of lighting control during night time. There are also some unavoidable collateral effects such as the reflected light from intentionally illuminated areas.

The impact of light pollution can be observed in almost all aspects of the everyday (or rather "every night") lives of human beings, but it also affects flora and fauna. Starting from the most prominent and well observed issue of sky glow to the disturbance of sleep patterns due to intrusive light at night, a variety of negative issues have already been studied [2-4]. Some of the effects are related to short-term risk and safety such as glare emanating from light sources during driving, some are related to scientific research such as astronomy, and some are long-term effects such as those on the human circadian rhythm. The impact of light pollution in ecology and biodiversity is a topic that has already been under study for many years and which has revealed some alarming findings [5].

The common denominator in all the studies and assessment efforts of the impact from light pollution is the ability to measure and properly quantify it. So far, no common methods nor instrumentation have been established for the measurement of light pollution. Each stakeholder and discipline group uses its own method either as a result of their own expertise or as a limitation in their competence. This lack of metrology standardization limits the assessment of its impact, but on the other hand it creates an urgent need for an organized effort towards its establishment. Regarding the legislation aiming to mitigate the impact, currently there is no common legal framework and there are no internationally accepted regulations. However, several local laws or decrees have appeared in the past few years either within Europe or internationally, aiming to partially regulate outdoor lighting in respect to the reduction of light pollution [6]. In this context, an investigation and internationally coordinated effort for a legal basis on the limitation of light pollution could pave the way for the implementation of legal metrology regulations or guidance on light pollution.

## 3 Light pollution under legal metrology

## 3.1 Pathway to measurement standardization

Even though the measurement of visible radiation in many applications is already standardized, the extension of the scope for light pollution aspects needs some additional effort on the part of the standardization bodies and the stakeholders involved in the field. In order to develop a metrology system for light pollution, the following key parameters should be commonly agreed and standardized.

## Quantities and metrics

Since the measurement of light pollution refers to the measurement of light and visible radiation in general, the specific quantities should be selected or defined adhoc for specific applications. In addition, specific metrics can be derived from the measurement quantities for the qualitative assessment. For example, in glare measurement there are specific glare metrics which are derived from the measurement of the luminance contrast of a scene, whereas no commonly accepted metrics have yet been defined for sky glow measurement. The quantities shall be defined using SI-based units of their derivatives.

## Instruments

Measurement devices that can serve the needs of light pollution measurement quantities are more or less currently state of the art. In most cases, photometric instrumentation such as Imaging Luminance Measurement Devices (ILMDs) and photometers cover the majority of human based responsivities (photopic,

scotopic, tri-chromatic, etc.). Regarding the spectral domain, spectrometers or hyperspectral imaging devices can be used correspondingly. However, new dedicated instruments may be developed for specific light pollution measurement applications upon request of potentially new measurement quantities.

## Measurement methods

As described in the previous chapter, current measurement methods are driven by the needs of the relevant stakeholders, where in many cases the lack of expertise complicates the measurement procedure and can lead to the measurement results not being trustworthy or reproduceable. The majority of the standardization effort should therefore be invested in the development of the measurement methods for each one of the aspects of light pollution. In this respect, measurements shall follow the way in which light propagates in 3D space. The established standardized methods are limited to ground measurements from specific typical observer positions. The new methods should take into account the measurement above and around the lighting installations (e.g. aerial measurements using drones or planes) and dedicated geometries. The spectral, temporal and spatial characteristics should also be considered for the holistic quantification of light propagation. A similar multi-aspect approach should be followed for sky glow measurement and assessment.

## National Metrology Institutes

The National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) should play a key role in the establishment of the metrology for light pollution. The development of the corresponding Calibration and Measurement Capabilities (CMCs) will lead the effort for the wide acceptance of the methods and instrumentation. Following the normal metrology pyramid, industry and stakeholders will have NMIs as the reference point for the measurement of light pollution.

## Stakeholders

The diversity of stakeholders around the issue of light pollution, namely astronomers, lighting engineers, biologists, environmentalists, medical researchers and others, mandates a significant effort towards the education and spread of knowledge towards those groups. No system can succeed if there are no end users who recognize and accept it. The educational and dissemination activities shall be carried out by standardization bodies, NMIs, and relevant organizations and authorities.

## Standardization organizations

The connection means in this standardization effort are the organizations that prepare, publish and maintain the measurement standards and technical guides. The effort in this case is more collaborative and is shared among all the relevant stakeholders. The standardization effort will also play a key role in the potential establishment of a legal metrology of light pollution.

## 3.2 Challenges for legal metrology

A step beyond the standardization of light pollution measurements is the potential or even the need to include them in the scheme of legal metrology. The challenge in this case extends to both industry and to the regulatory bodies, but also to society and science in general. As legal metrology refers to the system of rules and laws governing measuring instruments and methods in specific cases related to safety and trade, this concept can be extended to light pollution metrology.

The key point in this case is the common understanding of the negative effects of light at night and that legislative mitigation measures can only be applied and validated through a well-defined metrology system. So far, several regional or national laws, decrees or guides in Europe mandate specific mitigation or prevention measures, e.g. the light pollution decree in France [14]. This can act as the basis for the development of specific regulations related to the measurement of light pollution aspects including methods and instrumentation. The overall target is the protection of the general public in terms of their safety and health, and environmental protection.

Since light propagates in all directions and is reflected by surfaces, it is rather complicated to regulate all of its aspects. However, key applications where light emissions tend to contribute to light pollution and can easily be characterized and may act as the first implementation of legal metrology.

## Common outdoor lighting applications

Road lighting plays a significant role in the safety and comfort of drivers, cyclists and pedestrians but at the same time can easily increase the issue of light pollution in terms of glare, spill light and upward directed light. The measurement of the light propagation around road lighting installations can be standardized using a combination of ground and aerial measurements, whereas relevant metrics can classify the impact of the installation towards its surroundings. The same concept also applies to sports facilities and industrial infrastructures. In all these cases, the responsible authority

or legal entity shall take responsibility for the lighting installations and the verification of their minimum possible impact. However, the latter is too complicated to be applied to private lighting.

## Accredited and trustable measurement methods and instruments

The measurement of light at night on the applications mentioned can be carried out at a commonly agreed standardized and accredited level. As described earlier in the text, the state of the art in light measurement for road, sports and other facilities is already well established for ground observers and should be extended to additional relevant observers in 3D space. This will offer the holistic quantification of light in a trustworthy and repeatable way.

## Research evidence on the impacts

## Legislation initiatives

In order to ensure the acceptance of mitigation measures for the enforcement of legal metrology for light pollution, a systematic research effort is needed to deliver good evidence on the impact of light at night on humans, flora and fauna. Due to the multidiscipline character of the impact, the research groups frequently need to collaborate in multi-thematic research projects. In recent years, a number of multi-discipline research projects have demonstrated the key impact of light under specific conditions to the affected areas, but more research results are definitely needed.

## 4 Dedicated work and research projects

Efforts to standardize light pollution measurements are already underway with several ongoing TCs and research projects at both national and international levels.

From the perspective of the NMIs, METAS is currently developing facilities associated with drones that allow for an integrated assessment of light pollution in 3D space. LNE, France is working on the assessment of upward lighting using airplanes and RISE, Sweden, is experimenting with light pollution metrology.

A dedicated research project [15] will begin in September 2024 in Switzerland under the funding of the Swiss National Science Foundation (SNSF) on the impact of light on the environment and society and the standardization of light pollution measurements. This project, named "Sustainability potential of outdoor light technology: trade-off between social needs and environmental impact, Spotlight", is a multi-discipline research project which aims to link outdoor lighting at night with its ecological impact and to investigate the effects of its mitigation strategies for society. The project involves researchers from METAS, the Swiss Federal Institute for Forest, Snow and Landscape Research (WSL), the University of Zurich (UZH), and the Electricity Company of Kanton Zürich (EKZ). The key expected outcome of the project related to measurements is to set the basis for the metrology of light pollution and the assessment tools for its impact. METAS aims to develop a holistic approach in the quantification and assessment of light pollution produced by artificial light at night by utilizing ground and aerial means of visible radiation measurement. Additional research projects around ecology and light pollution are funded by the EU Horizon program, EURAMET, or national level funding agencies. This is expected to provide additional understanding regarding the impact of light at night which is a key component in the legislation effort for mitigation measures.

CIE and CEN have formed dedicated technical committees to gather state-of-the-art knowledge and to propose the best possible measurement techniques given the current technology and, in parallel, to propose emission limits for various light pollution aspects. Below are some key TCs working on the measurement or limitation of light pollution, and it is expected that they will form the basis for the legislation effort towards its mitigation:

CIE TC4-58 "Obtrusive Light from Colourful and Dynamic Lighting and its Limitation"

## 5 Conclusions and outlook

Light pollution is a multi-aspect negative effect of artificial lighting at night which, under specific conditions, has a significant impact on safety, human health, and the environment. As in all cases, the impact can only be documented and justified using a solid and standardized metrological system that is accepted and adopted by all the relevant parties.

Mitigation of the negative impact shall be based on legislation and a systematic control of the measurement and verification chain, thus, close to the concept of legal metrology. To this day, scattered efforts in the standardization of light pollution have been made in terms of research projects and under the work of relevant technical committees. The pathway towards a proper metrology system takes the path of a well-organized effort on the part of international organizations, national agencies, and society itself. The legal metrology authorities should take over the initiatives from the relevant stakeholders and together with NMIs they should pave the way towards the establishment of a legal metrology system of light pollution.

The momentum seems to be promising given the parallel efforts of various countries to minimize the negative effects of light at night; however, the final target is still relatively far away.

## Abstract

With the use of sensor technology, it is possible to ensure that lights only switch on and off at the right time and only in the places where light is required. The sensNORM was provided as input to the IEC community, which published a standard in 2020 under the number IEC 63180:2020 [3].

Human activity in our modern civilization greatly affects the climate system, causing, among other effects, global warming. Hence, there is an urgent need to reduce energy consumption in order to reduce our carbon footprint and slow down global warming.

In 2021, 11 % of the total current electricity consumption in Switzerland was used for illumination [1]. Thus, using energy in an efficient way in the lighting industry will have a big impact on saving energy and reducing costs. Especially the combination of high efficiency LED light sources with motion and presence sensors will reduce consumption significantly.

Passive Infrared Sensors (PIR) are useful elements to control lighting in buildings as well as outdoors. The purpose of these detectors is to detect the movements of persons, and to switch on the light only if there is an actual need for it. In this way an optimum degree of comfort and energy efficiency can be achieved. Currently, a variety of different manufacturers worldwide produce PIR sensors for various ranges, geometries and applications (Figure 1). To achieve their efficiency targets, these sensors must operate accurately.

Unfortunately, the performance of presence and motion sensors are hardly comparable with each other, as every manufacturer uses different measurement methods since there is no standardized industry procedure [4].

Therefore, a standardized measurement procedure is needed to characterize these motion detectors and provide planning professionals with the correct data to reach an energy-optimized lighting solution. Manufacturers in the field of motion and presence detectors have worked together and developed sensNORM [2], a measurement procedure for PIR sensors that guarantees reliable and comparable planning data for building and lighting planners. The measurement procedures described in the sensNORM document were then used as a basis to for the development of the international standard IEC 63180 in 2020 [3].

## 2 sensLAB

sensLAB, the first manufacturer-independent laboratory capable of testing according to sensNORM and IEC 63180, was built and inaugurated at the Swiss Federal Institute of Metrology METAS in 2020 (Figure 2). These dummies simulate a human body and are equipped with heating elements that keep the head, body and legs at a constant temperature (Figure 3). head: 35 $ \pm1 $ $ ^{\circ} \mathrm{C} $

body and legs: $ 2 8 \pm1^{\circ} \mathrm{C} $

A heated and automated test arm simulates the forearm movement of a person. The temperature of the test arm is also set to $ 3 5 \pm1 \mathrm{~} ^ {\circ} \mathrm{C} $

The motion sensor is mounted on a rod with the required mounting height, and is attached to a rotation stage at the ceiling drive, which is aligned parallel to the radial axis.

## Radial motion

The dummy moves towards the detector until the sensor is triggered (Figure 5, no.1). The dummy position is registered and displayed in a polar diagram (Figure 7). The dummy moves a transversal distance perpendicular to the detector (Figure 5, no. 2). ## 1 Abstract

The transition to a circular economy will place new demands on metrology. In this article, we discuss the recovery of phosphorus from sewage sludge for the production of recycling fertilizers. With diminishing reserves of phosphate rock, new solutions for the production of phosphorus fertilizers are essential to ensure an efficient and safe global food production system. Today, new technologies are being developed to recover phosphorus from sewage sludge. Further analytical research and adaptation of standard analytical methods are required to ensure that the new products, the recycling fertilizers, do not pose a risk to the environment and that they comply with legal limits for toxic elements.

Phosphorus is a vital element for all organisms as it is a key component of the DNA present in every living cell. Together with other essential elements such as nitrogen and potassium, it enables plant growth and is therefore crucial for food production worldwide. While the supply and dissolution of the phosphorus pool naturally present in soils is slow, intensive agricultural production requires the rapid replenishment of this element to keep sufficient soil phosphorus available for plants. However, the regular application of fertilizers has a significant impact on ecosystems. In particular, when the excess of nutrients enters natural waters, it leads to a deterioration of their natural equilibrium. The excess of phosphorus in lakes, rivers and oceans increases the growth of microorganisms and algae that eventually deplete the oxygen content in the water. This process, which is called eutrophication, causes an abrupt decrease of the biodiversity in water bodies. Wastewater treatment prevents the direct introduction of one of the most important sources of phosphorus into the environment and is therefore crucial for ecosystems around the world.

However, the current system of phosphorus fertilization presents a second major problem: the scarcity of primary phosphorus resources. Today, phosphorus is mainly extracted from mined phosphate rock, whose reserves are concentrated in a few regions of the world. The world's largest phosphate exporters are Morocco, Western Sahara, China and the USA, which means that most countries are completely dependent on phosphate imports. In addition, phosphate rock is a finite natural resource, recently identified by the EU as a critical raw material (European Commission, 2023) and estimated to be exhausted within the next 50-100 years (Cordell et al., 2009; Steen, 1998). Due to the growing world population, the demand for these fertilizers has increased dramatically, by 50-100 % in the last 15 years (Cordell et al., 2009; EFMA, 2000).

In addition to this availability issue, mineral fertilizers produced from phosphate rock also often contain significant amounts of toxic elements such as uranium, thorium and cadmium (Abbes et al., 2020). These toxic elements accumulate in agricultural soils and crops, ultimately threatening human health.

## 3 The recycling of phosphorus from sewage sludge

To address these fertilizer-related problems, phosphorus use can be reduced by a more efficient application of fertilizer only during the period when plants need it. However, due to the increasing demand for fertilizers and the diminishing reserves of phosphate rock, new solutions for phosphorus sourcing are needed. Today, large amounts of phosphorus are lost in manure, waste (especially in slaughterhouse waste) and wastewater. The latter seems particularly promising for the recovery of this valuable element as it contains concentrated phosphorus. In addition, wastewater collection and treatment are centralized and well organized in many

It is estimated that about 3 megatons of phosphorus are lost in wastewater worldwide each year, which represents about 15 % of the global phosphorus use (~44 megatons) (Qadir et al., 2020). In some European countries, where wastewater collection is carefully organized, the percentage of phosphorus precipitated from wastewater and ending up in the sewage sludge would cover up to 50 % of the annual phosphorus import (see below).

In order to avoid the eutrophication of natural waters, phosphorus is precipitated in wastewater treatment plants using a precipitation agent (usually iron or aluminum). This means that most of the phosphorus is concentrated in the sewage sludge. However, the direct use of sewage sludge as fertilizer is highly controversial because it contains significant amounts of organic and inorganic pollutants. In Switzerland, for example, the use of sewage sludge as fertilizer is totally prohibited. Many countries are therefore looking for other solutions to reuse the phosphorus of sewage sludge in fertilizers.

Several European countries have thus already decided to make phosphorus recovery from sewage sludge mandatory in the near future. This will be the case in less than ten years in Germany and Switzerland for example, while in the Netherlands the Dutch Phosphate Value Chain Agreement is also driving the

rapid development of phosphorus recycling. Several technologies are therefore under development to recover phosphorus from sewage sludge for its reuse as mineral fertilizer.

One option for recovering phosphorus from wastewater is to precipitate it as struvite $ \mathrm{(M g N H_{4} P O_{4} \cdot 6 H_{2} O)} $ by adding magnesium to wastewater. Struvite is a valuable slow-release fertilizer with a high bioavailability. In Europe, it is well accepted as a recycling fertilizer and as such is also recognized by the EU fertilizer ordinance. But unfortunately, the reaction of struvite precipitation is not efficient enough to capture all the phosphorus present in the wastewater and most of it still ends up in the sludge.

Therefore, other recycling methods are desired especially to recover the large phosphorus pool in sewage sludge. Sewage sludge ash (SSA) is produced by the incineration of sewage sludge and has a high potential for phosphorus recycling. In many European countries (such as Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium Poland, Austria and Switzerland), sewage sludge is incinerated in large quantities, resulting in a large production of SSA. Incineration removes the water from the sludge, thereby increasing the concentration of phosphorus, and at the same time it destroys organic pollutants. It has been estimated that about 19000 tons of phosphorus could be recovered from SSA in Germany each year (Krüger & Adam, 2015). In Switzerland,

In recent years, two different types of industrial processes have been explored to extract phosphorus from SSA, separate it from toxic elements and increase its availability to plants (e.g., Ash2Phos, TetraPhos, Phos4Life and many more).

The first type of phosphorus recycling process is based on the leaching of the ash in order to concentrate the phosphorus (i.e., Phos4Life, 2023). In some processes hydrochloric acid leaching additionally allows the recovery of iron. The resulting product is phosphoric acid, which has a high bioavailability and can be easily transformed into recycling fertilizers (in the form of pellets or liquid fertilizer). There are various processes using different acids and temperatures, all aimed at achieving high phosphorus recovery rates while eliminating the toxic elements. The recycling of phosphorus offers this solution. Additionally, phosphorus recycling from sewage sludge for the production of recycling fertilizers, is an exemplary circular economy technology. It therefore also inscribes itself in the 12th UN goal which aims at responsible consumption and production.

## 4 New challenges for metrology

With these novel phosphorus recycling techniques, new products, the recycling fertilizers, will be introduced to the market. Both the production and control of these novel recycling fertilizers present new analytical challenges. Although the industrial processes of phosphorus recovery aim at eliminating toxic elements from the SSA, it is nevertheless crucial to monitor them in the ashes as well as in the final products to ensure the correct operation of the industrial process and to avoid soil pollution and eventually exposing wildlife and human beings to these toxic elements.

On one hand, the composition of the SSA needs to be controlled on a regular basis prior to phosphorus extraction in order to optimize the production of a safe phosphoric acid. The chemical composition of SSA can vary significantly between locations and over time, which means that phosphorus recovery processes need to be adapted locally and over the year. After the delivery

of SSA to phosphorus recovery plants, the content of phosphorus and toxic elements must be determined in order to assess the suitability of each batch for the recycling process. This requires the availability of standardized methods and adapted reference materials to ensure the quality of these measurements, which are crucial for the functioning of the recycling stations. As these problems related to the matrix of SSA have gained a lot of interest in recent years, the BAM (Bundesanstalt für Materialforschung und -prüfung, Germany) has just recently produced two different certified reference materials (CRM) for SSA (BAM, 2022): an iron-rich SSA and an aluminum-rich SSA. This is because the precipitant (Al versus Fe) varies depending on the type of wastewater treatment plant and accordingly will be more abundant in the resulting sewage sludge. The mass fractions of some major (Al, Ca, Cu, Fe, K, Mg, Na, P and Zn) and minor elements (As, Cd, Cr, Ni, Sn and Pb) are certified in both CRMs. The peculiarity of these CRMs is that the mass fractions have been certified twice:

the first set of values was obtained by digestion with aqua regia (a mixture of HCl and $ \mathrm{H N O}_{3} $ ), which corresponds to the sample preparation method defined by the European norm;

the second set of values corresponds to the total mass fractions of the elements, including their residual fractions that are not extracted by aqua regia, and were obtained by a total digestion of the samples (with HF and $ \mathrm{H N O}_{3} $).

This first production of CRMs representative for SSA by the BAM already shows some of the challenges for metrology in the field of phosphorus recycling. The SSA matrices are very heterogeneous and the different practices in each country or even region increase these differences. In addition, the norms are often not yet fully adapted to phosphorus recovery techniques. Therefore, a new project has been launched at METAS to produce a CRM representative of an average Swiss SSA, covering the range of matrices and levels of toxic elements that are relevant in our country.

On the other hand, the final products are regulated by the European Fertilizing Products Regulation (Regulation (EC) 2019/1009 of the European Parliament and of the Council, 2019), which lists maximum contents of toxic elements such as cadmium, mercury, lead and arsenic for recycling fertilizers. Standardized methods for determining the mass fractions of these elements in recycling fertilizers are therefore required. Although these products are similar to mineral fertilizers, for which standard methods exist, the matrices are different (i.e., different proportions of major elements) and therefore the methods and certified reference materials must be adapted. The different composition of recycling fertilizers compared to mineral fertilizers also raises new questions. For example, the

relatively high concentrations of copper and zinc in SSA and thus in recycling fertilizers, raise questions about the role of these elements either as micronutrients or as toxic elements (Kaur & Garg, 2021; Mir et al., 2021). Both of these elements are essential for plants and humans at lower concentrations, while higher concentrations are toxic. The impact of recycling fertilizers on the balance of copper and zinc, as well as other elements in soils, remain an open question.

In addition, not only are the total concentrations of essential and toxic elements important, but also their speciation, i.e., their chemical forms (e.g., their redox state or the mineral phases with which they are associated). The European regulation is based on total concentrations, and in some rare cases, on water soluble concentrations of toxic elements. This means that the bioavailability and the solubility of the elements are not completely taken into account. The speciation of an element influences its fate after its introduction into soils, as it determines its solubility, mobility and bioavailability.

As mentioned above, it is crucial that essential elements in fertilizers, such as phosphorus, have a high bioavailability so that they can be easily taken up by plants. Similarly, the speciation of toxic elements has an important influence on their toxicity. A water-soluble element will quickly migrate from the fertilizer into the soil pore water and eventually be transported to groundwaters or rivers. If the element is toxic, it will thus easily pollute the environment. A high bioavailability will determine whether a toxic element is easily taken up by plants, which increases the toxicity of the element.

The role an element plays in a plant, animal or human organism can also depend on its chemical state. For example, hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)) is classified as carcinogenic and highly toxic (International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), 2012), while trivalent chromium (Cr(III)) is an essential trace element for

humans (Dayan & Paine, 2001). The European Fertilizing Products Regulation (Regulation (EC) 2019/1009 of the European Parliament and of the Council, 2019) therefore specifically sets a maximum content of Cr(VI) in fertilizers. The chemical state of chromium in SSA and recycling fertilizers has been investigated throughout Germany (Krüger et al., 2017; Vogel et al., 2014; Vogel, Hoffmann, Krüger, et al., 2020). However, a more comprehensive monitoring covering SSA and recycling fertilizers from other countries as well as a standardized method for the determination of Cr(VI) is lacking. Another example is uranium for which the oxidation state U(VI) is generally more mobile and bioavailable than U(IV). It is therefore important to obtain more data on the chemical state of uranium in recycling fertilizers, even though the uranium concentration in recycling fertilizers seems to be generally lower than in the mineral fertilizers (Vogel, Hoffmann, Taube, et al., 2020). Overall, knowledge on the speciation of toxic elements in SSA and recycling fertilizers is scarce (Vogel, Hoffmann, Taube, et al., 2020). Additionally, to date standardized methods for the determination of elemental species in SSA and recycling fertilizers are still lacking.

A well-established approach for testing the solubility and the bioavailability of an element is the sequential solubility test. The EU Fertilizing Products Regulation (Regulation (EC) 2019/1009 of the European Parliament and of the Council, 2019) requires that the solubility of mineral phosphorus fertilizers should be determined using neutral ammonium citrate and water extractions. To date, no standard methods have been established to determine the bioavailability of toxic elements in recycling fertilizers. Existing analytical methods need to be tested with these new materials of SSA and recycling fertilizers to see if they are suitable for these matrices. Krüger et al. (2017) tested the wet chemical extraction of Cr(VI) and Cr(III) in these new matrices. As the bioavailability of essential as well as toxic elements in

recycled fertilizers may differ significantly from the availabilities in mineral fertilizers, it is necessary to further investigate the speciation of relevant elements in recycling fertilizers.

In a new project at METAS, existing speciation methods using high performance liquid chromatography coupled to inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (HPLC-ICPMS) will be tested for their suitability to these matrices and, if necessary, optimized. The coupling of HPLC with ICP-MS instruments is becoming increasingly available in industrial and control laboratories. It allows low detection limits (sub $ \mu\mathrm{g}\mathrm{L}^{-1} $ ) to be achieved with high precision ( $ \leq5\% $ for the detection) and species-specific isotopic dilution to be applied for multi-isotopic elements.

## 5 Conclusions and outlook

Metrology can make an important contribution to new knowledge on the determination of the mass fraction of relevant elements and of their elemental species in SSA and recycled fertilizers. Our role is not only to monitor toxic elements in the new fertilizer form, but also to define the appropriate standard analytical methods and provide the necessary tools, such as an adapted CRM, for the establishment of these methods in the laboratories.

This example of phosphorus recycling from sewage sludge shows the important contribution that metrology can make to the transition to a circular economy. New technologies and products for a circular economy can benefit from metrological research in their field. New CRMs of new matrices of interest as well as updated standard methods for the new products can contribute significantly to the transition to a circular economy.

## 1 Metrology: The science of measurement

The main goal of metrology is to ensure accuracy in measurements. Although cracking the mysteries of the atmosphere may seem more glamourous, metrology is just as intriguing and essential to day-to-day life. Metrology provides us with a common understanding of measurement units and is used all across the world to preserve the accuracy of the readings produced by measurement equipment. To ensure the precision of the measurements we rely on every day, from the moment we wake up until the time we go to bed, metrology is used subtly in the background. It is necessary for a wide range of sectors, including industry, scientific research, engineering, and healthcare. Metrology ensures that measurements accurately reflect the quantities under evaluation by employing a range of methods, tools, and standards.

## 2 A reliable foundation

Metrology is a global practice that ensures measurement equipment accuracy, uniformity, and traceability. It provides a benchmark for precision and credibility, creating a traceable calibration chain that connects a measuring instrument's accuracy to the SI Units. Accuracy is the degree of agreement between a measured value and the actual value, and metrology reduces errors and deviations by using traceable reference standards. Reproducibility is achieved through standardized processes, trustworthy equipment, and adherence to accepted principles. Metrology promotes collaboration, allows meaningful comparisons, and ensures accurate measurements in various applications, including industry, healthcare, and research.

## 6 Metrology for sustainable hydrogen energy applications

Hydrogen has been promoted as a clean fuel alternative that contributes to energy and climate goals, enhances air quality, and lowers noise levels. However, hydrogen fuel cells can degrade quickly in the presence of impurities, even at very low concentrations. The EU passed the 2014 Directive on the Deployment of Alternative Fuels Infrastructure in an effort to promote the use of cars that run on hydrogen. Hydrogen refueling stations are required to comply with the International Organization for Standardization (ISO)

standard ISO 14687-2:2012, which concerns fuel purity for proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells in road cars. To obtain the extremely low detection limits of the standard, however, confirmed measuring techniques along with a modification were needed, as the original standard was too broad to keep up with fuel cell performance developments. These techniques also required the range of hydrogen storage strategies in use to be taken into account, as well as the practical constraints posed by unstable or reactive contaminants. There was also a need for precise techniques to calculate the mass of hydrogen absorbed by storage tanks.

Hydrogen quality specifications were developed considering the possibility that impurities might be found in the three main ways to produce hydrogen and assessing the possibility that they could damage fuel cells. Both ISO 14687 and ISO 19880-8, whose 2019 update was influenced by the risk assessment, were developed using the outputs. Additionally, the impacts of $ \mathrm{N H}_{3}, $ HCl, and $ \mathrm{C_{4} C l_{4} F_{6}} $ pollutants on the long-term performance of PEM fuel cells were examined. Optimized analytical methods for impurity analysis, including multi-component analyzers, were developed in order to contribute to ISO 21087:2019. Traceable mass absorbed during storage was also quantified, and one of these measurements has an ISO 16111 standard proposed for it.

# Sustainability - What does it mean to metrology

</div>

DR PAMELA DAGG

Director of Legal Metrology

NSAI Principal Scientific Officer

NSAI Legal Metrology Services, Dublin, Ireland

## 1 Introduction

Sustainability is a term that is commonly used in our daily lives, but does anyone really know what it means or is it just another buzz word that everyone uses? Sustainability means simply living within our planet's resources and re-using resources and materials so that our natural resources are not exhausted.

How do you or your organisation become sustainable is the question that everyone needs to answer. How does this impact the OIML and the wider metrology world? And how do we (as metrologists) play a pivotal role in helping organisations, communities, countries, and individuals to become sustainable so that future generations can continue to live and enjoy this beautiful planet that we all call home? We all have a role to play in achieving sustainability and as metrology professionals we play a crucial role in achieving this goal.

Accurate measurement is the fundamental requirement for all scientific data, and metrology provides the vessel for the accurate and reliable measurement of data. Metrology is an essential component for climate science, as metrology provides the data which climate scientists and governments use to establish baselines and to set targets to enable us as citizens, governments, or organisations to achieve our common goal of being a sustainable society.

Legal metrology ensures legal and accurate measurements enabling consumer, business, and economic confidence. Metrology plays a pivotal role throughout society, including:

supporting the local and global economy;

supporting innovation and technical development; and

## 2 Global economy role

assisting in protecting the environment.

Every day consumers, traders and industry make decisions which are based on measurement information and data. These measurements and assigned values can affect global economies and wealth. A well-functioning economy has confidence in these metrology measurements/data, which provide transparency for consumers, businesses, and regulators. These metrology functions range from scientific and industrial to legal, and are essential pillars of the quality infrastructure that, among other things:

help ensure fair trade;

support and improve public safety;

provide consumer and business protection;

support scientific and technological innovations;

- support economies and government through revenue collection for tax and duties;

- support business through fees for services, such as taxi metering;

support utility metering: water, gas, and electricity;

support the provision of accurate measurements and analytical results, etc. in the health sector;

drive resource control and protection of the environment; and

- enable sustainable societies, organisations and countries.

Metrology plays a key role in global economies, as the value which is placed on our natural resources and commodities (such as gold, other precious metals, etc.) requires accurate quantification through measurement. It is acknowledged in the financial world that the environment and natural resources are valuable assets both for countries and organisations alike. These valuable assets have seen the introduction of corporate social responsibilities, natural capital reporting, in addition to sustainable development goals as a mechanism to promote sustainability and environmental protection.

## 3 Supporting innovation and technical development

The metrology system is a key resource for effective innovation and, more widely, for economic performance. Metrology, standards and accreditation are all deeply embedded in the modes of innovation practice across industry and commerce and in the public sector. They are complementary to, and supportive of, the other

drivers of innovation, such as new technologies knowledge from the research base, organisational and managerial changes, and marketing strategies.

National Measurement Systems are part of, and directly support several types of innovation polices, and strategy and can have a distinct impact on productivity. The innovation and efficiency-promoting roles of the infrastructure contribute to economic growth and productivity as well as to international competitiveness. Results of the research in metrology science and technology can stimulate new product development in the scientific and precision instrument and equipment sector, which then 'fans out' into use in industry, raising productivity through improved process and quality control. Accurate measurement "standards" underpin the technical standards that help to spread new and improved technologies through businesses and support international trade.

Measurement data underpin a wide range of public goods and services, including consumer protection (legal metrology), forensic science, environmental controls, safe medical treatment and food safety regulation.

## 4 Protecting the environment

Monitoring the environment is a key factor for measuring our impact on the planet and assessing how sustainable a society we are. Metrology, through accurate reliable measurements, allows for the quantification of our planet resources and their depletion, which can then be used to assess our sustainability. Metrology provides the mechanism for performing accurate measurements which quantify the emissions of our activities on the planet, and which are critical for climate science and climate impacts. The importance of accurate measurement cannot be understated as it forms the basis of our climate baseline and sets the net zero targets for each country/ organisation and in turn for each individual so we can protect our planet, future generations and ultimately our ability to survive on this planet. These metrological data assist climate scientists and governments in the formulation of climate action policies. These decisions made by governments which affect both the environment and how we live require the support of accurate traceable measurements. These policies and decisions can impact how we live, such as:

control of natural resources;

accurate assessment of environmental quality and indicators such as water, air and soil quality testing; and

transport.

## 5 How can the OIML assist?

The OIML plays an important role in assisting society, organisations and government in becoming sustainable. Innovation for sustainability has experienced rapid growth including the use of alternative sustainable fuels for domestic and commercial transport throughout our planet. The OIML has an important role to play in supporting national and international policies for a more sustainable, safer, and better future for everyone and our planet.

The OIML can lead, promote, and co-ordinate metrology organisations to develop safe and accurate universal measurement protocols for these alternative sustainable fuels. The OIML can also lead in the development and promotion of new and more sustainable measurement mechanisms to support government policies and industry which includes:

non-destructive testing where feasible, for example concerning packaged food goods, e.g. reduction in food waste and packaging waste;

carbon measurement (supporting net zero requirements and reducing green washing);

assisting in creating a circular economy by promoting the repair, reuse, recycling, and designing of more environmentally friendly and sustainable measuring meter and tests; and

- placing a value on natural assets and not just products.

Sustainability requires everyone to become involved and to play their part in providing a sustainable future for all. Governments, industry, society, and individuals all play an important role in this, with measurement being a critical part of achieving sustainability as without accurate measurements, baselines cannot be relied upon or targets achieved.

The OIML and metrologists around the world will continue to provide confidence and assurance in measurements and provide consumers and businesses with confidence, while supporting national and international policy as we all strive to be a sustainable society.

## Abstract

The Cuban experience in the establishment and implementation of public policies and legal norms on standardization, metrology, quality and accreditation including the formation and upgrading of potential human competencies in these fields and complement a governance management system built on science and innovation to guarantee decision-making based on data derived from safe, reliable and comparable measurements of various aspects of sustainability and to meet the Sustainable Development Goals, is presented.

Keywords: Science, innovation, metrology, sustainable development, sustainability

## 1 Introduction

According to data provided this year by the Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean (ECLAC) [1], most of the expenditure on science, technology and innovation programs and policies in the countries of the region of the Americas is assumed by the States. This expenditure is executed essentially by higher education institutions and certain research centers, unlike common practice in most developed countries, where companies are the main entities that finance and manage research and development expenses.

As a result of this particularity, this region must decisively implement public policies leading to a disruptive change in the ways of developing goods and services in sectors deemed strategic. The aim is to foster socioeconomic growth and process sustainability, supported by a coherent national quality infrastructure and highly qualified human resources.

In every country, the success, competitive advantage or failure of an undertaking depends on human potential as its most important resource. Human skills and abilities will have an impact on the quality and quantity of the results and the scientific activity of an organization, as well as on its capacity to both innovate and generate high value-adding goods and services which contribute to the national and global economy.

As the science of measurements and its applications, metrology is one of the constituent parts of the National Quality Infrastructure, as shown in Fig.1. Metrology delivers valid scientific evidence for data-based decision making and thus provides for the base of sustainable public policies from various points of view, especially those related to consumer protection, given by the requirement to comply with standards, technical regulations and good practices, increased business competitiveness, and access to international markets.

Given the intimate relationship that exists between the development of science and innovation and the measurement capabilities of a country, counting on highly qualified professionals in those fields is not enough to guarantee real and effective progress in the most advanced areas of knowledge or in the frontiers of knowledge. It is also necessary to develop measurement standards and methods that are traceable to the International System of Units (SI), to boost university-business-research center collaboration, to encourage the transfer of knowledge to the production and business sector, and to increase public investment in research and technological innovation with a view to the development, improvement and international recognition of the measurement capabilities of the National Metrology Service.

First of all, however, it is essential to make a greater effort to upgrade the metrological competencies of officials with decision-making power so that their performance can contribute to strengthening confidence in the goods and services produced by the country, promoting sustainable development, and ensuring social welfare.

The CIPM task group builds upon the long-standing collaboration with the World Meteorological Organization (WMO), and comparative measurements with the WMO's designated standards' laboratories which have participated in the CIPM's Mutual Recognition Arrangement since 2010. The WMO and the BIPM have organized a number of workshops to identify requirements and to progress measurement science in the field, with the most recent having been held in 2022 on

The granting of observer status to the BIPM at the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) during COP27 in 2022 represented a significant milestone in the engagement of the metrology community in climate action. The BIPM participated in COP28, represented by Dr Anna Cypionka, Director of the BIPM's International Liaison and Communication Department, and Dr Edgar Flores, Executive Secretary of the Sectorial Task Group, which took place at Expo City, Dubai, United Arab Emirates, from 30 November to 12 December 2023, and which was attended by over 70 000 people.

initiatives in 'Metrology in support of the physical science basis of climate change and climate observations' and 'Metrology as an integral component of operational systems to estimate greenhouse gas

emissions based on accurate measurements and analyses'. The call for abstracts is open until 31 May 2024 and further information can be found at https://bipm-cenv2024.org/.

# Renewal of the IAF/ILAC/OIML MoU

</div>

BIML

A renewed IAF/ILAC/OIML MoU has been signed by Ms Etty Feller (ILAC Chair), Mr Emanuele Riva (IAF Chair), and Dr Bobjoseph Mathew (CIML President)

The International Laboratory Accreditation Cooperation (ILAC), the International Accreditation Forum (IAF), and the International Organization of Legal Metrology (OIML) renewed their tripartite Memorandum of Understanding (MoU), which was first signed on 28 October 2007. The latest version, signed on 8 February 2024, marks over 16 years of formalised cooperation between the three organisations, and reconfirms their commitment to close collaboration which includes:

facilitating common approaches in the interpretation and implementation of standards;

harmonising assessment procedures;

- sharing interpretations of common issues relevant to conformity assessment bodies; and

establishing lists of and encouraging the use of legal metrology experts (LMEs) in accreditation and certification, and management system experts in OIML peer assessments.

The renewed MoU supersedes the MoU signed in October 2018 and it is intended to build on and enhance the long-standing relationships and excellent cooperation between the IAF, ILAC and the OIML. It contains improvements to the Articles associated with the implementation of the MoU through the appointment of Liaison Officers and the communication of information of relevance to areas of cooperation.

The renewed MoU continues to support the operation of the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS) and the evaluation of the competence of the OIML Issuing Authorities and Test Laboratories under Scheme A which shall be demonstrated by accreditation or peer assessment.

signatory to the IAF Multilateral Recognition Arrangement (MLA) or to a regional arrangement recognised by the IAF, for the scope of "Product - ISO/IEC 17065".

For the OIML Issuing Authority, when accreditation to ISO/IEC 17065 is chosen the accreditation body that carries out the assessment shall be an IAF Full Member

For the Test Laboratory, when accreditation is chosen the accreditation body that carries out the assessment shall be a signatory to the ILAC Mutual Recognition Arrangement (MRA) or to a regional arrangement recognised by ILAC for the scope of "testing ISO/IEC 17025".

When peer assessment is chosen, the assessment team shall comprise Management System Experts (team leaders) and Legal Metrology Experts from the ILAC IAF-OIML list of experts.

A Joint Assessment Procedure has also been developed between the IAF and the OIML for accreditation assessments of OIML Issuing Authorities according to ISO/IEC 17065, and a Joint Assessment Procedure has also been developed between ILAC and the OIML for accreditation assessments of Test Laboratories according to ISO/IEC 17025. These are available to download from the OIML-CS pages of the OIML website.

The renewed MoU may be downloaded as a PDF file from the "About/Memoranda of Understanding" section of the OIML website.

The renewed ILAC/IAF/OIML MoU was electronically signed by each signatory.

# Second Gulf Metrology Forum - Metrology for Sustainability

</div>

BIML

The Second Gulf Metrology Forum was held in Dubai on 22-23 April 2024. The event took place under the patronage of the Ministry of Industry and Advance Technology (MOIAT), United Arab Emirates, and was organised by MOIAT and the GULF Association for Metrology (GULFMET). The OIML supported the event, and played an important role in the Scientific Committee which was tasked with developing the technical programme for the event.

The Second Gulf Metrology Forum was organised in response to the latest international developments in metrology, with the aim of aligning Gulf policies, practices, and trends in metrology with global developments in measurement techniques and their industrial applications under the theme Metrology for Sustainability. The event was intended to contribute to the development of metrology activities in the Gulf region with the expectation that it would to lead to significant improvements in this field in the coming years.

The main focus of the forum was to raise awareness about the importance of metrology and its impact on economic growth, industrial development, consumer protection, and sustainable development. By emphasising the role of accurate measurements in ensuring fair trade practices, the aim is to create a business environment that thrives on trust and reliability and to promote the adoption of international metrology standards and best practices among Gulf countries. Aligning the measurement systems with global standards will enhance the interoperability of industries, facilitate international trade, and improve the competitiveness of the Gulf countries on a global scale.

The Forum featured a wide range of activities, including keynote speeches, expert panel discussions, and technical presentations. The event brought together professionals, scientists, researchers, and regulators to discuss the latest developments and innovations in metrology. It provided an excellent opportunity for participants to learn about the latest measurement techniques, their applications, and metrology's role in sustainability.

Mr Paul Dixon, BIML Assistant Director, represented the OIML at this event. Mr Dixon gave a welcome address as part of the opening ceremony and he participated in two panels discussions.

The theme of the first panel discussion was The Role of Metrology in Shaping a Sustainable Future. Mr Dixon was joined on the panel by representatives from BIPM, IMEKO, EUROLAB, UNIDO and MOIAT, and the topic of his intervention during the panel discussion was Sustainability and circularity- principles and practices in legal metrology. The topic outlined the role of legal metrology in the circular economy and how legal metrology contributes to the achievement of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals.

The theme of the second panel discussion was The Strategic Direction of the Gulf Countries. Mr Dixon was joined on the panel by representatives from BIPM, MOIAT, SASO and the Emirates Metrology Institute, and the topic of his intervention during the panel discussion was OIML vision and strategy. This topic helped direct discussions on how the Gulf countries could benefit from the OIML strategy when developing their strategies associated with legal metrology and its role in the Quality Infrastructure.

The Forum was very successful and the event will help to strengthen regional cooperation in metrology, fostering partnerships among Gulf countries and sharing resources, expertise, and knowledge. By collaborating on metrological research, capacity building initiatives, and the development of common infrastructure, the Gulf countries can collectively address the challenges that they face and drive progress in the field of metrology.

Holding such an event, which brought together such a wide range of experts from across the metrology and Quality Infrastructure communities, has demonstrated a commitment to advancing the field of metrology and harnessing its potential for economic growth and supporting efforts to ensure a sustainable future.

# OIML Certification System (OIML-CS)

</div>

![](page=6,bbox=[828, 454, 1059, 683])

## Introduction

The OIML-CS is a system for issuing, registering and using OIML Certificates and their associated OIML type evaluation reports for types of measuring instruments (including families of measuring instruments, modules, or families of modules), based on the requirements of OIML Recommendations.

The OIML-CS comprises two Schemes: Scheme A and Scheme B. Competence of the OIML Issuing Authorities and their Test Laboratories is demonstrated through self-declaration under Scheme B and accreditation or peer assessment under Scheme A.

The aim of the OIML-CS is to facilitate, accelerate and harmonize the work of national and regional bodies that are responsible for type evaluation and approval of measuring instruments subject to legal metrological control. In the same way, instrument manufacturers, who are required to obtain type approval in some countries in which they wish to sell their products, should benefit from the OIML-CS as it will provide evidence that their instrument type complies with the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendation(s).

It is a voluntary system and OIML Member States and Corresponding Members are free to participate. Participating in the OIML-CS commits, in principle, the signatories to abide by the rules of the OIML-CS that are established in OIML B 18:2022 Framework for the OIML Certification System (OIML-CS). Signatories voluntarily accept and utilize OIML type evaluation and test reports, when associated with an OIML Certificate issued by an OIML Issuing Authority, for type approval or recognition in their national or regional metrological controls.

The OIML-CS was launched on 1 January 2018 and has replaced the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the OIML Mutual Acceptance Arrangement (MAA).

## OIML certificates

OIML certificates issued under Scheme A and Scheme B can be downloaded from the database on the OIML website at https://www.oiml.org/en/oiml-cs/certificat_view.

The database also includes certificates issued under the former OIML Basic Certificate System and the MAA. Although these two systems are no longer in operation, the certificates remain valid.

## The OIML Bulletin is one, if not the only, international publication dedicated to legal metrology topics.

In accordance with CIML Resolutions 2019/30 and 2020/21, there is a clear desire amongst OIML Members for the Bulletin to continue to be an attractive publication for legal metrology worldwide, and for it to be an excellent advertisement for our Organisation.

This has been achieved through long-term planning of the future editions and identification of key topics of high interest, for instance, legal control of measuring instruments in the fields of energy, health and the environment, where important aspects such as new technology, legal requirements, or test/verification procedures will be addressed.

In addition, support has been sought from CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who have taken on the responsibility of acting as "Mentors" for future editions, certain key topics, or specific technical articles. The role of "Mentors" is to support the Editor of the Bulletin by coordinating the development of articles for a key topic through the identification of authors / experts. It is not necessarily expected that articles are written by the "Mentors" themselves, but by experts that a "Mentor" has identified and contacted.

In order to identify key topics of significant interest for future editions and "Mentors" to lead them, the BIML prepares, and makes available on the OIML website (www.oiml.org/en/publications/bulletin/future-editions), a plan for the upcoming four to six editions of the Bulletin.

The table on the following page is intended to be "dynamic", i.e. proposed key topics may be moved to other editions depending on available "Mentors" and authors for technical articles.

All CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives are encouraged to support the OIML Bulletin, to share their legal metrology experiences with the legal metrology community worldwide, and to take responsibility either as a "Mentor" for one of the next editions of the Bulletin, or by promoting it at TC/SC/Project Group meetings, RLMO meetings, CEEMS AG meetings, and other opportunities.

CIML Members and Corresponding Member Representatives who would like to be a "Mentor" for a specific edition / key topic, or who would like to suggest that a new key topic be added to the list, are asked to contact the BIML (chris.pulham@oiml.org).

OIML D 31, edition 2023 (E) - was developed by Project Group 4 of OIML Technical Subcommittee TC 5/SC 2 Software.

OIML D 31 specifies the general requirements applicable to legally relevant software-related functionality and security in measuring instruments and gives guidance for verifying the compliance of an instrument with these requirements.

It was approved for final publication by the International Committee of Legal Metrology at its 58th meeting in 2023 and will be submitted to the International Conference on Legal Metrology in 2025 for formal sanction.

The primary aim of this International Document is to provide OIML Technical Committees and Subcommittees with guidance for establishing appropriate requirements for software-related functionalities in measuring instruments covered by OIML Recommendations.

Furthermore, this International Document can provide guidance to OIML Member States and Corresponding Members in the implementation of OIML Recommendations in their national laws.
